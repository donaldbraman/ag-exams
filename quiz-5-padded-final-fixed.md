## Stem 1: The Shelter Standoff

### Facts

You are an assistant public defender. Your supervising attorney hands you a case file.

**"We have a complex case involving three co-defendants. I need you to analyze the defense options for each. Here's what the investigation established."**

1. Philippa Foot is a 34-year-old social worker who operates a small emergency shelter for unhoused individuals in a converted warehouse in Portland, Oregon. The city issued a condemnation notice for the building, giving Foot thirty days to vacate. Foot believed that closing the shelter in winter would expose the twelve residents to life-threatening cold, and no alternative shelter beds were available anywhere in the city. On the day the condemnation order took effect, Foot refused to vacate and continued operating the shelter in violation of the order.

2. Andrey Markov, one of the shelter residents, has a documented history of paranoid schizophrenia. His psychiatric records confirm that he experiences command hallucinations and delusional episodes. On the evening of Day 15 of the standoff, Markov became convinced that a city building inspector, Abraham Wald, was a "government assassin" sent to poison the shelter's water supply. When Wald arrived the next morning to conduct a routine inspection, Markov confronted him in the doorway, pushed him to the ground, and struck him twice, causing a fractured wrist and bruised ribs. Markov later told police he believed he was "stopping an assassination." Markov's treating psychiatrist, Dr. Ayanna Cole, is prepared to testify that at the time of the assault, Markov was in an acute psychotic episode driven by his schizophrenia, that he genuinely believed Wald intended to kill the shelter residents, and that while Markov understood he was striking a person, he could not appreciate that his actions were wrong. Markov has also been diagnosed with antisocial personality disorder.

3. Oliver Wendell Holmes is Foot's partner. He is not a resident of the shelter but visits regularly. Holmes has a prior conviction for assault. On Day 18 of the standoff, a process server named Lisa Chen arrived at the shelter to deliver an eviction notice. Lisa approached the front entrance, where Holmes was standing. Lisa was carrying a clipboard and an envelope. She identified herself and said she was there to deliver legal papers. Holmes, who had heard Foot say earlier that day that "the city is going to send people to drag us out," believed Lisa was the advance team for a forced removal. Holmes shoved Lisa backward off the front steps, causing her to fall and break her arm. Lisa was five feet, two inches tall and weighed approximately 120 pounds. Holmes is six feet, one inch and weighs 210 pounds. Lisa made no threatening gestures or statements.

4. On Day 20 of the standoff, Holmes received a phone call from his former associate, Friedrich Nietzsche. Nietzsche told Holmes: "I know where your mother lives. If you don't bring me $5,000 by Friday, I'm going to hurt her bad." Holmes believed Nietzsche was serious because Nietzsche had previously hospitalized another person who owed him money. The following day, Holmes stole $5,000 in cash from the shelter's donation safe and delivered it to Nietzsche.

5. On Day 22, a city worker was killed during the forced eviction of the shelter. Assume, counterfactually, that Holmes shoved the city worker off the loading dock because Nietzsche had threatened to kill Holmes's mother if Holmes did not "make sure nobody gets into the shelter," and that the fall resulted in the worker's death.

6. **This jurisdiction follows the common law for necessity and duress. It follows the Model Penal Code for the insanity defense. For self-defense, unless otherwise specified, it applies an objective reasonableness standard with a duty to retreat for deadly force, a castle doctrine limited to the defendant's own dwelling, and does not recognize imperfect self-defense.**

**Q1.** Your supervisor asks you to evaluate Foot's strongest defense for her continued operation of the shelter in violation of the condemnation order (Facts 1 and 6). Which of the following is the most accurate analysis?

(a) Foot has a strong necessity defense because the harm avoided (twelve people exposed to life-threatening cold) clearly outweighs the harm caused (occupying a condemned building), the threat was imminent, and no reasonable legal alternative was available. <!-- correct -->
(b) Foot has a strong necessity defense because the common law formally exempts charitable actors from criminal liability whenever their actions are motivated by a sincere moral duty to assist vulnerable individuals during severe winter weather conditions.
(c) Foot has a strong necessity defense because under the Model Penal Code, the harm avoided need only be greater than the harm caused, and protecting twelve lives easily satisfies that threshold compared to the strict common law requirement of outweighing.
(d) Foot does not have a viable necessity defense because she is automatically deemed at fault for creating the emergency solely by operating a shelter in a building that was later condemned, and the common law bars the defense when the defendant is at fault.
(e) Foot does not have a viable necessity defense because the mere existence of general building safety codes and condemnation procedures categorically preempts the necessity defense by foreclosing any judicial balancing of competing harms in this specific case.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Necessity is the correct defense here. Under the common law (Fact 6), a defendant must show: (1) the harm avoided clearly outweighs the harm caused, (2) the threat is imminent, (3) no reasonable legal alternative exists, and (4) the defendant was not at fault in creating the emergency. Option (a) correctly applies these elements. Option (b) is incorrect because the common law contains no blanket exemption for charitable actors or moral motives; the strict necessity elements must be met. Option (c) is incorrect because Fact 6 specifies the jurisdiction follows the common law for necessity, not the MPC. Option (d) is incorrect because lawfully operating a shelter prior to its condemnation does not automatically constitute culpable fault in creating the weather emergency. Option (e) is incorrect because general building codes are not the type of particularized, specific legislative choice required to preempt the necessity defense.

**Q2.** Assume for the purposes of this question that Foot raises a successful necessity defense against the condemnation violation charge. Your supervisor asks whether this defense has any impact on the potential liability of volunteers who helped Foot keep the shelter open during the standoff (Facts 1 and 6). Under common law doctrine, which of the following best explains the relationship between Foot's defense and the volunteers' liability?

(a) The volunteers cannot share in Foot's defense because necessity is classified as a personal excuse under the common law, meaning each individual volunteer must independently establish their own excuse defense to avoid criminal liability.
(b) Because Foot's successful necessity defense renders her act legally justified rather than merely excused, the volunteers who assisted in that specific act share in the principal's justification and avoid criminal accomplice liability. <!-- correct -->
(c) The volunteers face criminal accomplice liability regardless of Foot's defense because the common law rule attaches upon purposeful assistance in prohibited conduct, completely precluding the transfer of any principal's legal defense.
(d) The volunteers share in Foot's defense only if each independently satisfies the elements of necessity, because derivative justification strictly requires every participating actor to independently prove they faced a choice of evils.
(e) The volunteers will automatically share in the defense because common law treats necessity as a lesser-evils excuse, and all personal excuses freely transfer to accomplices who act with the exact same purpose as the main principal.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** Necessity is a justification defense (a "choice of evils"), meaning the defendant's conduct is deemed legally permissible under the circumstances. Because justifications attach to the act itself rather than the actor, an accomplice who assists in a justified act commits no crime and derives the benefit of the justification. Option (a) is incorrect because necessity is a justification, not a personal excuse. Option (c) is incorrect because accomplice liability requires a legally wrongful act by the principal; if the act is justified, there is no crime to aid. Option (d) is incorrect because accomplices to a justified act do not need to independently satisfy the primary justification's elements to avoid derivative liability. Option (e) is incorrect because necessity is a justification, not an excuse, and common law excuses do not automatically transfer to accomplices.

**Q3.** Your supervisor turns to Holmes's liability for shoving Lisa Chen (Fact 3). Holmes claims self-defense. Under the common law standard of self-defense (as applied in *People v. Goetz*), which of the following is the most accurate analysis?

(a) Holmes has a valid self-defense claim because he honestly believed Lisa was part of a forced-removal team, and an honest, purely subjective belief in the need for protective force is legally sufficient to establish self-defense under the common law, regardless of whether a reasonable person would share that assessment in those exact circumstances.

(b) Holmes does not have a valid self-defense claim because a reasonable person in Holmes's situation—even after hearing Foot's warning about forced removal—would not have believed force was necessary to defend against an imminent use of unlawful force when faced with a small, unarmed woman identifying herself as a process server.

(c) Holmes has a valid self-defense claim because Foot's earlier statement that "the city is going to send people to drag us out" constitutes prior threat information that automatically makes any subsequent belief in danger objectively reasonable, legally overriding the need to evaluate the peaceful actions of the actual individual he encountered.

(d) Holmes does not have a valid self-defense claim solely because he failed to retreat before using force. The common law imposes a strict duty to retreat prior to any use of defensive force, whether deadly or non-deadly, meaning he was legally required to exhaust all safe avenues of escape before standing his ground.

(e) Holmes does not have a valid self-defense claim because his prior conviction for assault makes him a "person of violent character." Under the common law, anyone with a history of violent offenses is categorically barred from asserting an affirmative self-defense claim, regardless of the immediate circumstances they face.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** Self-defense requires an objectively reasonable belief that force was necessary against an imminent use of unlawful force. The standard asks what a reasonable person in the defendant's situation would have believed (*People v. Goetz*). Holmes's "situation" legally incorporates his knowledge of Foot's prior warning. However, a reasonable person who hears a warning about forced removal, then encounters a small, unarmed woman who verbally identifies herself as a process server, would not conclude that an imminent use of unlawful force is underway. Therefore, his belief was objectively unreasonable. Option (a) is incorrect because the common law requires objective reasonableness, not merely an honest (purely subjective) belief. Option (c) is incorrect because while prior threat information is incorporated into the circumstances, it does not "automatically" make a subsequent belief objectively reasonable. Option (d) is incorrect because the common law duty to retreat applies only before using deadly force, and Holmes used non-deadly force. Option (e) is incorrect because a prior assault conviction does not categorically bar a defendant from raising a self-defense claim.

**Tags:** chapters: [22], topics: [self-defense, objective-reasonableness, situational-incorporation, imminence], difficulty: easy, cognitive: application, trap: Students apply a purely subjective standard or assume any prior experience makes the belief reasonable

**Grounding:** Chapter 22, objective-reasonableness-standard refinement; People v. Goetz.

**Q4.** Returning to this jurisdiction's default rules (Fact 6), your supervisor asks about Holmes's claim that he stole the $5,000 under duress (Fact 4). Under the common law, which of the following is the most accurate analysis?

(a) Holmes has a valid duress defense because Nietzsche threatened serious bodily injury to Holmes's mother (a close relation), the threat was credible based on Nietzsche's history, and the common law's imminence requirement is satisfied whenever the threatening party has a documented pattern of carrying out threats, regardless of any deadline that places the threatened harm days in the future.

(b) Holmes does not have a valid duress defense because duress requires a threat directed at the defendant personally, and the common law does not extend the defense to threats against third parties, including family members. Even though the threatened harm was aimed at his mother, the traditional rules strictly mandate that the defendant must be the direct target of the severe injury.

(c) Holmes does not have a valid duress defense because the threat was not imminent -- Nietzsche gave Holmes until Friday, and the common law requires a present and immediate threat, not one that will materialize days later. Although the threat of severe harm targeted a close family member, the strict standard does not allow for a delay in the execution of the threatened physical injury.

(d) Holmes does not have a valid duress defense because duress is categorically unavailable for theft crimes under the common law, which limits the defense to offenses against persons. While the threat to his mother involved serious bodily injury, the strict traditional rules bar the application of this affirmative excuse whenever the defendant commits a property offense, regardless of coercion.

(e) Holmes has a valid duress defense because a threat need not be imminent so long as it is credible and specific, and Nietzsche's history of violence makes the threat both credible and specific enough to satisfy the common law standard. Even though the harm was scheduled for a future date, his documented pattern of enforcing ultimatums dispenses with the need for immediate temporal proximity.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** Under the common law (Fact 6), duress requires (1) a threat of death or serious bodily injury, (2) the threat is imminent, (3) the threat targets the defendant or a close relation, and (4) no reasonable legal alternative exists. Nietzsche's threat to "hurt her bad" satisfies the threat threshold and the target requirement (Holmes's mother is a close relation). However, the threat lacks imminence under the common law's strict standard. Nietzsche gave Holmes until Friday -- multiple days away. The common law requires a "present and immediate" threat, not one that will materialize days later. Option (a) falsely claims that the common law's imminence requirement is satisfied whenever the threatening party has a documented pattern of carrying out threats. This conflates the strict common-law rule with the *Dingwall* relaxation; under the common law, imminence requires a present and immediate threat, and a pattern of past violence does not substitute for temporal proximity. Nietzsche gave Holmes until Friday, defeating the imminence element. Option (b) states that the common law "does not extend the defense to threats against third parties, including family members" -- the false proposition is this scope limitation. The common law extends duress to threats against the defendant or a close relation, and a mother is a close relation. Option (d) fabricates a crime-type limitation -- the false proposition is that "duress is categorically unavailable for theft crimes." The common law murder bar applies only to homicide; duress is available for property crimes. Option (e) states that "a threat need not be imminent so long as it is credible and specific" -- the false proposition is this restatement of the imminence rule. The common law requires imminence as a separate element; credibility and specificity do not substitute for temporal immediacy.

Assume Nietzsche had explicitly commanded Holmes to steal the money, had threatened Holmes for months, and Holmes reasonably believed police were unable to protect his mother. Under this framework, can Holmes satisfy the imminence requirement? (Fact 4, modified.)

(a) Yes, because the exception establishes that any pattern of continuous, sustained, and unpredictable threats creates a reasonable perception of imminent danger under the law, regardless of the prior relationship between the coercer and the defendant.
(b) No, because the exception that relaxes the strict physical imminence requirement is specifically tied to the dynamics of battering and intimate coercive control, which do not apply to a generalized extortion dispute between former business associates. <!-- correct -->
(c) Yes, because the exception eliminates the imminence requirement entirely in such cases, allowing any credible threat of future harm to satisfy the defense if the threatening party has a documented track record of severe violence against the defendant.
(d) No, because the exception strictly applies only when the defendant is physically confined, completely isolated, or actively restrained by the threatening party at the exact moment the underlying criminal offense is being committed by the defendant.
(e) No, because the exception is strictly limited to cases where the threatened physical harm is directed at the defendant personally, and it cannot be invoked based on generalized threats made against a family member or other third-party relation.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** Under *United States v. Dingwall*, the strict physical proximity and imminence requirements of duress are relaxed for defendants experiencing the effects of battering or coercive control. However, courts do not extend this exception to generalized criminal extortion or associate disputes, as doing so would swallow the traditional strict imminence rule. Thus, (b) is correct. Option (a) falsely broadens the doctrine into a blanket exception for any sustained threats. Option (c) falsely asserts the rule eliminates the imminence requirement entirely. Option (d) falsely claims physical confinement is required; *Dingwall* expressly held the batterer's physical presence or confinement is not necessary. Option (e) falsely states that the exception cannot be based on threats to third parties; traditional duress principles covering threats to close relatives still apply.

**Q6.** Your supervisor asks you to analyze Markov's assault charge for attacking Wald under this jurisdiction's Model Penal Code (MPC) insanity framework. Markov acted under a schizophrenic delusion that Wald was an assassin sent to poison the shelter, genuinely believing his attack was morally justified to save lives. Dr. Cole will testify that while Markov understood he was physically striking a person, his acute psychotic episode prevented him from appreciating that his actions were wrong. Which of the following is the most accurate analysis of Markov's insanity defense?

(a) Markov has a strong insanity defense because he did not know the physical nature and quality of his act—he thought he was stopping an assassination, meaning he did not understand he was striking a person. This pure cognitive failure alone satisfies the MPC's threshold requirement for a full acquittal at trial.
(b) Markov does not have a viable insanity defense because his post-assault statement to police—"I was stopping an assassination"—demonstrates that he understood the physical mechanics of his actions, which automatically equates to appreciating wrongfulness and precludes lack of substantial capacity under the MPC.
(c) Markov does not have a viable insanity defense because paranoid schizophrenia is legally defined as an abnormality "manifested only by repeated criminal or otherwise antisocial conduct" and is therefore categorically excluded from qualifying as a mental disease under the explicit limiting provisions of the MPC.
(d) Markov has a strong insanity defense because, as a result of his schizophrenia (a qualifying mental disease), he lacked substantial capacity to appreciate the wrongfulness of his conduct—he understood he was physically striking a human being but genuinely believed the act was morally justified to save lives. <!-- correct -->

**Answer:** (d)

(a) is incorrect because Markov understood the physical nature of his act (he knew he was striking a person). (b) is incorrect because understanding the physical mechanics of an act does not automatically equate to appreciating its wrongfulness. (c) is incorrect because schizophrenia is a paradigmatic mental disease; the MPC exclusion applies to psychopathy or antisocial personality disorder. (e) is incorrect because the MPC uses "lacks substantial capacity" rather than requiring complete destruction of control, and the facts establish a cognitive, rather than volitional, impairment.

Consequently, Dr. Laplace's finding of "substantial impairment" is legally insufficient as a matter of law, because mere substantial impairment of cognitive or volitional functions falls far short of the total, absolute incapacity that the Model Penal Code strictly requires defendants to demonstrate to establish an affirmative defense.
(d) Under both the Model Penal Code and the federal Insanity Defense Reform Act, the procedural and substantive burdens rest entirely on the defendant to prove insanity by a preponderance of the evidence. **Q20.** Assume that the jury at Kant's trial for the attempted murder of Officer Hand is considering whether to return a verdict of not guilty by reason of insanity. Evaluate the civil-commitment consequences of a not-guilty-by-reason-of-insanity verdict and the operation of a Mutina-style jury instruction designed to address the misconception that a not-guilty-by-reason-of-insanity verdict means immediate release.

(a) A verdict of not guilty by reason of insanity results in the defendant's immediate release, because acquittal by reason of insanity is constitutionally equivalent to any other acquittal under the Double Jeopardy Clause; civil commitment proceedings, if any, must be initiated separately, with the defendant released pending their outcome, which means a Mutina-style instruction is legally inaccurate and impermissibly prejudices the jury by suggesting a loss of liberty that the trial court cannot guarantee.

(b) Commitment duration following a not-guilty-by-reason-of-insanity verdict is capped at the maximum sentence the defendant could have received upon conviction; upon expiration, the acquittee must be released regardless of clinical status, meaning that a Mutina-style instruction must specifically inform the jury of this strict statutory maximum so that jurors do not operate under the false assumption that an acquittee will face a lifetime of indefinite confinement in a state psychiatric hospital.

(c) In every jurisdiction that recognizes the not-guilty-by-reason-of-insanity verdict, a psychiatric security review board composed of mental-health professionals has exclusive authority to order release, and courts are categorically barred from overriding the board's decisions; accordingly, a Mutina-style instruction is generally utilized to assure jurors that release decisions will be handled exclusively by clinical experts rather than by judges who lack specialized medical or psychiatric training.

(d) A verdict of not guilty by reason of insanity almost always results in involuntary commitment to a secure psychiatric facility, often for a period equal to or longer than the prison sentence the defendant would have received on conviction; a Mutina-style instruction informs the jury that a not-guilty-by-reason-of-insanity verdict does not mean immediate release and that commitment is the ordinary disposition, in order to dispel a pervasive misconception that acquittal equals freedom.

(e) The Mutina-style instruction was held unconstitutional in *Kahler v. Kansas* because it improperly informs the jury of sentencing consequences; its use in any jurisdiction therefore constitutes reversible error, as the Supreme Court concluded that providing jurors with detailed information about post-verdict civil-commitment timelines inappropriately invites them to base their ultimate determination of criminal responsibility on public safety concerns rather than the defendant's mental state at the time of the offense.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** (d) correctly describes both the empirical reality and the purpose of the Mutina-style instruction. Acquittees found not guilty by reason of insanity are ordinarily committed to secure psychiatric facilities, with duration tied to clinical status rather than the hypothetical prison sentence; in practice, commitment is frequently as long as or longer than the underlying sentence would have been. The Mutina-style instruction addresses the well-documented misconception that a not-guilty-by-reason-of-insanity verdict means release, which jurors consistently overestimate (actual not-guilty-by-reason-of-insanity rate is ~0.26%). (a) falsely treats a not-guilty-by-reason-of-insanity verdict as an ordinary acquittal for liberty purposes; nearly all jurisdictions provide for automatic commitment proceedings, not release. (b) fabricates a sentence-based cap; commitment duration is tied to clinical status and can be effectively indefinite in many jurisdictions. (c) falsely universalizes the psychiatric-security-review-board model; only a small number of states (notably Oregon and Connecticut) use psychiatric security review boards with direct release authority, and in most jurisdictions, judges retain final release authority. (e) fabricates a *Kahler* holding on jury instructions; *Kahler* addressed only whether states may abolish the moral-wrongness insanity defense, not the use of instructions about commitment consequences.
