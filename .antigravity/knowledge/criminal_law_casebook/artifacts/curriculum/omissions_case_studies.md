# Omissions: Case Studies & Pedagogical Rationale (Class 09)

The January 2026 research phase for Class 09 (Omissions) identified several key cases that demonstrate the conceptual tensions and modern applications of the duty to act in Criminal Law.

## Key Pedagogical Tensions
- **Individual Autonomy vs. Communal Duty**: The core tension between the common law's reticence to punish "Good Samaritans" and the state's interest in protecting vulnerable individuals.
- **Principle of Legality**: The challenge of judicial expansion of common law duties (e.g., "creation of peril") without violating notice requirements.
- **Medical and Expert Duty**: The boundaries of duty for social guests and those who provide means for risky behavior.

## Selected Case Opinions

### 1. People v. Oliver (1989)
- **Source**: 210 Cal. App. 3d 138, 258 Cal. Rptr. 138
- **Topic**: Special Relationship / Creation of Peril
- **Rationale**: A primary case for teaching how taking a person from a public place to a private home and providing means for drug use establishes a duty to seek medical aid. It bridges the gap between no-duty-to-rescue and affirmative-duty-to-care.

### 2. Commonwealth v. Levesque (2002)
- **Source**: 436 Mass. 443, 766 N.E.2d 50
- **Topic**: Duty to Report / Creation of Peril (Omission-based Manslaughter)
- **Rationale**: Successfully retrieved and edited in the final pipeline run (**Job `4bcf410b`**). It serves as the definitive proof of the restored Stage 3 data flow. The opinion clarifies that while starting a fire accidentally is not inherently criminal, the failure to report it once the peril is recognized creates a criminal duty.

### 3. State v. Lisa (2007)
- **Source**: 391 N.J. Super. 556, 919 A.2d 145; aff'd, 194 N.J. 409 (2008)
- **Topic**: Duty for Social Guests / Principle of Legality
- **Rationale**: Explores the boundary of moral vs. legal duty. The court resisted expanding the duty to act to a social guest who failed to call for help for an overdose victim, highlighting the strict construction of criminal duties.

### 4. State v. K.A.R. (2008)
- **Source**: 988 So. 2d 1150 (Fla. Dist. Ct. App. 2008)
- **Topic**: Judicial Creation of Duty
- **Rationale**: A modernization of the Principle of Legality. It examines whether courts can recognize new categories of duty (like a "duty to summon aid") without prior legislative or judicial precedent, reflecting the tension between evolving social norms and constitutional notice.

### 5. State v. LaBorde (2016)
- **Source**: 230 So. 3d 194 (La. 2017)
- **Topic**: Statutory Duty / Failure to Report
- **Rationale**: Useful for examining "Bad Samaritan" laws and the judicial conflation of different categories of omission liability. It provides a contemporary statutory counterpoint to common law rules.

### 6. Commonwealth v. Carter (2019)
- **Source**: 481 Mass. 352, 115 N.E.3d 559 (2019)
- **Topic**: Failure to Act / Encouragement of Suicide
- **Rationale**: A landmark modern case defining the boundaries of reckless conduct through verbal omission. It demonstrates how "wanton and reckless" behavior includes telling a vulnerable person to "get back in" a carbon-monoxide-filled truck and then failing to summon aid despite having the means to do so. Architectural retrieval verified in **Job `73558e2a`**; full-text extractive editing verified in **Job `f8425061`**.

### 7. State v. Miranda (2015)
- **Source**: 317 Conn. 741 (2015)
- **Topic**: Familial Duty / Special Relationship
- **Rationale**: A significant recent clarification of the and expansion of the "familial relationship" duty. It establishes that a person assuming a parental role (even if not biological) has a common-law duty to protect the child from abuse. Verified in **Job `73558e2a`**.

### 8. Norg v. City of Seattle (2023)
- **Source**: 200 Wash. 2d 749, 522 P.3d 580 (Wash. 2023)
- **Topic**: Public Duty Doctrine / Special Relationship
- **Rationale**: Specifically retrieved during the January 2026 specialized research refactor (**Job `718d99e4`**). This case is the primary "success indicator" for the recency bias mandate. It explores whether a municipality owes a duty to individuals in a specific medical emergency, reinforcing the "Special Relationship" exception to the public duty doctrine in a highly modern setting.

### 9. State v. Gargus (2013)
- **Source**: 462 S.W.3d 417 (Mo. Ct. App. 2013)
- **Topic**: Voluntary Assumption of Care
- **Rationale**: High-relevance case regarding elder abuse and the care of vulnerable adults. It clearly applies the "voluntary assumption" doctrine in a modern setting.

### 10. Lambert v. California (1957)
- **Source**: 355 U.S. 225
- **Topic**: Notice and Due Process / Passive Omission
- **Rationale**: Successfully retrieved in the final run. Explores the requirement of notice in crimes of omission (failure to register as a convicted person), highlighting that punishing a passive omission without knowledge of the duty violates Due Process.

### 11. State ex rel. Kuntz v. Thirteenth Judicial District Court (2000)
- **Source**: 299 Mont. 311, 995 P.2d 951
- **Topic**: Duty to Summon Aid in Self-Defense
- **Rationale**: Extracted in the final run. Examines whether a person who uses force in self-defense has a duty to aid their aggressor once they have reached safety.

### 12. State v. Steen (2020)
- **Source**: 952 N.W.2d 629 (Minn. 2020)
- **Topic**: Special Relationship / Taking Charge
- **Rationale**: Verified in the "Guard & Correct" loop (January 2026). It demonstrates how a person "takes charge" of an overdose victim, assuming a duty of reasonable care, and clarifies that the "special relationship" doctrine extends to situations where one party has custody of another during a medical emergency.

### 13. Modern Legacy Replacements (January 2026 Batch)
These cases were identified specifically as modern replacements for legacy precedents (*Jones*, *Beardsley*, *Pope*) using the "Inventory & Replace" workflow:

- **State v. Shell (2023; 998 N.W.2d 597)**: A highly recent Iowa Supreme Court case establishing duty or "taking charge" in a medical emergency (overdose/slumped over steering wheel), updating *People v. Beardsley*.
- **State v. Perea (2020)**: Modern application of duty based on a special relationship, updating *People v. Beardsley*.
- **The Crumbley Cases (James and Jennifer Crumbley, 2024)**: High-profile application of duty based on control and failure to act (parental responsibility for a child's school shooting). Modern "successor" to *Jones v. United States* in the context of creating peril/failure to intervene.
- **Nguyen v. State (2025)**: Recent clarification of the scope of duty for bystanders and third parties, updating *Pope v. State*.

## Statutory Counterpoints
- **Model Penal Code § 2.01(3)**: The foundational rule requiring an express legal duty.
- **Vermont Duty to Aid the Endangered (12 V.S.A. § 519)**: A rare U.S. "Bad Samaritan" statute, used to contrast the majority rule.
