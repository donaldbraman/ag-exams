## Stem 1: The Shelter Standoff

### Facts

You are an assistant public defender. Your supervising attorney hands you a case file.

**"We have a complex case involving three co-defendants. I need you to analyze the defense options for each. Here's what the investigation established."**

1. Philippa Foot is a 34-year-old social worker who operates a small emergency shelter for unhoused individuals in a converted warehouse in Portland, Oregon. The city issued a condemnation notice for the building, giving Foot thirty days to vacate. Foot believed that closing the shelter in winter would expose the twelve residents to life-threatening cold, and no alternative shelter beds were available anywhere in the city. On the day the condemnation order took effect, Foot refused to vacate and continued operating the shelter in violation of the order.

2. Andrey Markov, one of the shelter residents, has a documented history of paranoid schizophrenia. His psychiatric records confirm that he experiences command hallucinations and delusional episodes. On the evening of Day 15 of the standoff, Markov became convinced that a city building inspector, Abraham Wald, was a "government assassin" sent to poison the shelter's water supply. When Wald arrived the next morning to conduct a routine inspection, Markov confronted him in the doorway, pushed him to the ground, and struck him twice, causing a fractured wrist and bruised ribs. Markov later told police he believed he was "stopping an assassination." Markov's treating psychiatrist, Dr. Ayanna Cole, is prepared to testify that at the time of the assault, Markov was in an acute psychotic episode driven by his schizophrenia, that he genuinely believed Wald intended to kill the shelter residents, and that while Markov understood he was striking a person, he could not appreciate that his actions were wrong. Markov has also been diagnosed with antisocial personality disorder.

3. Oliver Wendell Holmes is Foot's partner. He is not a resident of the shelter but visits regularly. Holmes has a prior conviction for assault. On Day 18 of the standoff, a process server named Lisa Chen arrived at the shelter to deliver an eviction notice. Lisa approached the front entrance, where Holmes was standing. Lisa was carrying a clipboard and an envelope. She identified herself and said she was there to deliver legal papers. Holmes, who had heard Foot say earlier that day that "the city is going to send people to drag us out," believed Lisa was the advance team for a forced removal. Holmes shoved Lisa backward off the front steps, causing her to fall and break her arm. Lisa was five feet, two inches tall and weighed approximately 120 pounds. Holmes is six feet, one inch and weighs 210 pounds. Lisa made no threatening gestures or statements.

4. On Day 20 of the standoff, Holmes received a phone call from his former associate, Friedrich Nietzsche. Nietzsche told Holmes: "I know where your mother lives. If you don't bring me $5,000 by Friday, I'm going to hurt her bad." Holmes believed Nietzsche was serious because Nietzsche had previously hospitalized another person who owed him money. The following day, Holmes stole $5,000 in cash from the shelter's donation safe and delivered it to Nietzsche.

5. On Day 22, a city worker was killed during the forced eviction of the shelter. Assume, counterfactually, that Holmes shoved the city worker off the loading dock because Nietzsche had threatened to kill Holmes's mother if Holmes did not "make sure nobody gets into the shelter," and that the fall resulted in the worker's death.

6. **This jurisdiction follows the common law for necessity and duress. It follows the Model Penal Code for the insanity defense. For self-defense, unless otherwise specified, it applies an objective reasonableness standard with a duty to retreat for deadly force, a castle doctrine limited to the defendant's own dwelling, and does not recognize imperfect self-defense.**

### Questions

**Q1.** Your supervisor asks you to evaluate Foot's strongest defense for her continued operation of the shelter in violation of the condemnation order (Facts 1 and 6). Which of the following is the most accurate analysis?

(a) Foot has a strong necessity defense because the harm avoided (twelve people exposed to life-threatening cold) clearly outweighs the harm caused (occupying a condemned building), the threat was imminent, and no alternative shelter beds were available. <!-- correct -->

(b) Foot has a strong duress defense because the threat of freezing to death compelled her to continue operating the shelter, and a person of ordinary firmness would have yielded to the same pressure.

(c) Foot has a strong necessity defense because under the MPC, the harm avoided need only be "greater than" the harm caused, and protecting twelve lives easily satisfies that lower threshold.

(d) Foot does not have a viable necessity defense because she was at fault in creating the situation by choosing to operate a shelter in a building that was subject to condemnation, and the common law bars the defense when the defendant is at fault.

(e) Foot does not have a viable necessity defense because the legislature has already addressed the specific situation by enacting the building code and condemnation procedures, which preempts any judicial balancing of harms.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Necessity is the correct defense here because Foot faces a choice of evils, not coercion by another person. Under the common law (Fact 6), Foot must show: (1) the harm avoided clearly outweighs the harm caused, (2) the threat is imminent, (3) no reasonable legal alternative exists, and (4) she was not at fault in creating the emergency. Twelve lives threatened by winter exposure clearly outweigh the regulatory harm of occupying a condemned building. The threat is imminent (winter cold is present and immediate). No shelter beds are available (no legal alternative). And Foot did not create the emergency -- she was operating a lawful shelter until the city condemned the building. Option (b) misidentifies the defense: duress requires coercion by another person directing a threat at the defendant, not a natural emergency -- the false proposition is that a natural condition (freezing weather) can constitute the "threat" element of duress. Option (c) applies the MPC proportionality standard ("greater than"), but Fact 6 specifies the jurisdiction follows the common law for necessity, which requires "clearly outweigh" -- the false proposition is that the MPC standard governs. Option (d) misstates the fault element -- Foot did not create the emergency (the condemnation and winter weather created it); operating a shelter is not the kind of fault that bars necessity -- the false proposition is that operating a shelter in a building later condemned constitutes being "at fault in creating the emergency." Option (e) raises legislative preemption, which is a real doctrine (see *Oakland Cannabis*), but building codes are general safety regulations, not a legislative determination that occupying a condemned building is always worse than letting people freeze -- the false proposition is that a general building code constitutes the kind of specific legislative balancing that preempts necessity.

**Q2.** Your supervisor asks whether Foot's necessity defense has any impact on the potential liability of volunteers who helped Foot keep the shelter open during the standoff (Facts 1, 5, and 6). Which of the following best explains the relationship between Foot's defense and the volunteers' liability?

(a) The volunteers have no defense because necessity is a personal excuse that applies only to the person who made the choice of evils, and each volunteer would need to independently establish their own duress defense.

(b) If Foot's necessity defense succeeds, the volunteers who assisted her share in the justification because necessity is a justification -- the act itself is deemed right -- and any third party who assists in a justified act is also justified. <!-- correct -->

(c) The volunteers are liable as accomplices regardless of Foot's defense because the common law treats accomplice liability as strict -- once a person knowingly assists in a criminal act, no defense available to the principal transfers to the accomplice.

(d) The volunteers share in Foot's defense only if each volunteer independently demonstrated they had exhausted all legal alternatives before joining the shelter operation, because necessity's "no reasonable legal alternative" element must be proven individually by each participant.

(e) If Foot's necessity defense succeeds, the volunteers share in the defense, but only because the MPC extends all affirmative defenses to accomplices as a matter of statutory design, not because of the justification principle.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** The justification/excuse distinction has concrete consequences for derivative liability. Necessity is a justification -- the defendant's act is deemed the right choice under the circumstances. When an act is justified, the justification attaches to the act itself, not just to the actor. Any third party who knowingly assists in a justified act shares in that justification. Option (a) misclassifies necessity as an excuse -- the false proposition is that necessity is "a personal excuse." Excuses are personal to the actor (the act is still wrong, but the actor is not blamed), meaning helpers do not automatically benefit. Necessity is a justification, not an excuse. Option (c) states that accomplice liability is "strict" and that no principal's defense can transfer to an accomplice -- the false proposition is this rule of strict accomplice liability. When the principal's act is justified, there is no wrongful act to be an accomplice to. Option (d) adds an individual "no reasonable legal alternative" requirement for each participant -- the false proposition is that derivative justification requires each helper to independently satisfy every element. Derivative justification flows from the act being right, not from each participant's independent assessment. Option (e) reaches the right result but applies the wrong framework -- the false proposition is that the MPC's statutory design is the basis for the shared defense. Fact 6 specifies common law for necessity, and the principle that justification is shareable is a common law principle, not an MPC innovation.

**Q3.** Your supervisor turns to Holmes's liability for shoving Lisa Chen (Facts 3 and 6). Holmes claims self-defense. Under this jurisdiction's rules, which of the following is the most accurate analysis?

(a) Holmes has a valid self-defense claim because he honestly believed Lisa was part of a forced-removal team, and an honest belief in the need for force is sufficient to establish self-defense under this jurisdiction's standard.

(b) Holmes does not have a valid self-defense claim because a reasonable person in Holmes's situation -- knowing only that a small, unarmed woman identified herself as a process server delivering legal papers -- would not have believed force was necessary to defend against an imminent use of unlawful force. <!-- correct -->

(c) Holmes has a valid self-defense claim because Foot's earlier statement that "the city is going to send people to drag us out" constitutes the kind of prior threat information that automatically makes any subsequent belief in danger objectively reasonable.

(d) Holmes does not have a valid self-defense claim solely because he failed to retreat before using force, and this jurisdiction imposes a duty to retreat before any use of force.

(e) Holmes does not have a valid self-defense claim because his prior conviction for assault makes him a "person of violent character" who is categorically barred from raising self-defense under the common law.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** Self-defense requires an objectively reasonable belief that force was necessary to repel an imminent use of unlawful force. The standard asks what a reasonable person in the defendant's situation would have believed. Holmes's "situation" includes hearing Foot's statement earlier that day. But a reasonable person who hears a secondhand warning about possible forced removal, and then encounters a small, unarmed woman who identifies herself as a process server with legal papers, would not conclude that imminent unlawful force was about to be used. Lisa made no threatening gestures, carried only a clipboard and envelope, and verbally identified her purpose. Option (a) applies a purely subjective standard -- the false proposition is that "an honest belief in the need for force is sufficient" under this jurisdiction. Fact 6 specifies an objective reasonableness standard, consistent with *Goetz*'s holding that "reasonably believes" requires an objective element. Option (c) states that prior threat information "automatically makes" a subsequent belief "objectively reasonable" -- the false proposition is the word "automatically." Prior information is incorporated into the reasonableness inquiry, but the incorporation must still produce a reasonable conclusion when measured against the totality of circumstances. Option (d) states that the duty to retreat applies "before any use of force" -- the false proposition is that retreat is required for non-deadly force. The duty to retreat applies only to deadly force, not to all uses of force. Holmes used non-deadly force (a shove), so the retreat requirement is not triggered. Option (e) fabricates a categorical bar -- the false proposition is that a prior assault conviction bars a defendant from raising self-defense. No such rule exists; prior convictions do not eliminate the right to self-defense.

**Q4.** Returning to this jurisdiction's default rules (Fact 6), your supervisor asks about Holmes's claim that he stole the $5,000 under duress (Fact 4). Under the common law, which of the following is the most accurate analysis?

(a) Holmes has a valid duress defense because Nietzsche threatened serious bodily injury to Holmes's mother (a close relation), the threat was credible based on Nietzsche's history, and the common law's imminence requirement is satisfied whenever the threatening party has a documented pattern of carrying out threats, regardless of any deadline that places the threatened harm days in the future.

(b) Holmes does not have a valid duress defense because duress requires a threat directed at the defendant personally, and the common law does not extend the defense to threats against third parties, including family members.

(c) Holmes does not have a valid duress defense because the threat was not imminent -- Nietzsche gave Holmes until Friday, and the common law requires a present and immediate threat, not one that will materialize days later. <!-- correct -->

(d) Holmes does not have a valid duress defense because duress is categorically unavailable for theft crimes under the common law, which limits the defense to offenses against persons.

(e) Holmes has a valid duress defense because a threat need not be imminent so long as it is credible and specific, and Nietzsche's history of violence makes the threat both credible and specific enough to satisfy the common law standard.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** Under the common law (Fact 6), duress requires (1) a threat of death or serious bodily injury, (2) the threat is imminent, (3) the threat targets the defendant or a close relation, and (4) no reasonable legal alternative exists. Nietzsche's threat to "hurt her bad" satisfies the threat threshold and the target requirement (Holmes's mother is a close relation). However, the threat lacks imminence under the common law's strict standard. Nietzsche gave Holmes until Friday -- multiple days away. The common law requires a "present and immediate" threat, not one that will materialize days later. Option (a) falsely claims that the common law's imminence requirement is satisfied whenever the threatening party has a documented pattern of carrying out threats. This conflates the strict common-law rule with the *Dingwall* relaxation; under the common law, imminence requires a present and immediate threat, and a pattern of past violence does not substitute for temporal proximity. Nietzsche gave Holmes until Friday, defeating the imminence element. Option (b) states that the common law "does not extend the defense to threats against third parties, including family members" -- the false proposition is this scope limitation. The common law extends duress to threats against the defendant or a close relation, and a mother is a close relation. Option (d) fabricates a crime-type limitation -- the false proposition is that "duress is categorically unavailable for theft crimes." The common law murder bar applies only to homicide; duress is available for property crimes. Option (e) states that "a threat need not be imminent so long as it is credible and specific" -- the false proposition is this restatement of the imminence rule. The common law requires imminence as a separate element; credibility and specificity do not substitute for temporal immediacy.

**Q6.** Your supervisor now turns to Markov Markov's case (Fact 2). Markov is charged with second-degree assault for attacking Wald Wald. Dr. Cole is prepared to testify that Markov was in an acute psychotic episode caused by schizophrenia and could not appreciate that his actions were wrong. Under this jurisdiction's MPC insanity framework, which of the following is the most accurate analysis of Markov's insanity defense?

(a) Markov has a strong insanity defense because he did not know the nature and quality of his act -- he thought he was stopping an assassination, so he did not understand that he was striking an innocent person.

(b) Markov does not have a viable insanity defense because his post-assault statement to police -- "I was stopping an assassination" -- demonstrates that he understood the nature of his actions and therefore knew what he was doing was wrong.

(c) Markov does not have a viable insanity defense because paranoid schizophrenia with command hallucinations is a condition "manifested only by repeated antisocial conduct" and is excluded from the definition of mental disease or defect under MPC Section 4.01(2).

(d) Markov has a strong insanity defense because, as a result of his schizophrenia (a qualifying mental disease), he lacked substantial capacity to appreciate the wrongfulness of his conduct -- he understood he was striking a person but believed the act was morally justified to save lives, which means he could not appreciate it was wrong. <!-- correct -->

(e) Markov has a strong insanity defense because his schizophrenia entirely destroyed his capacity to control his behavior, satisfying the irresistible impulse test -- he could not have stopped himself from attacking Wald even if he had known the attack was wrong.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** Under MPC Section 4.01(1), a defendant is not responsible if, as a result of mental disease or defect, he lacks "substantial capacity" to "appreciate" the criminality (wrongfulness) of his conduct. Markov has a qualifying mental disease (paranoid schizophrenia -- a major psychotic disorder, not a personality disorder). The causal link is clear: Dr. Cole will testify the psychotic episode was driven by the schizophrenia. Markov understood he was striking a person (he knew the nature and quality of the act). But he believed he was saving lives from an assassin, meaning he could not appreciate that his act was wrong. This is the classic wrongfulness prong. Option (a) misapplies the nature-and-quality prong -- the false proposition is that Markov "did not know the nature and quality of his act." Markov knew he was hitting a person; the nature-and-quality prong applies to defendants who do not understand what they are physically doing (e.g., believing they are squeezing a grapefruit when strangling a child). Option (b) treats Markov's statement as evidence of sanity -- the false proposition is that understanding the nature of one's actions means one "knew what he was doing was wrong." The statement "I was stopping an assassination" is evidence of the delusion, not consciousness of guilt; it supports, rather than undermines, the claim that he could not appreciate wrongfulness. Option (c) states that paranoid schizophrenia is excluded under MPC Section 4.01(2) as a condition "manifested only by repeated antisocial conduct" -- the false proposition is this classification. Paranoid schizophrenia is the paradigmatic qualifying mental disease; the Section 4.01(2) exclusion targets personality disorders manifested solely by repeated antisocial conduct, not psychotic disorders. Option (e) invokes the irresistible impulse test and claims Markov's capacity to control his behavior was "entirely destroyed" -- the false proposition is that irresistible impulse is the applicable framework. Markov's problem was not that he knew it was wrong but could not stop; his problem was that he did not appreciate it was wrong at all. The volitional prong is not the best fit when the cognitive prong directly applies.

**Q7.** Assume, counterfactually, that Markov's case arose in a jurisdiction that follows the federal IDRA standard rather than the MPC. Your supervisor asks how this would change the analysis (Fact 2). Which of the following is the most accurate comparison?

**Q8.** Assume, counterfactually, that Markov's case arose in a jurisdiction that has abolished the insanity defense and follows the mens rea model (like Kansas). The jurisdiction's assault statute requires "purposely or knowingly causing bodily injury to another person." How would this change Markov's defense options? (Fact 2.)

(a) Markov would have no defense at all because abolition states treat mental illness as entirely irrelevant to criminal proceedings, and neither expert testimony nor psychiatric records would be admissible for any purpose.

(b) Markov could still raise an insanity defense because the Supreme Court held in *Kahler v. Kansas* that the Due Process Clause requires every state to provide at least a M'Naghten-style wrongfulness defense for mentally ill defendants.

(c) Markov could use his mental illness to negate the mens rea element of assault, but only if his expert testified that the schizophrenia prevented him from forming the specific intent required -- for example, that the psychotic episode prevented him from acting purposely or knowingly with respect to causing physical injury. ## Stem 2: State v. Kant

### Facts

1. Immanuel Kant was diagnosed with paranoid schizophrenia in 2022 by Dr. Peter Singer, Kant's treating psychiatrist, whose records document recurrent auditory hallucinations and persecutory delusions.

**Q18.** Assume that Kant was suffering from a qualifying mental disease (paranoid schizophrenia, in active psychotic episode) at the time of the Hand stabbing. (c) Kant fails the M'Naghten test because 18 U.S.C. § 17 requires a "severe" mental disease combined with clear-and-convincing-evidence proof of a specific danger to the public; paranoid schizophrenia alone, without that dangerousness showing, is insufficient to meet the federal standard.

**Answer:** (e)
**Explanation:** (e) correctly applies M'Naghten's wrongness prong. Jurisdictions split between legal and moral formulations of wrongfulness, and the two formulations generate different results here. Under a moral formulation, Dr. Singer's testimony that Kant believed killing the possessed host was morally required to defeat a demonic attack (Fact 13) goes directly to the prong: Kant could not appreciate the act's moral wrongness. Under a legal formulation, Fact 13 establishes the opposite result — Kant understood that the penal code prohibited killing a human being, and his delusion supplied a moral-override belief rather than a belief that the conduct was legally permitted. The doctrinal pivot is whether the delusion replaces the object of the act (killing a pure demon, to which no homicide statute applies) or merely rewrites the moral calculus (still killing a human, still legally prohibited, but subjectively justified by a demonic-possession narrative). (b) misapplies the "nature and quality" prong. (c) imports the federal Insanity Defense Reform Act into a state-law analysis (Kant is charged in state court, not federal), fabricates a "dangerousness to the public" element that the Insanity Defense Reform Act does not contain, and incorrectly describes the Act's operation. (d) inverts *Kahler*, which held that due process does *not* require any particular insanity formulation.
