### Grounding

**Q1.** Your supervisor asks you to evaluate Foot's strongest defense for her continued operation of the shelter in violation of the condemnation order (Facts 1 and 6). Which of the following is the most accurate analysis?

(a) Foot has a strong necessity defense because the harm avoided (twelve people exposed to life-threatening cold) clearly outweighs the harm caused (occupying a condemned building), the threat was imminent, and no alternative shelter beds were available. <!-- correct -->

(b) Foot has a strong duress defense because the threat of freezing to death compelled her to continue operating the shelter, and a person of ordinary firmness would have yielded to the same pressure.

(c) Foot has a strong necessity defense because under the MPC, the harm avoided need only be "greater than" the harm caused, and protecting twelve lives easily satisfies that lower threshold.

(d) Foot does not have a viable necessity defense because she was at fault in creating the situation by choosing to operate a shelter in a building that was subject to condemnation, and the common law bars the defense when the defendant is at fault.

(e) Foot does not have a viable necessity defense because the legislature has already addressed the specific situation by enacting the building code and condemnation procedures, which preempts any judicial balancing of harms.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Necessity is the correct defense here because Foot faces a choice of evils, not coercion by another person. Under the common law (Fact 6), Foot must show: (1) the harm avoided clearly outweighs the harm caused, (2) the threat is imminent, (3) no reasonable legal alternative exists, and (4) she was not at fault in creating the emergency. Twelve lives threatened by winter exposure clearly outweigh the regulatory harm of occupying a condemned building. The threat is imminent (winter cold is present and immediate). No shelter beds are available (no legal alternative). And Foot did not create the emergency -- she was operating a lawful shelter until the city condemned the building. Option (b) misidentifies the defense: duress requires coercion by another person directing a threat at the defendant, not a natural emergency -- the false proposition is that a natural condition (freezing weather) can constitute the "threat" element of duress. Option (c) applies the MPC proportionality standard ("greater than"), but Fact 6 specifies the jurisdiction follows the common law for necessity, which requires "clearly outweigh" -- the false proposition is that the MPC standard governs. Option (d) misstates the fault element -- Foot did not create the emergency (the condemnation and winter weather created it); operating a shelter is not the kind of fault that bars necessity -- the false proposition is that operating a shelter in a building later condemned constitutes being "at fault in creating the emergency." Option (e) raises legislative preemption, which is a real doctrine (see *Oakland Cannabis*), but building codes are general safety regulations, not a legislative determination that occupying a condemned building is always worse than letting people freeze -- the false proposition is that a general building code constitutes the kind of specific legislative balancing that preempts necessity.
