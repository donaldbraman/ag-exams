# Chapter 21: Necessity and Duress — Professor Notes

## Problem 1 Hypothetical: Prison Escape

**Hypothetical:** A prisoner at a state penitentiary is assaulted by three inmates. He reports the attacks to a guard, who tells him to "handle it yourself." Two days later, he receives a written threat. He escapes by climbing a fence and is caught the next morning walking along a highway. He immediately tells the arresting officers why he left.

### How students should distinguish

This hypothetical is designed to split on the *Lovercamp* factors — specifically, whether they function as strict prerequisites (*Sarnie*) or as weight-and-credibility considerations (*Unger*).

**Under *Unger* (Illinois — weight and credibility):**

The defense goes to the jury. The key factors:

- **Specific threat:** The assaults plus the written threat satisfy this. Unlike Sarnie's generalized concern about a dangerous environment, the prisoner here faces a direct, personal threat following actual violence.
- **Futile complaints:** He reported to the guard and was told to "handle it yourself." This is stronger than Unger's own facts — Unger never reported at all, and the court still let the defense reach the jury. The guard's dismissal is evidence of futility.
- **Surrender:** He told the arresting officers why he left. This is closer to the *Lovercamp* ideal than Unger, who was found in a motel and never voluntarily surrendered.
- **No violence:** He climbed a fence — no force against personnel or other inmates.

Under *Unger*, all of these are matters for the jury to weigh. The judge does not screen them as threshold requirements. A reasonable jury could credit the prisoner's account.

**Under *Sarnie* (California — prerequisites):**

The defense likely fails at the gatekeeping stage. The problems:

- **Imminence:** The written threat came two days before the escape. *Sarnie* required a "specific, immediate threat." A written threat received 48 hours earlier may not qualify as "immediate" — it is closer to Sarnie's generalized danger than to Unger's same-day phone call.
- **Exhaustion of alternatives:** He reported to one guard. *Sarnie* requires a "showing that a report to authorities would have been ineffective." One dismissive guard does not establish a pattern of futile complaints — he could have reported to a supervisor, a warden, or filed a grievance. The court would likely hold that he failed to exhaust available channels.
- **Surrender timing:** He was caught the next morning. This satisfies the *Lovercamp* "immediately reports" factor — but under *Sarnie*'s prerequisites approach, it may not save him if the other factors fail.

Under *Sarnie*, the judge screens these as prerequisites. The prisoner fails at least imminence and exhaustion, so the defense never reaches the jury.

### The key distinction students should articulate

The two frameworks allocate power differently between judge and jury:

| | *Unger* (weight and credibility) | *Sarnie* (prerequisites) |
|---|---|---|
| Who decides? | Jury | Judge |
| Standard | Could a reasonable jury credit this account? | Has the defendant satisfied each threshold element? |
| Effect of weak factors | Jury weighs them | Defense excluded entirely |
| Protects against | Judicial gatekeeping that screens out meritorious claims | Jury sympathy that excuses unjustified escapes |

The strongest student answers will note that the hypothetical is deliberately designed to fall in the gap between the two frameworks:

- **Strong enough for *Unger*:** Actual assaults, a specific threat, an attempt to report, and prompt surrender give the jury enough to work with.
- **Too weak for *Sarnie*:** The 48-hour gap between threat and escape, and the failure to escalate beyond one guard, likely fail the prerequisites.

The normative question — which is better — turns on whether students trust juries or judges more to sort genuine necessity from opportunistic escape. Students who emphasize wrongful conviction risk will favor *Unger*. Students who emphasize prison security and administrability will favor *Sarnie*.
