# Chapter 11 Revision Notes (for next year)

## Arc to develop

The chapter should trace the full lifecycle of strict liability and the regulatory state:

### 1. Origins: Industrial harm and the information-asymmetry problem
The chapter already covers this well (swill milk, pasteurization mandates, the dairy industry's resistance). Keep the existing material on how strict liability emerged because proving a seller *knew* milk was contaminated was functionally impossible in a multi-party supply chain. The regulatory state grew up around this insight: when the cost of requiring proof of knowledge is measured in lives, legislatures dispense with the knowledge requirement.

### 2. Expansion: Narcotics, traffic, firearms
Strict liability and related doctrines spread well beyond food safety. Narcotics offenses, traffic enforcement, and firearms regulation all adopted versions of the model. This expansion should be developed more fully in the revision.

### 3. Rebuttable presumptions as a middle ground
One important legislative technique that the current chapter underexplores: when a mental state is difficult to prove, legislatures sometimes write a *rebuttable presumption* of a culpable mental state into the statute itself. Two major examples:

- **Intent to distribute**: Possession of narcotics above a threshold quantity creates a rebuttable presumption of intent to distribute. The prosecution doesn't need to prove the defendant planned to sell — the quantity does the work, and the defendant can rebut.
- **Dangerous driving offenses**: Certain driving behaviors (e.g., excessive speed, BAC above a threshold) create rebuttable presumptions of recklessness or negligence. The legislature defines the proxy; the defendant can challenge it.

These rebuttable presumption statutes occupy a middle ground between true strict liability (no mental state at all) and traditional mens rea requirements (prosecution must prove knowledge or intent). They shift the burden without eliminating it entirely.

### 4. The Supreme Court's selective insistence on mens rea
The Court has read a required mental state into many federal offenses, particularly those carrying serious penalties (*Morissette*, *Staples*, *Rehaif*). But it has generally not challenged rebuttable presumption statutes. The distinction matters: the Court treats the *absence* of any mental-state requirement as constitutionally suspect for serious offenses, but treats *presumptions* that the defendant can rebut as compatible with due process. This line should be made explicit in the revision.

### 5. The current moment: Federal retreat and state-level enforcement
The chapter should be updated to reflect recent developments:

- **Executive order deprecating federal enforcement of strict liability offenses**: The current administration has signaled that federal enforcement of strict liability offenses is disfavored, representing a policy shift away from the public welfare offense model at the federal level.
- **Elimination of the Consumer Protection branch of DOJ**: The institutional infrastructure for federal public welfare enforcement is being dismantled, not just deprioritized.
- **Other shifts**: Budget cuts to enforcement agencies, reduced staffing, narrowed enforcement priorities.

The practical consequence is that **public welfare offense enforcement is increasingly a state-level function**. This connects directly to the existing Loper Bright / Carolene Products material: the deference architecture that sustained federal agency enforcement is contracting, and the institutional infrastructure is being actively withdrawn. States are becoming the primary venue for the regulatory bargain the chapter describes.

### 6. Implications for the regulatory bargain section
The chapter's closing "Regulatory Bargain" section should be revised to reflect this shift. The three tensions it identifies (dangerous-items heuristic as political, penalty asymmetry for mass harm, and Carolene Products capture) should be joined by a fourth: the federal government's active withdrawal from the enforcement side of the bargain, leaving states to carry the regulatory burden with fewer resources and less institutional support.
