### Problem 1 Hypothetical: Prison Escape -- Weight-and-Credibility vs. Prerequisites

**Doctrinal focus:** Whether the *Lovercamp* five-factor test operates as a set of strict prerequisites (as in *Sarnie*) or as considerations going to weight and credibility (as in *Unger*), and how the difference affects outcomes in prison escape cases.

**Facts:** A prisoner is assaulted by three inmates, reports to a guard who says "handle it yourself," receives a written threat two days later, escapes by climbing a fence, is caught the next morning, and immediately tells arresting officers why he left.

**Common law analysis (general):** Under the common law necessity framework, the prisoner must show: (1) choice of lesser evil (escape vs. continued assault); (2) harm avoided clearly outweighed harm caused; (3) imminent threat; (4) no fault in creating the emergency; and (5) no reasonable legal alternative. The assault history and written threat establish a plausible imminent threat. The prisoner did not create the emergency. The critical question is element (5) -- legal alternatives. The prisoner reported to a guard and was rebuffed. Whether this exhausts legal alternatives depends on which sub-framework applies.

**Analysis under *Unger* (weight-and-credibility approach):** Under *Unger*, the *Lovercamp* factors are not strict prerequisites but "matters which go to the weight and credibility of the defendant's testimony." This defendant fares well under *Unger*:

- *Specific threat of death, sexual attack, or serious bodily injury:* Yes -- three inmates assaulted him, and he received a written threat. This is stronger than Unger's case, where some threats came from inmates he did not know and an unidentified phone caller.
- *Complaint to authorities:* He reported to a guard and was told to "handle it yourself." This is stronger than Unger, who never reported at all. Under *Unger*'s approach, this evidence goes to the jury to evaluate, and a reasonable juror could find that the guard's dismissal made further complaints futile.
- *Resort to courts:* No time -- the written threat came two days after the assault, and escape followed. A jury could credit the urgency.
- *No force or violence against innocents:* He climbed a fence. No violence against personnel or other innocent persons. This factor is satisfied.
- *Immediate report upon reaching safety:* He immediately told the arresting officers why he left. This is significantly stronger than Unger, who never voluntarily turned himself in. Under *Unger*, the jury weighs this favorably.

**Conclusion under *Unger*:** The defendant is entitled to a necessity instruction. A reasonable jury could credit his account and acquit. The guard's dismissal of his complaint, combined with the written threat and immediate disclosure to arresting officers, gives the jury substantial basis to find necessity.

**Analysis under *Sarnie* (prerequisites approach):** Under *Sarnie* (applying *Lovercamp* as strict prerequisites), each factor must be satisfied as a matter of law before the defense reaches the jury:

- *Specific, immediate threat:* The assault and written threat likely satisfy this. Unlike Sarnie's generalized concern about his son's neighborhood, this prisoner faces a documented pattern of direct violence against him personally.
- *No time for complaint or history of futile complaints:* He reported to the guard and was rebuffed. This is a single complaint, not a "history" of futile complaints. Under *Sarnie*'s strict reading, a court might find this insufficient -- perhaps he should have gone up the chain of command, contacted a warden, or filed a formal grievance. The *Sarnie* court emphasized that the defendant "made no showing that a report to authorities would have been ineffective." Here, the guard's explicit dismissal is stronger, but a strict-prerequisites court could demand more.
- *No time or opportunity to resort to courts:* Two days elapsed between the assault and the escape. A strict court could hold that two days was enough time to seek legal intervention -- a writ, a call to a lawyer, a formal complaint. Under *Sarnie*'s approach, this is evaluated by the court as a threshold matter, not by the jury.
- *Immediate report upon reaching safety:* Satisfied -- he told the arresting officers immediately.

**Conclusion under *Sarnie*:** The case is close but likely fails. The single rebuffed complaint may not satisfy the "history of futile complaints" requirement as a strict prerequisite. The two-day gap between the last assault and the escape gives a court room to find that legal alternatives were not exhausted. A *Sarnie*-type court would likely affirm the conviction.

**Key split:** The frameworks diverge on *who decides* whether the defendant's efforts to seek help were adequate. Under *Unger*, the jury weighs the evidence and could credit the guard's dismissal as making further complaints futile. Under *Sarnie*, the judge makes this determination as a legal prerequisite. The same evidence -- one rebuffed complaint -- is sufficient for a jury under *Unger* but potentially insufficient as a matter of law under *Sarnie*.

**Best arguments on each side:**

- *For the prerequisites approach (Sarnie):* Prison escapes are inherently dangerous to the public. Strict prerequisites prevent inmates from manufacturing necessity claims by engineering situations where they can claim fear. Requiring exhaustion of alternatives as a threshold matter prevents abuse of the defense and gives correctional systems an opportunity to address the threat through institutional channels. Without strict prerequisites, any inmate who faces violence -- a tragically common occurrence -- could claim necessity.

- *For the weight-and-credibility approach (Unger):* Strict prerequisites operate as a judicial veto over the jury's fact-finding function. The question of whether complaints would have been futile is inherently factual -- it depends on the specific institution, the specific guards, and the specific threats. A guard who tells a prisoner to "handle it yourself" is powerful evidence that the institution will not protect him. Requiring multiple futile complaints before allowing the defense means the prisoner must endure additional assaults while building a record -- an absurd result. The jury is better positioned than the judge to evaluate the totality of the circumstances. Moreover, the other *Lovercamp* factors (no violence, immediate report) serve as practical safeguards against abuse.

### Problem 2 Hypothetical: Climate Activism -- Direct vs. Indirect Civil Disobedience

**Doctrinal focus:** Whether the necessity defense is available for climate civil disobedience; the distinction between direct and indirect civil disobedience; and the "legal alternatives" requirement in the context of political protest.

**Facts:** A sixth activist blocks a rail line carrying tar sands oil by chaining herself to the tracks. Unlike the valve-turners (indirect civil disobedience -- trespass and criminal mischief to protest climate policy), she violates a law directly related to the transportation she seeks to stop. She has no prior activist track record. She argues climate catastrophe is imminent and legal channels have proved futile over decades.

**Common law analysis:** The common law necessity defense requires: (1) choice of lesser evil; (2) harm avoided clearly outweighs harm caused; (3) imminent threat; (4) no fault in creating the emergency; and (5) no reasonable legal alternative. Climate activists face serious obstacles on elements (3) and (5).

*Imminence:* Climate change produces harms that are diffuse, long-term, and probabilistic. The common law imminence requirement demands a threat that is temporally proximate. While climate scientists can point to ongoing and accelerating harms, the causal chain between one day of rail blockage and the prevention of specific climate harm is attenuated. Most courts applying the common law would find this element unsatisfied.

*Legal alternatives:* This is the decisive element. Even if climate harms are imminent, the defendant must show no reasonable legal alternative existed. The defendant has lobbied, petitioned, and voted -- and argues these have proved futile "over decades." But under both *Higgins* and *Schoon*, the availability of the political process is itself the legal alternative.

**Analysis under the *Higgins* framework:** The *Higgins* court's analysis turns on the distinction between direct and indirect civil disobedience. Indirect civil disobedience -- violating a different law to protest broader policy -- triggers the per se rule from *Schoon*: "legal alternatives will never be deemed exhausted when the harm can be mitigated by congressional action." The valve-turners in *Higgins* committed trespass and criminal mischief to protest climate policy -- classic indirect civil disobedience.

This hypothetical presents a harder case because the activist's conduct is arguably *direct* civil disobedience: she is blocking the very rail line carrying the substance she opposes. She is violating a law (obstruction of rail transport) that is directly related to the harm she seeks to prevent. If the court treats this as direct civil disobedience, the *Schoon* per se rule does not apply, and the question of legal alternatives goes to the jury.

However, a *Higgins*-type court would likely resist this characterization. The activist is not challenging the law against obstructing rail lines -- she is protesting climate policy writ large. The rail blockage is a means of protest, not a direct challenge to the specific law she is violating. The harm she seeks to avert (climate catastrophe) is one that "can be mitigated by congressional action" -- through emissions regulations, carbon taxes, energy policy, etc. Under *Higgins*, the defense would likely be precluded.

Even if the court allowed the direct/indirect distinction to help her, the other elements present problems. She has no prior activist track record, which undermines the claim that she has exhausted legal alternatives. And the causal link between blocking one rail line for one day and averting climate catastrophe is attenuated.

**Conclusion under *Higgins*:** Defense precluded. The court would likely characterize this as indirect civil disobedience (protesting climate policy through rail blockage) and apply the *Schoon* per se rule. Even if treated as direct civil disobedience, the defendant's lack of prior activism undermines her exhaustion claim.

**Analysis under the *Ward* framework:** The *Ward* court took a fundamentally different approach. Rather than applying a per se rule, the Washington Court of Appeals held that Ward had "raised a genuine question of fact about whether reasonable legal alternatives existed." The court credited Ward's evidence that his "past successes in effectuating change through civil disobedience" supported the reasonableness of his belief that direct action was necessary.

Under *Ward*, the question is whether the defendant has presented sufficient evidence on all four elements for a rational juror to find in her favor. The key differences for this defendant:

- *No prior activist track record:* This cuts against her. Ward's claim was bolstered by evidence that he had tried legal channels and direct action in the past, and that his civil disobedience had previously "effectuat[ed] change." This defendant has no such record. A *Ward*-type court might still allow the defense if she can point to the broader movement's decades of unsuccessful legal advocacy, but the individual record matters.
- *Direct relationship to the harm:* The fact that she blocked the actual rail line carrying tar sands oil, rather than trespassing on a pipeline facility, may strengthen her causal connection argument. She can argue a more direct link between her action and the harm averted.

**Conclusion under *Ward*:** The defense would likely reach the jury, but the defendant's case is weaker than Ward's. The lack of a prior activist record undermines the claim that legal alternatives were exhausted. A rational juror could still find necessity, but the evidentiary foundation is thinner.

**Does the direct/indirect distinction matter here?** Yes, but only under *Higgins*. The *Higgins* framework imports the *Schoon* distinction as a doctrinal threshold: indirect civil disobedience triggers a per se bar on the necessity defense because the political process is always an available alternative. If this defendant's conduct is classified as direct civil disobedience, the per se bar does not apply and the defense can proceed. Under *Ward*, the distinction is irrelevant because *Ward* does not apply a per se rule at all -- it treats legal alternatives as a factual question for the jury regardless of the type of civil disobedience.

The classification here is genuinely debatable. She is blocking the specific transportation method she opposes (direct), but she is doing so to protest broader climate policy (indirect). Reasonable courts could characterize it either way.

**Key split:** The frameworks diverge on whether the availability of the political process constitutes a per se bar (*Higgins*) or a factual question for the jury (*Ward*). *Higgins* treats the political process as a legal alternative that is never exhausted as long as democratic channels remain open. *Ward* treats it as one factor among many, to be weighed by the jury against evidence that those channels have been tried and failed.

**Best arguments on each side:**

- *For the Higgins approach:* The necessity defense was designed for emergencies, not for political disagreements. If climate activists can invoke necessity whenever they believe the political process is too slow, there is no principled limit to the defense. Every protester believes her cause is urgent and that legal channels have failed. The political process -- however imperfect -- is the mechanism a democracy provides for resolving policy disputes. Allowing a jury to decide whether decades of lobbying have "exhausted" legal alternatives effectively puts climate policy on trial in every criminal case. This is legislating from the jury box. The *Schoon* per se rule prevents this by recognizing that Congress, not individual activists, determines energy and environmental policy.

- *For the Ward approach:* The per se rule effectively eliminates the necessity defense for the most consequential moral crises. If the political process were functioning, the defense would not be needed. The whole point of necessity is that normal channels have failed. The *Higgins* approach assumes that congressional action is always a reasonable alternative -- but what if Congress has been presented with overwhelming scientific evidence for decades and has failed to act? The jury is the institution best positioned to weigh whether, under the specific facts, legal alternatives were truly available. And there is a difference between saying the defense might succeed (which *Ward* allows) and saying it will succeed -- Ward still had to convince a jury. The safeguard is the jury's judgment, not a judicial per se bar. Moreover, the Sixth Amendment right to present a defense means that credible evidence on necessity should reach the jury, even if the court finds the claim unlikely to succeed.

### Problem 3 Hypothetical: Psilocybin, Legislative Preemption, and Medical Necessity

**Doctrinal focus:** Whether legislative preemption forecloses a medical necessity defense when the legislature of one state has affirmatively found medical value in a substance that another state (and the federal government) classifies as having no accepted medical use; and whether the quality of the legislative record matters.

**Facts:** A combat veteran with treatment-resistant PTSD uses psilocybin legally under Oregon's supervised therapeutic program. While traveling through a neighboring state that classifies psilocybin as Schedule I, he is arrested for possession. He asserts necessity, arguing psilocybin is the only treatment controlling his symptoms and without it he is a danger to himself.

**Common law analysis:** Under the common law necessity defense, the veteran must show: (1) lesser evil (possession vs. danger to self from untreated PTSD); (2) harm avoided clearly outweighs harm caused; (3) imminent threat; (4) no fault in creating the emergency; and (5) no reasonable legal alternative. The veteran has strong facts on several elements -- treatment-resistant PTSD, danger to self, no alternative treatment -- but faces the legislative preemption problem.

**Analysis under *Oakland Cannabis*:** The Supreme Court's holding in *Oakland Cannabis* is the controlling framework: "The defense cannot succeed when the legislature itself has made a 'determination of values.'" When Congress classified psilocybin as Schedule I -- "no currently accepted medical use" -- it performed the harm-balancing that the necessity defense asks courts to undertake. A court applying *Oakland Cannabis* cannot second-guess that legislative judgment.

The veteran's argument that his individual medical need constitutes a "greater harm" than the statutory violation is precisely the argument the *Oakland Cannabis* cooperative made. The Supreme Court rejected it categorically. The Controlled Substances Act's Schedule I classification is Congress's determination that the substance has no accepted medical use, and individual medical necessity cannot override that determination.

**Conclusion under *Oakland Cannabis*:** The defense is foreclosed. Legislative preemption bars the necessity defense regardless of the individual circumstances.

**Does Oregon's legislative process distinguish this case?** The veteran's strongest argument is that Oregon's legislature -- unlike California's at the time of *Oakland Cannabis* -- conducted a formal review of the medical evidence before legalizing therapeutic psilocybin use. Oregon Measure 109 (2020) established a regulatory framework based on clinical evidence. This is a more formal legislative "determination of values" than California's Compassionate Use Act, which was a ballot initiative.

The argument would be: *Oakland Cannabis* rests on deference to legislative determinations of values. Here, there are *two* legislative determinations -- federal (no medical use) and state (accepted medical use after formal review). Oregon's determination is more recent and based on more current medical evidence. Why should the federal determination automatically trump the state one for purposes of necessity analysis?

The answer, unfortunately for the veteran, is *Gonzales v. Raich*. The Supreme Court held that the federal government's Commerce Clause power permits prosecution of local medical users even in states that have legalized the drug. The state legislature's judgment does not constrain federal enforcement. And *a fortiori*, it does not constrain a *different* state's enforcement of its own Schedule I classification. The neighboring state that arrested the veteran has made its own legislative determination that psilocybin has no accepted medical use. That determination preempts the necessity defense just as the federal classification did in *Oakland Cannabis*.

**MPC analysis:** The MPC does not contain a specific legislative preemption rule in the text of Section 3.02, but the Official Commentary recognizes that the defense is unavailable "when the legislature has already addressed the specific conduct." Even under the MPC's more flexible framework, the neighboring state's Schedule I classification represents a legislative judgment that possession of psilocybin is never justified. The MPC would reach the same result.

However, the MPC's more flexible balancing test and its lack of a strict imminence requirement at least create a theoretical framework in which the argument is stronger. The veteran could argue that the harm of untreated PTSD (danger to self) is "greater than" the harm of possession, and that no reasonable alternative existed (the treatment is available only in Oregon, and he was in transit). But the legislative preemption doctrine forecloses this analysis.

**Key split:** There is no meaningful split between common law and MPC on this issue -- both recognize legislative preemption. The real tension is between *vertical* legislative preemption (federal over state) and *horizontal* legislative preemption (state A's classification vs. state B's classification). *Oakland Cannabis* and *Raich* establish that the federal classification controls, but neither case addresses whether one state's affirmative finding of medical value should affect another state's necessity analysis.

**Best arguments on each side:**

- *For foreclosing the defense (applying Oakland Cannabis):* Legislative preemption is a structural principle, not a case-by-case inquiry. If courts could second-guess Schedule I classifications based on individual medical need or conflicting state determinations, the classification system would be meaningless. Every defendant would claim that her state (or some other state) recognizes the medical value of the substance. The uniformity of federal drug scheduling depends on the per se rule. Moreover, the veteran had a legal alternative: he could have remained in Oregon or arranged his travel to avoid states where psilocybin is illegal. The fact that travel is inconvenient does not make possession necessary.

- *For allowing the defense:* The legislative preemption doctrine rests on the assumption that the legislature has made an informed determination of values. But what happens when that determination is demonstrably outdated? Psilocybin was classified as Schedule I in 1970, based on the drug policy of that era, not on current medical evidence. Oregon's legislature conducted a formal evidentiary review and reached the opposite conclusion. The preemption doctrine should not be a mechanism for freezing outdated legislative judgments against all challenge. When a veteran with treatment-resistant PTSD -- a condition with a high suicide rate -- is the only person who can prevent harm to himself, and the only effective treatment is one that another state's legislature has formally approved after medical review, the mechanical application of preemption produces an unjust result. The necessity defense exists precisely for cases where following the law produces a worse outcome than breaking it. A combat veteran who is a danger to himself without a treatment that is legal in one state and illegal in the next is the paradigm case.

### Problem 4 Hypothetical A: The Trolley Problem -- Necessity and Homicide

**Doctrinal focus:** Whether necessity can justify the deliberate killing of an innocent person; the common law categorical bar on necessity for homicide vs. the MPC's cost-benefit approach; and whether lives can be compared.

**Facts:** A woman driving on a narrow mountain road sees a runaway truck barreling toward a school bus full of children. She can steer her car into the truck, diverting it and saving the children, but she will certainly kill the truck's driver. She diverts the truck. The driver dies.

**Common law analysis:** The common law, as established in *Dudley and Stephens*, categorically bars necessity as a defense to homicide. The rule is absolute: necessity can never justify "the deliberate taking of an innocent life." The woman deliberately steered into the truck knowing it would kill the driver. The driver is innocent -- his brakes failed through no fault of his own. Under the common law, the defense is unavailable regardless of how many lives were saved.

The woman would be charged with murder (intentional killing) or at minimum voluntary manslaughter. She cannot invoke necessity. The common law treats the sanctity of individual life as inviolable -- no utilitarian calculus can justify choosing to end one life to save others. As Lord Coleridge asked in *Dudley and Stephens*: "Who is to be the judge of this sort of necessity? By what measure is the comparative value of lives to be measured?"

This produces what many students will perceive as an unjust result: a woman who saved a bus full of children is convicted of murder.

**MPC analysis:** The MPC (Section 3.02) does not exclude homicide from the necessity defense. The Official Commentary explicitly endorses the position that "conduct that results in taking life may promote the very value sought to be protected by the law of homicide." The MPC's dike hypothetical -- breaching a dike to save a town, killing farm inhabitants -- is closely analogous to this hypothetical.

Under the MPC, the woman must show that the harm avoided (death of a busload of children) was "greater than" the harm caused (death of the truck driver). The MPC Commentary states that "the life of every individual must be taken in such a case to be of equal value and the numerical preponderance in the lives saved compared to those sacrificed surely should establish legal justification for the act."

This is the easiest case for the MPC approach: many lives saved vs. one life lost. The defense succeeds. The woman is justified.

However, the chapter notes that "no court in any MPC-adopting jurisdiction has actually tested this in a reported case." The common law prohibition from *Dudley and Stephens* "remains the operative rule everywhere." So even in MPC jurisdictions, there is no guarantee a court would follow the Code's logic.

**Key split:** The frameworks diverge on whether lives can be compared and whether numerical preponderance can justify killing. The common law says no -- categorically. The MPC says yes -- the "object of the law of homicide is to save life," and a net saving of lives justifies the act. The philosophical divide maps onto the deontological/consequentialist distinction: the common law adopts a deontological rule (never deliberately kill an innocent), while the MPC adopts a consequentialist calculus (maximize lives saved).

**Best arguments on each side:**

- *For the common law categorical bar:* Once the law permits killing one innocent person to save others, there is no principled stopping point. Who decides how many lives justify taking one? What about quality-of-life comparisons? The categorical bar avoids the "who is to be the judge" problem that Lord Coleridge identified. It also prevents the defense from being used instrumentally -- if killing one person to save two is justified, then every defendant who claims to have been maximizing welfare has a defense. The rule's rigidity is its virtue: it removes the temptation to calculate lives and protects the principle that every individual life has equal and absolute value. The appropriate response to a case like this is executive clemency, not doctrinal exception.

- *For the MPC approach:* The categorical bar produces results that are not merely harsh but irrational. A woman who saves twenty children by sacrificing one adult is convicted of murder. The law of homicide exists to protect life, and the woman's action produced a net saving of life. The categorical bar does not actually protect the sanctity of life -- it protects the sanctity of *inaction*. The woman who does nothing and lets twenty children die faces no legal consequence. The woman who acts to save them is a murderer. This inverts the moral calculus the law should reflect. The MPC's approach is more honest: it acknowledges that in tragic choice situations, the law should prefer the outcome that preserves the most life. The concern about slippery slopes is addressed by requiring that the harm avoided be "greater than" the harm caused -- a standard that courts apply in every other necessity context.

### Problem 4 Hypothetical B: Fault in Creating the Crisis

**Doctrinal focus:** How the defendant's culpability in creating the conditions for the emergency affects the necessity defense; the common law's absolute bar for at-fault defendants vs. the MPC's fault-calibration rule.

**Facts:** Same scenario, but the woman was driving recklessly (well over the speed limit), and her excessive speed is why she reached the intersection at the precise moment she could intervene.

**Common law analysis:** Under the common law, the defense is barred if the defendant was "at fault in creating the emergency." The woman's reckless driving placed her at the intersection at the critical moment. Without her recklessness, she would not have been in a position to intervene (or to need the defense).

But there is a threshold question: did her recklessness "create the emergency"? The emergency -- the runaway truck -- existed independently of her driving. She did not cause the truck's brakes to fail. Her recklessness merely placed her at the scene. Arguably, the fault-in-creating-the-crisis element should apply only when the defendant's fault *caused* the threat that triggers the necessity claim, not merely when the defendant's unrelated fault placed her in a position to encounter it.

If the court takes the broad view (her recklessness created the conditions for the crisis), the defense is barred entirely. If the court takes the narrow view (she did not create the emergency itself), the defense survives this element but still fails under the categorical homicide bar.

**Conclusion under common law:** The defense fails regardless, because the common law bars necessity for homicide. But if homicide were not at issue, the fault element would be heavily litigated, with the outcome depending on whether "fault in creating the crisis" requires a causal connection to the underlying threat or merely to the defendant's presence at the scene.

**MPC analysis:** The MPC's fault-calibration rule is more nuanced. The question is not whether the defendant was at fault, but *how culpably* she was at fault, compared to the mental state required by each charged offense.

The woman's fault in creating the conditions for the crisis is *recklessness* (driving well over the speed limit, consciously disregarding a substantial risk).

Now apply the calibration rule charge by charge:

- *Intentional trespass / property damage (diverting the truck into the roadside):* These offenses require purposeful or knowing mental states. Purpose and knowledge are *higher* on the MPC hierarchy than recklessness. The woman's fault (recklessness) is *lower* than what these charges demand. **The necessity defense survives** for these charges.

- *Murder (intentional killing of the driver):* Murder requires purpose or knowledge. Again, the woman's fault in creating the crisis (recklessness) is lower than what murder demands. **The necessity defense survives** for murder -- but only because the MPC does not categorically bar necessity for homicide. The defense succeeds: the harm avoided (many children's lives) is greater than the harm caused (one driver's life), and the fault-calibration rule does not strip the defense.

- *Manslaughter (reckless homicide of the driver):* Manslaughter requires recklessness. The woman's fault in creating the crisis *matches* the mental state manslaughter requires. Under the MPC, because her culpability in creating the crisis is *equal to* the mental state the charge demands, **the defense fails** for manslaughter.

- *Negligent homicide:* Negligent homicide requires only negligence. Recklessness is *higher* than negligence. The woman's fault (recklessness) *exceeds* the mental state required. **The defense fails** for negligent homicide.

**The practical result under the MPC:** The woman has a necessity defense to murder (the most serious charge) but *not* to manslaughter or negligent homicide. This produces the counterintuitive result that she could be acquitted of the intentional killing but convicted of the reckless one. The logic: the MPC treats her reckless driving as the moral equivalent of reckless homicide -- she was reckless in creating the situation, so she bears responsibility at the recklessness level. But she is not morally equivalent to a purposeful killer, so the defense protects her against that charge.

**Key split:** The common law bars the defense entirely (both because of fault and because of the homicide bar). The MPC applies a calibrated, charge-by-charge analysis that can produce different outcomes for different charges arising from the same conduct. The common law treats fault as binary (at fault or not); the MPC treats it as a spectrum aligned with the hierarchy of mental states.

**Best arguments on each side:**

- *For the common law approach:* A defendant who is recklessly driving should not benefit from a crisis she would not have encountered but for her recklessness. The simplicity of the rule -- at fault means no defense -- avoids the complexity of the MPC's charge-by-charge calibration. And it sends a clear deterrent message: if you drive recklessly, you bear full responsibility for whatever follows.

- *For the MPC approach:* The common law's all-or-nothing rule is unjust. A reckless driver who saves twenty children should not be treated identically to a reckless driver who saves no one. The MPC's calibration matches liability to culpability: the woman is liable for what she is morally responsible for (reckless conduct) but not for what she is not (purposeful killing). The charge-by-charge approach is more complex, but it is also more precise. It avoids both over-punishment (convicting a lifesaver of murder) and under-punishment (letting reckless driving go unaddressed). The counterintuitive result -- acquitted of murder, convicted of manslaughter -- actually reflects a coherent moral judgment: the killing was justified, but the recklessness that put her there was not.

### Problem 5 Hypothetical: Imminence, Corrupt Authorities, and Transnational Threats

**Doctrinal focus:** Whether the imminence requirement is satisfied when threats are continuous but temporally distant from the criminal act; whether a defendant's belief that local police are corrupt negates the "reasonable legal alternative" element; and whether threats targeting people in a different country affect the analysis.

**Facts:** A college student studying abroad is told by a gang leader to carry heroin through U.S. customs. The gang leader shows photos of her family's U.S. home and threatens to have associates in her hometown kill her parents. She does not go to local police because she has seen them accept gang payments. She carries the package and is arrested two weeks later. The drugs were discovered in routine inspection (she did not consent to a search).

**Common law analysis:** Under the common law, duress requires: (1) a threat of death or serious bodily injury; (2) the threat must be imminent; (3) directed at the defendant or a close relation; (4) no reasonable opportunity to escape; and (5) duress is unavailable for murder.

- *Threat of death or serious bodily injury:* Satisfied -- the gang leader threatened to kill her parents.
- *Directed at defendant or close relation:* Satisfied -- parents are close relations.
- *Imminence:* This is the critical element. Two weeks passed between the threat and the arrest. Under the common law's strict imminence requirement, the threat must be temporally proximate to the criminal act. Two weeks is a substantial gap. The common law would likely find imminence unsatisfied.

However, *Contento-Pachon* (a Ninth Circuit case applying federal common law) found imminence satisfied despite a three-week gap because "the threat was continuous and the defendant reasonably believed it would be carried out." The student's situation is analogous: the gang's surveillance of her family (photos of the home) suggests continuous monitoring and an ongoing threat. A court following *Contento-Pachon* could find imminence satisfied.

But there is a critical distinction: the threat targets people in a *different country* from where the defendant is located. The gang leader is in Central America; the threatened parents are in the United States. The defendant could have contacted U.S. law enforcement -- the FBI, local police in her hometown, the U.S. embassy -- to warn them about the threat. This is a "reasonable legal alternative" she did not pursue.

*Reasonable legal alternative:* The student did not go to *local* police because they were corrupt. This is analogous to *Contento-Pachon*, where the court held that belief in police corruption was a question for the trier of fact. But unlike Contento-Pachon, this student had access to law enforcement she could trust -- U.S. authorities. She could have called the U.S. embassy, the FBI, or her hometown police. The fact that *local* police were corrupt does not exhaust her alternatives when non-corrupt authorities in the country where the threat would be carried out were available. Under the common law, this likely defeats the defense.

**Conclusion under common law:** The defense likely fails on both imminence (two-week gap) and reasonable alternatives (U.S. law enforcement was available).

**MPC analysis:** Under the MPC (Section 2.09), duress requires: (1) a threat of "unlawful force" (broader than death or GBH); (2) no strict imminence requirement; (3) threat against "any person" (broader than close relations); and (4) a "person of reasonable firmness" would not have resisted.

- *Unlawful force:* Satisfied -- death threats.
- *Imminence:* The MPC drops strict imminence. The question is whether a person of reasonable firmness would have yielded to the threat. The gang's demonstrated surveillance capability (photos of the family home), the threat's specificity (associates in her hometown), and the two-week timeline are all relevant to reasonableness but do not create a per se bar.
- *Threat against any person:* Satisfied -- parents.
- *Person of reasonable firmness:* This is the key question. A person of reasonable firmness, knowing that the gang had photographed her family's home and had associates in her hometown, might well yield to the threat. But the "reasonable firmness" standard also asks whether she explored alternatives. Could a person of reasonable firmness have contacted U.S. authorities? This remains a factual question under the MPC, but it weighs against her.

**Conclusion under MPC:** The defense is stronger but still faces the reasonable-alternatives problem. The MPC's flexible imminence standard helps the student, but the availability of trusted law enforcement (U.S. authorities) undermines the claim that she had no reasonable alternative. A jury applying the MPC standard could go either way.

**Does the transnational element change the analysis?** Yes, significantly. In *Contento-Pachon*, the defendant was in Colombia, the threats were from Colombian drug traffickers, and the Colombian police were corrupt. There was no trusted authority available *anywhere* in the relevant chain. Here, the threat targets people in the United States, where the student could have contacted trusted law enforcement. This distinction is potentially dispositive: the availability of an uncorrupted law enforcement alternative that the defendant failed to pursue undermines both the common law and MPC claims.

However, a student could argue that contacting U.S. authorities from abroad, while being watched by the gang, carried its own risks. If the gang discovered she had contacted police, they might accelerate the threat. The gang had demonstrated surveillance capability (photos). A person of reasonable firmness might rationally conclude that any contact with authorities would trigger the threatened harm before authorities could respond.

**Key split:** The common law's strict imminence requirement creates a temporal per se bar that likely defeats the defense. The MPC's flexible approach allows the jury to consider the totality of the circumstances, including the gang's surveillance capability and the student's fear. But both frameworks must address the reasonable-alternatives problem, and the availability of trusted U.S. law enforcement is the strongest factor against the defense under either framework.

**Best arguments on each side:**

- *For the common law strict imminence approach:* Two weeks is ample time to seek help. The student was not under physical restraint. She could have contacted the U.S. embassy, called the FBI, or reached her hometown police. The strict imminence requirement exists to prevent defendants from invoking duress for threats that could have been addressed through legal channels. If two weeks of inaction is compatible with "imminence," the requirement is meaningless.

- *For the MPC flexible approach:* The student faced a sophisticated criminal organization with demonstrated surveillance capability and transnational reach. Contacting authorities was not a simple phone call -- it was a calculated risk that could have triggered the very harm she feared. The gang's surveillance photos proved they could monitor her family. A person of reasonable firmness, knowing all this, might rationally conclude that compliance was the safest course. The imminence requirement was designed for the paradigm case of a gun to the head, not for sustained coercion by organized crime. The MPC's flexible standard better accommodates the reality of modern transnational threats, where the coercer's reach extends across borders and the threat is omnipresent.

### Problem 6 Hypothetical: Role-Based Expectations and Reasonable Firmness

**Doctrinal focus:** Whether the "person of reasonable firmness" standard incorporates professional role expectations; whether the behavior of other captives facing identical threats is admissible evidence; and the boundary between objective and subjective elements of the standard.

**Facts:** A civilian aid worker captured by insurgents in a conflict zone is threatened with death unless she makes a video denouncing her government's foreign policy. Other captives -- two journalists and a military contractor -- refuse and are beaten but not killed. The aid worker makes the video. She is charged with providing material support for terrorism.

**Common law analysis:** Under the common law, duress requires: (1) threat of death or serious bodily injury; (2) imminent; (3) directed at defendant or close relation; (4) no reasonable alternative; and (5) an ordinary person would have yielded.

- *Threat of death or GBH:* The insurgents threatened to kill her. Satisfied.
- *Imminence:* She was in captivity and the threat was immediate. Satisfied.
- *Directed at defendant:* Yes. Satisfied.
- *No reasonable alternative:* She was a captive in a conflict zone. No reasonable legal alternative existed. Satisfied.
- *Ordinary person would yield:* This is the critical element. The common law applies an objective "ordinary person" standard. The question is whether an ordinary person, threatened with death, would have made the video.

On one hand, the threat of death is the paradigmatic case for duress -- an ordinary person would yield to a credible death threat. On the other hand, other captives facing the *same* threats refused. If two journalists and a military contractor resisted, can the defendant argue that an ordinary person would have yielded?

The behavior of other captives is relevant but not dispositive under the common law. The standard is what an "ordinary person" would do, not what any particular person did. The other captives may have been unusually brave -- their resistance does not establish the baseline for ordinary persons. However, the evidence is admissible as probative of what a reasonable person *could* do, even if it is not determinative of what a reasonable person *would* do.

**The role question under common law:** Neither the common law nor the MPC explicitly addresses whether "reasonable firmness" varies by profession. The chapter notes this is "an open question with little case law outside the military context." *Fleming* established that a military officer may be held to a higher standard because of the duty to resist enemy exploitation. But the aid worker is a civilian.

A common law court would likely hold that the objective standard does not incorporate professional role. An "ordinary person" is not an "ordinary aid worker." The aid worker has no special duty to resist coercion comparable to a military officer's duty under the UCMJ (Uniform Code of Military Justice). She is evaluated against the standard of an ordinary civilian facing a death threat in captivity.

**Conclusion under common law:** The defense likely succeeds. A credible death threat in captivity, with no possibility of escape, satisfies all elements. The other captives' resistance is admissible but does not defeat the defense -- it shows what was possible, not what was required.

**MPC analysis:** The MPC applies a "person of reasonable firmness in his situation" standard. The MPC drafters specified that "situation" includes "stark, tangible factors" like size, strength, age, and health, but excludes "matters of temperament."

- *Situation factors:* The aid worker's specific physical circumstances in captivity -- isolation, vulnerability, lack of weapons or means of resistance -- are incorporated into the standard. These favor the defense.
- *Professional role:* Is an aid worker's professional background a "stark, tangible factor" or a "matter of temperament"? Training in hostage situations might be a tangible factor (relevant expertise). But the aid worker's general humanitarian mission is more akin to character than circumstance. A court would likely exclude professional role from the "situation" analysis unless the aid worker had specific training in resisting coercion.
- *Person of reasonable firmness:* Would a person of reasonable firmness, in the aid worker's situation, have yielded to the death threat? Almost certainly yes. The MPC standard asks whether the person would have been "unable to resist" the pressure. A death threat in captivity, with demonstrated willingness to carry it out (the beatings of other captives), would overcome reasonable firmness.

**Should other captives' behavior be admissible?** Under both frameworks, the behavior of other captives is relevant but presents complications:

- *Admissibility argument:* The evidence is probative of the objective question -- it shows that resistance was physically possible and survivable (beaten but not killed). It helps the jury calibrate what "reasonable firmness" requires in this specific situation.
- *Counter-argument:* The other captives are not the defendant. They may have had different physical capabilities, different information about the insurgents' intentions, or different psychological resources. The military contractor, in particular, may have had training in resistance to interrogation. Comparing the aid worker to individuals with different backgrounds, training, and risk tolerances distorts the objective standard. Moreover, the fact that the resisters were beaten but not killed is only known *in retrospect* -- the aid worker, at the moment of decision, could not know whether resistance would result in beating or death.

**Key split:** The frameworks do not diverge dramatically here. Both apply an objective reasonableness standard. The MPC's "situation" qualifier is slightly more favorable to the defendant because it incorporates specific circumstances of captivity. The main doctrinal question -- whether professional role matters -- is unresolved under both.

**Best arguments on each side:**

- *For incorporating professional role:* Certain professions carry heightened duties of resistance. Military officers have an explicit duty to resist exploitation. Doctors have a duty to maintain patient confidentiality even under pressure. If the law exempts these professionals from accountability under duress because they can claim "an ordinary person would yield," it undermines the very professional obligations that define their roles. The reasonable firmness standard should reflect the expectations society places on specific roles, particularly when those roles involve positions of trust or public responsibility.

- *Against incorporating professional role:* The aid worker is a civilian, not a soldier. She has no legal duty to resist coercion. Creating role-based tiers of reasonable firmness would produce a two-track system where certain civilians are held to standards they never agreed to bear. The MPC drafters deliberately excluded "matters of temperament" from the situation analysis, and professional identity is closer to temperament than to a "stark, tangible factor" like physical size. Moreover, the purpose of the duress defense is to recognize that human beings have limits. A death threat overcomes those limits regardless of the victim's job title. The appropriate comparison is not "what would a reasonable aid worker do?" but "what would a reasonable human being do when facing execution?"

### Problem 7 Hypothetical: Duress, Felony Murder, and Knowledge of Violence

**Doctrinal focus:** Whether duress is available as a defense to felony murder; whether the defendant's knowledge that her partner carried a loaded gun into the store changes the analysis; and the distinction between coerced participation in a felony and foreseeable homicide risk.

**Facts:** Two friends plan a convenience store robbery. One (the getaway driver) provides the car; the other enters the store with a gun, panics, and shoots the clerk. The getaway driver is charged with felony murder. She claims duress: her partner beat her repeatedly and threatened to kill her if she didn't participate. Unlike Reichard, the getaway driver *knew* her partner carried a loaded gun into the store.

**Common law analysis (murder bar):** Under the common law, duress is categorically unavailable as a defense to murder. The traditional rule, as stated in the chapter, "rests on the premise that one may not take a life to save one's own." This applies to felony murder: because the charge is murder, the defense is barred, full stop.

The defendant's knowledge of the gun is irrelevant under the common law murder bar because the defense is unavailable regardless of the defendant's mental state. Whether she knew about the gun or not, she is charged with murder, and duress does not apply to murder.

**Conclusion under common law:** Defense barred. Felony murder is murder. Duress is unavailable for murder.

**Analysis under the Michigan/Maryland exception (*Reichard*):** Under *Reichard*, "duress may be asserted as an affirmative defense to felony murder if the defense is available for the underlying felony." The logic: "in felony murder, the defendant is faced with a choice of being killed or committing some lesser felony" -- not choosing between lives. The chain of liability runs through the underlying felony (robbery), and if duress excuses the robbery, the felony murder charge collapses.

But this defendant's case is harder than Reichard's. The critical distinction: **the getaway driver knew her partner carried a loaded gun into the store.** This knowledge changes the analysis in several ways:

1. *Foreseeability of homicide:* Reichard "claimed she did not know Beatty intended to kill Cramton." This defendant knew her partner had a gun. A person who knows her co-felon is armed with a loaded gun can foresee the possibility of lethal violence. The *Reichard* court emphasized that the defendant was "not choosing between lives" -- but a defendant who knowingly sends an armed partner into a store arguably *is* choosing to risk an innocent life.

2. *Nature of the underlying felony:* Under *Reichard*, duress must be available for the underlying felony (robbery). The question is whether the defendant's knowledge of the gun transforms the underlying felony from simple robbery to armed robbery in a way that changes the duress analysis. Under *Reichard*'s logic, this should not matter -- duress can still apply to armed robbery as an underlying felony. But the knowledge of the gun makes the claim that the defendant had no reason to anticipate violence much less credible.

3. *Jury question:* *Reichard* directed that "the trial court must provide duress instructions if a rational view of the evidence supports a conclusion she aided Beatty with the robbery out of duress." The getaway driver can argue she participated in the robbery under duress. The question is whether her knowledge of the gun, combined with her decision to proceed, undermines the duress claim or constitutes a separate question for the jury.

**Conclusion under *Reichard*:** The defense is available in principle -- *Reichard* allows duress for felony murder when duress excuses the underlying felony. But the defendant's knowledge that her partner carried a loaded gun significantly weakens her claim. A jury could find that a person of reasonable firmness, knowing that her partner was armed and about to rob a store, would have refused to participate despite the threats -- or at minimum would have warned the store clerk or failed to show up. The knowledge of the gun makes "no reasonable alternative" much harder to establish.

**MPC analysis:** Under the MPC, duress is available as a defense to all crimes, including murder. The defendant must show that a "person of reasonable firmness in his situation" would have yielded to the threat.

The MPC would treat this as a straightforward duress case. The repeated beatings and death threats establish the coercive context. The question is whether a person of reasonable firmness would have participated in the robbery knowing her partner carried a loaded gun. The MPC's "situation" analysis incorporates the defendant's history of abuse -- expert testimony on battering would be relevant to explain why the defendant complied despite knowing the risks.

But even under the MPC, the knowledge of the gun creates tension. A person of reasonable firmness who knows that compliance will likely lead to an innocent person's death faces a different calculus than a person who thinks she is participating in a non-violent crime. The MPC's reasonable firmness standard may demand more resistance when the foreseeable harm to third parties is lethal.

**Does the defendant's awareness that violence was likely change the analysis?** Yes, under all three frameworks:

- Under the common law, it is irrelevant because the murder bar is categorical.
- Under *Reichard*, it weakens the duress claim for the underlying felony by undermining the "no reasonable alternative" element and making it harder for the jury to find that the defendant was genuinely coerced rather than complicit.
- Under the MPC, it raises the standard of what "reasonable firmness" requires -- a person of reasonable firmness may be expected to resist coercion when compliance will foreseeably result in an innocent person's death, even at personal risk.

**Key split:** The three frameworks diverge on whether the type of charge (murder vs. underlying felony) controls the availability of the defense. The common law uses a categorical rule (no duress for murder, period). *Reichard* disaggregates the charges (duress applies to the underlying felony, and if it succeeds, felony murder collapses). The MPC ignores the murder/non-murder distinction entirely and applies the same standard to all crimes. The knowledge-of-the-gun complication exposes the practical difference: only *Reichard* and the MPC allow the defense to reach the jury, but both must grapple with how the defendant's awareness of foreseeable lethal violence affects the reasonableness inquiry.

**Best arguments on each side:**

- *For the common law murder bar:* A defendant who knowingly assists an armed robber has placed an innocent life at risk. The murder bar exists precisely for this situation: we do not permit defendants to save their own lives by facilitating the deaths of others. Reichard's distinction between "choosing between lives" and "choosing between a lesser felony and death" collapses when the lesser felony foreseeably involves lethal violence. A getaway driver who knows her partner has a gun and will use it to threaten a store clerk is choosing to risk that clerk's life to save her own. That is the exact moral situation the murder bar addresses.

- *For the Reichard/MPC approach:* The murder bar punishes the victim of domestic violence for the act of her abuser. Reichard correctly identifies the logical flaw: in felony murder, the defendant is not the killer. She is charged with murder because of her participation in a lesser felony. If that participation was coerced, the chain of liability should break. The knowledge of the gun complicates the analysis but does not resolve it -- many domestic violence victims participate in escalating violence because their abuser's threats are credible and resistance has proved futile. The question of whether this particular defendant's knowledge of the gun, in the context of her abuse history, should defeat the defense is exactly the kind of factual determination a jury should make. Categorical bars prevent the jury from hearing the evidence that most matters.

### Problem 8 Hypothetical: Duress, Parental Duty, and Failure to Protect

**Doctrinal focus:** Whether the "person of reasonable firmness" standard demands more resistance from a parent whose crime is failing to protect her own child; whether expert testimony on battering is admissible to explain why a battered parent might not report abuse; and the tension between the duty to protect and the reality of coercive control.

**Facts:** A woman in an abusive relationship learns her partner is molesting her daughter. The partner threatens to kill both if she reports. She does not report. The abuse continues for six months before a teacher discovers it. She is charged with felony child endangerment for failing to protect her daughter. She asserts duress.

**Common law analysis:** Under the common law, duress requires: (1) threat of death or serious bodily injury; (2) imminent; (3) directed at defendant or close relation; (4) no reasonable alternative; and (5) ordinary person would yield.

- *Threat of death or GBH:* The partner threatened to kill the woman and her daughter. Satisfied.
- *Imminence:* The threat is ongoing but not tied to a specific moment. The abuse continued for six months. Under the common law's strict imminence requirement, the threat must be temporally proximate to the criminal act. But the criminal act here is an omission -- a continuous failure to report. The threat is also continuous: the partner lives in the home and the threat is ever-present. A common law court could find imminence satisfied because the threat accompanies every potential moment of reporting. Alternatively, the court could hold that six months is too long -- at some point during those six months, the defendant had an opportunity to report.

- *No reasonable alternative:* This is the most problematic element. The defendant could have called the police, contacted child protective services, told a teacher, confided in a family member, or gone to a domestic violence shelter. The common law evaluates these alternatives objectively. A court could hold that reasonable alternatives existed, particularly given the six-month timeframe.

- *Ordinary person would yield:* This is where the *Webb* analysis becomes relevant. The *Webb* jury split its verdict: it acquitted June Webb of being an accessory after the fact to murder but convicted her of failing to obtain medical care for her own child. The jury apparently held Webb to a higher standard when the victim was her child and she had a legal duty to protect him. This suggests that "ordinary person" analysis incorporates the defendant's legal obligations -- an ordinary *parent* would resist coercion to protect her child, even at personal risk.

**Conclusion under common law:** The defense likely fails. The six-month duration undermines imminence. Reasonable alternatives existed (police, CPS, teachers, shelters). And the *Webb* precedent suggests that a parent is held to a higher standard of resistance when the victim is her child.

**MPC analysis:** Under the MPC, duress requires a threat of unlawful force (satisfied), applies to threats against any person (satisfied), drops strict imminence (helping the defendant), and asks whether a "person of reasonable firmness in his situation" would have yielded.

The MPC's flexible imminence standard helps the defendant. The threat is continuous because the abuser lives in the home. The MPC does not require temporal proximity between threat and criminal act.

But the "person of reasonable firmness" standard is the critical question. Does "situation" include the defendant's status as a parent? The MPC drafters said "situation" includes "stark, tangible factors" like size, strength, age, and health. Parental status is arguably a different kind of factor -- it is a legal relationship that creates duties, not a physical characteristic. But the practical reality is that being a parent changes the situation: the defendant is not choosing between her own safety and obedience to the law; she is choosing between her own safety and her child's protection.

The *Webb* jury's implicit holding -- that a parent must resist coercion more forcefully when the victim is her child -- is consistent with the MPC's "reasonable firmness" standard. A person of reasonable firmness who is a parent might be expected to take greater risks to protect her child than to protect a stranger. This does not convert the standard into a subjective one; it asks what a reasonable *parent* would do, which is still an objective inquiry.

**Should expert testimony on battering be admissible?** Yes, under both frameworks, for several reasons:

1. *Explaining why the defendant did not report:* A person unfamiliar with domestic violence dynamics might assume that a mother who does not report her child's sexual abuse is complicit. Expert testimony explains that battered partners often do not report because they have learned -- through experience -- that reporting leads to escalated violence. This is an objective fact about the defendant's circumstances, not a subjective excuse.

2. *Contextualizing "reasonable alternatives":* Expert testimony helps the jury understand why alternatives that seem obvious to outsiders (call the police, go to a shelter) may not have been perceived as viable by someone in the defendant's situation. The *Dingwall* court held that "the 'reasonableness' of these alternatives must be viewed through the lens of the defendant's circumstances."

3. *The coercive control framework:* The chapter's discussion of coercive control is directly relevant. The abuser's threat is not a discrete event but part of a "regime of total control." Expert testimony can explain that the defendant's failure to act is not passivity but a survival strategy developed in response to systematic domination.

**Does the "person of reasonable firmness" standard demand more of a parent?** This is the central normative question, and reasonable students will disagree.

The *Webb* jury's split verdict suggests yes: a parent has a legal duty to protect her child, and that duty should be factored into the reasonableness analysis. The common law has always imposed heightened duties on parents -- criminal neglect and endangerment statutes exist precisely because parents bear special responsibility. If "reasonable firmness" is calibrated to the defendant's situation, and the situation includes being a parent, then reasonable firmness requires more resistance when the child is the victim.

But this creates a cruel paradox: the defendant who is *most* vulnerable to coercion (a battered parent) is held to the *highest* standard of resistance. The abuser exploits the parent's love for her child as a lever of control -- threatening to harm the child is the ultimate coercive tool. Holding the parent to a higher standard because the victim is her child means the abuser's most effective coercive strategy triggers the defendant's most demanding legal obligation. The doctrine penalizes the defendant for the very vulnerability the abuser exploits.

**Key split:** The common law's strict imminence requirement and categorical approach to legal alternatives make the defense harder for the mother. The MPC's flexible standards give the defense a better chance of reaching the jury. But both frameworks must confront whether parental duty raises the bar for reasonable firmness, and neither provides a clear doctrinal answer.

**Best arguments on each side:**

- *For holding parents to a higher standard:* Children are uniquely vulnerable. They cannot protect themselves, and the law imposes on parents a non-delegable duty to protect them. A "person of reasonable firmness" who is a parent should be expected to take extraordinary risks to prevent ongoing sexual abuse of her child. The alternative -- treating parental duty as no different from any other obligation -- means the law values the parent's safety above the child's protection. Six months of ongoing molestation represents a catastrophic failure of the protective duty, and the defendant's fear for her own safety, while understandable, does not excuse six months of inaction while her child suffers. Society expects parents to put their children first, even at great personal cost.

- *Against holding parents to a higher standard:* The heightened-duty argument punishes victims for being victims. The mother is not an indifferent bystander -- she is a battered woman trapped in a coercive relationship. Demanding that she take greater risks because the victim is her child ignores the reality that the abuser deliberately uses the child as leverage. The mother who does not report is not choosing her own safety over her child's -- she may genuinely believe that reporting will trigger the threatened violence against both of them. Expert testimony consistently shows that the most dangerous time for a domestic violence victim is when she tries to leave or report. Holding her to a higher standard of resistance because she is a parent means the law demands she risk her life and her child's life on the uncertain promise that the police will arrive in time. The MPC's "person of reasonable firmness in his situation" standard should incorporate the full reality of the defendant's coercive situation, including the abuser's demonstrated willingness to carry out threats. A truly reasonable person, fully informed of the dynamics of coercive control, might conclude that not reporting -- while agonizing -- was the choice most likely to keep both mother and child alive.

### Problem 8 (Reform) Hypothetical: Three Reform Pathways Applied to Chapter Cases

**Doctrinal focus:** Comparing three reform approaches -- traditional duress doctrine, Survivors' Justice Acts (sentencing reform), and a coercive control defense -- and evaluating which produces the most just outcome across different crime types.

**Facts (revisited):** The hypothetical asks us to return to three cases from the chapter:
- *Dingwall:* armed robberies committed while abusive boyfriend Stanley was at work
- *Romero:* robberies committed because Terrance struck her "almost every day" and said "if I can't have you, nobody else can"
- *Webb:* failure to protect her six-year-old son because Keith threatened to kill her, her other children, and her family

**Analysis under traditional duress framework:**

*Dingwall:* The defense faces obstacles on imminence (Stanley was at work during the robberies) and reasonable alternatives (she was alone in the stores and could have called police or walked away). The Seventh Circuit reversed the exclusion of the defense, but the underlying elements remain difficult. Imminence is the central problem. Under strict common law, the defense fails because Stanley was not present. Under the *Dingwall* court's expanded approach (continuous threat based on pattern of abuse), the defense reaches the jury but its success depends on expert testimony and jury evaluation.

*Romero:* Similar obstacles. The robberies were part of a pattern of coercion, but each robbery occurred at a different time and place, and Romero was not always under Terrance's immediate physical control. The Court of Appeal found that the absence of expert testimony on battered woman syndrome was fatal to the defense -- without it, the jury could not understand why Romero complied. The traditional framework requires case-by-case adjudication that depends heavily on whether the court admits expert testimony.

*Webb:* The hardest case. Webb's crime is an omission -- failure to protect -- not an affirmative act. The parental duty to protect creates a heightened standard. The jury convicted her on the failure-to-protect charges while acquitting on the accessory charge, implying that the coercion was sufficient to excuse post-hoc assistance but not sufficient to excuse failing to protect her child. Under traditional duress, the defense is weakest here.

**Analysis under Survivors' Justice Acts:**

A Survivors' Justice Act would not change the trial outcome for any of these defendants. If the defense fails and they are convicted, the sentencing court would be required or permitted to consider their abuse history as a mitigating factor. The practical effect:

- Dingwall might receive a substantially reduced sentence for the armed robberies.
- Romero might receive a reduced sentence for the robberies.
- Webb might receive a reduced sentence for the failure-to-protect charges.

The limitation: each still stands convicted. The label "criminal" attaches. The conviction remains on the record. The Survivors' Justice Act fills the gap *after* conviction but does not address whether the conviction itself was just. As the chapter notes, these laws "leave the conviction intact."

The strength of this approach is political feasibility and retroactive application. The Georgia Act passed with "near-unanimous support" because it was framed as victim protection. Incarcerated survivors can petition for resentencing. This provides practical relief to people already in prison.

**Analysis under a coercive control defense:**

A standalone coercive control defense would allow each defendant to argue that the "regime of domination" -- not a discrete threat at the moment of the crime -- overrode her will. This reframes the inquiry from "was there an imminent threat at the moment of the criminal act?" to "was the defendant operating under a demonstrated pattern of coercive control that eliminated her autonomy?"

- *Dingwall:* Stanley's pattern -- physical beatings, emotional abuse, pistol-whipping, shooting into the mattress -- establishes coercive control. The fact that he was at work during the robberies is irrelevant under this framework because the coercion is ambient, not episodic. The defense would likely succeed.

- *Romero:* Terrance's daily beatings and the statement "if I can't have you, nobody else can" constitute textbook coercive control. Again, the defense would likely succeed.

- *Webb:* Keith's systematic abuse of June, his other wife, the children, and his control over the household through violence establish coercive control. The defense would address the most difficult case -- failure to protect -- by recognizing that June's failure to act was not indifference or complicity but a rational response to a regime of total domination. Whether the defense succeeds depends on whether the coercive control framework can overcome the parental duty objection -- i.e., whether even complete coercive control can excuse a parent's failure to protect her child from ongoing sexual abuse or lethal neglect.

**Which approach produces the most just outcome?**

This is the central normative question, and the answer depends on one's theory of criminal law:

**Best arguments on each side:**

- *For the traditional duress framework (status quo):* The elements of duress -- imminence, reasonable alternatives, objective standard -- exist for good reasons. They prevent the defense from becoming available to every defendant who claims coercion. Relaxing these requirements risks allowing genuinely culpable defendants to escape liability by invoking past abuse. The framework can accommodate domestic violence through expanded admissibility of expert testimony (the *Dingwall* approach) without abandoning its structural safeguards. And the traditional framework forces case-by-case adjudication, which is appropriate for a defense that asks the law to excuse criminal conduct.

- *For Survivors' Justice Acts:* These laws provide the most practical, politically achievable relief. They do not require courts to rethink foundational doctrine. They apply retroactively to people already incarcerated. They work with the existing system rather than against it. The criticism that they "leave the conviction intact" is less compelling than it appears: in practice, what matters most to incarcerated survivors is the length of the sentence, not the label. Reducing a 20-year sentence to 5 years is a more tangible form of justice than an acquittal that never comes because the duress defense fails at trial.

- *For a coercive control defense:* Only a standalone defense addresses the structural mismatch between duress doctrine and domestic violence reality. The traditional framework was "built for the gun-to-the-head scenario" and "struggles with sustained coercion." Sentencing reform accepts the conviction and works around it. A coercive control defense confronts the injustice directly: the defendant was not a criminal but a victim, and the law should recognize that at the adjudication stage, not merely at sentencing. The focus shifts from the defendant's psychology (what is wrong with her that she did not leave?) to the abuser's conduct (what did he do to make leaving impossible?). This is both more accurate and more just.

- *Does the answer differ by crime type?* Yes. For robbery (Dingwall, Romero), a coercive control defense most clearly aligns with justice -- the defendants were forced to commit crimes they would not otherwise have committed, and the traditional framework's imminence requirement artificially excludes them. For failure to protect a child (Webb), the analysis is harder because the crime involves a duty to a vulnerable third party. Even the most expansive coercive control defense must grapple with whether *any* level of coercion can excuse a parent's failure to protect her child from ongoing lethal abuse. A Survivors' Justice Act may be the most appropriate response for this category: it acknowledges the coercion at sentencing while preserving the principle that parents bear a non-delegable duty to protect their children. For homicide, the coercive control defense is most controversial because the common law murder bar reflects a deeply held intuition that one may not take a life to save one's own -- though Reichard and the MPC suggest this intuition may be more malleable than the common law acknowledges.
