## After: The Dan Markel Murder -- Duration and Withdrawal

### Discussion Questions: The Markel Conspiracy

1. Prosecutors alleged a single conspiracy among all defendants. Defense attorneys argued for multiple smaller conspiracies. What evidence would support each characterization? Why does the distinction matter?

2. Under *Pinkerton*, Charlie Adelson is liable for first-degree murder even though Garcia pulled the trigger. Is this just? Does it matter that murder was the very object of the conspiracy, rather than an incidental crime?

3. Suppose Donna Adelson gave Charlie money believing he would use it to hire a lawyer to fight the custody battle. Unknown to her, Charlie used it to pay the killers. Is Donna part of the conspiracy? What would prosecutors need to prove?

4. Wendi Adelson has never been charged. Assume she complained bitterly to her brother about Markel and said she wished he were dead, but never agreed to any specific plan. Is she part of the conspiracy? What additional evidence would prosecutors need?

5. The Markel case took nearly a decade to prosecute fully. What challenges does a sprawling conspiracy case pose for prosecutors? For defendants? For the justice system's goal of timely resolution?

## After: The Elements of Conspiracy -- Breaking Down the Elements (Overt Act)

### Discussion Questions

1. Why might the law permit conspiracy liability to attach much earlier than attempt liability? What dangers does group criminality present that individual preparation does not?

2. Consider the "overt act" requirement. If the act can be entirely legal and remote from the crime, what work is this requirement actually doing? Could a jurisdiction eliminate it entirely?

3. The federal conspiracy statute caps punishment at five years but permits cumulative punishment for both the conspiracy and the completed crime. How does this differ from the MPC approach? Which better serves the purposes of criminal punishment?

## After: Note -- The Bilateral-Unilateral Debate

### Discussion Questions

1. Which approach better serves the rationale for conspiracy? If conspiracy is justified by the "group danger" of coordinated criminal activity, the bilateral approach makes sense: no real group, no real danger. If conspiracy is justified by the defendant's demonstrated criminal intent, the unilateral approach is more logical. Which rationale do you find more persuasive?

2. Consider the practical implications. Under the bilateral rule, an undercover officer who infiltrates a drug ring and "agrees" to participate cannot be the basis of a conspiracy charge against other members (at least not a conspiracy with the officer alone). Does this create a significant gap in law enforcement? Or does it simply mean that prosecutors must prove a conspiracy among the *genuine* participants?

3. How does this debate interact with the entrapment defense? If a government agent proposes a criminal scheme and a defendant agrees, should the legal system address this through the bilateral conspiracy rule, the entrapment defense, or both?

## After: State v. Pond

### Discussion Questions

1. The majority holds that conspiracy requires specific intent as to every element of the target offense, even elements that carry no specific intent requirement for the principal. What justifies this heightened mental state requirement?

2. Consider the dissent's argument: If you can be convicted of the substantive offense without intending the weapon element, why should you escape conspiracy liability for the same crime? Is this truly an "irrational gap" in the law?

3. How does the court's holding interact with the *Pinkerton* doctrine? Under *Pinkerton*, could Pond be held liable for the armed robbery committed by his coconspirators even though he cannot be convicted of conspiring to commit it?

4. Apply this holding to the Garcia case. If Garcia agreed to participate in a plot to "scare" Dan Markel but claims he never intended for Markel to be killed, could he be convicted of conspiracy to commit murder? What evidence might the prosecution use to prove his intent?

## After: People v. Koenig

### Discussion Questions

1. *Koenig* distinguishes between crimes of negligence (which cannot be the object of a conspiracy) and general intent crimes (which can be). Why does this distinction matter? What is the logical basis for holding that one can conspire to commit a general intent crime but not a crime of negligence?

2. The court notes that "a person cannot agree and specifically intend to accomplish an unintended result." Apply this principle to homicide. Can there be a conspiracy to commit involuntary manslaughter? Reckless murder? What about felony murder?

3. How does this analysis apply to the Purdue Pharma case? The executives pleaded guilty to "misbranding," a strict liability offense. Could they have been charged with conspiracy to commit misbranding? Would that require proof of a mental state higher than what the underlying offense requires?

## After: People v. Lauria

### Discussion Questions

1. *Lauria* draws a line between knowledge and purpose. A telephone answering service that knowingly serves prostitutes is not a conspirator. But what if the service specifically advertised to prostitutes? What if it offered a "premium package" with coded message delivery? At what point does the supplier cross from knowledge to purpose?

2. The court suggests that for "aggravated" crimes, knowledge alone may suffice to infer intent. Apply this principle to modern examples. Is a gun dealer who sells to a buyer he suspects plans a mass shooting a conspirator? What about a cryptocurrency exchange that processes transactions it knows are connected to ransomware?

3. How does *Lauria* interact with *Pond*? *Pond* requires that a conspirator specifically intend every element of the target offense. *Lauria* requires purpose rather than mere knowledge. Together, do these principles adequately protect individuals from overbroad conspiracy liability?

4. Consider the Purdue Pharma case. Wholesalers and pharmacies continued to distribute OxyContin in suspicious quantities. Under *Lauria*, what would prosecutors need to prove to charge them as coconspirators rather than mere suppliers?

## After: Pinkerton v. United States

### Discussion Questions

1. Under traditional accomplice liability, Daniel Pinkerton would need to have aided, abetted, or encouraged the specific crimes for which he was convicted. How does *Pinkerton* liability differ from accomplice liability? What additional conduct does it reach?

2. Justice Rutledge objects that the majority's rule creates "vicarious criminal responsibility as broad as, or broader than, the vicarious civil liability of a partner." Is the partnership analogy apt? What are the differences between civil partnership liability and criminal conspiracy?

3. Daniel was in prison when some of Walter's crimes were committed. Under the majority's reasoning, how could Daniel withdraw from the conspiracy? What "affirmative action" would be sufficient?

4. Consider the limiting principle suggested by the majority: *Pinkerton* liability does not apply if the substantive offense "could not be reasonably foreseen as a necessary or natural consequence of the unlawful agreement." How much work does this limitation actually do? In a drug trafficking conspiracy, what crimes could *not* be reasonably foreseen?

## After: Interstate Circuit v. United States

### Discussion Questions

1. The Court states that "agreement" was "not a prerequisite to an unlawful conspiracy" in this case. If agreement is the core of conspiracy, how can there be a conspiracy without an agreement? What is doing the work that "agreement" normally does?

2. Consider the Court's reasoning: each distributor knew that the others were being asked to participate, knew that cooperation was essential, and chose to participate anyway. Is this sufficient to infer an agreement? Or does this reasoning effectively eliminate the agreement requirement in cases of coordinated conduct?

3. How does this case apply to modern platform economics? If several rideshare companies independently adopt surge pricing algorithms that produce similar price increases during high-demand periods, have they "conspired" to fix prices?

4. The *Interstate Circuit* standard makes sense in antitrust cases where the harm is economic and the penalties relatively modest. Should the same relaxed standard for inferring agreement apply to violent conspiracies where defendants face decades in prison?

## After: United States v. Pires

### Discussion Questions

1. How does *Pires* apply the *Interstate Circuit* principle to a criminal case? What evidence allowed the jury to infer that Pires and Eli had reached an agreement, even without direct proof of a conversation where they agreed to traffic Daisy?

2. The court notes that the "agreement need not be express; it may be inferred from the defendants' coordinated actions and the circumstances of the case." What are the limits of this principle? At what point does coordination stop being evidence of agreement and start being mere parallel conduct?

3. Compare this case to *Interstate Circuit*. In both cases, the court inferred agreement from coordinated conduct. What distinguishes the two? Is there a meaningful difference between business competitors who independently adopt the same pricing strategy and two individuals who independently exploit the same victim?

4. The court approved expert testimony about "the language and dynamics of sex trafficking." How might similar expert testimony be used in drug conspiracy cases? In corporate fraud cases? What are the risks of allowing experts to explain the "patterns" of criminal behavior?

## After: United States v. Alvarez

### Discussion Questions

1. The panel distinguished between "planning to assist" in a crime and "agreeing" to commit a conspiracy. Is this a meaningful distinction? If Alvarez intended to help unload marijuana (a crime), why shouldn't that intent suffice to establish agreement to the broader conspiracy?

2. The en banc majority and the dissent disagree about what inferences a reasonable jury could draw from Alvarez's nod and smile. What additional evidence would definitively establish (or negate) Alvarez's membership in the conspiracy?

3. Consider the policy implications. The panel's approach protects minor players from the full weight of conspiracy liability but may make it harder to prosecute organized crime. The en banc approach makes prosecution easier but risks conflating the mastermind and the day laborer. Which approach better serves the purposes of the criminal law?

4. How would Alvarez's case be analyzed under traditional accomplice liability? If he actually unloaded the marijuana, would he be guilty as an accomplice? If so, why should the conspiracy inquiry be any more demanding?

## After: United States v. Pauling -- The Buyer-Seller Exception

### Discussion Questions

1. The court distinguishes between a "buyer-seller" relationship (not a conspiracy) and a "shared purpose to further distribution" (a conspiracy). What specific facts moved Pauling from the first category to the second? Could a street-level dealer ever fall into the "buyer-seller" exception?

2. The court requires proof beyond a reasonable doubt for drug quantity but allows inference of conspiracy from circumstantial evidence. Is this asymmetry justified? Why might the law be more demanding about quantity than about the existence of an agreement?

3. Consider the "fronting" (credit) arrangement between Pauling and Low. Why does this suggest conspiracy rather than a simple commercial relationship? Would a grocery store that extends credit to a regular customer be in a "conspiracy" with that customer?

4. Pauling faced a mandatory minimum sentence if the conspiracy involved 100 grams of heroin. The government proved 89 grams. Should prosecutors be required to prove quantity to the same standard as conspiracy, or does the nature of drug trafficking justify inference-based calculations?

## After: Kotteakos v. United States

### Discussion Questions

1. *Kotteakos* establishes that a "wheel without a rim" constitutes multiple conspiracies rather than one. What would constitute a "rim" in a loan fraud scheme? If the borrowers had met at Brown's office and discussed their plans, would that suffice?

2. Why does it matter whether defendants are charged with one conspiracy or many? Consider the procedural consequences: joinder, hearsay admissibility, and *Pinkerton* liability.

3. Apply the wheel/chain analysis to a drug distribution network. A supplier sells to five dealers who sell to street-level distributors. Is this one conspiracy or five? What additional facts would help answer the question?

4. Consider the Young Thug/YSL RICO prosecution. The government alleges that all 28 defendants participated in a single criminal enterprise. Under *Kotteakos*, what must the government prove to establish that YSL was one conspiracy rather than a loose collection of individuals who happened to know the same people?

5. The Court emphasizes "transference of guilt" as a danger of trying multiple conspiracies as one. In an era of complex financial crime and overlapping criminal networks, how should courts balance the government's interest in efficient prosecution against defendants' interest in being tried only for their own agreements?

## After: Structural Limits on Conspiracy -- Wharton's Rule and Gebardi

### Discussion Questions

1. Wharton's Rule prevents conspiracy charges for crimes that inherently require two participants. But virtually every drug transaction requires a buyer and a seller. Why doesn't Wharton's Rule preclude conspiracy charges in drug cases? What distinguishes a drug sale from adultery or dueling for purposes of this rule?

2. *Gebardi* holds that a person the legislature intended to protect cannot be charged with conspiring to commit the offense from which she was exempted. Apply this principle to statutory rape. If a sixteen-year-old and a twenty-year-old agree to have sex in a jurisdiction where the age of consent is eighteen, can they be charged with conspiracy to commit statutory rape? Should they be?

3. Consider the relationship between *Gebardi* and the Purdue Pharma case. The patients who obtained OxyContin prescriptions were not charged as coconspirators. Is this because the controlled substances statutes are designed to protect them (the *Gebardi* principle)? Or simply because prosecutors exercised discretion not to charge them?

4. Both Wharton's Rule and *Gebardi* limit conspiracy liability. But they operate differently: Wharton's Rule is a structural argument about the nature of certain crimes, while *Gebardi* is a statutory interpretation argument about legislative intent. Which provides a more principled basis for limiting conspiracy? Can you think of a case where one doctrine would apply but not the other?

## After: United States v. Valle

### Discussion Questions

1. The court distinguishes between "genuine" agreement and "fantasy role-play." What facts would have moved Valle from the fantasy category into genuine conspiracy? If he had actually traveled to Sauer's house? If he had purchased restraints?

2. Valle used his law enforcement credentials to access a database and search for potential victims. The court characterizes this as "troubling" but insufficient. Do you agree? How should courts weigh steps that are illegal but not themselves criminal (like policy violations) in evaluating conspiracy?

3. Consider the First Amendment implications. The court notes that "the right to think and to express thoughts, even repugnant ones" is protected. But conspiracy is a crime of agreement. Can an agreement ever be protected speech? What about song lyrics that describe criminal activity (as in the Young Thug case)?

4. How would *Valle* apply to online discussions about terrorism? About mass shootings? Should the "fantasy" defense be available for all categories of crime, or only for some?

## After: Corporate Conspiracy

### Discussion Questions: The Asymmetry of Conspiracy Enforcement

1. Both Purdue Pharma and the Garcia conspirators agreed to commit serious crimes. Why did the government pursue felony conspiracy charges against all participants in the Garcia case but allow Purdue executives to plead to misdemeanors? What factors might explain this difference?

2. In the Garcia case, the conspiracy extended from the shooter to family members who allegedly financed the killing. In the Purdue case, the conspiracy was attributed only to the corporate entity, not to the individual executives who made the decisions. Is this distinction justified?

3. The Purdue plea agreement imposed a $3.544 billion fine, "the largest penalties ever levied against a pharmaceutical manufacturer." By the time of the plea, Purdue's conduct had contributed to hundreds of thousands of deaths and trillions of dollars in social costs. Does the penalty reflect the harm? How should we measure proportionality in corporate conspiracy cases?

4. Consider the procedural advantages of conspiracy: venue in any district where an overt act occurred, statements of coconspirators admissible against all, collective trial of multiple defendants. How might these features have been deployed against Purdue executives? Why weren't they?

## After: Variation -- The Young Thug RICO Case

### Application Questions

1. Under the traditional conspiracy analysis, what must the prosecution prove to convict Young Thug of conspiracy? How does Georgia's RICO statute modify these requirements?

2. The prosecution cites song lyrics as "overt acts in furtherance of the conspiracy." Under *Hyde v. United States*, an overt act need not be illegal or even substantial; it need only be "done to effect" the conspiracy "in any degree." Do song lyrics satisfy this standard? What are the First Amendment implications?

3. Consider *Pinkerton* liability. If Young Thug is convicted of RICO conspiracy, can he be held liable for murders, armed robberies, and carjackings committed by alleged YSL members, even crimes committed before he joined or after he may have left the organization? What facts would the prosecution need to prove?

4. Compare this case to the Purdue Pharma prosecution. Both involve allegations of a criminal enterprise with a hierarchy, coordinated activity, and multiple participants. Both involve conduct causing substantial harm. Why does the YSL case result in individual felony charges against dozens of defendants while the Purdue case resulted in corporate guilty pleas and executive misdemeanors?

5. The indictment identifies YSL as a "criminal street gang." How might this characterization affect jury perception? Does it invoke the "group danger" rationale for conspiracy, or does it risk the "birds of a feather" prejudice that Justice Jackson warned against?

## After: The Rationales for Conspiracy -- Criticisms

### Discussion Questions: Evaluating Conspiracy Doctrine

1. The "group danger" rationale suggests that conspiracy is justified because groups are more effective at committing crimes. But accomplice liability already addresses group criminality. What does conspiracy add that accomplice liability does not provide?

2. If conspiracy doctrine is primarily a tool for law enforcement (allowing early intervention, plea leverage, and evidentiary advantages), should these practical benefits drive the substantive definition of the crime? Or should substantive criminal liability be defined by principles of culpability, with enforcement tools developed separately?

3. Consider the role of race in conspiracy enforcement. Drug conspiracy prosecutions disproportionately target Black and Latino defendants, often for conduct that, viewed individually, would not warrant severe punishment. Does the "group danger" rationale justify this disparate impact?

4. The Model Penal Code rejected *Pinkerton* liability and prohibited cumulative punishment for conspiracy and the completed offense. Most reformed state codes have followed the MPC. Yet federal law retains these features. Why might Congress and federal prosecutors prefer the traditional approach?

## After: Synthesis -- Conspiracy as System Architecture

### Final Discussion Questions

1. We began this chapter by noting that conspiracy is "among the most powerful weapons in the criminal law arsenal." After examining the doctrine, do you believe this power is justified? What limiting principles would you propose?

2. Consider the Garcia case one final time. Multiple defendants were convicted of conspiracy to commit murder, from the shooter to family members who allegedly arranged and financed the killing. Is this the appropriate scope for conspiracy liability? Should the grandmother who allegedly helped arrange the hit face the same conspiracy charge as the man who pulled the trigger?

3. The Purdue Pharma case involved a decade-long conspiracy that contributed to hundreds of thousands of deaths. The Garcia case involved a conspiracy that killed one person. Which conspiracy caused more harm? Which conspirators received harsher punishment? What does this tell us about the design of our criminal legal system?

4. If you were asked to draft a new conspiracy statute, what would it look like? Consider: the definition of agreement, the mens rea requirement, whether to retain *Pinkerton* liability, whether to permit cumulative punishment, and what procedural rules should apply.
