### Structural / Categorization Issues

#### highly-extraordinary-result classified as "limit"
- **Map classifies** "highly-extraordinary-result" as a **Liability Limit** in the Elements section header and the Coverage Checklist.
- **Issue:** This is not a liability limit (a doctrine that categorically bars or reduces liability like withdrawal or Wharton's Rule). It is a standard for applying proximate cause — part of the prima facie element of legal causation. The common law "highly extraordinary" standard defines when proximate cause is *absent*, but it is not a separate affirmative limit on liability.
- **Verdict:** MISCATEGORIZED — Should be classified as an element (legal cause standard), not a limit. The Coverage Checklist should show "element" not "limit" for this refinement.

#### risking-vs-causing-distinction classified as "limit"
- **Map classifies** "risking-vs-causing-distinction," "mpc-catastrophe-statute," and "pa-nj-catastrophe-variants" as **Liability Limits** in the Coverage Checklist.
- **Issue:** These statutes are not limits on liability — they are *alternative liability bases* (a different type of offense that eliminates causation). Calling them "limits" is misleading; they are better classified as extensions or as a separate category. However, the v4 prompt uses "limit" for "doctrines that categorically bar or reduce liability" — these do the opposite. They could reasonably be classified as extensions (they expand liability by eliminating the causation requirement) or could be treated as a distinct category.
- **Verdict:** MISCATEGORIZED — risking-vs-causing-distinction, mpc-catastrophe-statute, pa-nj-catastrophe-variants, and failure-to-prevent-affirmative-duty should be classified as **extensions** (they expand liability beyond the prima facie causation-requiring result crime to cover risk creation). The Liability Limits section header is genuinely confusing here.

#### "highly-extraordinary-result" in Liability Limits vs. Elements
- **Map's Elements section** lists "Highly extraordinary result" and "Unforeseeable result" under **Liability Limits**. But the refinement is filed under the "Legal Cause — Common Law Approach" section of Refinements, which is appropriate.
- **Issue:** The Elements section's "Liability Limits" list includes "Highly extraordinary result" as a categorical bar, but actually the highly-extraordinary-result standard is just the California formulation of the proximate cause element — not a separate doctrine that "categorically bars" liability.
- **Verdict:** MISCATEGORIZED — consistent with the issue above. "Highly extraordinary result" and "Unforeseeable result" in Liability Limits should move to prima facie elements or be described as specific formulations of the legal causation element.
