## Elements

### Prima Facie Elements

1. **Factual cause (but-for cause)** — The defendant's conduct must have actually contributed to the result: the harm would not have occurred but for the defendant's act. This is primarily an empirical question.
2. **Legal cause (proximate cause)** — The connection between the defendant's conduct and the result must be close enough to fairly hold the defendant responsible. This is a normative question about what should count.

### Liability Extensions

1. **Risking catastrophe / widespread harm (conduct crime variant)** — Pennsylvania (§ 3302) and New Jersey (§ 2C:17-2) statutes extend criminal liability to the *reckless creation of risk* of catastrophe or widespread harm, eliminating the causation requirement entirely. These are conduct crimes — no harm need occur and no causal chain need be traced.
2. **Failure to prevent or mitigate (NJ)** — New Jersey imposes a separate affirmative duty on corporate managers who learn of a risk of widespread harm under their management or control and fail to take reasonable measures to prevent or mitigate it — extending liability to pure omissions.

### Liability Limits

1. **Intervening superseding cause** — When a third party's voluntary, independent act comes between the defendant's conduct and the harmful result, that act may sever the causal chain and relieve the defendant of liability.
2. **Unforeseeable result (common law)** — If the actual result was not a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the defendant's conduct, the defendant is not the proximate cause under the common law approach. Under California's formulation, the defendant is not liable if the result was so unexpected that it would be "highly extraordinary" to attribute it to the defendant's conduct.
3. **Too remote or accidental (MPC)** — Under MPC § 2.03, even where but-for causation is satisfied, the legal cause element fails if the actual result was too remote or accidental in its occurrence to have a just bearing on the actor's liability.

### Causation Requirement — Scope

#### result-vs-conduct-crime: When Causation Applies — Result Crimes Only
- **Rule:** Causation is an element only of "result crimes" — offenses that require the defendant's conduct to produce a specific forbidden result (murder requires causing death; arson requires causing a fire). Conduct crimes (assault, sexual assault, fraud) require no causal chain.

#### two-component-inquiry: Two-Part Causation Inquiry
- **Rule:** Every major framework requires two independent showings: (1) factual cause — did the defendant's conduct actually contribute to the result? — and (2) legal cause (proximate cause) — is the connection close enough to fairly ground criminal liability? Both must be satisfied.

### Factual Cause — But-For Test

#### but-for-test-basic: Standard But-For Test
- **Rule:** Factual causation requires that the prohibited result would not have occurred "but for" the defendant's conduct. If the same harm would have happened regardless of what the defendant did, the defendant did not cause it.
- **Source:** Chapter § "The Common Law Approach"; MPC § 2.03(1)(a)
- **Trap:** Students choose [concluding no factual causation when multiple factors contributed] because they confuse ["but-for cause"] with ["the only cause"].
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** If the victim would have died from an independent cause at the exact same moment regardless of the defendant's act, the defendant's conduct is not the but-for cause of death.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal
- **Practiced:** Slides 6, 11 — Professor presented the but-for test and explicitly clarified that it requires necessity, not sole causation.

#### concurrent-sufficient-causes: Concurrent Sufficient Causes — But-For Limitation
- **Rule:** When two independent acts each would have been sufficient to cause the harm, and both occur simultaneously, a strict but-for test yields the counterintuitive result that neither actor caused the harm (remove either act and the other would still have caused the result). Courts address this by finding each actor a but-for cause, or by using alternative causation tests.
- **Source:** Slide 7 hypothetical (two people shoot a third simultaneously)
- **Trap:** Students choose [concluding that neither of two simultaneous shooters caused the death] because they confuse [a strict mechanical application of the but-for test] with [the common-sense responsibility required for simultaneous causes].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If the two shots were fired sequentially rather than simultaneously, one shot would be the but-for cause of death while the other would be attempted murder.
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC) — different jurisdictions handle overdetermination differently; MPC's "antecedent but for" language shares the same tension.
- **Practiced:** Slide 7 — Professor presented this as a discussion question asking students how courts resolve the overdetermination problem.

### Legal Cause — Common Law Approach

#### proximate-cause-foreseeability: Common Law Proximate Cause — Reasonable Foreseeability
- **Rule:** Under the common law, the defendant is the proximate cause of a result only if that result was a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the defendant's conduct. Unforeseeable results sever the causal chain.
- **Source:** Chapter § "The Common Law Approach"; *People v. Acosta*
- **Trap:** Students choose [thinking "foreseeable" means "the defendant predicted it"] because they confuse [subjective foresight] with [the objective reasonableness standard].
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** If a meteorite strikes the defendant's assault victim while he staggers, the death is not proximately caused by the punch because the manner of death was not reasonably foreseeable, even though the punch set the scenario in motion.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority — common law/traditional; MPC uses a different standard.
- **Practiced:** Slides 6, 8 — Professor introduced proximate cause via the "reasonable person" foreseeability standard.

#### judge-controls-causation: Common Law — Judge Controls the Causation Inquiry
- **Rule:** Under the common law, courts decide as a matter of law whether a result was foreseeable and whether an intervening cause was sufficient to sever the causal chain. The jury finds facts within the framework the judge establishes; the judge, not the jury, makes the ultimate proximate cause determination.
- **Source:** Chapter § "The Common Law Approach"
- **Trap:** Students choose [assuming juries always decide causation] because they confuse [factual questions for the jury] with [legal questions for the judge, such as proximate cause].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If the judge submits the proximate cause question to the jury without a controlling legal standard, the case is decided under the MPC model, not the common law model.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority — common law; MPC deliberately reverses this allocation.

#### highly-extraordinary-result: California Standard — Not "Highly Extraordinary"
- **Rule:** California's common law formulation asks whether the result was "highly extraordinary." If a result was "a possible consequence which reasonably might have been contemplated" — not highly extraordinary — the defendant is the proximate cause. This is assessed by objective conditions, not the defendant's subjective state of mind.
- **Source:** *People v. Acosta*
- **Trap:** Students choose [treating "highly extraordinary" as equivalent to "unusual" or "rare"] because they confuse [an unprecedented specific mechanism of harm] with [an unforeseeable type of result].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If the helicopter crash was the *only* such crash in recorded police pursuit history, the court found it not "highly extraordinary" because the emotional dynamics of any pursuit create an appreciable probability of negligent pursuer conduct — but if the causal mechanism required an unprecedented chain of independent decisions, it might cross the threshold.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority — California's formulation; other states phrase the standard differently.
- **Practiced:** Slide 9 — Professor presented *Acosta* and the helicopter crash facts; students applied foreseeability reasoning.

#### sufficiently-direct-cause-standard: New York Standard — Sufficiently Direct Cause
- **Rule:** New York applies a "sufficiently direct cause" test: criminal liability attaches if the defendant's conduct was a sufficiently direct cause of the death, and the ultimate harm was something that should have been foreseen as reasonably related to the defendant's acts. The defendant's conduct need not be the sole or exclusive cause.
- **Source:** *People v. Arzon* (citing *People v. Kibbe*)
- **Trap:** Students choose [concluding no liability in Arzon when a second independent fire contributed] because they confuse ["not the sole cause"] with ["not a cause"].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If the firefighter's death had been caused by a stray bullet from an unrelated shooting rather than from being trapped between the two fires, the causal chain from the defendant's arson would have been severed by a truly independent, unforeseeable force.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority — New York's formulation.
- **Practiced:** Slides 10, 13 — Professor presented *Arzon* and then hypotheticals testing what would sever the causal chain (stray bullet, heart attack, probabilistic scenarios).

#### foreseeability-specificity-level: Level of Specificity for Foreseeability — Warner-Lambert Rule
- **Rule:** *Warner-Lambert* requires that the specific ignition mechanism (the *how* of the harm) be foreseeable, not merely that some explosion or death was foreseeable. Defendants who create a broad undifferentiated risk of harm are not liable if the specific triggering cause was neither foreseen nor foreseeable.
- **Source:** *People v. Warner-Lambert Co.*
- **Trap:** Students choose [finding causation in Warner-Lambert based on a general foreseeability analysis] because they confuse [the general foreseeability of a result] with [the specific foreseeability of the causal mechanism].
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If Warner-Lambert's records had identified one of the two specific ignition theories as the likely cause and they had failed to address it, foreseeability of that mechanism would have been established, potentially satisfying proximate cause.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority — New York's heightened specificity requirement; *Acosta*'s "appreciable probability" standard is more permissive.
- **Practiced:** Slide 12 — Professor presented *Warner-Lambert* and the Freshen-Up gum facts; Slide 14 asked students to reconcile the three cases.

#### acosta-arzon-warner-lambert-tension: Reconciling Acosta, Arzon, and Warner-Lambert
- **Rule:** The three cases apply different levels of specificity: *Acosta* (appreciable probability of some harmful consequence), *Arzon* (ultimate harm reasonably related to defendant's acts), *Warner-Lambert* (specific ignition mechanism must be foreseeable). They may not be fully consistent, and the tension may be explained by character-based assessments or policy distinctions rather than doctrinal coherence.
- **Source:** Chapter § "Questions" after the three cases; Slide 14
- **Trap:** Students choose [assuming cases apply a single uniform foreseeability test] because they confuse [a rigid doctrinal rule] with [contested applications driven by moral assessments of defendants].
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If the *Warner-Lambert* defendants were fleeing police in a high-speed chase rather than operating a factory, a court applying *Acosta*'s standard might have found liability.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority — these are competing formulations within the common law tradition.
- **Practiced:** Slide 14 — Professor directly asked students to reconcile the three cases and offered "character-based assessment" as one possible explanation.

### Legal Cause — Intervening Causes

#### dependent-vs-independent-intervening-cause: Dependent vs. Independent Intervening Causes
- **Rule:** A dependent intervening cause (set in motion by the defendant's act, such as negligent medical treatment of a wound the defendant inflicted) generally does not sever the causal chain. An independent intervening cause (arising from an unrelated source, such as a third party's deliberate, voluntary decision to harm the victim) is the most potent chain-breaker.
- **Source:** Chapter § "The Common Law Approach"
- **Trap:** Students choose [treating all intervening causes as chain-breakers that relieve the defendant of liability] because they confuse [intervening causes set in motion by the defendant's conduct] with [intervening causes that arise independently].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** Negligent medical treatment of a stab wound the defendant inflicted (dependent) would not sever the chain; a third party's deliberate decision to shoot the already-hospitalized victim (independent, voluntary) would.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal — common law; MPC addresses this through the "too remote or accidental" standard.

### Legal Cause — MPC Approach

#### mpc-legal-cause-standard: MPC Legal Cause — Too Remote or Accidental
- **Rule:** Under MPC § 2.03(2)-(3), legal cause for crimes of recklessness and negligence is established unless the actual result was "too remote or accidental in its occurrence to have a [just] bearing on the actor's liability." This is a single flexible standard; there is no separate "proximate cause" doctrine as such.
- **Source:** MPC § 2.03; Chapter § "The Model Penal Code (MPC)"
- **Trap:** Students choose [thinking the MPC merely restates the common law foreseeability test] because they confuse [the common law foreseeability test] with [the MPC's flexible, explicitly normative standard of justice].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** A result that would be "unforeseeable" under the common law might still satisfy the MPC standard if it is not "too remote or accidental" to have a just bearing — the standards are not equivalent.
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC)
- **Practiced:** Slide 16 — Professor quoted MPC § 2.03 language and compared it to the common law.

#### mpc-just-bearing-bracketed-word: The Bracketed "Just" — Normative Transparency Debate
- **Rule:** The ALI bracketed the word "just" in § 2.03 to reflect genuine disagreement: some drafters worried that openly inviting the jury to make a justice judgment gave too much unguided discretion; others argued that concealing the normative character of the inquiry behind doctrinal formalism was dishonest. The bracketed word is a signal that legal cause is irreducibly normative.
- **Source:** Chapter § "The Model Penal Code (MPC)"
- **Trap:** Students choose [treating the bracketed word as a typo or drafting error] because they confuse [a mechanical drafting error] with [a substantive debate about whether the law should disclose its normative judgments].
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If the MPC deleted "just" and required only that the result not be "too remote or accidental," the standard would still be normative but would appear more objective — the debate is about transparency, not about whether the question is ultimately normative.
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC)

#### mpc-jury-question: MPC — Causation Is a Jury Question
- **Rule:** The MPC deliberately transfers the legal cause determination from judges to juries. Rather than having judges decide whether a result was "foreseeable" or an intervening act "broke the chain," the MPC submits the ultimate normative question — whether the result is too remote or accidental to ground criminal liability — to the jury.
- **Source:** Chapter § "The Model Penal Code (MPC)"
- **Trap:** Students choose [assuming the judge-controlled foreseeability analysis is the default] because they confuse [the common law allocation of proximate cause to judges] with [the MPC's allocation of ultimate legal cause to the jury].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If a judge directs a verdict on proximate cause under MPC jurisdictions, that ruling would undercut the MPC's deliberate institutional design.
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC)
- **Practiced:** Slide 16 — Professor noted the MPC's shift of causation to the jury and what that means for institutional design.

#### mpc-mental-state-differentiation: MPC § 2.03 — Different Standards for Purpose/Knowledge vs. Recklessness/Negligence
- **Rule:** MPC § 2.03 applies different legal cause standards depending on the actor's mental state. For purposeful or knowing result crimes (§ 2.03(2)), legal cause fails if the actual result was not within the actor's purpose or contemplation. For reckless or negligent result crimes (§ 2.03(3)), legal cause fails if the actual result was not within the risk the actor was aware of (or should have been aware of). In both cases, the "too remote or accidental" exception applies.
- **Source:** MPC § 2.03(2)-(3); Chapter § "The Model Penal Code (MPC)"
- **Trap:** Students choose [applying the same MPC causation standard to all result crimes] because they confuse [a single unified causation test] with [varying standards and threshold inquiries based on mental state under MPC section 2.03].
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** A purposeful killer who intends to shoot victim A but instead kills victim B may satisfy MPC § 2.03(2)(a)'s "different person" exception; a reckless driver who creates a risk of injury to pedestrians but instead kills a bystander one block away satisfies the § 2.03(3) risk-of-which-actor-was-aware inquiry differently.
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC)

### Psychology of Causal Attribution

#### culpable-causation-phenomenon: Culpable Causation — Moral Assessment Contaminates Causal Judgment
- **Rule:** Psychologist Mark Alicke's research shows that people's moral assessment of an actor's purpose contaminates their causal judgment — participants find the same actor more likely to be the "cause" of an accident when they are told the actor was hiding cocaine than when hiding an anniversary present, even though the physical causal chain is identical. Doctrine assumes causation is determined first, then blame follows; the psychological reality often runs in reverse.
- **Source:** Chapter § "The Psychology of Causal Attribution — Culpable Causation"
- **Trap:** Students choose [assuming culpable causation is only a concern for jury trials] because they confuse [jury vulnerability to bias] with [the reality that culpable causation affects any fact-finder, including judges].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If participants are told the driver was speeding to hide an anniversary present rather than cocaine, identical physical facts produce significantly lower findings of causation — demonstrating that the causal judgment is not purely empirical.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal — empirical finding about human cognition, not jurisdiction-specific.
- **Practiced:** Slides 3, 15 — Professor framed the psychology section through "Legal Realism" and directly contrasted "how causation doctrine says it works" vs. "how it works in practice."

#### moral-contamination-broader: Broader Moral Contamination — Knobe Effect, Cognitive Biases
- **Rule:** Beyond Alicke, empirical research shows that (1) people judge that actors *intentionally* or *causally* produced harmful side effects more readily than beneficial ones even with identical conduct (the Knobe effect); (2) cognitive dissonance leads fact-finders to resist causal conclusions that are psychologically costly; (3) hyperbolic discounting makes temporally distant harms feel less "caused"; and (4) affinity-driven cognition means group identity shapes which causal chains people find persuasive. These biases produce systematic distortions no doctrinal test can fully eliminate.
- **Source:** Chapter § "Beyond Alicke: Moral Contamination Across the Board"
- **Trap:** Students choose [treating moral contamination as a single isolated effect] because they confuse [a singular bias of culpable causation] with [a cluster of interacting and compounding biases].
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** A juror who works in the pharmaceutical industry applying cognitive dissonance may unconsciously reject causation findings against a corporation not because evidence is weak but because the conclusion is psychologically costly — identical evidence would produce different outcomes across different jurors.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal — empirical claims about human cognition.

#### causation-as-narrative: Causation as Narrative Instrument
- **Rule:** Because factfinders do not assess causation in isolation from moral judgment, causation is the element most sensitive to narrative. A prosecutor who establishes the moral story first — that the defendant's conduct was wrong — will find foreseeability expanding and intervening causes shrinking. A defense attorney who disrupts that narrative will find the same doctrine generating reasonable doubt. Understanding this is not manipulation; it is working with how human beings actually reason about cause and responsibility.
- **Source:** Chapter § "Synthesis: Causation as Institutional Design — Practical Implications"
- **Trap:** Students choose [treating causation as a purely doctrinal analysis of elements in isolation] because they confuse [strict doctrinal rules] with [the narrative dimension and moral framing of how causation functions in the real world].
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If the prosecutor leads with the defendant's moral character before presenting the causal chain, jurors are more likely to find foreseeability; if the defense can first establish the defendant as sympathetic, the same causal chain appears more attenuated.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal — practical observation about litigation.

### Catastrophe and Widespread Harm Statutes

#### risking-vs-causing-distinction: Conduct Crime vs. Result Crime — Risking vs. Causing
- **Rule:** Some statutes respond to the causation problem by eliminating the causation requirement altogether: they criminalize the *reckless creation of risk* of catastrophe or widespread harm, not the production of actual harm. These are conduct crimes. The prosecution need not trace a causal chain to any particular injury — only prove the risk was recklessly created.
- **Source:** Chapter § "Beyond Causation: Risking Catastrophe and Widespread Harm"; MPC § 220.2; PA 18 Pa.C.S. § 3302; NJ N.J.S.A. 2C:17-2
- **Trap:** Students choose [assuming all criminal statutes targeting large-scale harm require proving causation of that harm] because they confuse [the elements of result crimes] with [the elements of conduct crimes that explicitly remove causation].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** Under Warner-Lambert facts, if Pennsylvania's § 3302 "risking catastrophe" prong applied, the prosecution would not need to identify the ignition source at all — only prove the dust conditions recklessly created a catastrophic risk.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority — MPC pioneered it; PA and NJ adopted variants; not universal.
- **Practiced:** Slides 21, 22 — Professor walked through both the "causing" and "risking" provisions of PA and NJ statutes, emphasizing that the risking variant eliminates causation.

#### mpc-catastrophe-statute: MPC § 220.2 — Causing or Risking Catastrophe
- **Rule:** MPC § 220.2 distinguishes causing a catastrophe (result crime — traditional causation required) from recklessly creating a risk of catastrophe in the employment of fire, explosives, or other dangerous means (conduct crime — no causation required). The MPC defined "catastrophe" as death or serious bodily injury to 10 or more persons, or substantial damage to a building or public utility. Risking catastrophe is classified as a misdemeanor under the MPC.
- **Source:** Chapter § "Beyond Causation: Risking Catastrophe and Widespread Harm"
- **Trap:** Students choose [citing MPC section 220.2 as support for felony-level liability for risking catastrophe] because they confuse [the MPC's misdemeanor classification] with [the harsher felony penalties adopted by state legislatures].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** Under the MPC, a defendant who recklessly creates a catastrophic risk faces only misdemeanor liability; Pennsylvania's version upgrades the same conduct to a third-degree felony.
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC) — MPC is the baseline; states modified it significantly.

#### pa-nj-catastrophe-variants: PA and NJ Modifications — Felony Enhancement and Affirmative Duties
- **Rule:** Pennsylvania (18 Pa.C.S. § 3302) upgraded risking catastrophe from misdemeanor to third-degree felony. New Jersey (N.J.S.A. 2C:17-2) went further: uses "widespread" (lower threshold — 5 persons, not 10), adds two-step enhancements for hazardous material violations, and creates a freestanding "failure to prevent or mitigate" offense imposing an affirmative duty on corporate managers who know a risk is being created.
- **Source:** Chapter § "Beyond Causation: Risking Catastrophe and Widespread Harm"
- **Trap:** Students choose [treating state statutes as identical to MPC section 220.2] because they confuse [a direct adoption of the model code] with [substantial state modifications including new duties and penalties].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** Under NJ's affirmative duty prong, a corporate manager who learns of a risk of widespread harm and fails to take reasonable measures is guilty of a separate offense even if the manager did not create the risk — a pure omission crime with no parallel in the MPC.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority — Pennsylvania and New Jersey are specific; not all MPC states adopted these enhancements.
- **Practiced:** Slides 21, 22 — Professor presented both PA and NJ statutes and the distinctions between "causing" and "risking" provisions.

#### failure-to-prevent-affirmative-duty: New Jersey — Failure to Prevent or Mitigate as Independent Offense
- **Rule:** New Jersey N.J.S.A. 2C:17-2 creates a freestanding fourth-degree offense for knowingly failing to take reasonable measures to prevent or mitigate a risk of widespread harm after learning that such a risk has been or is being created by an activity under the person's management or control. This is an affirmative duty imposed on corporate managers in a legal tradition generally reluctant to criminalize omissions.
- **Source:** Chapter § "Beyond Causation: Risking Catastrophe and Widespread Harm"
- **Trap:** Students choose [missing the affirmative duty prong entirely] because they confuse [the risk-creation offense] with [the separate omission-based charge for failing to prevent a risk].
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** A corporate manager who joins a company after the dangerous condition was created, learns about it, and fails to act could be guilty under NJ's failure-to-prevent prong even though they played no role in creating the risk.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority — New Jersey specific; not adopted universally.

### Corporate Causation Problems

#### corporate-causation-problem: Structural Causation Difficulty in Corporate Prosecutions
- **Rule:** Corporate harms unfold over time, through complex organizational structures, across diffuse populations. Traditional but-for analysis — built for individual-on-individual violence — strains because the causal chain between a boardroom decision and a specific death passes through layers of management, multiple regulatory regimes, and years of delayed consequences. Proving that any particular individual's conduct was the but-for cause of a specific injury may be practically impossible.
- **Source:** Chapter § "Corporate Causation and the Limits of Traditional Doctrine"
- **Trap:** Students choose [assuming corporations can be held liable for deaths based on broad moral responsibility] because they confuse [general corporate moral blame] with [the distinct legal requirement of individual but-for causation against specific executives].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** PG&E pleaded guilty as a corporation to 84 counts of manslaughter, yet no individual executive was charged — demonstrating that corporate-level guilt does not automatically translate to individual but-for causation for any specific death.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal — the structural problem exists under both common law and MPC.
- **Practiced:** Slides 17, 18, 19, 20 — Professor presented all four corporate case studies (PG&E, BP, PCA, Flint).

#### pge-camp-fire-corporate-guilty-no-individual: PG&E — Corporate Plea Without Individual Charges
- **Rule:** PG&E pleaded guilty to 84 counts of involuntary manslaughter for the Camp Fire but no individual executives were charged. The corporate form absorbs responsibility, and when the corporation accepts blame, the causal and culpability analysis required to charge individuals may never be performed. The maximum criminal fine was $3.5 million — a fraction of the harm caused.
- **Source:** Chapter § "In the News: PG&E and the Camp Fire (2020)"
- **Trap:** Students choose [treating corporate guilty pleas as evidence that individual liability could easily have been proven] because they confuse [corporate blame] with [individual criminal causation].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If PG&E had contested the charges and prosecutors had been required to identify specific decisions by specific executives that were the but-for cause of specific deaths, the prosecution might have foundered on the same causation problems that defeated the BP individual prosecutions.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal — federal and state
- **Practiced:** Slide 17 — Professor discussed PG&E and asked about the absence of individual charges.

The corporation was guilty; no individual could be proven the but-for cause.
- **Source:** Chapter § "In the News: BP Deepwater Horizon (2012)"
- **Trap:** Students choose [assuming the supervisors were acquitted because they acted reasonably] because they confuse [an acquittal based on reasonableness] with [dropped charges due to the inability to isolate individual but-for causal contributions].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If Kaluza or Vidrine had been the sole person with authority to order a key safety test and had personally refused, individual but-for causation might have been established — the problem was the distributed nature of the decision-making.
- **Jurisdiction:** federal
- **Practiced:** Slide 18 — Professor discussed BP and the difficulty of establishing individual but-for causation in complex organizational disasters.

The sentencing guidelines' fraud-based loss enhancements produced a 28-year sentence, harsher than most manslaughter sentences, without requiring proof of causation for any specific death.

- **Trap:** Students choose [concluding the fraud strategy was legally weaker than a homicide charge] because they confuse [the superficial label of the offense] with [the severe sentencing reality of fraud charges with loss enhancements].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If the PCA peanut paste had been sold directly to consumers rather than as an ingredient in hundreds of products, the causal chain to specific deaths would have been traceable and homicide charges might have been viable.
- **Jurisdiction:** federal
- **Practiced:** Slide 19 — Professor discussed PCA and the prosecutorial strategy of charging fraud instead of homicide.

#### flint-systemic-causation-limits: Flint Water Crisis — Limits of Traditional Doctrine for Systemic Harms
- **Rule:** The Flint water crisis involved harms that were diffuse (lead exposure producing effects over years) and systemic (cascading governmental failures across multiple agencies and levels). - **Jurisdiction:** state (Michigan) — illustrates general structural limits applicable broadly.
- **Practiced:** Slide 20 — Professor discussed Flint and the limits of traditional causation doctrine for systemic institutional harms.

#### fossil-fuel-attribution-science: Fossil Fuel Hypothetical — Attribution Science and Causation
- **Rule:** Attribution science can establish that a specific emitter's cumulative emissions contributed to climate change in ways that increased the probability and severity of specific weather events. However, this is probabilistic, population-level reasoning. It does not establish that any particular death would not have occurred but for the specific emitter's emissions — the but-for test, strictly applied, may be unsatisfiable at the individual death level. "Risking" statutes eliminate this problem by focusing on risk creation, not harm causation.
- **Source:** Chapter § "A Hypothetical at the Frontier — The Fossil Fuel Prosecution"
- **Trap:** Students choose [concluding attribution science solves the causation problem for climate prosecutions] because they confuse [population-level probabilistic evidence of increased risk] with [the individual but-for causation required for homicide].
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If attribution science could establish that a specific death would not have occurred but for MegaCarbon's emissions (individual but-for causation), the traditional homicide causation problem would be solved — but current attribution science operates at the population/probability level, not the individual certainty level.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority — hypothetical applying PA and NJ statutes.

#### causation-institutional-design: Causation as Institutional Design — Structural Choices
- **Rule:** Every element of causation doctrine involves an institutional design choice: (1) level of generality — how specifically must the harm be described for foreseeability? (2) who decides — judge (common law) or jury (MPC)? (3) what counts — tracing a causal chain vs. criminalizing risk creation (catastrophe statutes). Each choice produces different enforcement patterns. Doctrine works well for individual violence; it works poorly for diffuse, delayed, statistical harms — which is why corporate actors may escape liability that individuals could not.
- **Source:** Chapter § "Synthesis: Causation as Institutional Design — Structural Questions"
- **Trap:** Students choose [treating causation doctrine as a neutral empirical inquiry] because they confuse [objective empirical fact-finding] with [a set of legal design choices that produce predictable patterns of liability and impunity].
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** Changing who decides causation (judge vs. jury) changes the outcome: juries applying community standards of justice might find liability in *Warner-Lambert* that a judge applying the specificity requirement would not.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal — structural analysis applicable to all frameworks.

## Cases

### People v. Acosta
- **Holding:** A defendant who leads police on a high-speed chase is the proximate cause of deaths resulting from a mid-air collision between two police helicopters involved in the pursuit, because a two-helicopter collision was a "possible consequence which reasonably might have been contemplated" and not a "highly extraordinary result."
- **Reasoning:** The emotional dynamics of any police pursuit create an appreciable probability that one of the pursuers may act negligently or recklessly, so a result of that type — even if the specific mechanism was unprecedented — was not highly extraordinary.
- **Facts:** Acosta led police on a 48-mile high-speed chase; four police helicopters tracked him; the Costa Mesa helicopter performed a maneuver that caused a mid-air collision with the Newport Beach helicopter, killing three occupants.
- **Teaches:** highly-extraordinary-result, proximate-cause-foreseeability, acosta-arzon-warner-lambert-tension
- **Source type:** main case
- **Discussion questions:** yes

### People v. Arzon
- **Holding:** A defendant who set a fire on one floor of a building is the proximate cause of a firefighter's death even though the firefighter died from a combination of the defendant's fire and a separate independent fire on another floor, because the defendant's fire was a sufficiently direct cause and the harm was reasonably related to the acts.
- **Reasoning:** The defendant's conduct need not be the sole cause; it must be a sufficiently direct cause, and it was foreseeable that firefighters would respond to the blaze and be placed in a position of particular vulnerability to independent forces.
- **Facts:** Defendant allegedly set fire to a couch on the fifth floor of an abandoned building; firefighters responding to the blaze were enveloped by smoke from an independent second fire on the second floor; firefighter Martin Celic died.
- **Teaches:** sufficiently-direct-cause-standard, dependent-vs-independent-intervening-cause, acosta-arzon-warner-lambert-tension
- **Source type:** main case
- **Discussion questions:** yes (through shared Questions section)

### People v. Warner-Lambert Co.
- **Holding:** Corporate defendants cannot be held criminally liable for the deaths of factory workers killed in an explosion where the specific ignition mechanism was neither foreseen nor foreseeable, even though the defendants knew of a general risk of explosion from ambient dust conditions.
- **Reasoning:** Requiring foreseeability of the specific triggering cause — not merely a general risk of harm — prevents defendants from being held as "guarantors" for any result that flows from a general dangerous condition; the proof of the specific ignition mechanism was speculative only.
- **Facts:** Warner-Lambert manufactured chewing gum using magnesium stearate dust and liquid nitrogen; despite knowing of elevated explosion risks, defendants had not eliminated the hazard; an explosion killed six workers; investigators advanced two competing ignition theories, both speculative.
- **Teaches:** foreseeability-specificity-level, acosta-arzon-warner-lambert-tension, risking-vs-causing-distinction
- **Source type:** main case
- **Discussion questions:** yes (through shared Questions section)

### People v. Kibbe
- **Holding:** Defendants who abandoned an intoxicated robbery victim by a dark road could be convicted of murder when a passing truck struck and killed the victim, because the ultimate harm was something that should have been foreseen as being reasonably related to the defendants' acts.
- **Reasoning:** The defendants' act of abandoning a helpless, intoxicated person on a highway at night made it foreseeable that some motorist-related harm would occur, making the truck strike a dependent rather than superseding cause.
- **Facts:** Defendants robbed and abandoned an intoxicated victim by a dark road in cold weather; the victim was struck and killed by a passing truck.
- **Teaches:** sufficiently-direct-cause-standard, dependent-vs-independent-intervening-cause
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** no

### PG&E Camp Fire (2020)
- **Holding:** PG&E pleaded guilty to 84 counts of involuntary manslaughter for starting the Camp Fire through failing to maintain aging electrical equipment; no individual executives were charged.
- **Reasoning:** The corporate entity could accept responsibility for systemic failure, but the complexity of attributing specific deaths to specific individual decisions made individual homicide charges impractical.
- **Facts:** PG&E's faulty transmission line ignited the 2018 Camp Fire, killing 84 people; the company had known for years about equipment deterioration and deferred maintenance; maximum criminal fine was $3.5 million.
- **Teaches:** corporate-causation-problem, pge-camp-fire-corporate-guilty-no-individual
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** yes (through Discussion Questions section)

### BP Deepwater Horizon (2012)
- **Holding:** BP pleaded guilty as a corporation to 11 counts of felony manslaughter; charges against individual well-site supervisors were dropped because prosecutors could not prove individual but-for causation for the explosion.
- **Reasoning:** The causal chain between supervisors' specific decisions and the explosion involved multiple mechanical failures, other personnel's engineering judgments, and systemic organizational failures — no single individual's act was provably the but-for cause of the disaster.
- **Facts:** April 2010 explosion killed 11 workers; two supervisors charged with manslaughter had their charges dropped in 2015; Vidrine pleaded to a misdemeanor; Kaluza was acquitted.
- **Teaches:** bp-individual-causation-failure, corporate-causation-problem
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** yes (through Discussion Questions section)

### Peanut Corporation of America (2014)
- **Holding:** PCA executives were convicted on fraud charges (not homicide) for shipping salmonella-contaminated peanut products; Stewart Parnell received a 28-year sentence driven by sentencing guidelines' fraud-based loss enhancements.
- **Reasoning:** Tracing specific deaths to specific contaminated shipments was impossible given the peanut paste's presence in hundreds of downstream products; fraud charges avoided the causation problem while the loss enhancements produced severe sentences.
- **Facts:** 2008-2009 salmonella outbreak killed 9, sickened 714; PCA knew of contamination from its own tests; Parnell emailed "just ship it" in response to contamination concerns.
- **Teaches:** pca-fraud-not-homicide-strategy, corporate-causation-problem
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** yes (through Discussion Questions section)

### Flint Water Crisis (2021)
- **Holding:** Most charges against Flint officials, including involuntary manslaughter charges against the state health director, were dropped; prosecutions foundered primarily on causation difficulties.
- **Reasoning:** Lead exposure and Legionnaires' disease deaths involved cascading governmental failures across multiple agencies and levels of government; linking specific official decisions to specific deaths — traditional causation — was extraordinarily difficult.
- **Facts:** 2014 water source switch exposed over 100,000 residents to lead; 12 deaths linked to Legionnaires' disease; former Governor Snyder charged with misdemeanor willful neglect; most charges eventually dropped.
- **Teaches:** flint-systemic-causation-limits, corporate-causation-problem, causation-institutional-design
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** yes (through Discussion Questions section)

### Commonwealth v. Scatena
- **Holding:** Pennsylvania's "risking catastrophe" statute requires only that the defendant recklessly created a risk; the absence of actual harm does not defeat liability.
- **Reasoning:** The statute's conduct-crime structure means liability is complete upon risk creation, not upon harm occurring.
- **Facts:** Not detailed in chapter — cited for the proposition that PA § 3302 does not require actual harm.
- **Teaches:** pa-nj-catastrophe-variants, risking-vs-causing-distinction
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** no
