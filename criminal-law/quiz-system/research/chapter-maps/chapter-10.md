## Elements

### Prima Facie Elements

For **mistake of fact** to serve as a defense, the prosecution must prove each element beyond a reasonable doubt, and a defendant's mistake defeats the charge only if it negates a required mental state:

1. The offense has a required mental state (mens rea) attached to one or more material elements.
2. The defendant held an actual belief inconsistent with the facts that constitute the offense.
3. That belief negates the required mental state for the element at issue.

For **mistake of law** to serve as a defense (under an exception), the defendant must show:

1. The conduct was lawful under an applicable exception (statute expressly requires knowledge of illegality; mistake concerns collateral law; or defendant reasonably relied on an official statement).
2. The reliance was genuine and reasonable under the circumstances.

### Liability Extensions

- **Scienter presumption (Morissette):** When Congress enacts a criminal statute silent on mental state, courts presume that mens rea is required for all material elements — including attendant circumstances, not just the act.
- **"One for all" presumption (MPC 2.02(4)):** A single mens rea term in a statute applies to all material elements unless contrary purpose plainly appears.
- **Imputed corporate knowledge:** A corporation's "knowledge" for mistake analysis includes the aggregate knowledge of all its agents — internal documents, research, and reports are all attributed to the entity.

### Liability Limits

- **Ignorance of the law is no excuse (traditional rule):** A defendant's mistaken belief that conduct was lawful provides no defense, even if sincere and reasonable.
- **Strict liability for age (majority rule):** Even an honest and reasonable mistake about a victim's age provides no defense to statutory rape, at least for victims below the lower threshold.
- **No defense for unreasonable corporate mistake:** When a corporation's own internal documents contradict its claimed ignorance, no rational jury will credit the claimed mistake.

## Refinements

### Mistake of Fact — Basic Rule

#### mof-mental-state-negation: Mistake Defeats Only the Mental State It Negates
- **Rule:** A mistake of fact is a defense if and only if it negates the specific mental state required for a material element of the offense. A mistake about one element does not defeat liability on another.
- **Source:** Problem: Mistake and Theft (hypothetical, lines 19–28); MPC § 2.04(1)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to argue any honest mistake defeats liability] because they confuse [negating the mental state for one element] with [defeating the whole charge].
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** If the offense has no mens rea for the disputed element (strict liability), the mistake provides no defense regardless of its reasonableness.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal
- **Practiced:** Slides 6–8 — Phone theft hypotheticals: three scenarios practiced in class, applying the theft statute element by element

#### mof-element-matching: Which Element Does the Mistake Address?
- **Rule:** A mistake must be matched to the specific element it negates. Mistaking the object (phone vs. organizer) does not negate "property of another." Mistaking ownership (believing item is yours) does negate "property of another."
- **Source:** Problem: Mistake and Theft scenarios 2 and 3 (lines 25–28)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to treat any factual error as categorically defeating the charge] because they confuse [an unmapped factual error] with [a mistake that maps to the specific statutory element it touches].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** In scenario 2 (phone vs. organizer), the defendant knows the object belongs to someone else — the mistake is about the object's type, not its ownership; conviction stands.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal
- **Practiced:** Slides 7–8 — "Hypos: After a Faculty Meeting" worked through each scenario with the statutory text on screen

### MPC Framework

#### mpc-four-mental-states: The Four MPC Mental States
- **Rule:** The MPC defines four levels of culpability — purposely (conscious object), knowingly (aware the circumstances exist), recklessly (consciously disregards a substantial and unjustifiable risk), and negligently (should have been aware of the risk). These are mutually exclusive categories arranged in descending order of blameworthiness.
- **Source:** MPC § 2.02(2) (lines 38–57)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to equate recklessness and negligence based on risk] because they confuse [recklessness, which requires conscious disregard] with [negligence, which requires only that the defendant should have been aware].
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** Whether the defendant actually thought about the risk and chose to ignore it (recklessness) versus simply never thought about it (negligence) determines which standard applies.
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC)
- **Practiced:** Slide 10 — "Mental State Gloss on MPC Definition" table; Slide 11 — MPC Default Rules reviewed in class

#### mpc-one-for-all: The "One for All" Presumption (MPC 2.02(4))
- **Rule:** When a statute specifies a single mens rea term without distinguishing among elements, that term applies to all material elements of the offense — including attendant circumstances — unless a contrary purpose plainly appears.
- **Source:** MPC § 2.02(4) (line 56); Morissette (lines 83–111)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to apply a stated mental state only to the act verb and not to circumstance elements] because they confuse [the grammatical target of the mental state] with [its legal application to circumstance elements like "property of the United States"].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If the statute expressly limits the mental state to one element (e.g., "knowing the property is stolen"), the "one for all" presumption is rebutted.
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC)
- **Practiced:** Slides 20, 23 — MPC Hypos applying "one for all" to Morissette and Elonis statutes

#### mpc-silence-recklessness: Silence Implies Recklessness (MPC 2.02(3))
- **Rule:** When a statute specifies no mental state, the MPC reads in purposely, knowingly, or recklessly. The MPC never defaults to strict liability; the legislature must plainly express that intent.
- **Source:** MPC § 2.02(3) (lines 54–55); chapter text (line 69)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to assume statutory silence means strict liability] because they confuse [the common law tradition] with [the MPC's explicit default rules].
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** If legislative history or the statutory scheme "plainly" indicates intent to impose strict liability, the recklessness default can be displaced.
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC)
- **Practiced:** Slide 11 — MPC Default Rules; Slide 27 — MPC Hypo applying silence rule to Elonis statute

### Common Law Framework

#### cl-specific-general-intent: Specific Intent vs. General Intent (Common Law)
- **Rule:** At common law, specific intent crimes require a particular purpose beyond the act (e.g., theft requires intent to permanently deprive). For specific intent crimes, an honest mistake — even an unreasonable one — is a defense. For general intent crimes, only an honest and reasonable mistake qualifies.
- **Source:** Chapter text (lines 77–78)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to apply the "honest mistake" standard universally] because they confuse [the honest mistake standard] with [the reasonable mistake standard required for general intent crimes].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If the crime is classified as strict liability, no mistake — however honest or reasonable — provides a defense.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (common law jurisdictions; superseded by MPC in adopting states)
- **Practiced:** Slides 13–14 — Common Law Mens Reas and "When Mens Rea Is Clear" slides

#### cl-malum-in-se-test: Common Law Value-Laden Test for Ambiguous Mens Rea
- **Rule:** When the scope of a stated mens rea is unclear, common law courts ask whether the act, if the facts were as the defendant imagined, would have been innocent (malum in se vs. malum prohibitum). If the imagined conduct was still morally wrong, courts restrict the defense; if innocent, courts allow it.
- **Source:** Chapter text (lines 75–77); Slide 18
- **Trap:** Students choose [to treat this test as a clear rule] because they confuse [a clear predictive rule] with [an explicitly value-laden test dependent on how courts characterize the underlying conduct].
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** Whether the imagined conduct is characterized as morally blameworthy or innocent — the same act may be classified differently by different courts.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (common law)
- **Practiced:** Slides 17–18 — "But there is a method of sorts..." and "Common Law Mistake of Fact / When Scope is Ambiguous"

### Presumption Against Strict Liability

#### morissette-presumption: The Morissette Presumption (Federal)
- **Rule:** When Congress enacts a criminal statute that is silent on mens rea, courts presume that Congress intended to require mens rea, not impose strict liability, because the intent requirement is "inherent in the idea of the offense." This presumption is rebuttable only if strict liability intent is clearly established.
- **Source:** Morissette v. United States (1952) (lines 83–111)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to think statutory silence automatically means strict liability] because they confuse [reasoning from the text's omission] with [the presumption against strict liability].
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** If Congress expressly imposes strict liability (e.g., public welfare offense statutes), the Morissette presumption is rebutted.
- **Jurisdiction:** federal only (though many state courts adopt analogous presumptions)
- **Practiced:** Slides 19–20 — Morissette case slides with annotated statute

#### rehaif-status-element: Scienter Must Extend to Status Elements (Rehaif)
- **Rule:** Under the Morissette presumption, the government must prove the defendant knew both the conduct (possessing a firearm) and the attendant circumstance (belonging to a prohibited class). The distinction is between knowing facts that make conduct criminal versus knowing the conduct is criminal.
- **Source:** Rehaif v. United States (2019) (lines 115–119)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to assume "knowingly" modifies only the act verb] because they confuse [statutory drafting of background status] with [a legal status element that must be modified by the mental state].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If the defendant knew he possessed a firearm but had no awareness of his prohibited status, the knowledge element for the status is not satisfied.
- **Jurisdiction:** federal only
- **Practiced:** Slides 21, 28 — Rehaif slides including 2023 postscript

#### elonis-threats-mens-rea: Threats Require More Than Negligence (Elonis)
- **Rule:** A federal threats statute silent on mental state requires more than a negligence standard (a reasonable person test). The Morissette presumption compels reading in at least recklessness; a defendant's honest belief that posts were not threatening is a defense even if unreasonable.
- **Source:** Elonis v. United States (2015) (lines 123–125)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to apply a reasonable person test to threats] because they confuse [the objective standard of tort negligence] with [the higher default mental state for federal crimes].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** Whether the Court would require knowledge or accept recklessness remains unsettled; under recklessness, a defendant who consciously disregarded the threatening nature of the post could still be convicted even with an honest belief the posts were not threatening.
- **Jurisdiction:** federal only
- **Practiced:** Slides 22–26 — Elonis case presentation and holding; Slide 27 — MPC hypo specifically analyzing the recklessness outcome (the key practiced item for the unsettled knowledge/recklessness question)

### Mistake of Law — Exceptions

#### mpc-culpability-illegality: MPC § 2.02(9) — Culpability as to Illegality Is Never Required
- **Rule:** Under MPC § 2.02(9), knowledge of — or recklessness or negligence as to — whether conduct constitutes an offense is never itself an element of the offense, unless the statute expressly provides otherwise. The MPC thus codifies the traditional "ignorance of law" rule as a default: the prosecution need not prove the defendant knew his conduct was criminal.
- **Source:** MPC § 2.02(9) (lines 150–152)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to apply the § 2.04 mistake defense to ignorance of illegality] because they confuse [§ 2.02(9) where prosecution need not prove knowledge of illegality] with [§ 2.04 where a mistake must negate a material element].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If a statute expressly requires the defendant to "know" that his conduct is illegal (as in federal tax "willfulness"), § 2.02(9)'s default is displaced by the statute — that is the Cheek exception.
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC)

#### mol-traditional-rule: Ignorance of the Law Is No Excuse (Traditional Rule)
- **Rule:** A sincere and reasonable belief that conduct was lawful provides no defense. Courts justify this by pointing to the law's expectation-setting function: allowing the defense would encourage ignorance and invite fabricated claims.
- **Source:** Chapter text (lines 145–148); MPC § 2.02(9) (lines 150–152)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to think a sincere belief in legality is always relevant] because they confuse [mistake of law] with [mistake of fact].
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** If the statute itself requires knowledge of illegality (e.g., "willfully"), the traditional rule yields to the statutory requirement.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal
- **Practiced:** Slides 30–31 — "Mistake of Law" section divider and doctrine summary

#### mol-collateral-law: Collateral Law vs. Penal Law Exception
- **Rule:** Mistakes about collateral law (background legal rules that determine a factual predicate the penal law incorporates — like the validity of a divorce) can serve as a defense. Mistakes about the penal law itself (the statute creating the offense) generally cannot.
- **Source:** Long v. State (Del. 1949) (lines 157–158); chapter text (lines 157)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to apply the "ignorance of the law" rule to collateral law mistakes] because they confuse [the penal statute] with [the external legal framework it incorporates].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** Whether the prior divorce was governed by the law of the state where it occurred (collateral law) versus whether bigamy is illegal (penal law) — only the latter is barred.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (varies by state)

#### mol-willfulness-exception: Willfulness Exception — Statute Requires Knowledge of Illegality (Cheek)
- **Rule:** When a federal criminal statute uses "willfully," courts interpret it to require voluntary, intentional violation of a known legal duty. A defendant who genuinely (even unreasonably) believed he had no duty cannot be convicted, because he lacked the required mental state.
- **Source:** Cheek v. United States (1991) (lines 159); Bryan v. United States (1998) (line 159)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to mislabel Cheek as a mistake of fact case and extend it beyond the tax context] because they confuse [a mistake of fact and a universal definition of "willfully"] with [a mistake of law exception about a legal duty that varies by statutory context].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If the defendant held the belief that the tax law was unconstitutional (rather than that he had no duty), Cheek does not protect him — constitutional challenges to the law are not "mistakes of law."
- **Jurisdiction:** federal only
- **Practiced:** Not directly covered in slides

#### mol-official-statement: Reasonable Reliance on Official Statements (Cox; Albertini)
- **Rule:** A defendant who reasonably relied on an official statement of the law — from a court decision, administrative order, or government official charged with enforcing the law — has a complete defense even if that statement later proved erroneous.
- **Source:** Cox v. Louisiana (1965) (lines 177–181); United States v. Albertini (1987) (lines 183–190); MPC § 2.04(3) (lines 165–173)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to argue personal attorney advice counts as an official statement] because they confuse [personal attorney advice] with [an official statement from a government body or officer charged with interpreting the law].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** In Albertini, the court held the reliance defense applied up through the point at which Albertini had relied on the Ninth Circuit's decision, but expressly declined to decide whether the defense extended beyond the point at which the Supreme Court granted certiorari — the precise outer boundary of the reliance window was left open.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal (codified in MPC; recognized at common law)
- **Practiced:** Not directly covered in slides

#### mol-marrero-no-personal-reading: Personal Misreading of Ambiguous Statute Is Not Reliance (Marrero)
- **Rule:** A defendant's own reasonable interpretation of an ambiguous statute does not qualify as reliance on an "official statement of the law." The defense requires reliance on an authoritative external source — court decision, agency order, or official guidance — not the defendant's private legal analysis.
- **Source:** People v. Marrero (N.Y. 1987) (lines 161)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to treat a reasonable personal reading of a statute as an official reliance defense] because they confuse [a reasonable personal reading of a statute] with [the official reliance defense].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If the defendant had received a formal opinion from the state attorney general interpreting the exemption as covering him, the result would have been different.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (states split on how broadly to define "official")

#### mol-advice-of-counsel: Advice of Counsel — Not a Full Official Reliance Defense
- **Rule:** Advice of private counsel generally does not qualify as an "official statement" under MPC § 2.04(3), but can support a broader showing of good-faith efforts to ascertain the law. Full disclosure to counsel and genuine reliance are required; partial disclosure defeats the defense.
- **Source:** Problem: The "Advice of Counsel" Defense (lines 237–251); Long v. State (Del. 1949) (line 157)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to treat lawyer advice as equivalent to official reliance] because they confuse [lawyer advice as a component of a good-faith showing] with [an "official statement" qualifying for official reliance].
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If attorneys were not fully informed of the underlying facts, the defense fails because good-faith reliance requires full disclosure.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority

### Strict Liability and Age

#### sl-prince-malum-in-se: The Prince Principle — Morally Suspect Conduct Bears the Risk
- **Rule:** When a defendant's underlying conduct is morally problematic — "wrong in itself" — he bears the risk that the facts are worse than he imagined. The mistake defense is unavailable even if the defendant honestly and reasonably believed the attendant circumstance made the conduct lawful.
- **Source:** Regina v. Prince (1875) (lines 296–300)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to apply the honest/reasonable mistake standard universally] because they confuse [the universal application of the mistake standard] with [the Prince restriction for malum in se conduct].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If the defendant's conduct (even with the facts as believed) would have been entirely innocent (Justice Brett's test), the defense should apply — the majority and dissent split on this question.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (malum in se limitation followed by majority; Brett's test adopted by MPC approach)

#### sl-strict-liability-age-majority: Majority Rule — Strict Liability for Victim's Age
- **Rule:** The majority of U.S. jurisdictions impose absolute strict liability as to the victim's age in statutory rape. No mistake of age, however honest or reasonable, provides a defense.
- **Source:** Chapter text (lines 304–305, 314)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to assume the MPC's reasonable mistake defense is the majority rule] because they confuse [the MPC's reasonable mistake defense] with [the majority rule of strict liability for age].
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** Whether the victim is above or below the lower threshold (typically 12–14) — strict liability is universal below that threshold; some jurisdictions allow a reasonable mistake defense for victims between the lower threshold and the age of consent.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority

#### sl-mpc-reasonable-mistake-age: MPC Middle Ground — Reasonable Mistake of Age Defense
- **Rule:** The MPC (§ 213.6(1)) allows a defense if the defendant reasonably believed the child to be above the critical age. This applies especially for victims closer to the age of consent, where strict liability may punish genuinely blameless conduct.
- **Source:** Chapter text (lines 308–310)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to think the MPC eliminates all strict liability for age] because they confuse [eliminating all strict liability for age] with [retaining strict liability for very young victims while creating a defense only for older minors].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** Whether the victim is close to the age of consent (defense potentially available under MPC) versus very young (strict liability even under MPC).
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC)

#### sl-tiered-approach: Tiered Strict Liability for Age
- **Rule:** Many jurisdictions use a tiered approach: strict liability for victims below a lower threshold (12–14), a possible reasonable mistake defense or lesser penalties between that threshold and the age of consent, and full culpability requirements above the age of consent.
- **Source:** Chapter text (lines 316–318)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to apply a single age-strict-liability rule across all age ranges] because they confuse [a single age-strict-liability rule] with [a tiered system where analysis changes depending on the victim's age band].
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** The exact age of the victim relative to the jurisdiction's tier thresholds determines which rule applies.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority

#### sl-reasonable-opportunity-to-observe: Federal Sex Trafficking — Reasonable Opportunity to Observe
- **Rule:** Under 18 U.S.C. § 1591, if the defendant had a "reasonable opportunity to observe" the victim, the government need not prove any mental state as to age. Personal contact with the victim triggers heightened responsibility to ascertain age.
- **Source:** Chapter text (lines 318); United States v. X-Citement Video, Inc. (1994) (mentioned line 318)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to apply the general knowledge/recklessness standard in § 1591] because they confuse [the general knowledge/recklessness standard] with [the "reasonable opportunity to observe" provision that eliminates the mental state requirement].
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** Whether the defendant had any in-person contact with the victim — remote transactions may not trigger the "reasonable opportunity to observe" exception.
- **Jurisdiction:** federal only

#### sl-chilling-effect: Chilling Effect and the Morally Suspect Distinction
- **Rule:** Courts and legislatures are more willing to impose strict liability when the underlying conduct — even if the facts were as believed — is morally suspect (sexual contact with someone who appears young) than when the conduct is entirely innocent (salvaging junk, posting words online).
- **Source:** Chapter text (lines 322–327)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to treat the chilling effect as an absolute argument against strict liability] because they confuse [an absolute argument against strict liability] with [a balancing test that weighs the value of chilled conduct against the harm avoided].
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** Whether the underlying conduct (setting aside the element that makes it criminal) is "entirely innocent" or "morally suspect" determines whether the chilling effect argument has purchase.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal

### System Design

#### sys-corporate-imputed-knowledge: Corporate Imputed Knowledge and Mistake
- **Rule:** A corporation's knowledge for purposes of the mistake analysis includes the aggregate knowledge of all its agents — internal documents, research reports, and compliance data are all attributed to the entity. A corporation cannot claim a mistake of fact if its own internal records show the relevant facts.
- **Source:** Chapter text (lines 253–258); Purdue Pharma hypothetical (lines 259–274); ExxonMobil hypothetical (lines 276–288)
- **Trap:** Students choose [to analyze corporate knowledge as though it depends on what the CEO personally knew] because they confuse [what the CEO personally knew] with [the law aggregating knowledge across all agents].
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** Whether the corporation possessed internal documents documenting the risk — if so, the claimed ignorance is implausible regardless of any individual officer's testimony.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (respondeat superior vs. specific employee models vary by jurisdiction)

#### sys-distributional-asymmetry: Distributional Asymmetry in Mistake Defenses
- **Rule:** Sophisticated defendants (corporations, wealthy individuals) can invest in legal advice, create paper trails of due diligence, and invoke reliance defenses unavailable to those who cannot afford legal counsel or navigate complex regulatory systems.
- **Source:** Chapter text (lines 340–343); Florida voter fraud variation (lines 207–235); Purdue Pharma hypotheticals
- **Trap:** Students choose [to analyze mistake defenses in isolation] because they confuse [a purely neutral doctrine] with [a framework that systematically advantages those with resources to generate exculpatory paper trails].
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** Whether the defendant could have realistically known and complied with the law — the distributional argument is strongest when genuine compliance was practically impossible.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal

## Clusters

### cluster-mpc-defaults
- **Refinements:** mpc-four-mental-states, mpc-one-for-all, mpc-silence-recklessness, morissette-presumption
- **Why they cluster:** The MPC's systematic defaults and the federal common law presumption against strict liability are two routes to the same outcome — all can be tested on a single statute with a missing or ambiguous mens rea term.
- **Scenario hook:** A federal environmental statute prohibits "knowingly discharging" a contaminant into a waterway; the defendant claims he did not know the water was a protected waterway — ask whether the mental state reaches the "protected" status element under both MPC and federal common law.

### cluster-mens-rea-scope
- **Refinements:** mof-mental-state-negation, mof-element-matching, rehaif-status-element, elonis-threats-mens-rea, mpc-one-for-all
- **Why they cluster:** All test whether a stated mens rea term reaches an attendant circumstance element or only the act verb, using different statutory contexts, building from the basic negation rule to specific federal applications.
- **Scenario hook:** A firearms statute prohibits "knowingly shipping" a firearm to a person "convicted of a felony" — the defendant knew he shipped a firearm but claims he did not know the recipient had a prior felony conviction.

### cluster-common-law-ambiguity
- **Refinements:** cl-specific-general-intent, cl-malum-in-se-test, sl-prince-malum-in-se
- **Why they cluster:** The common law's value-laden approach to ambiguous mens rea reaches its logical endpoint in Prince — all three refinements turn on whether the imagined conduct was morally blameworthy.
- **Scenario hook:** A defendant charged with assault claims he thought his "victim" had consented to a physical confrontation — the analysis depends on whether nonconsensual fighting is already wrong in itself.

### cluster-mistake-of-law-exceptions
- **Refinements:** mpc-culpability-illegality, mol-traditional-rule, mol-official-statement, mol-marrero-no-personal-reading, mol-collateral-law
- **Why they cluster:** These refinements collectively define where the "ignorance of law" bar falls and where official reliance carves out a safe harbor — a single fact pattern can implicate all five.
- **Scenario hook:** A defendant is prosecuted for operating a business without a license; she relied on a city clerk's verbal assurance that no license was required, having also consulted her own lawyer and a state agency website.

### cluster-willfulness-counsel
- **Refinements:** mol-willfulness-exception, mol-advice-of-counsel
- **Why they cluster:** Both defenses arise when a defendant claims good-faith reliance on legal guidance; both turn on full disclosure and the complexity of the underlying legal obligation.
- **Scenario hook:** A cryptocurrency exchange founder claims he relied on his lawyers' advice that commingling customer funds was lawful; the government argues the lawyers were not fully informed of the scope of the conduct.

### cluster-strict-liability-age
- **Refinements:** sl-strict-liability-age-majority, sl-mpc-reasonable-mistake-age, sl-tiered-approach, sl-reasonable-opportunity-to-observe, sl-prince-malum-in-se
- **Why they cluster:** All five refinements map the spectrum from total strict liability to reasonable mistake defense, with the exact outcome turning on victim age, jurisdiction, and whether defendant had personal contact.
- **Scenario hook:** A defendant in a sex trafficking prosecution claims he reasonably believed the victim was 17 (above the state age of consent), but she was 15; he communicated with her only online.

### cluster-corporate-mistake
- **Refinements:** sys-corporate-imputed-knowledge, sys-distributional-asymmetry, sl-chilling-effect
- **Why they cluster:** All three refinements concern how institutional actors and the design of the mistake doctrine interact — corporations, distributional effects, and the chilling-effect calculus all arise together in the Purdue/Exxon discussions.
- **Scenario hook:** A pharmaceutical company charged with homicide for opioid deaths argues it did not know its marketing strategy would cause deaths, despite internal tracking reports — compare to a small pharmacy owner who claims the same ignorance.

## Cases

### Morissette v. United States (1952)
- **Holding:** Silence in a federal criminal statute as to mental state does not eliminate the mens rea requirement; courts presume Congress intended to require intent for larceny-type offenses.
- **Reasoning:** The intent requirement is so deeply embedded in the common law concept of crime that courts should not infer its abandonment from legislative silence, especially for offenses that merely adopt well-established common law crimes.
- **Facts:** A Michigan scrap collector gathered spent bomb casings from an apparently abandoned government bombing range, flattened them, and sold them for $84, believing they were abandoned property.
- **Teaches:** morissette-presumption, mpc-one-for-all, mof-element-matching
- **Source type:** main case
- **Discussion questions:** yes

### Rehaif v. United States (2019)
- **Holding:** The government must prove the defendant knew both that he possessed a firearm and that he belonged to the prohibited class (in this case, that he was unlawfully in the country).
- **Reasoning:** Knowing the facts that make conduct criminal — not just that the conduct was criminal — is the relevant scienter inquiry, and the Morissette presumption extends this to status elements.
- **Facts:** A foreign student dismissed from his university visited a firing range and shot two firearms; he was prosecuted under § 922(g) for alien-in-possession, but the trial court did not require the jury to find he knew of his unlawful immigration status.
- **Teaches:** rehaif-status-element, morissette-presumption, mpc-one-for-all
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** yes

### Elonis v. United States (2015)
- **Holding:** A federal threats statute silent on mental state requires more than a negligence standard; the Morissette presumption compels reading in at least knowledge or recklessness.
- **Reasoning:** A "reasonable person" standard would reduce culpability to negligence, and courts have long been reluctant to infer that a negligence standard was intended in criminal statutes.
- **Facts:** Anthony Elonis posted graphically violent "rap lyrics" on Facebook targeting his estranged wife, co-workers, a kindergarten class, and an FBI agent after his wife left him; the trial court used a reasonable-person standard.
- **Teaches:** elonis-threats-mens-rea, morissette-presumption, mpc-silence-recklessness
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** yes

### Regina v. Prince (1875)
- **Holding:** A defendant who takes an underage girl from parental possession is guilty even if he honestly and reasonably believed she was an adult, because the underlying conduct was morally wrong.
- **Reasoning:** When a defendant is already doing something "wrong in itself," he takes on the risk that attendant circumstances make his conduct criminal — the malum in se character of the act negates the mistake defense.
- **Facts:** Henry Prince took a fourteen-year-old girl from her father's possession, honestly and reasonably believing she was eighteen; he was convicted of taking an unmarried girl out of the possession of her father.
- **Teaches:** sl-prince-malum-in-se, sl-strict-liability-age-majority, cl-malum-in-se-test
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** yes

### Cox v. Louisiana (1965)
- **Holding:** A defendant cannot be convicted for conduct that government officials expressly authorized — doing so would violate due process as an indefensible form of entrapment by the state.
- **Reasoning:** The government cannot direct a citizen's conduct and then punish him for following those directions; the Due Process Clause forbids this form of official entrapment.
- **Facts:** Reverend B. Elton Cox led a civil rights demonstration in Baton Rouge; police officials told him he could demonstrate 101 feet across the street from the courthouse, and he did — he was then prosecuted for picketing "in or near" a courthouse.
- **Teaches:** mol-official-statement, mol-traditional-rule
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** yes

### United States v. Albertini (1987)
- **Holding:** A defendant who acts in reasonable reliance on a judicial decision instructing that his conduct is lawful cannot be convicted for that conduct, even after the Supreme Court later reverses the decision.
- **Reasoning:** Holding someone criminally liable for conduct a court told him was protected would be an act of "intolerable injustice" — the reliance defense extends to published appellate decisions.
- **Facts:** Peace activist James Albertini, relying on a Ninth Circuit decision that reversed his earlier conviction for trespassing on Hickam Air Force Base, returned to distribute leaflets at open house events; he was convicted for the later visits after the Supreme Court reversed the Ninth Circuit.
- **Teaches:** mol-official-statement, mol-traditional-rule
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** yes

### Long v. State (Del. 1949)
- **Holding:** A defendant charged with bigamy may raise a reasonable reliance defense based on attorney advice that his prior out-of-state divorce was valid, because questions of divorce law are "more frequently perplexing than obvious to a layman."
- **Reasoning:** The penal statute (bigamy) incorporates collateral law (domestic relations); a reasonable mistake about collateral law — even if not from an official governmental source — can negate culpability.
- **Facts:** Defendant relied on an attorney's advice that his prior out-of-state divorce was valid before remarrying; the divorce later proved invalid.
- **Teaches:** mol-collateral-law, mol-advice-of-counsel
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** no

### People v. Marrero (N.Y. 1987)
- **Holding:** A defendant's personal reasonable interpretation of an ambiguous statutory exemption does not constitute reliance on an "official statement of the law" and does not provide a mistake of law defense.
- **Reasoning:** Allowing a personal misreading of a statute as a defense would encourage individuals to seek exemptions in statutory language and undermine the certainty the rule of law provides.
- **Facts:** A federal corrections officer carried a weapon in New York, believing a "peace officer" exemption covered him; the majority rejected his defense despite the arguably reasonable reading of the statute.
- **Teaches:** mol-marrero-no-personal-reading, mol-official-statement, mol-traditional-rule
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** yes

### Cheek v. United States (1991)
- **Holding:** "Willfully" in federal criminal tax statutes requires proof that the defendant voluntarily and intentionally violated a known legal duty; a genuine belief — even an unreasonable one — that he had no duty to pay taxes negates willfulness.
- **Reasoning:** The extraordinary complexity of the tax code makes it plausible that a defendant could genuinely misunderstand his obligations, justifying departure from the traditional "ignorance of law" rule.
- **Facts:** John Cheek, a tax protester, genuinely believed (based on anti-tax advocacy) that he had no legal obligation to pay federal income taxes; he stopped filing returns and was prosecuted for willful tax evasion.
- **Teaches:** mol-willfulness-exception, mol-traditional-rule
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** yes
