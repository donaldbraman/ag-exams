### Mental State Threshold Refinements

#### ordinary-negligence-threshold
- **Claim:** *Williams* applied ordinary negligence ("ordinary caution") as the manslaughter standard under Washington's then-applicable statute; this is no longer the law anywhere.
- **Chapter support:** *Williams* holding ("the death of the victim is the proximate result of only simple or ordinary negligence"); Note: Washington's Statutory Reform states explicitly that no state retains ordinary negligence for manslaughter.
- **Trap claim:** Students assume *Williams* reflects current doctrine.
- **Status:** VERIFIED. Rule, trap, and current-law note all grounded in chapter text.

#### gross-criminal-negligence
- **Claim:** Majority standard requires a "gross deviation from the standard of care"; this is objective, not subjective.
- **Chapter support:** Florida Standard Jury Instructions callout (piling up adjectives); MPC § 2.02(2)(d) quoted in chapter introduction; Washington's reformed statute quoted in the Washington Statutory Reform note.
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

#### mpc-manslaughter-negligent-homicide-split
- **Claim:** MPC bifurcates recklessness (manslaughter, § 210.3) and negligence (negligent homicide, § 210.4) with subjective awareness as the dividing line.
- **Chapter support:** Both MPC code sections quoted verbatim; MPC Official Commentary (§ 2.02) quoted extensively, criticizing traditional law's "muddled descriptions."
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

### Negligence-Recklessness Divide in Practice

#### constructive-recklessness-welansky
- **Claim:** *Welansky* establishes that recklessness can be constructive/objective — "knowing facts that would cause a reasonable man to know the danger is equivalent to knowing the danger." The exact quote from the jury instruction: "A man may be reckless within the meaning of the law although he himself thought he was careful."
- **Chapter support:** *Welansky* opinion, jury instruction quoted verbatim on p. 156 of chapter.
- **Trap claim:** Students confuse MPC subjective recklessness with *Welansky*'s constructive standard.
- **Status:** VERIFIED. Both the rule and the quote are exact.

#### omission-duty-recklessness
- **Claim:** *Welansky* holds that wanton or reckless conduct can consist of intentional omissions where there is a duty of care.
- **Chapter support:** *Welansky*: "where, as in the present case, there is a duty of care for the safety of business visitors invited to premises which the defendant controls, wanton or reckless conduct may consist of intentional failure to take such care."
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

#### reasonable-person-standard-social-context
- **Claim:** *Williams* court held defendants' ignorance and fear of government removal were no legal excuse; the objective reasonable person standard applies.
- **Chapter support:** *Williams* holding: "the conduct of a defendant, regardless of his ignorance, good intentions and good faith, fails to measure up to the conduct required of a man of reasonable prudence, he is guilty of ordinary negligence." Note on Indian child removal history included in chapter.
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

#### criminal-negligence-philosophical-rationale
- **Claim:** Thomas argues for reprobation/objective accountability; Huigens argues for character-based culpability as the theoretical foundation for punishing negligence.
- **Chapter support:** Thomas excerpt (National Times, 1994) quoted verbatim; Huigens excerpt (1 Buffalo Crim. L. Rev. 431) quoted verbatim; discussion questions ask students to compare them.
- **Status:** VERIFIED. Citations confirmed (Huigens is cited as 1 Buffalo Criminal Law Review 431, 439, 447, 450-55 (1998)).

#### medical-negligence-criminalization
- **Claim:** Vaught convicted of criminally negligent homicide (not manslaughter) in Tennessee; granted judicial diversion after probation; prosecution based on override mode use.
- **Chapter support:** RaDonda Vaught "In the News" callout box: "convicted in March of criminally negligent homicide and gross neglect of an impaired adult"; "granted Vaught a judicial diversion"; "Vaught circumvented safeguards by switching the hospital's computerized medication cabinet into 'override' mode."
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

### Depraved Heart Murder Refinements

#### extreme-indifference-standard
- **Claim:** MPC § 210.2(1)(b) requires "recklessness manifesting extreme indifference to the value of human life"; the question of whether recklessness meets this standard "must be left directly to the trier of fact."
- **Chapter support:** MPC § 210.2 callout box quoted verbatim; MPC Official Commentary quoted verbatim: "Whether recklessness is so extreme that it demonstrates similar indifference is not a question, it is submitted, that can be further clarified. It must be left directly to the trier of fact."
- **Status:** VERIFIED. Exact language confirmed.

#### likely-if-not-certain-standard
- **Claim:** Maryland's depraved heart murder standard requires conduct "reasonably likely, if not certain, to cause death"; the court evaluates inherent dangerousness combined with environmental risk factors; *Beckwitt*'s murder conviction reversed because the conduct did not meet this threshold.
- **Chapter support:** *Beckwitt* holding: "Beckwitt's conduct, although demonstrating a reckless disregard for human life, was 'not the type of conduct that [was] likely, if not certain, to cause death, and thus does not rise to the level of opprobrious conduct that depraved heart murder proscribes'"; analysis explicitly references "inherent dangerousness of the act engaged in, as judged by a reasonable person[,] ... combined with environmental risk factors."
- **Status:** VERIFIED. Direct quotations confirmed.

#### jury-delegation-rationale
- **Claim:** MPC delegates both causation (§ 2.03) and gradation (§ 210.2) to the finder of fact; *Warner-Lambert* illustrates a case where a judge removed the case on causation grounds, incompatible with MPC design; Emma Southon's quote used to frame why murder is a social/moral judgment.
- **Chapter support:** Southon quote from *A Fatal Thing Happened on the Way to the Forum* (2021) reproduced verbatim in chapter. *Warner-Lambert* analysis: "Under the MPC, both the causation question and the gradation question would fall to the finder of fact." Section 2.03 causation standard referenced.
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

### Drunk Driving Homicide Refinements

#### four-approaches-overview
- **Claim:** Four distinct approaches: (1) Murder, (2) Standalone vehicular homicide statutes (majority), (3) General manslaughter, (4) Aggravated DUI.
- **Chapter support:** Chapter lists these four approaches explicitly in "Four Approaches" section with headers numbered 1–4.
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

#### watson-advisement-implied-malice
- **Claim:** *People v. Watson*, 637 P.2d 279 (Cal. 1981): BAC .23, driving 84 mph, killed two people; held liable for second-degree murder based on implied malice. Watson advisement codified at Cal. Veh. Code § 23593. California penalty: 15 years to life.
- **Chapter support:** All details confirmed verbatim: "BAC of .23," "84 miles per hour," "killed two people," "Cal. Veh. Code § 23593," "15 years to life in California."
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

#### vehicular-homicide-strict-liability
- **Claim:** Texas, Connecticut, and New Jersey (under its 2017 "Ralph and David's Law") impose strict liability for DUI deaths — prosecution need only prove intoxication and causation.
- **Chapter support:** Chapter text: "Texas, Connecticut, and New Jersey (under its 2017 'Ralph and David's Law') impose what amounts to strict liability: the prosecution need only prove that the defendant was intoxicated and caused the death."
- **Status:** VERIFIED. "Ralph and David's Law" detail confirmed.

#### prior-convictions-as-mental-state-vs-enhancer
- **Claim:** California uses prior convictions as evidence of mental state; Pennsylvania uses them as sentencing enhancers (mandatory minimum increases per victim); Oklahoma uses prior DUI as felony enabler supporting felony murder.
- **Chapter support:** Chapter "The Severity Spectrum" section: "In California, prior convictions are evidence of mental state"; "In Pennsylvania, they are sentencing enhancers"; "In Oklahoma, a prior DUI conviction makes a subsequent DUI a felony, which can support a second-degree felony murder charge when death results."
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

#### mpc-intoxication-recklessness
- **Claim:** MPC § 2.08(2): voluntary intoxication does not negate recklessness; unawareness of risk while intoxicated is "immaterial" if the actor would have been aware while sober.
- **Chapter support:** MPC § 2.08(2) quoted verbatim in chapter: "when recklessness establishes an element of the offense, if the actor, due to self-induced intoxication, is unaware of a risk of which he would have been aware had he been sober, such unawareness is immaterial."
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

#### per-victim-stacking
- **Claim:** Pennsylvania and Louisiana require consecutive sentences per victim; Pennsylvania example: one prior DUI + two deaths = ten-year mandatory minimum.
- **Chapter support:** Chapter: "Pennsylvania and Louisiana require consecutive sentences for each victim killed"; "a defendant with one prior DUI who kills two people faces a ten-year mandatory minimum."
- **Status:** VERIFIED.
