## Element Verification

### Prima Facie Elements

- [x] **Enterprise** — Confirmed. Chapter § B: "A threshold question in any RICO prosecution is what constitutes an 'enterprise.'" *Boyle v. United States* three-part test stated in Pinson opinion at lines 69–70.
- [x] **Pattern of racketeering activity** — Confirmed. 18 U.S.C. § 1961 callout: "requires at least two acts of racketeering activity [...] within ten years [...] after the commission of a prior act." Chapter: "related and suggest continuing criminal activity" (line 24).
- [x] **Predicate offenses** — Confirmed. 18 U.S.C. § 1961 callout: "dozens of predicate offenses — specific crimes that, when committed as part of a pattern, can trigger RICO liability" (line 24); statutory callout lists murder, kidnapping, mail fraud, wire fraud.
- [x] **Nexus: conduct through the enterprise** — Confirmed. 18 U.S.C. § 1962(c) callout: "to conduct or participate, directly or indirectly, in the conduct of such enterprise's affairs through a pattern of racketeering activity."
- [x] **Interstate commerce nexus** — Confirmed. 18 U.S.C. § 1962 callout: "enterprise which is engaged in, or the activities of which affect, interstate or foreign commerce" (appears in subsections a, b, c).

### Liability Extensions

- [x] **RICO conspiracy (§ 1962(d))** — Confirmed. 18 U.S.C. § 1962(d) callout: "It shall be unlawful for any person to conspire to violate any of the provisions of subsection (a), (b), or (c)." *Rich* holding: "an agreement to associate with and participate in a yet-to-be-formed racketeering enterprise that would affect interstate commerce constitutes a completed offense under Section 1962(d)." *Salinas* summarized: "A conspirator must intend to further an endeavor which, if completed, would satisfy all of the elements of a substantive criminal offense."

### Liability Limits

- [x] **Hub-and-spoke without a rim** — Confirmed. *Pinson* holding: "the evidence was insufficient to prove the existence of a single RICO enterprise" because the participants "did not all share a common purpose or a relationship with one another." Chapter § B note: "A common actor at the hub does not, by itself, create the relationships among associates that Boyle requires."
- [x] **Participation vs. patronage** — Confirmed. *Christensen* discussion questions explicitly raise the issue: "What does his conviction suggest about the liability of 'customers' of criminal enterprises?" Insys discussion question (DQ 3) directly asks students to compare Christensen and the doctors along participation/patronage lines.
- [x] **Gang statute constitutional floor: membership vs. participation** — Confirmed. *State v. O.C.* note case: "Florida's original gang statute allowed sentence enhancement for any defendant who was a 'member' of a gang." "The Florida Supreme Court held this provision unconstitutional because it could punish mere association." "This distinction parallels the requirements of the federal RICO statute, which punishes participation in an enterprise's affairs through racketeering activity, not membership."
- [x] **Gang pattern: more than reputational benefit (AB 333)** — Confirmed. Cal. Penal Code § 186.22(e) callout: "offenses commonly benefited a criminal street gang, and the common benefit from the offenses is more than reputational." *Cooper* holding: "failure to instruct the jury on the new requirements established by Assembly Bill 333 was not harmless beyond a reasonable doubt."

## Refinement Verification

### boyle-three-part
- [x] Confirmed. *Pinson* opinion quotes *Boyle*: "An association-in-fact enterprise must have at least three structural features: a purpose, relationships among those associated with the enterprise, and longevity sufficient to permit these associates to pursue the enterprise's purpose." *Boyle v. United States*, 556 U.S. 938, 946 (2009).
- [x] Trap confirmed: Chapter discussion question 1 after *Pinson* directly addresses the "relationships among associates vs. common defendant" distinction.
- [x] Slide tag confirmed: Slide 8 ("threshold question"), Slide 9 (Boyle's three-part test with each element named).

### hub-spoke-no-rim
- [x] Confirmed. *Pinson* holding: "the evidence was insufficient to prove the existence of a single RICO enterprise." Chapter § B note uses "hub-and-spoke" terminology explicitly: "Picture a wheel: the hub is the central actor, the spokes are the separate schemes or co-conspirators radiating outward, and the rim is the connection among the spokes that holds the wheel together."
- [x] *Kotteakos* reference confirmed: Chapter § C notes "RICO's enterprise element changes the analysis" and cites *Kotteakos v. United States*, 328 U.S. 750 (1946).
- [x] Slide tags confirmed: Slides 10–11 apply *Boyle* to *Pinson* and show hub-and-spoke diagram.

### rim-holds
- [x] Confirmed. *Christensen* holding: "the PIA enterprise had continuity of structure, personnel, and purpose." Court: "Each participant knew that their services were part of a larger illegal operation, even if they did not know the identities of specific clients or the details of particular investigations."
- [x] Trap confirmed: Chapter opinion notes "Arneson and Turner may not have known the full extent of Pellicano's activities with other clients" — the map's trap correctly identifies this as a student error point.
- [x] Slide tags confirmed: Slide 12 (*Christensen*); Slide 15 (enterprise structure diagram); Slide 16 (participation vs. patronage).

Exam architects should note this point is available for discussion questions but is not independently testable as a RICO element.
