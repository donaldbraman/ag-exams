## Elements

### Prima Facie Elements

The insanity defense is an **excuse** — the defendant admits committing the prohibited act but claims mental illness removed the moral responsibility that punishment requires.

To establish the affirmative defense of insanity (under the most common formulation), the defendant must prove:

1. The defendant was suffering from a **mental disease or defect** at the time of the offense. (Not a personality disorder alone; not a condition manifested only by repeated antisocial conduct.)
2. As a **result of** that mental disease or defect, the defendant either:
   a. **(Cognitive prong)** Did not know the nature and quality of the act, OR did not know that the act was wrong; OR
   b. **(Volitional prong, MPC/some jurisdictions)** Lacked substantial capacity to conform conduct to the requirements of law.
3. The **causal link** runs from the qualifying mental disease to the incapacity — not merely from a co-occurring disorder.

Burden: In most jurisdictions, the **defendant bears the burden of proof** (preponderance in a majority; clear and convincing in federal courts and a few states). In a minority, the prosecution must disprove insanity beyond a reasonable doubt.

### Liability Extensions

*(The insanity defense is an excuse with no formal liability extensions. Post-acquittal civil commitment is addressed in the procedural context below.)*

### Liability Limits

1. **Personality disorder exclusion** — A mental abnormality constituting solely a personality disorder (e.g., antisocial personality disorder) does not qualify as "mental disease or defect" and cannot by itself support the insanity defense.
2. **Sociopath/antisocial conduct exclusion** — MPC § 4.01(2) and many state statutes exclude conditions "manifested only by repeated criminal or otherwise antisocial conduct."
3. **Mens rea model (abolition states)** — Four states (Idaho, Kansas, Montana, Utah) abolished the affirmative defense; mental illness evidence is only admissible to negate the specific statutory mens rea element.
4. **Voluntary intoxication nexus** — Mental illness caused or substantially aggravated by voluntary intoxication is treated with suspicion and often excluded by statute or instruction.

## Refinements

### Mental Disease or Defect (Threshold Requirement)

#### qualifying-mental-disease: What Counts as "Mental Disease or Defect"
- **Rule:** Not every mental condition qualifies. The legal term is not coextensive with the DSM. Conditions that qualify include schizophrenia and major psychotic disorders; conditions that do not qualify by statute include personality disorders (standing alone) and antisocial conduct. Courts distinguish qualifying from non-qualifying diagnoses as a threshold legal determination.
- **Source:** MPC § 4.01(2); ORS 161.295(2); *State v. Meiser*; Durham note (psychopathic personality reclassification problem)
- **Trap:** Students assume any DSM diagnosis automatically qualifies as "mental disease or defect" because the legal term sounds medical, missing that the legislature draws the legal boundary and personality disorders are expressly excluded by most statutes.
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** If the defendant's only diagnosis were antisocial personality disorder with no co-occurring schizophrenia, the defense fails at the threshold — the incapacity inquiry is never reached.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal (exclusion of personality disorders alone appears in MPC and most state statutes; the precise boundary varies)
- **Practiced:** Slide 6 — overview of insanity defense standards; identifying "mental disease" as the gateway concept

#### durham-product-test: The Durham Rule and Its Failure
- **Rule:** Durham (D.C. Cir. 1954) provided that an accused is not responsible if the unlawful act was the "product" of mental disease or defect. The test was abandoned because it effectively delegated the legal determination of responsibility to psychiatrists — when St. Elizabeth's reclassified "psychopathic personality" as a "mental disease," defendants who would have been convicted became eligible for acquittal, exposing how diagnosis drives outcome under a purely causal test.
- **Source:** *Durham v. United States*, 214 F.2d 862 (D.C. Cir. 1954); *United States v. Brawner*, 471 F.2d 969 (D.C. Cir. 1972)
- **Trap:** Students choose Durham as the broadest and most defendant-friendly test without noticing its fatal flaw: it creates an accountability gap when psychiatric classifications shift, allowing medical sociology rather than legal standards to drive acquittal rates.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If St. Elizabeth's had not reclassified psychopathic personality, the diagnostic-creep problem would not have materialized and Durham might have survived longer.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (New Hampshire alone retains a causal product test; all other jurisdictions abandoned it)

### The Cognitive Prong

#### mnaghten-nature-quality: Not Knowing the Nature and Quality of the Act
- **Rule:** Under the first M'Naghten prong, the defendant is excused if, due to mental disease, they did not know the *nature and quality* of the act — for example, a psychotic parent who believes she is squeezing a grapefruit when she is actually strangling her child. This prong addresses cognitive impairment so severe the defendant did not understand what they were physically doing.
- **Source:** *M'Naghten's Case*, 10 Cl. & Fin. 200 (H.L. 1843); chapter introduction
- **Trap:** Students apply the "nature and quality" prong to defendants who understood what they were doing physically but didn't understand it was wrong, missing that this first prong is about physical act comprehension, not moral comprehension.
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** If the defendant knew she was strangling a child but believed God commanded it, the first prong is not met; she must rely on the second (wrongness) prong.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal (the traditional test; still the baseline in majority of states)
- **Practiced:** Slide 6 — "COGNITIVE/MORAL INCAPACITY: ...unable to understand the nature or wrongness of their acts"

#### mnaghten-wrongness: Not Knowing the Act Was Wrong
- **Rule:** Under the second M'Naghten prong, the defendant is excused if they knew what they were doing but, due to mental disease, did not know it was *wrong*. The classic example is a delusional defendant who believes God commanded the killing. The standard focuses on cognitive awareness of the act's moral or legal character, not on the ability to control the act.
- **Source:** *M'Naghten's Case*; *Kahler v. Kansas*; chapter introduction
- **Trap:** Students conflate "did not know it was wrong" with "knew it was wrong but could not stop" — the wrongness prong speaks only to cognition, not volition; a defendant who knew the act was wrong but could not control themselves must rely on the volitional prong.
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** If Kansas had retained the moral wrongfulness prong, Kahler could have argued that severe depression prevented him from understanding that killing his family was wrong — the constitutional issue in *Kahler* would not have arisen.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal (all traditional tests include this prong; Kansas and the abolition states are the exception)
- **Practiced:** Slide 6 — moral incapacity as a distinct prong; Slide 8 — IDRA returns to M'Naghten-style cognitive test

#### mpc-appreciate-criminality: "Appreciate" vs. "Know" — The MPC Softening
- **Rule:** The MPC replaces M'Naghten's "know" with "appreciate," requiring only that the defendant lacked "substantial capacity" to appreciate the criminality (or wrongfulness) of their conduct. "Appreciate" is broader than "know" — it encompasses emotional as well as cognitive understanding, reaching defendants who knew abstractly that their act was wrong but could not emotionally grasp its significance. "Substantial" incapacity suffices; complete impairment is not required.
- **Source:** MPC § 4.01(1); chapter Section C; *State v. Meiser* (analysis)
- **Trap:** Students assume "appreciates" and "knows" are interchangeable and apply the MPC test with the same restrictive interpretation as M'Naghten, missing that the MPC's "appreciate" standard was designed to reach a broader set of cognitively aware but meaningfully impaired defendants.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** A defendant who intellectually recited that murder was illegal but dissociatively lacked any emotional comprehension would fail M'Naghten's "know" test but might satisfy the MPC's "appreciate" standard.
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC) — roughly twelve states use MPC-style language; majority use M'Naghten; federal court uses IDRA cognitive-only test

### The Volitional Prong

#### irresistible-impulse: The Volitional Supplement to M'Naghten
- **Rule:** Four states supplement M'Naghten with an "irresistible impulse" test, asking whether — even if the defendant knew the act was wrong — mental disease destroyed the capacity to control behavior. The test addresses the criticism that M'Naghten ignores mentally ill defendants who know what they are doing and know it is wrong but genuinely cannot stop themselves.
- **Source:** *Parsons v. State*, 2 So. 854 (Ala. 1887); chapter Section B (Irresistible Impulse note); Slide 11
- **Trap:** Students assume "irresistible impulse" requires that the defendant was unable to form a plan or that the act was literally instantaneous — courts and commentators have long struggled to distinguish "irresistible" impulses from merely "unresisted" ones, and the test does not require impulsive behavior in the colloquial sense.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If the defendant planned the crime in advance over several days, the irresistible-impulse defense becomes much harder to sustain, even if the underlying disorder caused the planning itself.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (four states add irresistible impulse to M'Naghten; MPC integrates the volitional prong as the "conform" element)
- **Practiced:** Slide 11 — "Irresistible Impulse: A supplement to M'Naghten Rule in 4 states"; Slide 12 — film reference (*Anatomy of a Murder*); Slide 13 — Lorena Bobbitt case discussion

#### mpc-conform-conduct: MPC Volitional Prong — "Conform Conduct to Requirements of Law"
- **Rule:** The MPC's second disjunctive basis for the defense asks whether the defendant lacked substantial capacity to "conform his conduct to the requirements of law." This is the most expansive volitional standard: unlike the irresistible impulse test, it does not require sudden uncontrollable behavior; it asks broadly whether, due to mental disease, the defendant lacked sufficient control capacity. The post-Hinckley reforms eliminated this prong from most state and all federal formulations.
- **Source:** MPC § 4.01(1); *State v. Meiser* (ORS 161.295(1)); chapter Sections C and D
- **Trap:** Students apply the MPC conform-conduct prong even in jurisdictions that have expressly eliminated it post-Hinckley, because they treat the MPC as a universal default rather than recognizing it as a minority position.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** Had the federal courts not eliminated this prong after Hinckley, defendants with volitional disorders (impulse-control disorders, severe PTSD) would have had a viable federal insanity claim that IDRA now forecloses.
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC) — MPC jurisdictions retain it; M'Naghten-only and federal jurisdictions do not

### Burden of Proof and Standard

#### burden-allocation-post-hinckley: Burden Shifts After Hinckley
- **Rule:** Before 1982, most jurisdictions placed the burden on the *prosecution* to disprove insanity beyond a reasonable doubt once the defendant raised the issue. After Hinckley's acquittal, the majority of states shifted the burden to the *defendant*, with the standard ranging from preponderance of the evidence (majority) to clear and convincing evidence (federal courts; some states). A shrinking minority still place the burden on the prosecution.
- **Source:** Chapter Section D; 18 U.S.C. § 17(b); Slide 8; Slide 9
- **Trap:** Students assume the prosecution always bears the burden of disproving defenses beyond a reasonable doubt because that is the general rule for criminal defenses, missing that insanity is a major exception where the defendant-bears-burden rule is now dominant.
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** If a defendant presents substantial but not overwhelming psychiatric evidence, the choice between preponderance and clear-and-convincing burden can determine whether the defense succeeds or fails.
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC) — varies by state; federal: clear and convincing; majority of states: preponderance; minority: prosecution disproves BRD
- **Practiced:** Slide 8 — IDRA burden shifts and raises standard; Slide 9 — state variation in burden allocation; Slide 10 — *State v. Green* (prosecution-bears-burden example)

#### federal-idra-requirements: Federal IDRA — Severe Mental Disease, No Volitional Prong
- **Rule:** 18 U.S.C. § 17 requires (1) *severe* mental disease or defect (not merely any qualifying condition), (2) the defendant was *unable* to appreciate — not merely substantially impaired — the nature and quality or wrongfulness of the acts, (3) no volitional prong, and (4) defendant proves by clear and convincing evidence. IDRA is the most restrictive major test.
- **Source:** 18 U.S.C. § 17; chapter Section D; Slide 8
- **Trap:** Students conflate the federal IDRA standard with M'Naghten because both focus on cognition only, missing that IDRA adds the "severe" requirement and eliminates the MPC's "substantial capacity" softening — "unable" is stricter than "lacks substantial capacity."
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** A defendant with a significant but not severe psychotic disorder might satisfy MPC "substantial capacity" but fail the IDRA "unable to appreciate" + "severe" threshold.
- **Jurisdiction:** federal only
- **Practiced:** Slide 8 — lists four key IDRA reforms

### The Mens Rea Model (Abolition States)

#### mens-rea-model-abolition: Eliminating the Affirmative Defense
- **Rule:** Four states (Idaho, Kansas, Montana, Utah) abolished the traditional insanity defense, permitting mental illness evidence only to negate the specific statutory mens rea element of the charge. A defendant who knew they were killing but could not appreciate that killing was morally wrong cannot be acquitted — they can only use psychiatric evidence to contest whether they formed the specific intent required.
- **Source:** Chapter Section D; *Kahler v. Kansas*, 589 U.S. 271 (2020); Slide 6 ("MENS REA: ...5 states")
- **Trap:** Students conclude that abolition states provide no protection at all for mentally ill defendants, missing that defendants can still use mental illness to negate mens rea (cognitive incapacity that prevents formation of intent is still relevant) — the gap is for defendants who formed the required intent but could not appreciate its wrongfulness.
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** A delusional defendant who deliberately planned a killing but believed God ordained it had the required intent (purpose to cause death) and cannot be acquitted under the mens rea model, even though the same defendant would likely be acquitted under M'Naghten.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (four states only; *Kahler* confirms constitutionality)
- **Practiced:** Slide 6 — "MENS REA: ...5 states" (slide includes Alaska)

#### kahler-constitutional-floor: No Constitutional Requirement for Moral Incapacity Test
- **Rule:** *Kahler v. Kansas* (2020) held that the Due Process Clause does not require states to provide a separate insanity defense for defendants who, due to mental illness, could not appreciate that their act was morally wrong. A state may satisfy due process by permitting mental illness evidence only to negate mens rea. The constitutional floor is some recognition of mental illness as bearing on criminal responsibility — not any particular test. States retain "substantial open" discretion over insanity doctrine.
- **Source:** *Kahler v. Kansas*, 589 U.S. 271 (2020); Chapter Section II
- **Trap:** Students assume *Kahler* opened the floodgates for other states to abolish the defense because the constitutional barrier was lifted, missing the post-*Kahler* note's observation that no state has enacted abolition legislation since the decision — the constitutional door being open does not mean states will walk through it.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If Kansas had also eliminated evidence of mental illness's effect on mens rea formation, the constitutional calculus in *Kahler* would have been different — the Court emphasized that Kansas preserved the cognitive-incapacity (mens rea) pathway to acquittal.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal (constitutional interpretation applies to all states)
- **Practiced:** Slide 6 — comparison table in chapter callout (Oregon/Federal/Kansas)

#### radue-admissibility-gatekeeping: Admissibility Gatekeeping in Abolition States
- **Rule:** In *State v. Radue* (Idaho 2025), the Idaho Supreme Court held that mental health expert testimony is admissible in a mens rea jurisdiction only if it directly negates the specific statutory intent element. General testimony about a defendant's mental condition (that it made the act less culpable broadly, e.g., dissociation, postpartum conditions) is inadmissible if it does not connect to the formation of the specific intent charged. Defense experts must use the statutory language of intent elements, not clinical framings.
- **Source:** Post-*Kahler* Landscape note; Final Discussion Question 5
- **Trap:** Students assume that because mental illness evidence is "admissible" in abolition states, a psychiatrist can testify broadly about how the defendant's condition affected them — *Radue* shows that expert framing must be tied to statutory intent language or the evidence is excluded entirely.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If the expert had testified that Ireland's dissociation prevented formation of "willfulness" (the statutory element) rather than generally impairing her cognition, the testimony would have been admissible under *Radue*.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (Idaho rule; applicable in mens rea jurisdictions)

### Co-Occurring Disorders and the Personality Disorder Exclusion

#### personality-disorder-exclusion: Standing Alone vs. Contributing
- **Rule:** Statutes exclude "any abnormality constituting *solely* a personality disorder" from the definition of mental disease or defect. The word "solely" does the work: a personality disorder that stands alone cannot qualify, but a personality disorder that co-occurs with a qualifying mental illness (e.g., schizophrenia) does not automatically disqualify the defense. The exclusion applies to the *first element* (qualifying disease) — it does not impose a requirement that the qualifying disease be the *sole cause* of the incapacity.
- **Source:** *State v. Meiser*; ORS 161.295(2); MPC § 4.01(2)
- **Trap:** Students read the personality disorder exclusion as a "taint" rule — if any personality disorder contributed to the incapacity, the defense fails — because they conflate the exclusion's application to the qualifying disease element with an additional causation requirement that the legislature never added.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If Oregon's statute had said "solely as a result of mental disease or defect" rather than "as a result of mental disease or defect," the result in *Meiser* would have been different and the defense would have failed.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal (the exclusion appears in MPC and most state statutes; *Meiser*'s causation reading may vary by state)
- **Practiced:** (not directly practiced in slides; discussed in chapter text)

#### co-occurring-causation: Causal Link When Multiple Diagnoses Present
- **Rule:** When a defendant has both a qualifying diagnosis (schizophrenia) and a non-qualifying diagnosis (antisocial personality disorder), the GEI/insanity defense is available if the qualifying disease caused the required incapacity — even if the non-qualifying disorder also contributed. The prosecution cannot defeat the defense merely by showing that the personality disorder played some role, absent a statutory "sole cause" requirement.
- **Source:** *State v. Meiser* (Analysis Section A); expert testimony of Dr. Choi and Dr. Beaver
- **Trap:** Students assume the prosecution can defeat the defense by pointing to post-offense conduct (fleeing, hiding) as evidence that the antisocial personality disorder was operative, missing that fleeing is consistent with psychotic behavior and is not determinative of which disorder "caused" the incapacity.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If Dr. Choi had testified that the antisocial personality disorder — not the schizophrenia — was the primary driver of the conduct, the causal link to a qualifying disease would have failed.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (*Meiser* is Oregon; other states may differ on causation analysis)

### The "Blackout" Defense and Voluntariness

#### blackout-as-excuse: Dissociation and the Affirmative Defense Framework
- **Rule:** A defendant who commits an act while unconscious (blackout, dissociative episode, coma, convulsion) is not criminally responsible because the act was not volitional. Ohio treats this as an *affirmative defense* — an excuse "peculiarly within the knowledge of the accused" — that the defendant must prove by a preponderance of the evidence. It is legally distinct from insanity, though both involve claims of mental-state incapacity.
- **Source:** *State v. Ireland*, 155 Ohio St.3d 287 (2018); Chapter (Definition of Mental Disease section)
- **Trap:** Students assume that because the blackout defense goes to voluntariness (an element the prosecution must prove), it cannot be treated as an affirmative defense — missing the court's holding that when the defense is peculiarly within the accused's knowledge, Ohio law permits shifting the burden to the defendant even if the claim overlaps with an element.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If the Ohio legislature had classified voluntariness as an element explicitly enumerated in the mental-state statute (rather than a background actus reus requirement), the constitutional argument for the defendant would have been much stronger.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (Ohio's treatment; other jurisdictions may classify blackout as negating actus reus rather than as an affirmative defense)

### Post-Acquittal Consequences and Jury Instructions

#### ngri-civil-commitment: NGRI Does Not Mean Immediate Release
- **Rule:** A verdict of "not guilty by reason of insanity" (NGRI) does not result in immediate release. Virtually all jurisdictions provide for automatic or expedited civil commitment proceedings. The defendant may be committed to a mental health facility for as long as they remain mentally ill and dangerous — which in serious cases is effectively indefinite. Public misperception that NGRI means immediate release significantly distorts how juries apply the defense.
- **Source:** Chapter Section D (GBMI discussion); *Commonwealth v. Chappell*; chapter introduction (Hinckley); Slide 7 (Hinckley institutionalization)
- **Trap:** Students (and jurors) assume NGRI means the defendant is immediately freed because they focus on the "not guilty" label and do not realize it triggers automatic commitment proceedings that can last decades.
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** If juries understood that NGRI leads to indefinite civil commitment in a secure facility (not immediate release), the acquittal rate for insanity pleas might increase, as jurors' primary stated objection is fear of dangerous release.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal
- **Practiced:** Slide 7 — Hinckley's decades at St. Elizabeth's, eventual release only in 2018

#### mutina-instruction: Informing Juries About Post-Acquittal Commitment
- **Rule:** Massachusetts (and some other jurisdictions) requires a *Mutina* instruction — telling jurors that an NGRI verdict will result in civil commitment, not release. The instruction is designed to dispel the fear that drives "guilty" verdicts when jurors worry a dangerous person will walk free. *Chappell* updated the instruction to omit specific time frames and instead explain that commitment continues until a court finds the defendant no longer mentally ill or dangerous.
- **Source:** *Commonwealth v. Chappell*, 473 Mass. 191 (2015)
- **Trap:** Students assume that giving jurors accurate information about commitment consequences will always benefit the defense, but the *Mutina* instruction can cut both ways — it reduces fear of immediate release but may also remind jurors they are deciding a commitment outcome, not just acquitting.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If the instruction had specified only "forty days" maximum commitment (as the old model instruction implied by its structure), jurors might have convicted based on fear that the defendant would be released quickly.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (Massachusetts requires it; *Shannon v. United States* holds federal courts need not give it)

#### consciousness-of-guilt-in-insanity: Post-Offense Conduct as Evidence of Sanity
- **Rule:** Post-offense conduct — changing clothes, abandoning a victim's vehicle, lying to police, making self-serving statements — is admissible and properly submitted to the jury as evidence bearing on criminal responsibility (not just as evidence that the defendant committed the act). Such conduct may show the defendant understood the wrongfulness of the actions and had the capacity to plan and execute an escape. However, courts must recognize that psychotic individuals can also engage in evasive behavior.
- **Source:** *Commonwealth v. Chappell*; Eddie Ray Routh note; *State v. Meiser* (fleeing)
- **Trap:** Students assume post-offense evasive conduct conclusively proves sanity because it shows rational planning, missing that psychotic individuals who experience partial insight (knowing police punish killing, without appreciating its full moral wrongness) can also flee — the evidence is relevant but not determinative.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If Chappell had remained at the scene, made no attempt to conceal his actions, and showed no awareness that capture was possible, the prosecution would have had significantly weaker consciousness-of-guilt evidence.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal

### GBMI and Verdict Alternatives

#### gbmi-verdict: Guilty But Mentally Ill as a Compromise
- **Rule:** Thirteen states offer a "Guilty But Mentally Ill" (GBMI) verdict, which acknowledges mental illness at the time of the offense without granting acquittal. The defendant receives a standard criminal sentence but is theoretically entitled to psychiatric treatment during incarceration. Research consistently shows GBMI defendants receive little more treatment than other prisoners — correctional systems lack resources and typically house GBMI defendants in general populations.
- **Source:** Chapter Section D; Final Discussion Question 4
- **Trap:** Students assume GBMI is a meaningful middle ground that ensures treatment, because the label suggests mental illness is being taken seriously — missing the empirical evidence that GBMI does not deliver meaningful treatment in practice and may simply provide juries a way to avoid the harder acquittal question.
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** If correctional systems were adequately resourced to provide genuine psychiatric treatment, the GBMI verdict would function as a meaningful intermediate option rather than a symbolic compromise that changes little in practice.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (thirteen states; not universal)
- **Practiced:** Slide 9 — state variations on verdict options referenced in context of post-Hinckley reforms

### Battered Woman Syndrome and the Volitional Defense

#### bws-and-irresistible-impulse: BWS as Irresistible Impulse Evidence
- **Rule:** Evidence of Battered Woman Syndrome (BWS) is relevant both to self-defense (explaining why an abuse survivor reasonably perceived imminent danger) and potentially to an insanity defense (supporting an irresistible impulse theory by showing the abuse destroyed volitional capacity). BWS evidence can be introduced under a standard "not guilty" plea — it does not require an insanity plea. Failure to investigate and present BWS evidence in appropriate cases constitutes ineffective assistance of counsel.
- **Source:** *State v. Curley*, 250 So. 3d 236 (La. 2018); Chapter (Irresistible Impulse and BWS section); Slide 13
- **Trap:** Students assume BWS evidence can only be offered in an insanity case (as the lawyer in *Curley* mistakenly believed), missing that it is equally available to explain the reasonableness of the defendant's self-defense claim — why a reasonable abuse survivor might perceive lethal danger even when others would not.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If Curley's attorney had understood that BWS expert testimony was available outside an insanity plea and had presented it, the jury would have had a framework for understanding why Curley's perception of danger was reasonable rather than judging her by the myth that a reasonable person would simply leave.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal (BWS admissibility rules vary, but the principle that BWS is admissible in self-defense cases is broadly recognized)
- **Practiced:** Slide 13 — Lorena Bobbitt case; Slide 14 — Lorena Gallo today; class discussion of irresistible impulse framework and domestic violence cases

#### bws-irresistible-framing-problem: Is Irresistible Impulse the Right Frame for BWS?
- **Rule:** Using the irresistible impulse test as the doctrinal vehicle for battered women's defenses medicalizes what may be a rational — if illegal — adaptive response to extreme and chronic danger. Framing a domestic violence survivor's act as the product of an irresistible impulse imports a pathology narrative that may not accurately describe the defendant's psychology and may reinforce stigma about domestic violence survivors. The irresistible impulse frame is a legal tool, not a medical diagnosis.
- **Source:** Chapter Final Discussion Question 4 (Lorena Bobbitt); post-*Curley* text
- **Trap:** Students assume the irresistible impulse defense is the most sympathetic available frame for battered defendant cases because it fully excuses — missing that labeling survivors as unable to control themselves may be both factually inaccurate and normatively problematic, compared to a self-defense frame that vindicates their decision as reasonable.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If Lorena Bobbitt had been tried in a jurisdiction with a robust imminence exception for chronic abuse (cf. *State v. Norman* dissent), she might have been acquitted on self-defense grounds without requiring an insanity or irresistible impulse label.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal (doctrinal choice is universally available; framing critique applies across jurisdictions)
- **Practiced:** Slide 13 — Bobbitt case used to raise exactly this question

- **Refinements:** irresistible-impulse, bws-and-irresistible-impulse, bws-irresistible-framing-problem, mnaghten-wrongness
- **Why they cluster:** These refinements connect the doctrinal volitional tests to their most contested real-world application — domestic violence survivors — and surface the tension between legal doctrine and medical/moral framing.
- **Scenario hook:** Catina Curley, after twelve years of documented abuse, shoots her husband during an argument; her attorney is considering an insanity plea based on irresistible impulse, a self-defense claim supported by BWS testimony, or both — which approach is doctrinally strongest, and what framing best serves her interests?

### cluster-burden-and-standards
- **Refinements:** burden-allocation-post-hinckley, federal-idra-requirements, kahler-constitutional-floor, mpc-appreciate-criminality
- **Why they cluster:** These refinements address who must prove what, at what standard, under different tests — a cross-cutting practical question that determines whether any insanity defense actually succeeds.
- **Scenario hook:** A defendant raises insanity in a federal prosecution; she has extensive psychiatric documentation of severe schizophrenia with command hallucinations — does the burden and standard of proof affect the outcome differently than it would in a state that applies the MPC?

## Cases

### M'Naghten's Case
- **Holding:** A defendant is not criminally responsible if, due to mental disease, they did not know the nature and quality of the act, or did not know it was wrong.
- **Reasoning:** The House of Lords concluded that criminal responsibility requires that the actor possess the cognitive capacity to know what they are doing and that it is wrong — mental disease that destroys this capacity removes the foundation for criminal liability.
- **Facts:** Daniel M'Naghten, suffering from paranoid delusions, attempted to assassinate the British Prime Minister and killed his secretary, believing the PM was conspiring against him.
- **Teaches:** mnaghten-nature-quality, mnaghten-wrongness
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** no

### Durham v. United States
- **Holding:** An accused is not criminally responsible if the unlawful act was the "product" of mental disease or defect.
- **Reasoning:** The D.C. Circuit sought to free psychiatrists from the rigid right-wrong vocabulary of M'Naghten and allow broader expert testimony about how mental illness affected the defendant's conduct.
- **Facts:** A D.C. criminal defendant challenged the M'Naghten standard as too narrow; the court adopted a broader causal product test that was later abandoned after diagnostic-classification problems emerged.
- **Teaches:** durham-product-test, qualifying-mental-disease
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** no

### Kahler v. Kansas
- **Holding:** The Due Process Clause does not require states to provide a separate insanity defense for defendants who, due to mental illness, could not appreciate that their conduct was morally wrong; a state may satisfy due process by permitting mental illness to negate mens rea only.
- **Reasoning:** The Court held that insanity doctrine sits at the intersection of unsettled psychiatry and contested moral theory, has never crystallized into a single constitutional baseline, and must remain an area of state governance rather than constitutional mandate.
- **Facts:** James Kahler killed his wife, two daughters, and grandmother-in-law after a marital breakdown; he raised insanity arguing that severe depression prevented him from knowing the killings were morally wrong; Kansas permitted mental illness evidence only to negate mens rea.
- **Teaches:** kahler-constitutional-floor, mens-rea-model-abolition, mnaghten-wrongness
- **Source type:** main case
- **Discussion questions:** yes

### State v. Meiser
- **Holding:** A defendant with both a qualifying mental disease (schizophrenia) and a non-qualifying personality disorder (antisocial personality disorder) may establish the GEI defense by showing that the qualifying disease caused the incapacity, even if the personality disorder also contributed — the exclusion for personality disorders applies to the qualifying disease element, not as a "sole cause" requirement for causation.
- **Reasoning:** The Oregon Supreme Court applied a textual analysis: the statute uses "solely" in the exclusion for personality disorders but not in the causation element, indicating the legislature did not intend a sole-cause causation requirement.
- **Facts:** Defendant, diagnosed with schizophrenia and antisocial personality disorder, attacked and killed a homeowner with a machete; both experts agreed schizophrenia was the primary driver of the incapacity; the state argued the personality disorder also contributed.
- **Teaches:** personality-disorder-exclusion, co-occurring-causation, qualifying-mental-disease, mpc-conform-conduct
- **Source type:** main case
- **Discussion questions:** yes

### State v. Ireland
- **Holding:** Blackout (a dissociative or unconscious state caused by disease or injury) is an affirmative defense that the defendant must prove by a preponderance of the evidence, and treating it as such does not violate due process.
- **Reasoning:** The court held that blackout meets the statutory definition of an "affirmative defense" — it is an excuse peculiarly within the knowledge of the accused, on which the accused can fairly be required to adduce supporting evidence — and applying this classification does not unconstitutionally shift the prosecution's burden regarding voluntariness.
- **Facts:** Ireland was charged with felonious assault; he presented expert testimony that he experienced a dissociative episode due to PTSD at the time of the incident; he sought a jury instruction treating blackout as negating consciousness rather than as an affirmative defense.
- **Teaches:** blackout-as-excuse, burden-allocation-post-hinckley
- **Source type:** main case
- **Discussion questions:** yes

### Commonwealth v. Chappell
- **Holding:** Post-offense conduct (changing clothes, abandoning a car, lying to police) is properly submitted to the jury as consciousness-of-guilt evidence bearing on criminal responsibility in an insanity case; the *Mutina* instruction should be revised to omit specific time frames and instead explain that commitment continues until a court finds the defendant no longer mentally ill or dangerous.
- **Reasoning:** The court held that post-offense evasive behavior is relevant to the insanity inquiry because it may show the defendant understood the wrongfulness of the act and had planning capacity; the instruction revision was necessary to dispel juror misperception that NGRI leads to brief confinement.
- **Facts:** Earl Chappell, a resident of a mental health group home diagnosed with schizophrenia, stabbed and killed his counselor; he presented psychiatric testimony supporting an insanity defense; his post-offense conduct of changing clothes, driving away, and lying to police was introduced as consciousness-of-guilt evidence.
- **Teaches:** consciousness-of-guilt-in-insanity, mutina-instruction, ngri-civil-commitment
- **Source type:** main case
- **Discussion questions:** yes

### State v. Curley
- **Holding:** Defense counsel's failure to investigate and present BWS expert testimony constituted deficient performance and prejudice under *Strickland*, requiring relief, where counsel was ignorant (not strategically choosing) to forgo BWS evidence that was available outside an insanity plea.
- **Reasoning:** BWS expert testimony is admissible in a self-defense case (not only in an insanity case) to explain why a domestic abuse survivor may reasonably perceive imminent danger when others would not; counsel's failure to even consult a BWS expert, when abuse documentation was extensive, fell below the objective standard of reasonableness.
- **Facts:** Catina Curley was convicted of second-degree murder for shooting her husband; she had suffered years of documented abuse; her attorney withdrew the NGBRI plea and presented only self-defense, without BWS expert testimony, because he did not know BWS could be used outside an insanity plea.
- **Teaches:** bws-and-irresistible-impulse, bws-irresistible-framing-problem, irresistible-impulse
- **Source type:** main case
- **Discussion questions:** yes

### Parsons v. State
- **Holding:** The M'Naghten test alone is insufficient; a defendant whose mental disease destroys freedom of will (the capacity to choose between right and wrong) is not criminally responsible even if they perceive that the act is wrong.
- **Reasoning:** The Alabama court held that criminal responsibility requires both intellectual discrimination and freedom of will; punishing a defendant who cannot exercise will, even if capable of perceiving right from wrong, violates the foundational premise of criminal liability.
- **Facts:** Nineteenth-century Alabama murder case in which the court adopted the irresistible impulse supplement to M'Naghten's purely cognitive test.
- **Teaches:** irresistible-impulse, mpc-conform-conduct
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** no

### Eddie Ray Routh (American Sniper case)
- **Holding:** Guilty of capital murder; jury rejected insanity defense under Texas M'Naghten test.
- **Reasoning:** Prosecution argued that Routh's post-offense conduct (confessing, apologizing, attempting to evade police) demonstrated he knew his actions were wrong; jury found he did not meet the insanity standard despite schizophrenia diagnosis.
- **Facts:** Routh, an ex-Marine with schizophrenia, shot and killed Chris Kyle and Chad Littlefield at a gun range; defense argued he was in a psychotic episode; prosecution argued his symptoms were self-induced by substance use and his post-offense conduct showed awareness of wrongdoing.
- **Teaches:** consciousness-of-guilt-in-insanity, mnaghten-wrongness, mens-rea-model-abolition
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** yes (Application Questions section)

### State v. Radue (Idaho 2025)
- **Holding:** Mental health expert testimony in a mens rea jurisdiction is admissible only if it directly negates the specific statutory intent element; general testimony about mental condition not tied to statutory intent language is properly excluded.
- **Reasoning:** The Idaho Supreme Court held that in abolition states, the only permissible use of mental illness evidence is to negate the specific mens rea required by the statute; testimony about broader culpability or general mental state is inadmissible because the legislature eliminated the affirmative defense precisely to restrict mental illness claims to mens rea.
- **Facts:** Idaho murder defendant sought to introduce expert testimony on dissociation and postpartum conditions; the trial court excluded it as not directly negating the "willfulness" element; the Idaho Supreme Court affirmed.
- **Teaches:** radue-admissibility-gatekeeping, mens-rea-model-abolition, qualifying-mental-disease
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** yes (Final Discussion Question 5)
