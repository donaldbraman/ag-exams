### Premeditation Refinements

#### no-time-too-short
- **Claim:** *Carroll* holds that any conscious decision to kill, however brief, satisfies premeditation.
- **Chapter support:** *Carroll* discussion, ¶ quoting *Earnest* and *Drum* formulations; court's reasoning affirmed.
- **Trap claim:** Students confuse premeditation with prolonged planning.
- **Status:** VERIFIED. Both the rule and the trap are well-grounded in the chapter's Carroll discussion.

#### cardozo-dispensing-power
- **Claim:** Cardozo described the premeditation standard as a "dispensing power" hidden in a "mystifying cloud of words."
- **Chapter support:** "A Mystifying Cloud of Words?" section, direct quotation from Cardozo's address.
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

#### drum-qualification-dropped
- **Claim:** *Drum* included a qualification that premeditation must not be "the immediate offspring of rashness and impetuous temper," which *Carroll* dropped.
- **Chapter support:** Chapter states explicitly: "The *Carroll* court, and many courts since, dropped the qualification and kept only the headline rule." The original *Drum* qualification is quoted verbatim.
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

#### mpc-no-premeditation
- **Claim:** MPC § 210.2 does not distinguish first- from second-degree intentional murder.
- **Chapter support:** MPC § 210.2 text quoted; chapter commentary: "The Code does not distinguish between premeditated and unpremeditated intentional killings."
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

### Common Law Voluntary Manslaughter Refinements

#### four-elements-common-law
- **Claim:** Four elements: adequate provocation, actual heat of passion, insufficient cooling time, causal connection.
- **Chapter support:** Chapter explicitly lists all four elements in numbered format under "Voluntary Manslaughter: Heat of Passion."
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

#### historical-categories-honor
- **Claim:** Common law recognized narrow categories; doctrine rooted in honor culture; *Mawgridge* framed adultery as "highest invasion of property."
- **Chapter support:** *Mawgridge's Case* (1707) quoted directly; honor culture discussion and Cohen et al. callout box.
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

#### words-alone-girouard
- **Claim:** *Girouard* holds that words alone cannot constitute adequate provocation.
- **Chapter support:** *Girouard v. State*, 321 Md. 532 (1991), discussed; quoting *Sims v. State*: "Insulting words or gestures, no matter how opprobrious, do not amount to an affray."
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

#### thornton-discovery-adultery
- **Claim:** *Thornton* majority held discovery of adultery in flagrante delicto is classic voluntary manslaughter.
- **Chapter support:** *State v. Thornton*, 730 S.W.2d 309 (Tenn. 1987), majority opinion: "this was a classic case of voluntary manslaughter and no more."
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

#### cooling-time-drowota
- **Claim:** Drowota dissented on second-degree murder grounds, cataloguing four hours of calculated acts.
- **Chapter support:** Drowota dissent quoted and discussed; Note & Questions summarizes: "Drowota catalogued four hours of calculated actions: deflating the tire, retrieving the gun, purchasing film, photographing the license plate, and threatening the victim before firing."
- **Pivot fact check:** Map states if Thornton had shot upon first seeing the couple, the cooling-time issue disappears. **Chapter support:** The four-hour sequence is the crux of Drowota's argument; if the shooting happened at first contact, the sequence collapses.
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

#### peacock-four-hours
- **Claim:** Peacock killed four hours after discovery; prosecution accepted manslaughter plea; Judge Cahill sentenced to 18 months.
- **Chapter support:** Facts stated in chapter; Judge Cahill's sentencing remarks quoted directly; Baltimore Sun cited.
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

#### legislative-override-adultery
- **Claim:** Maryland § 2-207(b) (enacted 1997) bars spousal adultery as adequate provocation; § 2-207(c) also bars identity characteristics.
- **Chapter support:** Statute quoted verbatim in chapter callout; legislative history (Delegate Pitkin, HB 270, 1997) stated.
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

### MPC Extreme Emotional Disturbance Refinements

#### eed-standard-elements
- **Claim:** EED is a subjective-objective hybrid; no categorical limits; no cooling-time requirement; disturbance need not come from victim.
- **Chapter support:** MPC § 210.3(1)(b) quoted; chapter states all four differences explicitly.
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

#### eed-expands-old-categories
- **Claim:** Under MPC, words can be a source of EED; *Girouard* would likely get a manslaughter instruction.
- **Chapter support:** Chapter states directly: "*Girouard* might have received a manslaughter instruction, because under the MPC words *can* be a source of extreme emotional disturbance."
- **Status:** VERIFIED.

#### nourse-unintended-consequences
- **Claim:** Nourse's study found MPC's primary beneficiaries were men who killed partners attempting to leave; defense expanded from "classical adultery" to "modern dating and moving and leaving."
- **Chapter support:** Chapter discusses Nourse's study with Georgetown Law citation; quotes "modern dating and moving and leaving" directly.
- **Status:** VERIFIED.
