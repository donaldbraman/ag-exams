### Slide 5

-   **Discussion:**

-   **Practiced:** Applying felony murder rules (MPC vs. traditional), identifying predicate felonies, understanding the burden of proof for *mens rea*, comparing prosecutorial advantages of different rules.

### Slide 7
-   **Content:** An illustration of a house with a red roof and green trees on the left. On the right, the title "3. Intentional Homicide" followed by:
    -   **Facts:** "David, during a trial separation from his wife, discovers she is seeing another man. He forces his way into their home and kills his wife and her boyfriend with his bare hands"
    -   **Questions:** "(1) What would the prosecution need to prove under a traditional felony murder rule? (2) What will the Defense argue? (3) How would this turn out under the Model Penal Code?"
-   **Doctrine:** This is the first "David" hypothetical from the chapter text, where the killing is *intentional*. It explores how felony murder might interact with or be superseded by direct murder charges, and the role of defenses like provocation. This scenario implicitly raises the "merger doctrine."
-   **Discussion:**
    -   (1) **Traditional felony murder:** The underlying felony is likely burglary (entering a dwelling unlawfully with intent to commit a crime, i.e., assault/murder). However, the *merger doctrine* (discussed later in the slides) would likely prevent the assaultive aspect of the burglary from serving as the predicate felony for felony murder, as it would "swallow" all other murder charges. If felony murder is not applicable due to merger, the prosecution would pursue direct murder charges, needing to prove premeditation and deliberation for first-degree murder.
    -   (2) **Defense:** David's attorney would argue for voluntary manslaughter based on "heat of passion" or "provocation" (discovering spousal infidelity). They would also argue against premeditation and deliberation, seeking a reduction to second-degree murder.
    -   (3) **Model Penal Code:** Under the MPC, the prosecution would need to prove David acted with purpose or knowledge to kill (MPC 210.2(1)(a)). The felony murder presumption (MPC 210.2(1)(b)) applies to *reckless* killings. If the killing was intentional, it would be charged under the purposeful/knowing prong. David could still argue for extreme emotional disturbance (the MPC equivalent of heat of passion) to reduce the charge to manslaughter.
-   **Practiced:** Applying felony murder, recognizing the limitations of felony murder (merger doctrine), understanding traditional murder *mens rea* (premeditation, intent), and defenses (provocation/heat of passion, extreme emotional disturbance).
