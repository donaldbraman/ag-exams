### Slide 8
- **Content:**
    - "The proximity test pushes the line late"
    - "Once crossed, abandonment provides no defense"
- **Doctrine:** This slide succinctly identifies the fundamental flaw or "core problem" with the common law's dangerous proximity test: it requires a defendant to come very close to completing the crime before liability attaches, but once that threshold is met, the opportunity for a defense based on abandonment is lost.
- **Discussion:** The professor would explain how this creates a problematic incentive structure: defendants who have taken significant steps but are not yet "dangerously near" have no legal incentive to stop, and those who *are* "dangerously near" also have no incentive to stop because liability has already attached. This sets up the rationale for the MPC's alternative approach.
- **Practiced:** Critically evaluating the common law proximity test and understanding its limitations regarding incentives for renunciation.
