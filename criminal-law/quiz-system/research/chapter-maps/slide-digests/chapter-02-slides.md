### Slide: Why Do We Punish?

- **Content:** Lists the three classical theories — deterrence (Bentham), retribution (Kant), and expressive condemnation. Closes with the lawyer's task: show how they converge.
- **Doctrine:** Introduces all three foundational theories as a set; frames the legal skill as deploying them jointly, not selecting one.
- **Refinement keys:** `deterrence-rational-actor`, `retribution-categorical-imperative`, `expression-insult-message`
- **Practiced:** Yes — students must recognize the three theories, distinguish them, and understand the advocacy move of showing convergence.

### Slide: Deterrence (Bentham)

- **Content:** Bentham quote on pain/pleasure; the core rational actor logic; "make crime not pay."
- **Doctrine:** Rational actor model as the engine of deterrence; expected cost vs. expected benefit.
- **Refinement keys:** `deterrence-rational-actor`
- **Practiced:** Yes — students apply the formula (expected cost > expected benefit → no crime).

### Slide: The Hubcap Theft Problem

- **Content:** Hypothetical: rampant hubcap theft, 1-in-5,000 catch rate, $50 gain, 6-month max penalty. Asks what penalty would deter.
- **Doctrine:** Probability-weighted deterrence calculation; introduces the catch-rate/severity trade-off.
- **Refinement keys:** `deterrence-rational-actor`, `deterrence-four-limits`
- **Practiced:** Yes — students work through the cost-benefit logic with concrete numbers.

### Slide: Bentham's Formula

- **Content:** Formal equation G < Pr × Pen; applies numbers from the hubcap hypothetical; derives the $250,000 required penalty.
- **Doctrine:** Quantitative formulation of deterrence; shows that low detection probability requires extremely high penalties to deter.
- **Refinement keys:** `deterrence-rational-actor`
- **Practiced:** Yes — students compute the deterrence-required penalty.

### Slide: When Fines Won't Work

- **Content:** Judgment-proof problem — thieves can't pay $250,000. Solution: imprisonment. Congress used this logic with FACE Act against Operation Rescue.
- **Doctrine:** Introduces incapacitation/imprisonment as the utilitarian substitute when financial deterrence fails; connects to real legislation.
- **Refinement keys:** `deterrence-specific-general-incapacitation`, `deterrence-rational-actor`
- **Practiced:** Yes — students apply the mode-switch from fines to imprisonment.

### Slide: 15 Years for Hubcap Theft?

- **Content:** Shows the internal logic (big theft + high detection ≈ small theft + low detection). Introduces Bentham's own objection: punishment is always evil; it is "unprofitable" when mischief exceeds prevention.
- **Doctrine:** Two-sided nature of Bentham — he endorses deterrence but equally insists punishment is itself an evil; introduces the cost side of the utilitarian ledger.
- **Refinement keys:** `deterrence-four-limits`
- **Practiced:** Yes — students must recognize that deterrence logic has a built-in limit in Bentham's framework.

- **Refinement keys:** `deterrence-four-limits`

### Slide: Retribution (Kant)

- **Content:** Kant quote on treating persons as ends, not means. Core rule: punishing to deter others violates dignity; punishment must be deserved.

- **Doctrine:** Retributive proportionality as a floor-and-ceiling constraint on punishment, not merely an upper limit.
- **Refinement keys:** `retribution-proportionality`
