### Slide 2
- **Content:** The slide presents a block quote attributed to "Bergen v. US" (likely a typo for *Berger v. United States*). The quote defines the prosecutor's role: "The United States Attorney is the representative not of an ordinary party to a controversy, but of a sovereignty whose obligation to govern impartially is as compelling as its obligation to govern at all; and whose interest, therefore, in a criminal prosecution is not that it shall win a case, but that justice shall be done. As such, he is in a peculiar and very definite sense the servant of the law, the twofold aim of which is that guilt shall not escape or innocence suffer. He may prosecute with earnestness and vigor—indeed, he should do so. But, while he may strike hard blows, he is not at liberty to strike foul ones. It is as much his duty to refrain from improper methods calculated to produce a wrongful conviction as it is to use every legitimate means to bring about a just one."
- **Doctrine:** This slide introduces the *Berger* standard, a foundational ethical principle for prosecutors, defining them as "ministers of justice" whose primary goal is to ensure justice, not merely to secure convictions. This standard is explicitly discussed in a callout box in the chapter.
- **Discussion:** The professor would likely ask students to analyze the implications of the "minister of justice" role. They might prompt discussion on what constitutes "striking foul blows" versus "hard blows" in practice, and how this aspirational standard might conflict with the adversarial nature of the legal system or the pressures prosecutors face.
- **Practiced:** Ethical duties of prosecutors, the "minister of justice" concept, distinguishing ethical from unethical prosecutorial conduct.
