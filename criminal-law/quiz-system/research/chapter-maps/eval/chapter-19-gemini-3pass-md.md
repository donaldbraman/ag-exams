## Doctrines

### 1. inferring-agreement
*   **Rule:** An agreement to commit a crime need not be explicit, written, or verbal; it may be inferred from coordinated conduct and fragments of evidence that, taken together, have no plausible innocent explanation.
*   **Split:** Null.
*   **Levers:**
    *   Spikes in communication (phone records) synchronized with critical events.
    *   Flow of money/payments (e.g., checks for work never performed).
    *   Coordinated physical movements (cell tower data).
    *   Ambiguous statements indicating shared consciousness of guilt ("probably the both of us").
*   **Traps:** Students [conclude no conspiracy exists because there is no explicit "meeting of the minds"] because [they confuse the evidentiary difficulty of proving a secret agreement with the legal requirement of the agreement itself].
*   **Cases:** The Dan Markel Murder Case Study, *United States v. Alvarez*
*   **Lines:** [[19, 28], [56, 58], [379, 386]]
*   **Block Count:** 4
*   **Cross-chapter:** accomplice-liability (Ch. 18)
*   **Hypo Seeds:** 
    *   Two people walk into a bank from different doors; one disables the alarm while the other holds the bag, without ever speaking.
    *   A group of people simultaneously start throwing rocks at a store after a leader shouts "Look at those windows," but no one spoke beforehand.
*   **Exam Lens:** Prosecution

### 2. conspiracy-mens-rea-purpose
*   **Rule:** To be guilty of conspiracy, a defendant must act with the specific purpose of promoting or facilitating the commission of the object crime, even if the object crime itself requires a lower mental state (like recklessness).
*   **Split:** Null (Majority rule).
*   **Levers:**
    *   Defendant's knowledge of the plan vs. active desire for its success.
    *   Defendant's active participation in planning vs. mere presence.
*   **Traps:** Students [apply a "knowledge" or "recklessness" standard to the agreement] because [they confuse the mens rea of the target offense with the heightened mens rea required for the inchoate crime of conspiracy].
*   **Cases:** *State v. Pond*
*   **Lines:** [[59, 60], [107, 107]]
*   **Block Count:** 2
*   **Cross-chapter:** mens-rea-purpose (Ch. 4)
*   **Hypo Seeds:**
    *   A driver agrees to take a friend to a "job" knowing it's a burglary but only cares about the $50 gas money, not whether the burglary succeeds.
    *   A and B agree to set a fire to "see what happens," but the target crime (Arson) requires purpose to destroy a building.
*   **Exam Lens:** Defense

### 3. overt-act-requirement
*   **Rule:** Most jurisdictions require at least one conspirator to perform an act in furtherance of the agreement, though the act itself may be legal, trivial, or remote from the crime's completion.
*   **Split:** MPC 5.03(5) waives the overt act requirement for first and second-degree felonies.
*   **Levers:**
    *   Legality of the act (e.g., buying a plane ticket).
    *   Remoteness from the target crime.
    *   Attribution: Any conspirator's act satisfies the requirement for all.
*   **Traps:** Students [argue an overt act is insufficient for liability] because [they confuse the "minimal" overt act of conspiracy with the "substantial step" required for attempt].
*   **Cases:** *Hyde v. United States*
*   **Lines:** [[44, 44], [50, 50], [61, 62]]
*   **Block Count:** 3
*   **Cross-chapter:** substantial-step-test (Ch. 17)
*   **Hypo Seeds:**
    *   Conspirators agree to build a bomb; one checks out a chemistry book from the library.
    *   After agreeing to a hit, one conspirator calls the other just to say "I'm ready," but takes no other action.
*   **Exam Lens:** Prosecution

### 4. bilateral-vs-unilateral-conspiracy
*   **Rule:** The bilateral approach requires two or more "guilty minds," while the unilateral approach focuses only on whether the individual defendant believed they were agreeing to a crime.
*   **Split:** Traditional/Common Law (Bilateral) vs. MPC/Modern (Unilateral).
*   **Levers:**
    *   Status of the co-conspirator (undercover agent or informant).
    *   Defendant's subjective belief in the reality of the agreement.
*   **Traps:** Students [argue a defendant is innocent in a unilateral jurisdiction because the "agreement" was a sting] because [they confuse the social danger of a group with the individual culpability of the defendant].
*   **Cases:** *State v. Pacheco*, *Garcia v. State*, *United States v. Valigura*
*   **Lines:** [[63, 74]]
*   **Block Count:** 1
*   **Cross-chapter:** entrapment (Ch. 17)
*   **Hypo Seeds:**
    *   A, B, and C agree to a heist. B and C are both undercover agents unknown to each other. Is A liable?
    *   A agrees with B to rob a bank. B was lying and immediately calls the police.
*   **Exam Lens:** Both

### 5. intent-to-target-elements
*   **Rule:** A conspirator must specifically intend that every element of the planned offense be accomplished, including aggravating factors like the use of a weapon.
*   **Split:** *Pond* (Majority) requires specific intent for all elements; Dissent argues this creates an "irrational gap" where conspiracy is harder to prove than the completed crime.
*   **Levers:**
    *   Lookout's knowledge of a weapon.
    *   Specific mention of aggravating factors during the agreement phase.
*   **Traps:** Students [assume a lookout is liable for "conspiracy to commit armed robbery" simply because a partner used a gun] because [they confuse *Pinkerton* liability for the substantive crime with the specific intent required for the conspiracy charge itself].
*   **Cases:** *State v. Pond*
*   **Lines:** [[84, 132]]
*   **Block Count:** 1
*   **Cross-chapter:** accomplice-liability (Ch. 18)
*   **Hypo Seeds:**
    *   A agrees to help B "scare" a victim; B brings a real gun and points it.
    *   A agrees to transport stolen goods, not knowing they will cross state lines (where the federal statute requires knowledge of the interstate element).
*   **Exam Lens:** Defense

### 6. conspiracy-to-negligent-crimes
*   **Rule:** A person cannot be convicted of conspiracy to commit a crime of negligence or an unintended result, as one cannot "agree" to be negligent.
*   **Split:** Null.
*   **Levers:**
    *   Mental state of the target offense (Negligence vs. Intent).
*   **Traps:** Students [find a defendant guilty of "conspiracy to commit involuntary manslaughter"] because [they confuse the agreement to engage in risky conduct with the specific intent to achieve a prohibited result].
*   **Cases:** *People v. Koenig*
*   **Lines:** [[136, 137]]
*   **Block Count:** 1
*   **Cross-chapter:** involuntary-manslaughter (Ch. 11)
*   **Hypo Seeds:**
    *   Two drivers agree to race at 100mph through a school zone. A pedestrian is killed. Can they be charged with conspiracy to commit manslaughter?
*   **Exam Lens:** Defense

### 7. supplier-liability-knowledge-vs-purpose
*   **Rule:** A supplier of legal goods/services is a conspirator only if they have the purpose to further the crime, which can be inferred from a "stake in the venture" or the "aggravated nature" of the crime.
*   **Split:** Null.
*   **Levers:**
    *   Inflated prices (premium for criminality).
    *   Volume of business (disproportionate to legitimate use).
    *   Gravity of the crime (misdemeanor vs. felony/violence).
    *   Specialized services (coded messages).
*   **Traps:** Students [find a merchant guilty for selling ordinary goods to a known criminal] because [they confuse "knowledge of criminal use" with "purpose to facilitate."]
*   **Cases:** *People v. Lauria*, *United States v. Falcone*
*   **Lines:** [[146, 192]]
*   **Block Count:** 2
*   **Cross-chapter:** accomplice-liability-mens-rea (Ch. 18)
*   **Hypo Seeds:**
    *   A wholesaler sells 5,000 lbs of sugar to a known moonshiner at 3x the market price.
    *   A pharmacist fills 500 OxyContin prescriptions for the same "patient" over a week, charging a $100 "processing fee" per pill.
*   **Exam Lens:** Both

### 8. pinkerton-liability
*   **Rule:** A conspirator is vicariously liable for all substantive crimes committed by co-conspirators that are (1) in furtherance of the conspiracy and (2) reasonably foreseeable.
*   **Split:** Federal/Traditional (Follows *Pinkerton*) vs. MPC/Modern (Rejects *Pinkerton*).
*   **Levers:**
    *   Whether the act was "in furtherance" of the shared goal.
    *   Foreseeability of the specific crime (e.g., is a shooting foreseeable in a drug deal?).
    *   Withdrawal: Liability continues until affirmative withdrawal.
*   **Traps:** Students [fail to hold a "ringleader" liable for a specific robbery committed by a subordinate] because [they confuse the requirements of accomplice liability (aiding/abetting) with the broader reach of *Pinkerton* (foreseeability)].
*   **Cases:** *Pinkerton v. United States*
*   **Lines:** [[243, 282], [284, 298]]
*   **Block Count:** 2
*   **Cross-chapter:** accomplice-liability (Ch. 18)
*   **Hypo Seeds:**
    *   A and B agree to shoplift. B gets caught and punches a security guard. Is A liable for the assault?
    *   While on a drug run for the conspiracy, B decides to rob a gas station for extra cash. Is A liable?
*   **Exam Lens:** Prosecution

### 9. mpc-rejection-of-pinkerton
*   **Rule:** Under the MPC, conspiracy membership alone is insufficient for vicarious liability; the defendant must meet the specific requirements of accomplice liability (purpose to promote/facilitate) for each substantive offense.
*   **Split:** MPC Section 2.06 vs. *Pinkerton*.
*   **Levers:**
    *   Presence of "purpose to promote" the specific substantive act.
*   **Traps:** Students [apply *Pinkerton* in an MPC jurisdiction] because [they confuse the general "partnership in crime" concept with the MPC's stricter individual culpability requirement].
*   **Cases:** Null.
*   **Lines:** [[300, 305]]
*   **Block Count:** 1
*   **Cross-chapter:** accomplice-liability-mens-rea (Ch. 18)
*   **Hypo Seeds:**
    *   A acts as a lookout for a burglary. B kills the homeowner. Under MPC, is A liable for murder if he only intended the burglary?
*   **Exam Lens:** Defense

### 10. wheel-and-rim-conspiracy
*   **Rule:** Multiple parties dealing with a common "hub" only form a single conspiracy if there is a "rim"—evidence that the "spokes" knew of each other and had a shared stake in a common venture.
*   **Split:** Null.
*   **Levers:**
    *   Mutual awareness between "spokes."
    *   Interdependence of the transactions.
    *   Shared "overall plan" vs. distinct individual frauds.
*   **Traps:** Students [group all customers of a single drug dealer into one conspiracy] because [they confuse "having a common supplier" with "having a common agreement."]
*   **Cases:** *Kotteakos v. United States*
*   **Lines:** [[346, 377]]
*   **Block Count:** 1
*   **Cross-chapter:** Null.
*   **Hypo Seeds:**
    *   A broker helps 50 people file fake tax returns. None of the 50 know each other.
    *   A dealer sells to 10 street-level pushers who compete for the same corners.
*   **Exam Lens:** Defense

### 11. chain-conspiracy
*   **Rule:** In a vertical chain of distribution (e.g., manufacturer to wholesaler to retailer), an agreement is inferred because each party knows their success depends on the performance of the others.
*   **Split:** Null.
*   **Levers:**
    *   Knowledge that others are necessary to the scheme.
    *   Essential nature of cooperation for the plan's success.
*   **Traps:** Students [argue a distributor isn't in a conspiracy with a retailer they've never met] because [they confuse "direct contact" with "legal agreement."]
*   **Cases:** *Interstate Circuit v. United States*
*   **Lines:** [[319, 344]]
*   **Block Count:** 1
*   **Cross-chapter:** Null.
*   **Hypo Seeds:**
    *   A brings drugs across the border; B transports them to the city; C sells them. A and C never meet.
*   **Exam Lens:** Prosecution

### 12. buyer-seller-exception
*   **Rule:** A simple agreement to buy or sell drugs for personal use does not constitute a conspiracy to distribute; there must be a shared purpose to further the distribution.
*   **Split:** Null.
*   **Levers:**
    *   Prolonged course of dealing.
    *   Use of coded language.
    *   "Fronting" (credit arrangements).
*   **Traps:** Students [charge a one-time drug buyer with conspiracy] because [they confuse the "agreement to buy" with the "agreement to distribute."]
*   **Cases:** *United States v. Pauling*
*   **Lines:** [[393, 396]]
*   **Block Count:** 1
*   **Cross-chapter:** Null.
*   **Hypo Seeds:**
    *   A buys 10 kilos of cocaine but claims it's for a very long personal vacation.
    *   A gives B a gram of heroin for free, expecting B to pay him back after B sells his own stash of weed.
*   **Exam Lens:** Defense

### 13. whartons-rule
*   **Rule:** An agreement to commit a crime that inherently requires two people (e.g., adultery, bribery) cannot be prosecuted as a conspiracy unless more people participate than are logically necessary.
*   **Split:** Judicial presumption (can be overridden by legislature).
*   **Levers:**
    *   Number of participants (2 vs. 3+).
    *   Nature of the crime (Does it *require* two people?).
*   **Traps:** Students [charge two people with "conspiracy to duel"] because [they confuse the "agreement to commit a crime" with the "substantive crime of dueling" itself].
*   **Cases:** *Iannelli v. United States*
*   **Lines:** [[401, 402]]
*   **Block Count:** 1
*   **Cross-chapter:** Null.
*   **Hypo Seeds:**
    *   A gives a bribe to B (the official) through C (the bagman). Does Wharton's Rule apply?
*   **Exam Lens:** Defense

### 14. legislative-exemption-rule
*   **Rule:** If a legislature defines a crime to protect or exempt a specific class of participants (e.g., the victim or a minor), that person cannot be charged with conspiracy to commit that crime.
*   **Split:** Null.
*   **Levers:**
    *   Statutory intent to protect a class.
    *   Whether the party's participation is an "inseparable incident" of the crime.
*   **Traps:** Students [charge a transported woman with conspiracy to violate the Mann Act] because [they confuse "voluntary participation" with "criminal liability."]
*   **Cases:** *Gebardi v. United States*
*   **Lines:** [[403, 408]]
*   **Block Count:** 1
*   **Cross-chapter:** Null.
*   **Hypo Seeds:**
    *   An adult and a 15-year-old agree to have sex. Can the 15-year-old be charged with conspiracy?
*   **Exam Lens:** Defense

### 15. withdrawal-and-renunciation
*   **Rule:** Withdrawal terminates future liability for coconspirators' crimes; it requires affirmative action to defeat the conspiracy or communication to all members.
*   **Split:** Traditional (Withdrawal ends future liability) vs. MPC (Renunciation/Thwarting required for complete defense).
*   **Levers:**
    *   Communication to all members.
    *   Reporting to police (MPC).
    *   Timing (must be before the overt act in some jurisdictions).
*   **Traps:** Students [think simply "quitting" the group ends liability] because [they confuse "ending participation" with the legal requirement of "affirmative withdrawal" or "thwarting."]
*   **Cases:** *Pinkerton v. United States* (mentioning withdrawal)
*   **Lines:** [[261, 281, 480]]
*   **Block Count:** 1
*   **Cross-chapter:** renunciation (Ch. 17)
*   **Hypo Seeds:**
    *   A tells B "I'm out" and goes home. B robs the bank anyway. Is A liable for the robbery?
    *   A calls the police to report the plan, but the police arrive too late to stop the robbery.
*   **Exam Lens:** Defense

### 16. coconspirator-hearsay-exception
*   **Rule:** Statements made by one conspirator in furtherance of the conspiracy are admissible against all members of the conspiracy.
*   **Split:** Null.
*   **Levers:**
    *   "In furtherance" requirement.
    *   Existence of the conspiracy (preliminary determination).
*   **Traps:** Students [object to a partner's confession being used against the defendant] because [they confuse the general rule against hearsay with the specific "agency" theory of conspiracy].
*   **Cases:** *Kotteakos v. United States* (prejudice from variance)
*   **Lines:** [[31, 372, 390, 438]]
*   **Block Count:** 1
*   **Cross-chapter:** Null.
*   **Hypo Seeds:**
    *   A tells his wife about the robbery plan. Is this statement admissible against B?
    *   A tells B "The guns are in the trunk" while driving to the bank. Is this admissible against C?
*   **Exam Lens:** Prosecution

### 17. merger-rule
*   **Rule:** Traditionally, conspiracy does not merge with the completed crime; the MPC prohibits convicting a defendant of both conspiracy and the completed crime.
*   **Split:** Traditional (No merger) vs. MPC (Merger).
*   **Levers:**
    *   Jurisdiction (MPC vs. Common Law).
*   **Traps:** Students [assume a defendant can only be punished for the robbery, not the conspiracy to rob] because [they confuse the merger rule of attempt/solicitation with the non-merger rule of traditional conspiracy].
*   **Cases:** Null.
*   **Lines:** [[479, 491]]
*   **Block Count:** 1
*   **Cross-chapter:** merger (Ch. 17)
*   **Hypo Seeds:**
    *   In an MPC state, A is convicted of conspiracy to rob and the robbery itself. Can he be sentenced for both?
*   **Exam Lens:** Defense

### 18. manufactured-conspiracy-doctrine
*   **Rule:** Under the unilateral approach, the government can "manufacture" a conspiracy by having an agent propose a crime and secure a defendant's agreement, triggering liability even if no real group exists.
*   **Split:** Bilateral vs. Unilateral (See Doctrine 4).
*   **Levers:**
    *   Quantity of drugs supplied by the government (triggers mandatory minimums).
    *   Financial incentives (civil forfeiture).
*   **Traps:** Students [argue there is no conspiracy because the government "created" the crime] because [they confuse "entrapment" (a defense) with the "elements of conspiracy" (which are met under the unilateral rule)].
*   **Cases:** Null.
*   **Lines:** [[441, 459], [493, 502]]
*   **Block Count:** 2
*   **Cross-chapter:** entrapment (Ch. 17)
*   **Hypo Seeds:**
    *   An agent insists on selling 5kg of cocaine (mandatory minimum) to a defendant who only asked for 10 grams.
*   **Exam Lens:** Both

## Cases

### 1. State v. Pond
*   **Lines:** [79, 132]
*   **Holding:** To be convicted of conspiracy, a defendant must specifically intend that every element of the planned offense be accomplished, including aggravating factors like the display of a weapon.
*   **Facts:** A lookout for a convenience store robbery was convicted of conspiracy to commit second-degree robbery after his partner gestured as if he had a gun; the lookout argued he never intended or agreed to the use of a weapon.
*   **Doctrines:** ["intent-to-target-elements", "conspiracy-mens-rea-purpose"]

### 2. People v. Lauria
*   **Lines:** [150, 192]
*   **Holding:** Knowledge of a customer's criminal activity is insufficient for conspiracy liability unless the supplier has a "stake in the venture" or the crime is of an "aggravated nature."
*   **Facts:** The owner of a telephone answering service knew some of his clients were prostitutes using the service for business but charged them standard rates and did not facilitate the crimes beyond providing the service.
*   **Doctrines:** ["supplier-liability-knowledge-vs-purpose"]

### 3. Pinkerton v. United States
*   **Lines:** [247, 282]
*   **Holding:** A conspirator is liable for the substantive crimes of co-conspirators committed in furtherance of the conspiracy and within its foreseeable scope, even if they did not personally participate in those crimes.
*   **Facts:** Two brothers conspired to violate tax laws; one brother was in prison when the other committed substantive tax offenses, yet both were held liable for the substantive acts.
*   **Doctrines:** ["pinkerton-liability", "withdrawal-and-renunciation"]

### 4. Interstate Circuit v. United States
*   **Lines:** [319, 344]
*   **Holding:** Agreement can be inferred where competitors, knowing that concerted action is invited and essential for success, adhere to a common scheme that departs from previous business practices.
*   **Facts:** Eight film distributors all received a letter from a theater manager demanding price increases and restrictions, and all eight simultaneously complied despite no direct evidence of a meeting between them.
*   **Doctrines:** ["chain-conspiracy", "inferring-agreement"]

### 5. Kotteakos v. United States
*   **Lines:** [346, 377]
*   **Holding:** A "wheel without a rim"—where multiple parties deal with a common agent but have no knowledge of or stake in each other's dealings—constitutes multiple separate conspiracies, not a single conspiracy.
*   **Facts:** Thirty-two defendants were charged in a single conspiracy for obtaining fraudulent loans through the same broker, though the borrowers were independent of one another.
*   **Doctrines:** ["wheel-and-rim-conspiracy", "coconspirator-hearsay-exception"]

### 6. United States v. Alvarez
*   **Lines:** [379, 386]
*   **Holding:** A defendant's nod, smile, and question about a flight, combined with assisting in loading/unloading, can be sufficient to infer agreement to join a conspiracy.
*   **Facts:** A man at an airport nodded and smiled when asked if he would help unload a plane arriving with marijuana and then helped unload household appliances from a truck.
*   **Doctrines:** ["inferring-agreement"]

*   **Citation:** MPC § 5.03
*   **Lines:** [36, 45]
