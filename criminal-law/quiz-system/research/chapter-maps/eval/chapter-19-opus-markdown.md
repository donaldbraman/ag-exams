## Doctrines

### 1. conspiracy-agreement
- **Rule:** Conspiracy requires an agreement between two or more persons to commit a crime; the agreement need not be express and can be inferred from coordinated conduct.
- **Split:** Traditional approach allows broad inference from parallel conduct; MPC requires actual agreement.
- **Levers:** Presence of explicit communication, parallel conduct with no innocent explanation, awareness of others' participation, circumstantial evidence chain (phone records, financial flows, cell data).
- **Traps:** Students [choose to acquit because no single piece of evidence proves agreement] because [they evaluate each piece of circumstantial evidence in isolation rather than considering the cumulative inference from coordinated conduct].
- **Cases:** Interstate Circuit v. United States, Kotteakos v. United States, United States v. Alvarez
- **Lines:** [[19, 27], [55, 57], [315, 317], [319, 344], [346, 392]]
- **Block count:** 5
- **Cross-chapter:** attempt-agreement (Ch. 17)
- **Exam lens:** both
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - Two suppliers independently raise prices after receiving the same letter naming both as addressees; test whether parallel conduct plus mutual awareness suffices for agreement.
  - A nod and a smile at a drug loading site; test the minimum conduct from which agreement can be inferred (Alvarez scenario).
  - Phone records show a chain of calls spiking around key events, but each individual call has a plausible innocent explanation; test cumulative inference.

### 2. overt-act-requirement
- **Rule:** Most jurisdictions require at least one conspirator to commit an overt act in furtherance of the conspiracy, but the act can be entirely legal and trivial -- far less than the "substantial step" required for attempt.
- **Split:** Federal law (18 U.S.C. Section 371) requires an overt act; some federal statutes (e.g., drug conspiracy under 21 U.S.C. Section 846) do not; MPC requires overt act except for first- and second-degree felonies.
- **Levers:** Nature of the act (legal vs. illegal), how remote from the target crime, whether any conspirator performed it.
- **Traps:** Students [require a substantial step for conspiracy] because [they confuse the overt act requirement for conspiracy with the substantial step requirement for attempt].
- **Cases:** Hyde v. United States
- **Lines:** [[61, 61]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** substantial-step-test (Ch. 17)
- **Exam lens:** prosecution
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - Conspirator purchases a plane ticket to the city where the target lives but takes no further action; test whether this trivial legal act satisfies the overt act requirement.
  - One conspirator makes a phone call arranging a meeting while another does nothing; test whether a single conspirator's overt act satisfies the requirement for all.

### 3. bilateral-vs-unilateral-conspiracy
- **Rule:** Under the bilateral approach, conspiracy requires at least two genuinely agreeing minds; under the unilateral approach (MPC), it is sufficient that the defendant believed the agreement was genuine and acted with purpose to commit the crime.
- **Split:** Traditional/common law jurisdictions follow the bilateral rule; MPC and a majority of reformed state codes follow the unilateral rule.
- **Levers:** Whether coconspirator is undercover agent, whether defendant believed agreement was genuine, whether there was any genuine group danger.
- **Traps:** Students [find no conspiracy when one party is an undercover agent] because [they apply the bilateral rule without recognizing that the majority of reformed jurisdictions use the unilateral approach].
- **Cases:** State v. Pacheco, Garcia v. State, United States v. Valigura
- **Lines:** [[63, 73]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** entrapment (Ch. 17)
- **Exam lens:** both
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - Defendant agrees with an undercover officer to rob a bank and takes preparatory steps; test bilateral vs. unilateral outcomes.
  - Defendant believes she is conspiring with a genuine criminal but her only coconspirator is a government informant who feigns agreement; test whether defendant's culpability depends on facts unknown to her.
  - Two genuine criminals agree, but one secretly plans to tip off police; test whether this is distinguishable from the undercover agent scenario.

### 4. conspiracy-mens-rea-purpose
- **Rule:** Conspiracy requires purpose (specific intent) to agree and purpose that the object crime be committed; most jurisdictions require purpose even if the underlying crime would be satisfied by a lesser mental state.
- **Split:** Some jurisdictions debate whether conspirators must intend every element of the target offense or just the conduct elements.
- **Levers:** Whether the defendant intended every element of the target crime, distinction between specific intent as to agreement vs. as to target offense elements.
- **Traps:** Students [apply the mens rea of the target offense to the conspiracy charge] because [they fail to recognize that conspiracy elevates the mens rea requirement to purpose for all elements of the target crime].
- **Cases:** State v. Pond
- **Lines:** [[59, 59], [75, 136]]
- **Block count:** 2
- **Cross-chapter:** mens-rea-hierarchy (Ch. 5)
- **Exam lens:** defense
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - Three people agree to rob a store; one displays what appears to be a weapon without prior discussion; test whether the others conspired to commit armed robbery.
  - Defendant agrees to participate in a scheme that turns out to constitute securities fraud; test whether conspiracy to commit a general intent crime requires specific intent to agree.

### 5. conspiracy-to-target-offense-elements
- **Rule:** To be convicted of conspiracy to commit a specific offense, the defendant must specifically intend that every element of the planned offense be accomplished, including aggravating elements that carry no specific intent requirement for the principal.
- **Split:** Majority (Pond) requires specific intent as to all elements; dissent argues this creates an irrational gap by requiring higher mens rea for conspiracy than for the completed offense.
- **Levers:** Whether an aggravating element (e.g., weapon display) was discussed or planned, whether the element was foreseeable, whether the defendant knew about the aggravating factor.
- **Traps:** Students [convict the conspirator of the aggravated offense because the weapon was used during the crime] because [they confuse what suffices for the principal (no specific intent as to weapon) with what is required for the conspirator (specific intent as to every element)].
- **Cases:** State v. Pond, People v. Koenig
- **Lines:** [[79, 136]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** accomplice-mens-rea (Ch. 18)
- **Exam lens:** defense
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - Defendant agrees to a simple robbery; his partner unexpectedly brandishes a knife; test whether defendant is guilty of conspiracy to commit armed robbery.
  - Defendant agrees to help a friend "scare" someone; friend kills the victim; test the Pond principle against conspiracy to commit murder.
  - Defendant conspires to commit a general intent crime (e.g., battery); test whether conspiracy can attach to general intent crimes per Koenig.

### 6. knowledge-vs-purpose-supplier
- **Rule:** A supplier who knows a customer is using lawful goods or services for criminal purposes is not a conspirator unless the supplier acts with the purpose of furthering the criminal enterprise; purpose may be inferred from (1) a stake in the venture, (2) the absence of legitimate use, or (3) the aggravated nature of the crime.
- **Split:** All jurisdictions require more than knowledge, but they differ on what inferential bridges suffice.
- **Levers:** Whether supplier has a financial stake beyond normal fees, whether supplier charges inflated prices, whether goods have legitimate uses, gravity of the crime (felony vs. misdemeanor), whether supplier took affirmative steps to facilitate crime.
- **Traps:** Students [convict the knowing supplier of conspiracy] because [they treat knowledge of criminal use as equivalent to purpose to further the criminal enterprise].
- **Cases:** People v. Lauria, United States v. Falcone
- **Lines:** [[146, 241]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** accomplice-knowledge-vs-purpose (Ch. 18)
- **Exam lens:** both
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - A landlord rents an apartment knowing the tenant deals drugs; the landlord charges market-rate rent; test whether knowledge alone suffices.
  - A chemical supplier sells precursors at a 300% markup to a buyer the supplier knows is manufacturing methamphetamine; test the stake-in-the-venture inference.
  - A web hosting company knows a client's site facilitates sex trafficking (an aggravated crime); test the gravity inference.

### 7. pinkerton-vicarious-liability
- **Rule:** Under the Pinkerton doctrine, each conspirator is liable for all foreseeable substantive crimes committed by coconspirators in furtherance of the conspiracy, without proof of the traditional elements of accomplice liability.
- **Split:** Federal courts and some states follow Pinkerton; MPC and a majority of reformed state codes reject it, requiring traditional accomplice liability elements instead.
- **Levers:** Whether the substantive offense was in furtherance of the conspiracy, whether it was reasonably foreseeable as a natural consequence, whether the defendant had withdrawn.
- **Traps:** Students [limit Pinkerton liability to crimes the defendant specifically agreed to] because [they confuse the Pinkerton foreseeability standard with the narrower requirement of actual agreement or intent].
- **Cases:** Pinkerton v. United States
- **Lines:** [[243, 313]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** accomplice-liability (Ch. 18), attempt-liability (Ch. 17)
- **Exam lens:** both
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - A joins a drug conspiracy; coconspirator B kills a rival dealer during a turf dispute; test whether the murder was foreseeable.
  - Daniel Pinkerton is in prison when Walter commits substantive offenses; test whether incarceration constitutes withdrawal.
  - A conspirator agrees only to drive a getaway car for a robbery; during the robbery a coconspirator assaults a bystander; test foreseeability limits.

### 8. mpc-rejection-of-pinkerton
- **Rule:** Under the MPC, a conspirator can only be held liable for coconspirators' crimes if traditional accomplice liability elements are met (purpose to promote or facilitate the specific offense), not merely through conspiracy membership and foreseeability.
- **Split:** MPC approach (majority of reformed states) vs. traditional Pinkerton approach (federal courts, unreformed states).
- **Levers:** Whether the defendant actually aided, abetted, or encouraged the specific offense, whether the defendant had purpose as to the specific crime.
- **Traps:** Students [apply Pinkerton liability in an MPC jurisdiction] because [they forget that the MPC explicitly rejects vicarious liability through conspiracy and requires accomplice-liability elements].
- **Cases:** Pinkerton v. United States (by contrast)
- **Lines:** [[299, 305], [474, 480]]
- **Block count:** 2
- **Cross-chapter:** accomplice-liability (Ch. 18)
- **Exam lens:** defense
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - In an MPC jurisdiction, A and B conspire to rob a bank; B shoots the guard; test whether A is liable for the shooting absent evidence A aided or encouraged it.
  - Compare outcomes for the same bank robbery hypothetical under Pinkerton vs. MPC.

### 9. single-vs-multiple-conspiracies
- **Rule:** The distinction between a single conspiracy and multiple separate conspiracies determines joinder, hearsay admissibility, and the scope of Pinkerton liability; a single conspiracy requires a common purpose linking all participants, not merely a shared hub figure.
- **Split:** null
- **Levers:** Whether participants knew of each other, whether there was a "rim" connecting spokes of the wheel, whether participants shared a common objective beyond individual transactions.
- **Traps:** Students [find a single conspiracy whenever there is a common intermediary] because [they confuse a hub-and-spoke structure (wheel without a rim) with a single unified conspiracy (wheel with a rim)].
- **Cases:** Kotteakos v. United States, Interstate Circuit v. United States
- **Lines:** [[315, 317], [346, 391]]
- **Block count:** 2
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** defense
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - A lawyer helps ten different clients file fraudulent tax returns; none of the clients know each other; test whether this is one conspiracy or ten.
  - A drug supplier sells to five independent dealers who know of each other and coordinate territory; test whether mutual awareness creates the "rim."
  - A broker helps multiple borrowers defraud a federal program; some borrowers know each other and coordinate, others do not; test a mixed wheel-with-partial-rim scenario.

### 10. tacit-agreement-inference
- **Rule:** Agreement can be inferred from parallel conduct when each party knew concerted action was contemplated and invited, knew cooperation was essential, and participated in the scheme -- even without direct evidence of communication among the parties.
- **Split:** null
- **Levers:** Whether each party knew others were invited to participate, whether conduct represented a radical departure from prior practice, whether the conduct is explainable by independent self-interest.
- **Traps:** Students [require direct evidence of communication between conspirators] because [they fail to recognize that Interstate Circuit allows inference of agreement from conscious parallelism plus mutual awareness].
- **Cases:** Interstate Circuit v. United States
- **Lines:** [[319, 344]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** prosecution
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - Five competing gas stations all raise prices on the same day after each receives a letter from a major customer naming all five; test whether parallel conduct plus mutual awareness supports an inference of agreement.
  - Three competing bidders all submit identical bids after learning each other's identities; test conscious parallelism.

### 11. buyer-seller-exception
- **Rule:** A mere agreement to purchase drugs for personal use or resale does not establish a conspiracy; conspiracy requires evidence of a shared purpose to further distribution, indicated by hallmarks such as prolonged dealings, coded language, and credit arrangements like fronting.
- **Split:** null
- **Levers:** Duration of the relationship, use of coded language, credit arrangements (fronting), volume and frequency of transactions, whether buyer participated in distribution.
- **Traps:** Students [find conspiracy whenever a buyer purchases drugs from a seller] because [they fail to apply the buyer-seller exception and instead treat any drug transaction as a conspiratorial agreement].
- **Cases:** United States v. Pauling
- **Lines:** [[393, 396]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** defense
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - A buyer purchases drugs weekly from the same dealer for personal use over two years; test whether longevity alone moves the relationship from buyer-seller to conspiracy.
  - A dealer fronts product to a buyer who resells it and returns the proceeds; test whether credit arrangements convert a buyer-seller relationship into a conspiracy.

### 12. whartons-rule
- **Rule:** An agreement between two persons to commit a crime that by definition requires two participants (e.g., dueling, bigamy, bribery) cannot be prosecuted as a conspiracy; the rule is a judicial presumption that legislatures can override and does not apply when more persons participate than the crime logically requires.
- **Split:** The Supreme Court treats it as a presumption, not a constitutional bar; legislatures can override it.
- **Levers:** Whether the substantive crime inherently requires two participants, whether more people participated than necessary, whether the legislature has specifically authorized conspiracy charges.
- **Traps:** Students [apply Wharton's Rule to bar conspiracy charges whenever exactly two people commit a bilateral crime] because [they treat the rule as absolute rather than as an overridable judicial presumption].
- **Cases:** Iannelli v. United States
- **Lines:** [[397, 401]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** defense
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - Two people agree to engage in an illegal duel; test whether Wharton's Rule bars a conspiracy charge.
  - A bribery scheme involves the bribe-payer, the bribe-recipient, and a go-between; test whether the third participant takes the case outside Wharton's Rule.

### 13. legislative-exemption-gebardi
- **Rule:** When a statute deliberately exempts one class of participants from criminal liability, that class cannot be prosecuted for conspiring to commit the offense; the legislature's protective intent cannot be circumvented through a conspiracy charge.
- **Split:** null
- **Levers:** Whether the statute explicitly or implicitly exempts a class of participants, whether the exempted party's participation is an inseparable incident of the crime.
- **Traps:** Students [convict the exempted party of conspiracy even though the statute exempts them from the substantive offense] because [they fail to recognize that the legislative exemption extends to conspiracy charges under the Gebardi principle].
- **Cases:** Gebardi v. United States
- **Lines:** [[402, 407]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** defense
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - A statutory rape victim (the minor) voluntarily travels across state lines with the defendant; test whether the minor can be charged with conspiracy to violate a statute that protects them.
  - A human trafficking victim who cooperates with her trafficker; test whether the Gebardi principle shields the victim from conspiracy charges under a statute designed to protect trafficking victims.

### 14. corporate-conspiracy
- **Rule:** Corporations can be charged with conspiracy because they act through their agents, and coordination among employees and entities makes conspiracy a natural fit for corporate prosecutions; however, conspiracy charges against corporate executives remain rare and often result in penalties disproportionate to harm.
- **Split:** null
- **Levers:** Whether individual executives can be shown to have personally agreed to the criminal objective, whether the corporation as entity admitted to conspiracy while shielding individuals, institutional factors that explain non-prosecution of executives.
- **Traps:** Students [assume corporate conspiracy convictions necessarily establish individual executive liability] because [they confuse entity-level guilt with individual criminal responsibility].
- **Cases:** Purdue Pharma (DOJ plea)
- **Lines:** [[409, 439]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** prosecution
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - A pharmaceutical company admits to conspiracy to defraud regulators, but no individual executive is charged; test whether entity-level conspiracy establishes individual liability.
  - Internal emails show executives knew about illegal kickbacks; test what additional evidence would be needed to charge them individually with conspiracy.

### 15. manufactured-conspiracy-stings
- **Rule:** In reverse sting operations, the government supplies drugs and recruits participants, then charges defendants with conspiracy; under the unilateral approach, agreement with an undercover agent suffices for conspiracy even though no genuine criminal group existed.
- **Split:** Bilateral jurisdictions may not sustain conspiracy charges in pure sting scenarios; unilateral jurisdictions sustain them.
- **Levers:** Whether the government initiated the criminal plan, whether the defendant was predisposed, whether the government supplied the means, quantity attributed (triggering mandatory minimums).
- **Traps:** Students [dismiss sting-based conspiracy charges as entrapment] because [they confuse the manufactured conspiracy problem (which goes to the legitimacy of the prosecution) with the entrapment defense (which requires proving government inducement plus lack of predisposition)].
- **Cases:** (no named case; policy/empirical discussion)
- **Lines:** [[441, 459]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** entrapment (Ch. 17), substantial-step-test (Ch. 17)
- **Exam lens:** defense
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - An undercover agent proposes a drug deal, supplies the drugs, and arranges the transaction; the defendant agrees and shows up; test conspiracy liability under bilateral vs. unilateral approaches.
  - A peripheral participant (the "girlfriend") is swept into a conspiracy and sentenced based on total drug quantity she never handled; test the proportionality problem.

### 16. conspiracy-withdrawal
- **Rule:** A conspirator can terminate future liability by withdrawing from the conspiracy, but traditional law requires affirmative action to defeat the conspiracy's purpose plus communication to coconspirators; the MPC is more lenient, treating withdrawal as terminating future liability.
- **Split:** Traditional approach requires affirmative steps to thwart the conspiracy plus communication; MPC allows withdrawal to terminate future liability more easily.
- **Levers:** Whether the defendant took affirmative steps to defeat the conspiracy, whether the defendant communicated withdrawal to coconspirators, timing of the withdrawal relative to substantive offenses.
- **Traps:** Students [treat mere cessation of participation as withdrawal] because [they fail to recognize that traditional law requires affirmative action to thwart the conspiracy, not just stopping participation].
- **Cases:** Pinkerton v. United States
- **Lines:** [[261, 261], [480, 480]]
- **Block count:** 2
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** defense
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - A conspirator stops attending meetings and answering calls but never communicates withdrawal; test whether silence constitutes withdrawal.
  - A conspirator tips off the police about the planned crime; test whether this constitutes sufficient affirmative action to defeat the conspiracy.

### 17. conspiracy-cumulative-punishment
- **Rule:** Under traditional law, conspiracy and the completed substantive offense are separate crimes, permitting cumulative punishment; the MPC adopts a merger rule that bars cumulative punishment for conspiracy and the target offense.
- **Split:** Traditional (cumulative punishment permitted) vs. MPC (merger, no cumulative punishment).
- **Levers:** Whether the jurisdiction follows merger, whether the conspiracy had objectives beyond the completed offense.
- **Traps:** Students [assume conspiracy merges with the completed offense in all jurisdictions] because [they apply the MPC merger rule in traditional jurisdictions that permit cumulative punishment].
- **Cases:** (discussed in system architecture table)
- **Lines:** [[474, 480]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** merger-doctrine (Ch. 17)
- **Exam lens:** both
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - Defendant is convicted of both conspiracy to commit robbery and robbery itself; test whether cumulative sentencing is permitted under traditional vs. MPC approaches.

## Cases

### Dan Markel Murder (case study)
- **Lines:** [9, 31]
- **Holding:** Illustrative case study; no appellate holding extracted. Demonstrates all major conspiracy elements: inferring agreement from circumstantial evidence, scope of a single conspiracy, Pinkerton vicarious liability.
- **Facts:** Murder-for-hire plot in which Wendi Adelson's family allegedly recruited Sigfredo Garcia through intermediary Katherine Magbanua to kill Dan Markel during a custody dispute; agreement inferred from phone records, financial transfers, and cell tower data.
- **Doctrines:** [conspiracy-agreement, pinkerton-vicarious-liability, single-vs-multiple-conspiracies]

### Interstate Circuit v. United States
- **Lines:** [319, 344]
- **Holding:** Agreement among competitors can be inferred from conscious parallelism when each party knew concerted action was contemplated and invited, and each participated in the scheme.
- **Facts:** Film exhibitor sent identical letters to eight distributors, naming all as addressees, demanding coordinated restrictions on subsequent-run theaters; all distributors complied despite radical departure from prior practice.
- **Doctrines:** [conspiracy-agreement, tacit-agreement-inference, single-vs-multiple-conspiracies]

### Kotteakos v. United States
- **Lines:** [346, 377]
- **Holding:** Where a common figure (hub) deals separately with multiple groups who have no knowledge of or dealings with each other, the result is multiple separate conspiracies, not one single conspiracy; trying all defendants together on a single-conspiracy theory is prejudicial.
- **Facts:** Loan broker Simon Brown helped numerous unrelated borrowers obtain fraudulent FHA loans; borrowers did not know of each other or share a common purpose beyond individual loans.
- **Doctrines:** [conspiracy-agreement, single-vs-multiple-conspiracies]

### United States v. Alvarez
- **Lines:** [379, 386]
- **Holding:** A nod, a smile, and a promise to be present at the unloading site, combined with surrounding circumstances, was sufficient for a jury to infer agreement to participate in an importation conspiracy.
- **Facts:** Day laborer Alvarez drove a truck to an airport where others arranged a marijuana importation flight; he nodded, smiled, and asked if an undercover agent was going on the plane, then unloaded appliances.
- **Doctrines:** [conspiracy-agreement, tacit-agreement-inference]

### State v. Pond
- **Lines:** [79, 132]
- **Holding:** To be convicted of conspiracy, a defendant must specifically intend that every element of the target offense be accomplished, including elements that carry no specific intent requirement for the principal.
- **Facts:** Defendant served as lookout for a convenience store robbery; coconspirators implied they had a weapon during the robbery, but no prior discussion of weapon use occurred.
- **Doctrines:** [conspiracy-mens-rea-purpose, conspiracy-to-target-offense-elements]

### People v. Lauria
- **Lines:** [150, 193]
- **Holding:** A supplier who knows his goods or services are being used for criminal purposes is not guilty of conspiracy absent evidence of purpose to further the criminal enterprise, which can be inferred from a stake in the venture or the aggravated nature of the crime.
- **Facts:** Telephone answering service operator knowingly served prostitutes who used his service for business, but charged normal rates, had no financial stake in their earnings, and prostitution was a misdemeanor.
- **Doctrines:** [knowledge-vs-purpose-supplier]

### Pinkerton v. United States
- **Lines:** [247, 282]
- **Holding:** Each conspirator is liable for substantive crimes committed by coconspirators in furtherance of the conspiracy that were reasonably foreseeable, even without direct participation.
- **Facts:** Brothers Walter and Daniel Pinkerton conspired to commit revenue fraud; Walter committed substantive offenses while Daniel was in prison; Daniel was convicted of the substantive offenses based solely on the ongoing conspiracy.
- **Doctrines:** [pinkerton-vicarious-liability, conspiracy-withdrawal]

### Garcia v. State
- **Lines:** [69, 69]
- **Holding:** Under the unilateral approach, a defendant who agrees with a police informant/undercover detective to commit a crime is guilty of conspiracy regardless of the agent's feigned agreement.
- **Facts:** Woman asked a police informant to kill her husband, then paid an undercover detective $200 with a photograph and the husband's daily habits.
- **Doctrines:** [bilateral-vs-unilateral-conspiracy]

### State v. Pacheco
- **Lines:** [67, 67]
- **Holding:** Under the bilateral approach, if the defendant's only coconspirator is a government agent feigning agreement, there is no conspiracy.
- **Facts:** Referenced for the bilateral rule; details not excerpted in chapter.
- **Doctrines:** [bilateral-vs-unilateral-conspiracy]

### United States v. Valigura
- **Lines:** [73, 73]
- **Holding:** The unilateral theory "does nothing to further the public policy concern of preventing group criminal activity" when the coconspirator was undercover.
- **Facts:** Military conspiracy charge dismissed where coconspirator was undercover.
- **Doctrines:** [bilateral-vs-unilateral-conspiracy]

### United States v. Falcone
- **Lines:** [172, 172]
- **Holding:** A seller who knows goods are being used illegally is not guilty of conspiracy unless he had a "stake in the venture."
- **Facts:** Seller of goods to buyer using them for illegal purposes; referenced for the stake-in-the-venture test.
- **Doctrines:** [knowledge-vs-purpose-supplier]

### Gebardi v. United States
- **Lines:** [403, 406]
- **Holding:** When a statute deliberately exempts one party from liability, that party cannot be prosecuted for conspiring to violate the statute.
- **Facts:** Man and woman voluntarily traveled together across state lines; government charged both with conspiracy to violate the Mann Act, which was designed to protect the transported woman.
- **Doctrines:** [legislative-exemption-gebardi]

### Iannelli v. United States
- **Lines:** [401, 401]
- **Holding:** Wharton's Rule is a judicial presumption, not a constitutional command; legislatures can override it.
- **Facts:** Referenced for characterization of Wharton's Rule.
- **Doctrines:** [whartons-rule]

### Hyde v. United States
- **Lines:** [61, 61]
- **Holding:** Holmes dissent: "it does not matter how remote the act may be from accomplishing the purpose, if done to effect it" -- establishing the minimal nature of the overt act requirement.
- **Facts:** Referenced for Holmes's observation about overt acts.
- **Doctrines:** [overt-act-requirement]

### Krulewitch v. United States
- **Lines:** [487, 489]
- **Holding:** Jackson concurrence warning that conspiracy trials create dangers of guilt by association: "birds of a feather are flocked together."
- **Facts:** Referenced for Jackson's warning about the prejudicial nature of conspiracy trials.
- **Doctrines:** [single-vs-multiple-conspiracies]

### United States v. Pauling
- **Lines:** [395, 396]
- **Holding:** A mere buyer-seller relationship does not constitute a conspiracy; conspiracy requires evidence of shared purpose to further distribution, shown by hallmarks like prolonged dealing, coded language, and fronting.
- **Facts:** Referenced for the buyer-seller exception rule.
- **Doctrines:** [buyer-seller-exception]

### People v. Koenig
- **Lines:** [136, 136]
- **Holding:** A defendant can conspire to commit a general intent crime because specific intent to agree and commit the prohibited conduct is sufficient; but conspiracy cannot attach to crimes of negligence.
- **Facts:** Securities fraud case; court held conspiracy applies to general intent crimes but not negligence crimes.
- **Doctrines:** [conspiracy-to-target-offense-elements]

### Purdue Pharma (DOJ Plea)
- **Lines:** [413, 431]
- **Holding:** Purdue pleaded guilty to three felony conspiracy counts (defrauding the U.S. and anti-kickback violations) with $3.544 billion fine and $2 billion forfeiture, but no individual executive was convicted of conspiracy or any felony.
- **Facts:** From 2007-2017, Purdue misrepresented its anti-diversion program to the DEA, marketed opioids to providers it had reason to believe were diverting, and paid kickbacks to doctors through its speaker program.
- **Doctrines:** [corporate-conspiracy]
