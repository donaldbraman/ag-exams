## Doctrines

### 1. conspiracy-agreement-element
- **Rule:** Conspiracy requires an agreement between two or more persons to commit a crime; the agreement need not be express and may be inferred from coordinated conduct, but the inference must be supported by evidence that collectively excludes innocent explanation.
- **Split:** None on the inference rule itself, but jurisdictions split on how much coordination is sufficient; *Alvarez* (en banc) extended inference to a nod and a smile while a nine-judge dissent protested.
- **Levers:** Whether conduct has an innocent explanation; whether communication patterns are circumstantially linked to key criminal events; whether financial flows track the coordination chain; whether parties knew they were being asked to join a scheme others were also joining.
- **Traps:** Students say there is no conspiracy because there was no explicit agreement because they confuse the requirement of agreement with the requirement of an express or documented agreement; the law allows agreement to be inferred entirely from circumstantial evidence.
- **Cases:** Interstate Circuit v. United States, Kotteakos v. United States, United States v. Alvarez, Pinkerton v. United States (Dan Markel narrative)
- **Lines:** [[0, 30], [315, 395]]
- **Block count:** 2
- **Cross-chapter:** substantial-step-test (Ch. 17)
- **Exam lens:** both

**Hypo seeds:**
  - Two employees at a company exchange encrypted messages that spike around the dates of a series of fraudulent transactions; no message says "let's commit fraud." Is the pattern sufficient for conspiracy?
  - A defendant is present at every meeting of a drug trafficking crew but never speaks. Cell data places her near three distribution sites. Is silence plus presence enough for agreement?
  - A warehouse manager signs off on shipments she knows are mislabeled but claims she thought it was a tax matter, not drug trafficking. Does her awareness of the criminality satisfy agreement?

### 2. conspiracy-mens-rea-purpose
- **Rule:** Conspiracy requires the defendant to act with *purpose* — not merely knowledge — that the object crime be committed; even if the underlying offense requires only a lesser mental state, the conspirator must specifically intend to promote or facilitate the crime.
- **Split:** Most jurisdictions require purpose; some follow a knowledge standard for the agreement element while requiring purpose only as to the object crime.
- **Levers:** Whether the defendant had a financial stake in the criminal enterprise; whether the defendant took steps beyond a routine commercial transaction; whether the crime is of an aggravated or serious nature.
- **Traps:** Students say knowledge of criminal use is enough to establish conspiracy because they confuse the knowledge element (a prerequisite) with the purpose element (the dispositive mental state); *Lauria* holds knowledge is necessary but not sufficient.
- **Cases:** People v. Lauria, State v. Pond
- **Lines:** [[58, 72], [145, 206]]
- **Block count:** 2
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** both

**Hypo seeds:**
  - A gun shop owner sells ammunition to a customer, knowing the customer has threatened to shoot his neighbor. Is knowledge sufficient for conspiracy to commit murder?
  - A cryptocurrency exchange processes transactions it knows are from a sanctioned drug marketplace but charges standard fees and has thousands of legitimate customers. Under *Lauria*, is this conspiracy?
  - A rideshare driver regularly picks up customers at known drug houses and drives them to meet buyers. He knows what they're doing. He charges normal fares. Is he a conspirator?

### 3. conspiracy-specific-intent-target-elements
- **Rule:** To conspire to commit a crime, a defendant must specifically intend that *every element* of the target offense be accomplished, including elements that carry no specific intent requirement for the principal who actually commits the crime.
- **Split:** *Pond* majority (Connecticut) requires specific intent to all elements; dissent argues this creates an irrational gap by requiring higher mental state for inchoate offense than for the completed crime; MPC aligns with majority view.
- **Levers:** Whether the aggravating element of the target offense (e.g., display of a weapon) was discussed in the conspiratorial planning; whether the defendant had reason to anticipate the element would be present.
- **Traps:** Students say the conspirator only needs the same mental state as would be required for the completed offense because they confuse the mental state for the substantive crime with the separately-analyzed mental state required for the conspiracy charge; *Pond* holds conspiracy imposes a *higher* (purpose) standard as to all elements.
- **Cases:** State v. Pond, People v. Koenig
- **Lines:** [[74, 135]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** prosecution

**Hypo seeds:**
  - A defendant agrees to rob a store but specifically says "no guns." His co-conspirator brings a gun anyway. Is the defendant guilty of conspiracy to commit armed robbery?
  - Two defendants plan a burglary; one intends only to take property, while the other secretly plans to assault any occupant encountered. Is the first defendant guilty of conspiring to commit aggravated assault?
  - Defendant agrees to help with a fraud that involves mailing materials. Mailing makes it federal mail fraud. Did defendant conspire to commit mail fraud if she didn't know the scheme would use the mail?

### 4. lauria-supplier-intent-inference
- **Rule:** A supplier of goods or services who knows a customer is using them criminally can be held as a conspirator only if intent to participate is shown by: (1) direct evidence of intent, (2) a special interest in the criminal enterprise (e.g., profit-sharing or inflated prices), or (3) the aggravated nature of the crime from which intent can be inferred by knowledge alone.
- **Split:** Courts disagree on how "aggravated" a crime must be to trigger the knowledge-equals-intent inference; misdemeanors clearly do not qualify; serious felonies generally do.
- **Levers:** Whether the supplier's pricing was inflated above market rate; whether the supplier had a financial stake in the criminal proceeds; gravity of the crime being facilitated; whether the service was designed or advertised for criminal use; whether the supplier had legitimate customers.
- **Traps:** Students say Lauria is guilty of conspiracy because he knew the prostitutes were using his service for illegal purposes because they confuse proof of knowledge with proof of intent to participate; *Lauria* holds knowledge alone is insufficient absent a stake, inflated pricing, or an aggravated crime.
- **Cases:** People v. Lauria
- **Lines:** [[147, 239]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** both

**Hypo seeds:**
  - A web hosting provider hosts a dark-web marketplace at 10x normal rates and knows its only tenants are drug vendors. Is it a co-conspirator?
  - A shipping company charges standard rates, knows one client ships counterfeit goods, and continues service. Does knowledge plus the federal counterfeiting statute (a felony) satisfy Lauria's aggravated-crime inference?
  - A platform earns ad revenue from algorithmically amplified criminal content and has internal documents showing employee awareness of child exploitation material. Under *Lauria*'s framework, does ad revenue constitute a "stake in the venture"?

### 5. bilateral-unilateral-conspiracy
- **Rule:** Under the bilateral approach, conspiracy requires at least two genuinely agreeing minds, so a defendant cannot be convicted of conspiracy if the only "coconspirator" is an undercover agent who never intends to follow through; under the MPC unilateral approach, the defendant's own belief in a genuine agreement and purpose to commit the crime is sufficient.
- **Split:** Traditional jurisdictions follow the bilateral rule; the MPC and a majority of reformed state codes adopt unilateral; federal circuits mostly follow bilateral for completed conspiracy but allow unilateral in attempt-to-conspire.
- **Levers:** Whether the defendant's only "partner" was a government agent; whether there were other genuine coconspirators besides the agent.
- **Traps:** Students say there is no conspiracy when the government is involved because they confuse the bilateral rule (which most reformed codes have abandoned) with the current majority rule; under MPC unilateral approach the defendant's own criminal purpose is what counts.
- **Cases:** State v. Pacheco, Garcia v. State, United States v. Valigura
- **Lines:** [[62, 72], [441, 495]]
- **Block count:** 2
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** defense

**Hypo seeds:**
  - A defendant negotiates a drug deal entirely with an undercover officer posing as a supplier. No real drugs ever exist. In a unilateral jurisdiction, is this conspiracy?
  - In a bilateral jurisdiction, a defendant agrees with an informant to commit arson. The informant immediately reports to police. Is there a conspiracy?
  - A defendant in a unilateral jurisdiction arranges with one genuine partner and two undercover agents to rob a bank. Does the presence of the genuine partner cure the bilateral defect for all purposes, or only as between the defendant and the genuine partner?

### 6. overt-act-requirement
- **Rule:** Most jurisdictions require at least one conspirator to have committed an overt act in furtherance of the conspiracy, but this requirement is minimal — any act, however legal or trivial, suffices if done to advance the conspiracy; the MPC exempts first- and second-degree felony conspiracies from the overt act requirement.
- **Split:** Federal law (18 U.S.C. § 371) requires an overt act; the MPC requires an overt act only for lower-grade felonies and misdemeanors; the common law historically did not require an overt act.
- **Levers:** Whether the act was done with intent to further the conspiracy; whether any member committed any act (does not have to be the defendant).
- **Traps:** Students say conspiracy requires a substantial step like attempt because they confuse the overt act threshold for conspiracy (any act, however trivial) with the more demanding substantial step test for attempt; purchasing a plane ticket or making a phone call is enough.
- **Cases:** Hyde v. United States (Holmes dissent cited), MPC § 5.03(5)
- **Lines:** [[37, 60], [43, 52]]
- **Block count:** 2
- **Cross-chapter:** substantial-step-test (Ch. 17)
- **Exam lens:** prosecution

**Hypo seeds:**
  - Three co-conspirators agree to commit a bank robbery. One drives past the bank once to look at it. Is the overt act requirement satisfied?
  - In a first-degree felony conspiracy in an MPC jurisdiction, no overt act is committed before arrest. Can the conspiracy charge proceed?
  - A conspirator makes a single phone call to confirm the meeting place. The call itself is legal. Does this satisfy the overt act requirement in a § 371 federal prosecution?

### 7. pinkerton-vicarious-liability
- **Rule:** Under the *Pinkerton* doctrine, each member of a conspiracy is criminally liable for all substantive offenses committed by any coconspirator in furtherance of the conspiracy that were reasonably foreseeable as a necessary or natural consequence of the unlawful agreement, without requiring proof of the traditional elements of accomplice liability.
- **Split:** The *Pinkerton* doctrine governs federal practice and many states; the MPC and most reformed state codes reject it, requiring traditional accomplice liability elements (actual aid, encouragement, or direction) to hold one conspirator liable for another's substantive crime.
- **Levers:** Whether the offense was committed in furtherance of the conspiracy; whether the offense was reasonably foreseeable; whether the defendant had withdrawn from the conspiracy before the offense.
- **Traps:** Students say a peripheral conspirator is not liable for a crime committed by another because they require proof that the defendant personally participated in that specific act; under *Pinkerton*, foreseeability and furtherance of the conspiracy are sufficient — no direct participation required.
- **Cases:** Pinkerton v. United States
- **Lines:** [[28, 31], [242, 313]]
- **Block count:** 2
- **Cross-chapter:** accomplice-liability (Ch. 18)
- **Exam lens:** prosecution

**Hypo seeds:**
  - A drug conspiracy ringleader is in prison when his co-conspirators commit a violent robbery to collect a drug debt. He never knew the robbery would happen. Is he liable under *Pinkerton*?
  - A low-level drug courier joins a distribution conspiracy. The ringleader also conspires to launder money. Is the courier liable for money laundering?
  - In an MPC jurisdiction, a conspirator's co-member shoots a bystander while fleeing from a robbery they planned together. Is the conspirator liable for assault on the bystander?

### 8. pinkerton-mpc-rejection
- **Rule:** The MPC rejects the *Pinkerton* doctrine; under MPC § 2.06, a conspirator can only be held liable for a coconspirator's substantive offense if the traditional elements of accomplice liability are met — i.e., the defendant must have actually aided, abetted, or encouraged the specific offense with the purpose to promote or facilitate it.
- **Split:** MPC vs. federal/traditional common law; most reformed state codes follow MPC, making this a common CL/MPC comparison issue.
- **Levers:** Whether the defendant specifically aided the offense vs. merely belonged to a conspiracy; whether the defendant had purpose to facilitate the specific crime.
- **Traps:** Students say MPC still allows Pinkerton liability because conspiracy is a theory of group responsibility because they confuse the traditional doctrine with the MPC's deliberate rejection of it; under MPC, conspiracy membership alone never suffices for substantive liability.
- **Cases:** Pinkerton v. United States (rejected by MPC § 2.06)
- **Lines:** [[298, 305]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** accomplice-liability (Ch. 18)
- **Exam lens:** defense

**Hypo seeds:**
  - In an MPC state, defendant agreed to participate in a drug conspiracy but only drove the getaway car for one robbery. A different co-conspirator commits a separate murder. Is defendant liable for murder under MPC?
  - In a federal *Pinkerton* jurisdiction, same facts. Result?
  - A defendant in a reformed MPC state conspired to commit fraud. A co-conspirator separately commits extortion to silence a potential witness. Under MPC § 2.06, what must the prosecution prove to hold defendant liable for extortion?

### 9. single-vs-multiple-conspiracies
- **Rule:** A single conspiracy requires that all participants share a common purpose and mutual awareness of the overall scheme; a hub-and-spoke arrangement where a central figure deals separately with multiple parties who have no knowledge of or dealings with each other constitutes multiple conspiracies, not one, unless there is a "rim" connecting the spokes.
- **Split:** No doctrinal split, but courts disagree on how much mutual awareness is required to form the rim; *Interstate Circuit* found a rim from the structure of the letter; *Kotteakos* found no rim where borrowers never knew of each other.
- **Levers:** Whether participants knew other conspirators existed; whether all had a shared stake in a common criminal objective; whether evidence of one group's transactions was used to convict members of unrelated groups.
- **Traps:** Students say all participants in a scheme involving the same central actor are part of one conspiracy because they confuse participation in related transactions with participation in a single agreement; *Kotteakos* holds that separate conspiracies with the same central figure do not merge into one absent mutual awareness.
- **Cases:** Interstate Circuit v. United States, Kotteakos v. United States, United States v. Alvarez
- **Lines:** [[315, 395]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** defense

**Hypo seeds:**
  - A loan broker helps 10 different clients fraudulently obtain loans. Each client knows only about their own loan. Prosecution charges one conspiracy. Valid?
  - A drug kingpin supplies heroin to five independent street dealers who have no knowledge of each other. Is this one conspiracy or five?
  - A manufacturer distributes through five regional distributors. Each distributor agrees separately with the manufacturer to fix retail prices. Do they know others made the same deal? If so, is it one conspiracy?

### 10. conspiracy-procedural-advantages
- **Rule:** When defendants are charged in a single conspiracy, several procedural advantages flow to the prosecution: co-conspirators may be tried jointly, statements made by any conspirator in furtherance of the conspiracy are admissible against all under the co-conspirator hearsay exception, venue is proper wherever any overt act occurred, and *Pinkerton* liability attaches.
- **Split:** None on the doctrine; disputes arise over whether a single or multiple conspiracy exists, which determines whether these advantages apply.
- **Levers:** Whether defendants are part of one conspiracy or separate ones; whether statements were made in furtherance of the conspiracy; how broadly venue is defined.
- **Traps:** Students say co-conspirator statements are automatically admissible against all defendants in a joint trial because they confuse the basis for admissibility (furtherance of the conspiracy) with the mere fact of a joint trial; the co-conspirator exception requires the statement to be made in furtherance of the conspiracy.
- **Cases:** Kotteakos v. United States (prejudice from single-conspiracy charge)
- **Lines:** [[354, 391]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** prosecution

**Hypo seeds:**
  - A drug trafficker makes a statement to a supplier identifying other members of the distribution chain. At trial of those members, the prosecution offers the statement. Is it admissible?
  - Two defendants claim they were in separate conspiracies with the same broker. The prosecution charged one conspiracy and tried them together. What is the standard for determining prejudicial variance?
  - A conspiracy originates in New York; overt acts occur in California and Texas. Where is venue proper?

### 11. whartons-rule
- **Rule:** Wharton's Rule provides that an agreement between two persons to commit a crime that by definition requires the participation of two persons cannot be separately prosecuted as a conspiracy; however, the Rule is a rebuttable judicial presumption that does not apply when more persons participate than the offense logically requires, and legislatures may override it.
- **Split:** Wharton's Rule is recognized broadly but treated as a judicial presumption, not a constitutional rule (*Iannelli v. United States*), so its application varies by jurisdiction and offense.
- **Levers:** Whether more persons participated than the minimum required by the offense; whether the legislature intended to permit a conspiracy charge; the specific offense charged.
- **Traps:** Students say all crimes requiring two participants are immune from conspiracy charges because they confuse the presumption with an absolute bar; if additional parties participate beyond the minimum required for the offense, conspiracy may be charged.
- **Cases:** Iannelli v. United States
- **Lines:** [[397, 407]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** defense

**Hypo seeds:**
  - A, B, and C agree to engage in a duel between A and B. Only two persons are required for dueling. Can all three be charged with conspiracy to commit dueling?
  - Two persons agree to commit bribery: one to offer, one to receive. Is this prosecutable as conspiracy under Wharton's Rule?
  - A state legislature passes a statute expressly providing that bribery conspiracies may be prosecuted notwithstanding Wharton's Rule. Does the Rule still apply?

### 12. gebardi-legislative-exemption
- **Rule:** When the legislature defines a crime in terms that protect or exempt one class of participants from liability, that class cannot be prosecuted for conspiring to commit the offense; congressional silence about punishing that class is treated as a deliberate choice that the conspiracy statute may not circumvent.
- **Split:** No general split; applied case-by-case depending on whether legislative intent to exempt a class is sufficiently clear.
- **Levers:** Whether the legislature expressly exempted the class; whether the legislative history supports an inference of deliberate exclusion; whether the defendant's participation is the "inseparable incident" of the offense.
- **Traps:** Students say any person who participates in planning an offense can be charged with conspiracy because they confuse the general rule (anyone who agrees can conspire) with the *Gebardi* exception (the protected class is off-limits even as a co-conspirator).
- **Cases:** Gebardi v. United States
- **Lines:** [[401, 408]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** defense

**Hypo seeds:**
  - A minor is the victim of statutory rape under a law that explicitly does not punish the minor. Can she be charged with conspiring to commit statutory rape by agreeing to the encounter?
  - A statute prohibits employers from hiring undocumented workers but does not criminalize the worker's conduct. Can an undocumented worker be charged with conspiring with her employer to violate the statute?
  - The Mann Act punishes transportation for immoral purposes. The transported woman agrees to travel voluntarily. Can she be charged with conspiracy?

### 13. buyer-seller-exception
- **Rule:** A mere buyer-seller relationship does not establish a drug distribution conspiracy; a conspiracy requires evidence of a shared purpose to further drug distribution, marked by indicia such as a prolonged course of dealing, coded language, or "fronting" (credit arrangements that reflect mutual investment in continued distribution).
- **Split:** Recognized widely in federal circuits and many state courts; courts disagree on what combination of indicia is sufficient to move past buyer-seller into conspiracy.
- **Levers:** Whether the relationship was episodic or prolonged; whether either party extended credit or fronted product; whether the parties communicated in coded terms; whether the buyer was reselling to others in a manner the seller knew about.
- **Traps:** Students say a buyer who resells drugs is automatically a co-conspirator with his supplier because they confuse a downstream resale relationship with a shared conspiratorial purpose; *Pauling* holds resale alone is insufficient — there must be evidence the seller shared the buyer's distribution purpose.
- **Cases:** United States v. Pauling
- **Lines:** [[393, 396]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** defense

**Hypo seeds:**
  - A defendant buys cocaine twice from the same dealer. Both times he pays cash. No coded language, no fronting. Is this a drug conspiracy?
  - A drug dealer fronts $10,000 worth of heroin to a buyer, extending credit because he knows the buyer will resell and pay back from proceeds. Is this a conspiracy?
  - A buyer and seller exchange coded text messages like "I need twenty of the usual" over six months. The buyer resells. Is there a shared purpose to distribute?

### 14. conspiracy-withdrawal
- **Rule:** A conspirator can withdraw from a conspiracy but must take affirmative steps to defeat the conspiracy's purpose and, in most jurisdictions, communicate the withdrawal to coconspirators; mere cessation of participation is not withdrawal; successful withdrawal terminates future liability (but not liability for past offenses or the conspiracy itself).
- **Split:** Federal courts require only communication of intent to withdraw; some states and the MPC require affirmative steps to thwart the conspiracy; the MPC withdrawal also operates as a complete defense to the conspiracy charge if the defendant thwarts the conspiracy.
- **Levers:** Whether the defendant communicated withdrawal; whether affirmative steps to defeat the conspiracy were taken; whether the conspiracy continued and caused harm after withdrawal.
- **Traps:** Students say a conspirator who stops participating has automatically withdrawn because they confuse passive non-participation with the affirmative act required for legally effective withdrawal; *Pinkerton* makes clear that absence of affirmative withdrawal means the conspiracy continues.
- **Cases:** Pinkerton v. United States (Daniel in prison, no withdrawal)
- **Lines:** [[258, 262], [478, 481]]
- **Block count:** 2
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** defense

**Hypo seeds:**
  - A conspirator moves to another state and stops answering calls from the conspiracy. No communication to co-conspirators. Is this effective withdrawal?
  - A conspirator calls his partner and says "I'm out." The conspiracy continues; a crime is committed the next week. Is the withdrawing defendant liable for that crime?
  - Under MPC, a conspirator reports the conspiracy to police and gives them all relevant information, successfully preventing the planned crime. Does she avoid liability for the conspiracy itself?

### 15. conspiracy-drug-quantity-mandatory-minimums
- **Rule:** In federal drug conspiracy prosecutions, each conspirator may be held accountable for the total drug quantity handled by all members of the conspiracy, even quantities the defendant never personally touched; because federal mandatory minimums are triggered by total quantity, this can subject peripheral participants to sentences equal to organizers.
- **Split:** Some courts limit quantity attribution to reasonably foreseeable quantities; others apply strict Pinkerton-style total attribution.
- **Levers:** Whether the peripheral participant knew about the total quantity; whether the quantity was reasonably foreseeable; whether the defendant had a leadership or organizational role.
- **Traps:** Students say the defendant's sentence should be based only on quantities she personally handled because they confuse sentencing exposure in a single-actor case with the conspiracy rule that attributes all foreseeable co-conspirator quantities to each member.
- **Cases:** Pinkerton v. United States (doctrine); Drug sting/girlfriend problem discussion
- **Lines:** [[441, 459]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** defense

**Hypo seeds:**
  - A "girlfriend" defendant knew her partner was distributing heroin but was not involved in deals. Her partner handled 2 kilograms triggering a 10-year mandatory minimum. She is charged with conspiracy. What is her sentencing exposure?
  - A low-level runner in a cocaine ring is arrested. The conspiracy handled 50 kilograms total; he personally transported 200 grams. He has no cooperation information. What sentence does he face?
  - Under an MPC approach (no Pinkerton), is the runner liable for the full 50 kilograms?

### 16. manufactured-conspiracy-sting-operations
- **Rule:** In reverse sting operations, the government manufactures the conspiracy by supplying the drugs, the plan, and the recruitment; when combined with the unilateral conspiracy rule (agreeing with an undercover agent is sufficient) and mandatory minimums, this creates a mechanism for manufactured convictions; the primary doctrinal check is the entrapment defense, though it rarely succeeds.
- **Split:** No doctrinal split on the validity of sting operations themselves; split on the unilateral vs. bilateral rule determines whether a sting can produce a conspiracy conviction; entrapment doctrine varies across jurisdictions.
- **Levers:** Whether the defendant was predisposed to commit the crime before government contact; whether the government supplied the criminal opportunity, drugs, or plan; the defendant's prior criminal history.
- **Traps:** Students say a defendant who "agrees" with an undercover agent to buy government-supplied drugs in a unilateral conspiracy jurisdiction has a valid defense because the government created the crime, because they confuse the entrapment defense (which requires no predisposition) with the unilateral conspiracy rule (which focuses only on the defendant's own intent and belief).
- **Cases:** Garcia v. State (unilateral rule), United States v. Valigura (bilateral rule dismissal)
- **Lines:** [[441, 513]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** substantial-step-test (Ch. 17), entrapment (Ch. 17)
- **Exam lens:** defense

**Hypo seeds:**
  - DEA agents approach a man with no drug record, offer to sell him 5 kilograms of cocaine at below-market prices, and record him agreeing. No actual drugs change hands. Unilateral jurisdiction. Is this a completed conspiracy?
  - Same facts, bilateral jurisdiction. Is there a conspiracy?
  - A defendant has two prior drug convictions. DEA initiates contact, proposes a deal, supplies everything. He agrees immediately. Does the prior criminal history defeat an entrapment defense?

### 17. corporate-conspiracy-individual-liability-gap
- **Rule:** Corporations can be charged with conspiracy, but corporate guilty pleas do not automatically generate individual liability for executives; proving individual conspiracy requires showing that specific persons agreed to the criminal scheme, and prosecutors must make the individual-inference of agreement that collective corporate conduct does not establish automatically.
- **Split:** No doctrinal split; the gap is an enforcement and proof issue, not a legal one.
- **Levers:** Whether individual communications or directives can be traced to specific executives; whether corporate action was authorized at a high enough level; prosecutorial discretion in choosing to charge individuals.
- **Traps:** Students say if the corporation is guilty of conspiracy, its executives must also be guilty because they confuse the derivative liability of the corporation (which acts through agents) with the requirement of proving each individual's agreement and intent independently.
- **Cases:** Purdue Pharma guilty plea; Interstate Circuit (agreement-inference framework applied by analogy)
- **Lines:** [[409, 439]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** both

**Hypo seeds:**
  - A pharmaceutical company pleads guilty to conspiracy to defraud regulators. Its CEO approved the misleading reports in writing. Can the CEO be charged individually with conspiracy?
  - Same company. The CFO received reports showing the diversion but took no action. No direct evidence he ordered anything. Can he be charged?
  - Where does Purdue's executive team fall on the *Interstate Circuit*–*Kotteakos*–*Alvarez* spectrum of agreement inference?

### 18. conspiracy-mpc-merger-no-cumulative-punishment
- **Rule:** Under the MPC, conspiracy merges with the completed offense and does not support cumulative punishment; a defendant convicted of the substantive crime may not also be sentenced separately for the conspiracy to commit it.
- **Split:** Traditional/federal law allows cumulative punishment for conspiracy plus substantive offense; MPC merger rule prevents this.
- **Levers:** Whether the jurisdiction follows MPC merger; whether the conspiracy charged extends beyond the completed offense (e.g., an ongoing conspiracy to commit multiple crimes).
- **Traps:** Students say a defendant charged with both conspiracy and the completed crime faces two separate punishments in all jurisdictions because they confuse the traditional rule (cumulative punishment allowed) with the MPC merger rule (conspiracy merges into the completed offense).
- **Cases:** MPC comparison table (lines 472–481)
- **Lines:** [[471, 484]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** defense

**Hypo seeds:**
  - In an MPC jurisdiction, defendant is convicted of both conspiracy to commit robbery and robbery. Can the judge impose consecutive sentences?
  - In a federal court, defendant is convicted of conspiracy to distribute drugs (21 U.S.C. § 846) and the underlying distribution offense. Can both sentences run consecutively?
  - A conspiracy involves planning 10 robberies; defendant completes 3 before arrest. Does MPC merger apply to all 10, only the 3 completed, or none?

### 19. civil-asset-forfeiture-conspiracy-incentive
- **Rule:** Civil asset forfeiture allows law enforcement to seize property connected to criminal activity without a conviction; the federal Equitable Sharing Program permits local agencies to transfer seizures to federal authorities and receive up to 80% of proceeds back, bypassing state-law restrictions; this creates a financial incentive for agencies to conduct reverse stings and manufactured conspiracy prosecutions independent of crime-reduction value.
- **Split:** State reforms vary widely; four states have abolished civil forfeiture entirely; federal equitable sharing remains a loophole circumventing most state restrictions.
- **Levers:** Whether the agency can route seizures through federal equitable sharing; whether state law restricts forfeiture without a conviction; the size of the seizure relative to agency budget pressures.
- **Traps:** Students say state forfeiture reforms eliminate the financial incentive for manufactured conspiracies because they confuse state-level reform with the federal Equitable Sharing Program loophole that allows agencies to route seizures around state restrictions.
- **Cases:** None cited directly; DOJ statistics, ASU/National Academies studies, Sunrise FL example.
- **Lines:** [[495, 513]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** both

**Hypo seeds:**
  - A police department in a state that requires a conviction before forfeiture conducts a reverse sting and seizes $50,000 cash. Can it keep the money if the defendant is acquitted by routing through equitable sharing?
  - A state abolishes civil forfeiture entirely. Local police continue conducting reverse stings. How could the federal equitable sharing program undermine the state reform?
  - A city facing budget cuts dramatically increases reverse sting operations. Buyers are lured with below-market prices. Is the financial incentive legally cognizable as a defense to conspiracy charges?

## Cases

### State v. Pond
- **Lines:** [78, 131]
- **Holding:** To be convicted of conspiracy to commit robbery in the second degree, a defendant must specifically intend that every element of the planned offense be accomplished, including elements (like weapon display) that carry no specific intent requirement for the principal.
- **Facts:** Pond agreed with two others to rob a convenience store; no weapon was discussed; during the robbery, a co-conspirator gestured as if he had a weapon; Pond was convicted of conspiracy to commit second-degree robbery.
- **Doctrines:** [conspiracy-specific-intent-target-elements]

### People v. Lauria
- **Lines:** [149, 192]
- **Holding:** A supplier who knowingly provides goods or services for criminal use is not a conspirator absent direct evidence of intent, a special interest (stake in venture or inflated prices), or an inference of intent from the aggravated nature of the crime; knowledge of prostitutes' use of an answering service, without more, is insufficient.
- **Facts:** Lauria operated a telephone answering service he knew was used by prostitutes; he had no financial stake in their earnings and charged normal fees; prostitution is a misdemeanor.
- **Doctrines:** [lauria-supplier-intent-inference, conspiracy-mens-rea-purpose]

### Pinkerton v. United States
- **Lines:** [246, 281]
- **Holding:** Each conspirator is liable for substantive offenses committed by a co-conspirator in furtherance of the conspiracy that were reasonably foreseeable as a necessary or natural consequence of the unlawful agreement, even without proof of direct participation, as long as the conspiracy is ongoing and the conspirator has not withdrawn.
- **Facts:** Daniel Pinkerton was in prison when his brother Walter committed substantive tax violations pursuant to their ongoing conspiracy; Daniel took no direct part in Walter's specific crimes.
- **Doctrines:** [pinkerton-vicarious-liability, conspiracy-withdrawal]

### Interstate Circuit v. United States
- **Lines:** [319, 343]
- **Holding:** An inference of agreement among competing distributors can be drawn when each party knew all others were being asked to join a coordinated scheme and each adhered to the scheme with knowledge that concerted action was contemplated, even without direct evidence of an agreement among themselves.
- **Facts:** O'Donnell sent a letter to eight film distributors naming all as addressees and demanding anti-competitive conditions; each knew the others received the same demand and all complied.
- **Doctrines:** [conspiracy-agreement-element, single-vs-multiple-conspiracies]

### Kotteakos v. United States
- **Lines:** [346, 377]
- **Holding:** A hub-and-spoke arrangement where multiple conspirators deal separately with a central figure but have no knowledge of or dealings with each other constitutes multiple conspiracies, not one, and trying all defendants on a single conspiracy charge is prejudicial variance.
- **Facts:** Brown helped numerous borrowers obtain fraudulent FHA loans; the borrowers had no knowledge of each other; the government charged one conspiracy.
- **Doctrines:** [single-vs-multiple-conspiracies, conspiracy-procedural-advantages]

### United States v. Alvarez
- **Lines:** [379, 385]
- **Holding:** (En banc) A defendant's nod, smile, and promise to be at an unloading site, combined with surrounding circumstances of a drug importation operation, was sufficient for a jury to infer agreement to participate in a conspiracy.
- **Facts:** Alvarez drove a truck to an airport where others were arranging a drug flight to Colombia; an undercover agent asked if he would be at the unloading site; Alvarez nodded and smiled.
- **Doctrines:** [conspiracy-agreement-element, single-vs-multiple-conspiracies]

### Garcia v. State
- **Lines:** [68, 71]
- **Holding:** Under the unilateral approach, a defendant who believed she was entering a genuine agreement and acted with the purpose of committing the crime is guilty of conspiracy even if the only "co-conspirator" was a government informant who never intended to follow through.
- **Facts:** A woman asked a police informant to kill her husband and paid an undercover detective $200 along with a photo and information about her husband's routine.
- **Doctrines:** [bilateral-unilateral-conspiracy]

### State v. Pacheco
- **Lines:** [66, 66]
- **Holding:** Under the bilateral approach, conspiracy requires at least two genuinely agreeing minds; a defendant whose only co-conspirator was a government agent feigning agreement cannot be convicted of conspiracy.
- **Facts:** Cited as example of the bilateral rule; defendant's only co-conspirator was an undercover government agent.
- **Doctrines:** [bilateral-unilateral-conspiracy]

### United States v. Valigura
- **Lines:** [72, 72]
- **Holding:** The unilateral conspiracy theory does not further the public policy concern of preventing group criminal activity; a conspiracy charge was dismissed where the only co-conspirator was an undercover agent.
- **Facts:** Court of Appeals for the Armed Forces dismissed conspiracy charge where the sole co-conspirator was undercover.
- **Doctrines:** [bilateral-unilateral-conspiracy]

### United States v. Pauling
- **Lines:** [395, 396]
- **Holding:** A mere buyer-seller relationship does not establish a drug distribution conspiracy; a conspiracy requires evidence of a shared purpose to further drug distribution, such as prolonged dealing, coded language, or fronting.
- **Facts:** Second Circuit case establishing the buyer-seller exception in drug conspiracy cases.
- **Doctrines:** [buyer-seller-exception]

### People v. Koenig
- **Lines:** [135, 135]
- **Holding:** A defendant can conspire to commit a general intent crime; what cannot support conspiracy is a crime of negligence, because a person cannot agree and specifically intend to accomplish an unintended result.
- **Facts:** Court held conspiracy to commit securities fraud (a general intent crime) is cognizable.
- **Doctrines:** [conspiracy-specific-intent-target-elements]

### Gebardi v. United States
- **Lines:** [403, 407]
- **Holding:** When the legislature deliberately chose not to criminalize the conduct of one party to a bilateral transaction, that party cannot be prosecuted for conspiring to commit the offense; the Mann Act's non-punishment of transported women precluded charging a voluntarily transported woman with conspiracy to violate the Act.
- **Facts:** A man and woman voluntarily traveled together across state lines; the government charged both with conspiracy to violate the Mann Act.
- **Doctrines:** [gebardi-legislative-exemption]

### Iannelli v. United States
- **Lines:** [401, 401]
- **Holding:** Wharton's Rule is a judicial presumption that can be overridden by the legislature, not a constitutional command.
- **Facts:** Supreme Court clarified that Wharton's Rule is rebuttable and that legislatures may authorize conspiracy charges for crimes requiring two parties.
- **Doctrines:** [whartons-rule]
