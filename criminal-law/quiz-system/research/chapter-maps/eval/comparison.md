## Criterion-by-Criterion Analysis

### a. Doctrine Completeness (A: 4, B: 5, C: 5)

All three maps identify the core doctrines: agreement/inference, mens rea purpose, overt act, bilateral/unilateral, Pinkerton, MPC rejection, single vs. multiple conspiracies, supplier liability, buyer-seller, Wharton's Rule, Gebardi, withdrawal, and merger.

**Map A** identifies 18 doctrines but misses corporate conspiracy as a standalone doctrine. It also omits the procedural advantages of conspiracy (co-conspirator hearsay exception as an independent doctrine, joinder, venue), instead scattering these across other entries.

**Map B** identifies 17 doctrines and captures corporate conspiracy and the co-conspirator hearsay implications within the single-vs-multiple-conspiracies doctrine. It also identifies tacit agreement inference as a distinct doctrine from general agreement, which is a smart pedagogical choice since Interstate Circuit's conscious-parallelism rule is genuinely distinct from the general circumstantial-inference principle.

**Map C** identifies 19 doctrines and is the most comprehensive. It uniquely captures drug-quantity mandatory minimums as a separate doctrine (doctrine 15), civil asset forfeiture as a conspiracy incentive (doctrine 19), corporate conspiracy individual liability gap (doctrine 17), and conspiracy procedural advantages as a standalone entry (doctrine 10). These are all testable on an exam, particularly the quantity-attribution and forfeiture material that occupies the last quarter of the chapter.

### b. Doctrine Quality (A: 3, B: 4, C: 4)

**Map A** has some redundancy problems. Doctrines 2 (conspiracy-mens-rea-purpose) and 5 (intent-to-target-elements) overlap significantly -- both address Pond, and doctrine 5 is really a refinement of doctrine 2 rather than an independent testable rule. Similarly, doctrine 18 (manufactured-conspiracy-doctrine) substantially overlaps doctrine 4 (bilateral-vs-unilateral) since the manufactured conspiracy problem is just the unilateral rule applied to stings. Doctrine 6 (conspiracy-to-negligent-crimes) is arguably too narrow to warrant its own entry -- it is a one-sentence note in the chapter (line 136) that is better treated as a sub-point of the mens rea doctrine.

**Map B** also splits mens rea into doctrines 4 and 5, but handles the split more cleanly: doctrine 4 is about the general purpose requirement, while doctrine 5 is specifically about the Pond holding on intent to target offense elements. The distinction has exam utility because a question could test the general purpose requirement without involving element-by-element analysis. However, doctrine 10 (tacit-agreement-inference) could be folded into doctrine 1 (conspiracy-agreement) without loss -- Interstate Circuit is part of the agreement analysis, not an independent doctrine.

**Map C** has the cleanest separation. Doctrines 2 (conspiracy-mens-rea-purpose) and 3 (conspiracy-specific-intent-target-elements) are clearly delineated: doctrine 2 covers the general knowledge-vs-purpose distinction (Lauria), while doctrine 3 covers the Pond element-by-element analysis. Doctrine 4 (lauria-supplier-intent-inference) is kept separate from the general purpose doctrine, which is correct since Lauria's three-prong framework is its own testable analytical structure. The weakest entries are doctrines 15 (drug quantity/mandatory minimums) and 19 (civil asset forfeiture), which are more policy/systemic analysis than testable legal rules -- but the chapter does present them as doctrine-adjacent material that students should understand.

### c. Trap Specificity (A: 4, B: 3, C: 4)

The ideal trap format is: "Students [choose X] because [they confuse Y with Z]."

**Map A** consistently follows this format. Example from doctrine 3: "Students [argue an overt act is insufficient for liability] because [they confuse the 'minimal' overt act of conspiracy with the 'substantial step' required for attempt]." This names the specific wrong answer (arguing the act is insufficient) and the cognitive error (confusing overt act with substantial step). Most of Map A's traps are at this level.

**Map B** is weaker on trap specificity. Example from doctrine 1: "Students [choose to acquit because no single piece of evidence proves agreement] because [they evaluate each piece of circumstantial evidence in isolation rather than considering the cumulative inference from coordinated conduct]." This identifies the cognitive error but is somewhat generic. A more specific version would name the wrong answer choice (e.g., "Students select 'no conspiracy because no direct evidence of agreement'"). Several of Map B's traps follow a pattern of describing a reasoning error without specifying the concrete wrong answer a student would select on an exam.

**Map C** is generally strong. Example from doctrine 3: "Students say the conspirator only needs the same mental state as would be required for the completed offense because they confuse the mental state for the substantive crime with the separately-analyzed mental state required for the conspiracy charge." This names the wrong answer and the confusion clearly. However, Map C uses a slightly different format ("Students say X because they Y") rather than strict bracket notation -- functionally equivalent but less standardized.

### e. Case Coverage (A: 3, B: 5, C: 5)

The chapter contains these cases: Dan Markel (case study), State v. Pond, People v. Lauria, Pinkerton v. United States, Interstate Circuit v. United States, Kotteakos v. United States, United States v. Alvarez, Garcia v. State, State v. Pacheco, United States v. Valigura, United States v. Falcone, Gebardi v. United States, Iannelli v. United States, Hyde v. United States, People v. Koenig, United States v. Pauling, Krulewitch v. United States, and the Purdue Pharma plea.

**Map A** lists 6 cases in its Cases section: Pond, Lauria, Pinkerton, Interstate Circuit, Kotteakos, and Alvarez. It omits standalone entries for Garcia, Pacheco, Valigura, Falcone, Gebardi, Iannelli, Hyde, Koenig, Pauling, Krulewitch, and Purdue Pharma. Some are mentioned in doctrine entries, but the Cases section is significantly incomplete.

**Map B** lists 16 cases with individual entries. It captures virtually every case in the chapter, including Hyde, Krulewitch, Koenig, Gebardi, Iannelli, Pauling, Falcone, and Purdue Pharma. The only arguable omission is the Dan Markel case being labeled as a "case study" rather than a case, which is actually the correct characterization. Holdings are accurate throughout.

**Map C** lists 13 cases. It captures all the major cases plus Koenig, Gebardi, Iannelli, and Pauling. It omits Krulewitch, Falcone, and Hyde as standalone entries (though Hyde is referenced in doctrine 6's case field). Holdings are accurate and detailed.

### f. Hypo Seed Quality (A: 3, B: 4, C: 5)

**Map A** provides 1-2 hypo seeds per doctrine. Many are serviceable but some are trivial or formulaic. Example: "A and B agree to shoplift. B gets caught and punches a security guard. Is A liable for the assault?" This is fine for a quiz question but too simple for a 30-minute exam. The social media platform hypo for supplier liability is more interesting but is lifted almost directly from the chapter's own discussion questions.

**Map B** provides 2-3 hypo seeds per doctrine and they are generally more sophisticated. Example: "A joins a drug conspiracy; coconspirator B kills a rival dealer during a turf dispute; test whether the murder was foreseeable." This tests the limits of foreseeability under Pinkerton. The supplier hypos (landlord, chemical supplier, web hosting) present a useful spectrum. The weakest entries are the comparative hypos (e.g., "Compare outcomes for the same bank robbery hypothetical under Pinkerton vs. MPC") which are study guides rather than exam seeds.

**Map C** provides 2-3 hypo seeds per doctrine and they are the strongest overall. They frequently present multi-layered fact patterns that force students to apply doctrine at its boundary. Example from doctrine 15: "A 'girlfriend' defendant knew her partner was distributing heroin but was not involved in deals. Her partner handled 2 kilograms triggering a 10-year mandatory minimum. She is charged with conspiracy. What is her sentencing exposure?" This is directly usable as an exam question. The forfeiture hypo ("A police department in a state that requires a conviction before forfeiture conducts a reverse sting and seizes $50,000 cash. Can it keep the money if the defendant is acquitted by routing through equitable sharing?") tests systemic understanding at a level appropriate for a final exam.

Map C also uniquely includes a separate "Hypo Seeds" section that catalogs hypos already embedded in the chapter, with notes on how to vary them for an exam. This is extremely useful for exam design because it prevents an exam writer from accidentally duplicating a chapter hypo that students have already practiced.

### g. Cross-Chapter Links (A: 3, B: 3, C: 3)

All three maps score similarly here, and none is strong.

**Map A** links to Ch. 17 (attempt/substantial step, entrapment, renunciation, merger), Ch. 18 (accomplice liability), Ch. 4 (mens rea purpose), and Ch. 11 (involuntary manslaughter). The Ch. 4 and Ch. 11 references are useful but some are vague.

**Map B** links to Ch. 17 (attempt, substantial step, entrapment), Ch. 18 (accomplice liability, knowledge-vs-purpose), and Ch. 5 (mens rea hierarchy). The Ch. 5 link is accurate but most doctrines have null cross-chapter entries.

**Map C** links only to Ch. 17 (substantial step, entrapment) and Ch. 18 (accomplice liability). Many doctrines have null cross-chapter entries. The chapter's own cross-references to these chapters are captured, but there are missed opportunities -- for example, linking the Gebardi doctrine to statutory rape (Ch. 9-10) or linking the forfeiture discussion to theories of punishment (Ch. 1-2).

All three maps miss some natural cross-chapter connections. This is the weakest criterion across the board.

### h. Overall Usefulness for Exam Generation (A: 3, B: 4, C: 5)

**Map A** would be usable but requires significant cleanup. The redundancy between doctrines 2/5 and 4/18 would confuse an exam-writing agent, potentially generating overlapping questions. The incomplete case coverage means the agent might miss testing on cases the students studied. The line ranges are adequate but sometimes imprecise. The hypo seeds are serviceable but would need enrichment.

**Map B** is a strong map. The case coverage is outstanding -- the best of the three. The doctrine analysis is careful and the tacit-agreement-inference separation is pedagogically useful. The traps are the weakest element: they tend to describe reasoning errors generically rather than specifying the concrete wrong-answer a student would select. An exam-writing agent could use this map effectively but would need to sharpen the traps into distractor format.

**Map C** is the most exam-ready of the three. It uniquely captures the policy/systemic material (drug quantity attribution, forfeiture incentives, corporate liability gap) that occupies a substantial portion of the chapter and is very likely to appear on an exam. The traps are specific enough to generate distractor answers directly. The hypo seeds are the most sophisticated and exam-appropriate, with several that could be used nearly verbatim. The separate hypo-seed catalog at the end (noting which hypos students have already seen in class) is a practical feature no other map provides. The main weakness is that doctrines 15 and 19 (drug quantity, forfeiture) are more policy-analytical than strictly doctrinal, which might lead an exam agent to weight them equally with core legal doctrines.

---

## Verdict

**Winner: Map C (Sonnet)**

Map C is the most useful for exam generation. It captures doctrines the other maps miss (quantity attribution, forfeiture incentives, corporate liability gap, procedural advantages), its traps are specific enough for distractor generation, its hypo seeds are the most sophisticated, and its chapter-hypo catalog is a uniquely practical feature. The line ranges are the most accurate of the three.

Map B (Opus) is a close second, with the best case coverage of any map and careful doctrinal analysis. Its main weaknesses are less specific traps and less exam-ready hypo seeds.

Map A (Gemini 3-Pass) is the weakest, with doctrine redundancy, incomplete case coverage, and hypo seeds that are too simple for exam-level questions.
