## Elements

Conspiracy is the crime of agreeing with one or more persons to commit a crime, coupled with the intent that the crime actually be committed. A conviction requires:

1. **Agreement** — Two or more persons must reach a mutual understanding to pursue a criminal objective. This need not be explicit or written; it can be inferred from coordinated conduct. Most jurisdictions also require at least one overt act in furtherance of the conspiracy (though this requirement is minimal).

2. **Mens rea** — The defendant must (a) intend to agree (knowingly and voluntarily enter the conspiratorial relationship) and (b) intend that the object crime be committed. Most jurisdictions require *purpose* — not just knowledge — that the crime be committed. This means the conspirator must specifically intend every element of the target offense.

3. **Scope of agreement** — The conspiracy must be a single unified agreement, not multiple independent schemes. The structure of the agreement (who knows whom, whether there is a "rim to the wheel") determines who is liable and what evidence is admissible against each defendant.

4. **Vicarious liability (Pinkerton)** — Under the traditional *Pinkerton* doctrine (used in federal courts and some states), each conspirator is liable for the foreseeable substantive crimes of all coconspirators, without proof of individual participation. The MPC rejects this approach.

5. **Structural limits** — Certain crimes, by their nature, cannot support a conspiracy charge: Wharton's Rule bars conspiracy charges for crimes that definitionally require two participants; the *Gebardi* principle bars conspiracy charges against a class of participants the legislature deliberately chose not to punish.

## Refinements

### Element 1: Agreement

#### agreement-inference: Agreement Can Be Inferred from Coordinated Conduct
- **Rule:** Conspirators rarely announce their agreements explicitly. Courts permit — and routinely uphold — the inference of agreement from circumstantial evidence of coordinated conduct: phone patterns, financial flows, synchronized timing, and mutual awareness of a shared plan.
- **Source:** *Markel* case study (chapter introduction); *Justice Coleridge, 1837* (quoted in "Breaking Down the Elements")
- **Trap:** Students say "there was no agreement" because there was no explicit statement or written plan. Students confuse the form of an agreement with its existence — coordination and mutual awareness can substitute for express words.
- **Difficulty:** foundational

#### bilateral-vs-unilateral: The Undercover Agent Problem — Bilateral vs. Unilateral Conspiracy
- **Rule:** Traditional ("bilateral") jurisdictions require two genuinely agreeing minds — so a defendant who "agrees" only with an undercover agent cannot be convicted of conspiracy. The MPC (and most reformed states) adopt a "unilateral" approach: what matters is the defendant's own belief that she was entering a genuine agreement; the agent's secret intentions are irrelevant.
- **Source:** *State v. Pacheco*, 882 P.2d 183 (Wash. 1994) (bilateral); *Garcia v. State*, 394 N.E.2d 106 (Ind. 1979) (unilateral); MPC § 5.03 Official Commentary
- **Trap:** Students apply the unilateral rule and forget that bilateral jurisdictions still exist, or they apply the bilateral rule to MPC jurisdictions. Students also confuse bilateral/unilateral with the question of whether a real agreement occurred at all.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate

#### overt-act-minimal: The Overt Act Requirement Is Exceedingly Minimal
- **Rule:** Most jurisdictions require at least one overt act in furtherance of the conspiracy, but this act need not be substantial or illegal. Purchasing a plane ticket, making a phone call, or checking out a library book qualifies. This is far less demanding than attempt's "substantial step."
- **Source:** MPC § 5.03(5); *Hyde v. United States*, 225 U.S. 347 (1912) (Holmes, J., dissenting)
- **Trap:** Students apply the "substantial step" standard from attempt to conspiracy's overt act requirement. The two are distinct — the overt act for conspiracy can be trivially innocent.
- **Difficulty:** foundational

#### inference-limits: Not Every Coordinated Actor Is a Conspirator — The Inference Has Limits
- **Rule:** Agreement must be inferred carefully. In *Kotteakos*, multiple borrowers who all used the same fraudulent broker had no agreement with each other — each had a separate conspiracy with Brown, not a single overarching scheme. The government cannot bundle unrelated actors into one conspiracy merely because they shared a common central figure.
- **Source:** *Kotteakos v. United States*, 328 U.S. 750 (1946)
- **Trap:** Students assume that anyone who dealt with the same central figure is automatically part of the same conspiracy. The test is whether the parties had mutual awareness of and shared stake in a common plan — a "rim to the wheel."
- **Difficulty:** intermediate

#### interstate-circuit-awareness: Mutual Awareness Plus Knowing Participation Can Satisfy Agreement
- **Rule:** When parties know they are all being asked to join a coordinated scheme — and each knows the others are participating — their subsequent collective conduct can constitute agreement even without direct communication among them. The key is that each party knew that coordinated action was invited and chose to participate knowing the others would too.
- **Source:** *Interstate Circuit v. United States*, 306 U.S. 208 (1939)
- **Trap:** Students think there must be direct horizontal communication between all conspirators. *Interstate Circuit* holds that vertical communication (each getting the same letter naming all as addressees) plus coordinated action is sufficient.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate

#### alvarez-nod: Agreement Can Be Inferred from Minimal Conduct — and the Limits of That Inference
- **Rule:** Courts have upheld conspiracy convictions based on extremely thin evidence of agreement — in *Alvarez*, a nod of the head and a smile at a drug importation site were held sufficient for a jury to infer agreement. A strong dissent warned that "the prosecutor's net may well ensnare the innocent."
- **Source:** *United States v. Alvarez*, 625 F.2d 1196 (5th Cir. 1980) (en banc)
- **Trap:** Students assume that very minimal conduct cannot satisfy the agreement element. The lesson is not that nods are always enough, but that in context, juries have wide latitude — and that this latitude raises serious fair notice concerns.
- **Difficulty:** advanced

#### buyer-seller-exception: Buyer-Seller Relationship Alone Is Not a Conspiracy
- **Rule:** A simple buyer-seller drug transaction does not constitute conspiracy, even if the buyer knows she is buying for resale. A conspiracy requires a shared purpose to further distribution — evidenced by prolonged dealing, coded language, or "fronting" (credit extended by the seller), which signals mutual investment in the ongoing criminal enterprise.
- **Source:** *United States v. Pauling*, 924 F.3d 649 (2d Cir. 2019) (Note following *Alvarez*)
- **Trap:** Students charge conspiracy whenever a drug sale occurs. The buyer-seller exception means the mere act of purchase — even repeated purchase — does not establish the shared purpose element.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate

### Element 2: Mens Rea

#### purpose-not-knowledge: Conspiracy Requires Purpose, Not Just Knowledge
- **Rule:** Knowing that one's conduct facilitates a crime is not enough for conspiracy. The defendant must act with *purpose* — a specific intent to further the criminal enterprise. Under *Lauria*, a supplier's knowledge of criminal use, standing alone, is insufficient. Purpose can be inferred from (1) direct evidence, (2) a stake in the venture (e.g., inflated prices, profit-sharing), or (3) the aggravated nature of the crime.
- **Source:** *People v. Lauria*, 251 Cal. App. 2d 471 (1967)
- **Trap:** Students say the telephone answering service owner who "knew" about prostitution is guilty of conspiracy. *Lauria* requires purpose, not mere knowledge — the service had no stake in the prostitutes' earnings and the offense was a misdemeanor.
- **Difficulty:** foundational

#### aggravated-crime-inference: Grave Crimes Can Support Inference of Purpose from Knowledge
- **Rule:** When the crime in question is of a "grave character" (arson, serious felonies, crimes against children), knowledge that one's services are being used for that crime may — by itself — support the inference of conspiratorial purpose. For misdemeanor-level conduct, knowledge alone is insufficient.
- **Source:** *People v. Lauria*, 251 Cal. App. 2d 471 (1967)
- **Trap:** Students apply the aggravated-crime inference to low-level crimes. The inference is reserved for serious offenses where it is implausible to think a knowing supplier lacks a stake in the outcome.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate

#### pond-specific-intent-elements: Conspiracy Requires Specific Intent as to Every Element of the Target Offense
- **Rule:** A conspirator must specifically intend that every element of the planned offense be accomplished — including elements that carry no specific intent requirement for the principal. If a defendant agreed to commit robbery but did not specifically intend that a weapon would be displayed, he cannot be convicted of conspiracy to commit second-degree robbery (which requires weapon display).
- **Source:** *State v. Pond*, 315 Conn. 451 (2015)
- **Trap:** Students assume that the mental state required for the completed offense automatically carries over to conspiracy. *Pond* holds that conspiracy requires *more* — a specific intent as to every element — because the essence of conspiracy is agreement to bring about a specific criminal act.
- **Difficulty:** advanced

#### general-intent-conspiracy: Conspiracy Is Not Limited to Specific Intent Crimes
- **Rule:** *Pond* does not mean one can only conspire to commit specific intent crimes. A defendant can conspire to commit a general intent crime (e.g., securities fraud) because the conspirator's specific intent to agree and to accomplish the prohibited conduct satisfies the mens rea requirement. What cannot support conspiracy is a crime of *negligence* — one cannot agree to bring about an unintended result.
- **Source:** *People v. Koenig*, 58 Cal. App. 5th 771 (2020) (Note following *Pond*)
- **Trap:** Students read *Pond* to mean conspiracy is only available for specific intent crimes. The correct limit is that conspiracy cannot be based on negligence, not that the target offense must itself require specific intent.
- **Difficulty:** advanced

### Element 3: Scope of Agreement

#### single-vs-multiple-conspiracies: The Difference Between One Conspiracy and Many Matters Enormously
- **Rule:** Whether the government can charge a single conspiracy or must charge multiple separate conspiracies depends on whether all defendants shared a common criminal purpose and mutual awareness. The procedural stakes are high: in a single conspiracy, each coconspirator's statements are admissible against all, defendants can be jointly tried, and *Pinkerton* liability extends across the whole scheme.
- **Source:** *Kotteakos v. United States*, 328 U.S. 750 (1946)
- **Trap:** Students ignore the procedural consequences of the single-vs.-multiple distinction and treat it as purely substantive. The variance between a single conspiracy charge and the evidence proving only multiple ones is prejudicial because evidence "bleeds" across defendants.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate

#### wheel-and-spoke: "Wheel and Spoke" Without a Rim Is Multiple Conspiracies
- **Rule:** When a central figure ("hub") coordinates with multiple peripheral actors ("spokes"), and those peripheral actors have no awareness of or dealings with each other, the structure is a wheel without a rim — multiple separate conspiracies, not one. A single conspiracy requires that the spokes be connected by a common rim: shared purpose and mutual awareness.
- **Source:** *Kotteakos v. United States*, 328 U.S. 750 (1946)
- **Trap:** Students see one central figure and charge one conspiracy. The hub-and-spoke structure is *not* a single conspiracy unless there is a "rim" — the peripheral defendants must know of and share a stake in the common scheme.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate

### Element 4: Vicarious Liability (Pinkerton)

#### pinkerton-rule: Under Pinkerton, Each Conspirator Is Liable for Foreseeable Crimes of Coconspirators
- **Rule:** A conspirator who did not personally commit or aid a substantive offense is nonetheless criminally liable for it if the offense was committed in furtherance of the conspiracy and was a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the agreement. Membership in the conspiracy, plus foreseeability, is enough — no direct participation is required.
- **Source:** *Pinkerton v. United States*, 328 U.S. 640 (1946)
- **Trap:** Students assume that criminal liability requires personal participation or aiding. *Pinkerton* holds that the conspiracy itself supplies the criminal intent — one conspirator's act is the act of all, provided it was foreseeable and within the scope of the agreement.
- **Difficulty:** foundational

#### pinkerton-limits: Pinkerton Liability Has a Limiting Principle — But It Is Narrow
- **Rule:** *Pinkerton* liability does not extend to acts that were not in furtherance of the conspiracy, fell outside the scope of the unlawful project, or "could not be reasonably foreseen as a necessary or natural consequence of the unlawful agreement." In practice, this limitation has limited force because in ongoing criminal conspiracies, most related crimes are foreseeable.
- **Source:** *Pinkerton v. United States*, 328 U.S. 640 (1946) (majority opinion limiting language)
- **Trap:** Students think the foreseeability limit provides a robust defense. In a drug trafficking or organized crime conspiracy, it is very hard to argue that weapons possession, assault, or additional drug crimes were not foreseeable.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate

#### mpc-rejects-pinkerton: The MPC Rejects Pinkerton
- **Rule:** The MPC rejects vicarious *Pinkerton* liability. Under the MPC, a conspirator is only liable for a coconspirator's substantive offenses if she would be liable as an accomplice under MPC § 2.06 — that is, she actually aided, abetted, or encouraged the specific offense with the purpose of promoting it. Most reformed state codes follow the MPC.
- **Source:** MPC § 2.06; "The Model Penal Code Rejection of Pinkerton" callout
- **Trap:** Students assume *Pinkerton* applies in all jurisdictions. It is a federal rule and applies in some states, but a majority of reformed codes reject it.
- **Difficulty:** foundational

#### pinkerton-quantity-sentencing: Pinkerton's Interaction with Drug Quantity Sentencing Creates the "Girlfriend Problem"
- **Rule:** Because conspiracy attributes the total drug quantity handled by all coconspirators to each member, and because mandatory minimums are triggered by quantity, a peripheral participant (e.g., a defendant whose only connection was a relationship with a conspirator) can face the same sentence as the organizer. The cooperation escape valve favors those with information to trade — typically the organizers, not the peripheral members.
- **Source:** "Drug Stings and Manufactured Conspiracy" section
- **Trap:** Students assume that peripheral participation results in lower sentences. Under *Pinkerton* plus mandatory minimums, sentence is driven by total quantity, not by the defendant's individual role.
- **Difficulty:** advanced

### Element 5: Structural Limits

#### whartons-rule: Wharton's Rule Bars Conspiracy for Crimes That Inherently Require Two People
- **Rule:** When a crime by definition requires the participation of two people (dueling, bigamy, adultery, incest, bribery), an agreement between those two people to commit it cannot be prosecuted as a separate conspiracy. The conspiracy adds nothing beyond what is already an element of the substantive offense. The rule does not apply if more persons participate than the minimum the crime requires.
- **Source:** *Iannelli v. United States*, 420 U.S. 770 (1975) (cited in text)
- **Trap:** Students apply Wharton's Rule whenever two people are involved in a crime. The rule is limited to crimes that *definitionally require* two participants — it does not apply just because the crime happened to involve two people.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate

#### gebardi-principle: Legislature's Exemption of One Party Bars Conspiracy Charge Against That Party
- **Rule:** When the legislature deliberately chooses not to punish one party to a bilateral transaction, that party cannot be prosecuted for conspiring to commit the offense. The exemption implies that Congress did not intend the conspiracy statute to reach what the substantive statute deliberately left unpunished.
- **Source:** *Gebardi v. United States*, 287 U.S. 112 (1932) (Mann Act / transported woman)
- **Trap:** Students charge conspiracy against every party to a bilateral transaction. *Gebardi* holds that when the substantive statute protects a class of actors, the conspiracy statute cannot be used to circumvent that protection.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate

## Clusters

### cluster-agreement-inference-arc
- **Refinements:** agreement-inference, interstate-circuit-awareness, alvarez-nod, inference-limits, wheel-and-spoke
- **Why they cluster:** These refinements all develop the central skill of determining whether an agreement exists from circumstantial evidence, and they span the full spectrum from sophisticated corporate actors to a day laborer's nod.
- **Scenario hook:** A mid-level distributor receives the same supply letter as five others, fulfills orders consistently, but has never met the other distributors — and one of them commits a violent crime during a transaction.

### cluster-mens-rea-purpose
- **Refinements:** purpose-not-knowledge, aggravated-crime-inference, pond-specific-intent-elements, general-intent-conspiracy
- **Why they cluster:** These refinements all turn on what mental state is required for conspiracy liability — from the knowledge/purpose distinction for suppliers to the specific intent requirement for each element of the target offense.
- **Scenario hook:** A warehouse manager knowingly stores packages he suspects contain drugs, receives slightly above-market compensation, and later learns his client planned an armed robbery using those supplies.

### cluster-vicarious-liability
- **Refinements:** pinkerton-rule, pinkerton-limits, mpc-rejects-pinkerton, pinkerton-quantity-sentencing, single-vs-multiple-conspiracies
- **Why they cluster:** These refinements govern how far criminal liability extends from the agreement itself — a cluster of exam issues that arise whenever a conspirator is charged with a crime she did not personally commit.
- **Scenario hook:** A driver in a drug distribution network never handles product directly but makes deliveries; a coconspirator commits an armed robbery while collecting a debt — in a federal prosecution, who is liable for what and for how much?

### cluster-undercover-operations
- **Refinements:** bilateral-vs-unilateral, pinkerton-quantity-sentencing, buyer-seller-exception
- **Why they cluster:** These refinements govern the reach of conspiracy law in government-initiated investigations, where the bilateral/unilateral question, quantity attribution, and the line between customer and coconspirator all arise together.
- **Scenario hook:** An undercover agent poses as a wholesale drug supplier, recruits a retail buyer into a distribution scheme, and records an agreement — in a MPC jurisdiction vs. a bilateral jurisdiction, what are the conspiracy charges and who is liable for what quantity?

### cluster-structural-limits
- **Refinements:** whartons-rule, gebardi-principle, overt-act-minimal
- **Why they cluster:** These refinements define the outer boundaries of conspiracy liability — when the doctrine categorically does not apply — and test whether students understand that not all coordinated criminal conduct is a conspiracy.
- **Scenario hook:** Two parties agree to participate in a transaction that the law punishes only one of them for, and one of them takes a preparatory step so minimal it would never qualify as attempt.

### cluster-lauria-suppliers
- **Refinements:** purpose-not-knowledge, aggravated-crime-inference, buyer-seller-exception
- **Why they cluster:** These refinements all apply to the "supplier problem" — when does a legitimate business providing goods or services to criminals cross into conspiracy — and they test the knowledge/purpose distinction in applied contexts.
- **Scenario hook:** A payment processor knowingly handles transactions for an illegal gambling operation, charges standard fees, and has no formal profit-sharing arrangement, but the gambling operation also involves money laundering that finances a violent cartel.

## Cases

### Markel Murder (Case Study)
- **Holding:** Not a decided case — used as an illustrative case study for conspiracy elements; Charlie Adelson was convicted of first-degree murder under *Pinkerton* liability.
- **Reasoning:** The agreement was inferred from phone records, financial flows, and coordinated timing despite no direct evidence of a joint meeting; *Pinkerton* attributed the shooter's acts to the alleged organizer.
- **Facts:** Charlie Adelson allegedly hired his girlfriend Magbanua to arrange the murder of his sister's ex-husband Dan Markel through a chain of intermediaries; Garcia shot Markel in his garage in 2014.
- **Teaches:** agreement-inference, pinkerton-rule, single-vs-multiple-conspiracies

### State v. Pond, 315 Conn. 451 (2015)
- **Holding:** To convict of conspiracy to commit second-degree robbery, the state must prove the defendant specifically intended that every element of the offense be accomplished, including the weapon element, even though the weapon element carries no specific intent requirement for the principal.
- **Reasoning:** Conspiracy is a specific intent crime whose essence is the agreement to commit a particular offense; the conspirator must intend to bring about every element of the crime that is the object of the conspiracy, not merely agree to engage in conduct that happens to produce that crime.
- **Facts:** Pond served as a lookout for a convenience store robbery; his coconspirators entered and one made a threatening gesture suggesting a weapon without displaying one; Pond was convicted of conspiracy to commit second-degree robbery but acquitted as an accessory.
- **Teaches:** pond-specific-intent-elements, general-intent-conspiracy

### People v. Lauria, 251 Cal. App. 2d 471 (1967)
- **Holding:** A telephone answering service owner who knew his customers were prostitutes was not guilty of conspiracy to commit prostitution because knowledge of criminal use, without more, does not establish conspiratorial purpose.
- **Reasoning:** Intent for conspiracy requires both knowledge and purpose; purpose can be inferred from a stake in the venture or the aggravated nature of the crime, but neither was present here — the service had no special interest in earnings and prostitution was a misdemeanor.
- **Facts:** Police investigating call-girl activity found that three prostitutes used Lauria's answering service for client messages; Lauria acknowledged knowing they were prostitutes; he charged standard rates and had no share in their earnings.
- **Teaches:** purpose-not-knowledge, aggravated-crime-inference

### Pinkerton v. United States, 328 U.S. 640 (1946)
- **Holding:** A conspirator who did not directly participate in the commission of substantive offenses is criminally liable for those offenses if they were committed by a coconspirator in furtherance of the conspiracy and were a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the unlawful agreement.
- **Reasoning:** In a continuing conspiracy, partners act for each other — the criminal intent formed at the time of the agreement supplies the intent for all acts done in execution of the common enterprise, making direct participation unnecessary.
- **Facts:** Brothers Daniel and Walter Pinkerton conspired to evade federal liquor taxes; Walter committed the substantive offenses; Daniel was in prison for part of the period and had no direct involvement in the specific acts charged.
- **Teaches:** pinkerton-rule, pinkerton-limits, mpc-rejects-pinkerton

### Interstate Circuit v. United States, 306 U.S. 208 (1939)
- **Holding:** Film distributors who received identical letters naming all of them as co-addressees, each knowing that the others were being asked to join, and who then collectively imposed the requested restrictions, had conspired even without direct communication among themselves.
- **Reasoning:** Knowing that concerted action was invited, and knowing that each other's participation was essential to the plan, the distributors' adherence to the scheme constituted agreement — it taxed credulity to believe their parallel conduct was coincidental.
- **Facts:** An exhibitor sent identical letters to eight major distributors demanding pricing and double-feature restrictions, naming all distributors as addressees; all distributors complied; the government proved no direct agreement among the distributors.
- **Teaches:** interstate-circuit-awareness, agreement-inference

### Kotteakos v. United States, 328 U.S. 750 (1946)
- **Holding:** Evidence proving multiple separate conspiracies between a central broker and distinct groups of borrowers cannot support a conviction for a single overarching conspiracy; such a variance is highly prejudicial.
- **Reasoning:** The gist of conspiracy is agreement; without mutual awareness and a shared common purpose, each borrower's separate scheme with Brown was its own conspiracy — there was no "rim to the wheel" binding the spokes into one.
- **Facts:** Simon Brown helped thirty-two borrowers submit fraudulent FHA loan applications; the borrowers had no knowledge of or dealings with each other; the government charged them all in a single conspiracy.
- **Teaches:** wheel-and-spoke, single-vs-multiple-conspiracies, inference-limits

### United States v. Alvarez, 625 F.2d 1196 (5th Cir. 1980) (en banc)
- **Holding:** Alvarez's nod, smile, and statement that he would be at the unloading site, combined with surrounding circumstances at a drug importation staging area, were sufficient for a jury to infer he had joined the importation conspiracy.
- **Reasoning:** The en banc majority concluded that a promise to appear at the unloading site, in the context of ongoing drug importation activity, constituted participation in the common criminal plan; the dissent (9 judges) warned that a nod and a smile put Alvarez in prison.
- **Facts:** An undercover DEA agent asked Alvarez, a day laborer at an airport staging area for a marijuana importation operation, whether he planned to be at the unloading site; Alvarez nodded, smiled, and asked if the agent was going on the plane, then unloaded appliances from a truck.
- **Teaches:** alvarez-nod, agreement-inference

### Gebardi v. United States, 287 U.S. 112 (1932)
- **Holding:** A woman who voluntarily traveled across state lines with a man for "immoral purposes" could not be convicted of conspiracy to violate the Mann Act, because Congress's deliberate choice not to punish the transported woman under the substantive statute precluded conspiracy liability.
- **Reasoning:** The Mann Act treated the woman's voluntary participation as an "inseparable incident" of all covered cases but chose not to punish it; to make it automatically punishable under the conspiracy statute would circumvent that legislative choice.
- **Facts:** A man and woman traveled voluntarily across state lines for immoral purposes; both were charged with conspiracy to violate the Mann Act, which criminalized transporting women across state lines for immoral purposes but did not punish the transported woman.
- **Teaches:** gebardi-principle
