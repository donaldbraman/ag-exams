## Elements

### Prima Facie Elements

1. **Agreement** — Two or more persons agree to commit a crime. The agreement need not be explicit; courts routinely infer it from coordinated conduct.
2. **Mens rea: purpose** — Each defendant must act with the *purpose* of committing the target crime, even if the underlying offense would require only knowledge or recklessness. Purpose must encompass every element of the target offense.
3. **Overt act** — In most jurisdictions, at least one conspirator must commit some act in furtherance of the conspiracy. The bar is extremely low — a phone call, a plane ticket, checking out a library book — far below the "substantial step" required for attempt.

### Liability Extensions

1. **Pinkerton vicarious liability** — Each conspirator is liable for all foreseeable crimes committed by any co-conspirator in furtherance of the conspiracy, without need to satisfy the traditional elements of accomplice liability.

### Liability Limits

1. **Wharton's Rule** — When a crime by definition requires the participation of two people (dueling, bigamy, bribery), an agreement between those two people cannot itself be prosecuted as a conspiracy.
2. **Legislative exemption (Gebardi principle)** — When the legislature deliberately chose not to criminalize the conduct of one party to a bilateral transaction, that party cannot be prosecuted for conspiring to commit the offense.
3. **Withdrawal** — A conspirator who affirmatively disavows the conspiracy's objectives and communicates those steps to co-conspirators (or discloses the scheme to law enforcement) may escape liability for subsequent acts. Withdrawal is allowed but demanding; mere abandonment is not enough.
4. **MPC merger** — Under the MPC, conspiracy merges with the completed offense so that a defendant cannot be punished for both the conspiracy and the substantive crime.
5. **Buyer-seller exception** — A bare agreement to buy and sell drugs does not, by itself, establish a drug distribution conspiracy; there must be evidence of a shared ongoing distribution purpose.

## Refinements

### Agreement

#### agreement-explicit-vs-inferred: Explicit Agreement Is Not Required
- **Rule:** Conspirators need not state in words what the object of their combination is. Agreement may be inferred whenever two or more persons pursue the same object by the same means, each performing a part so as to complete it.
- **Source:** Justice Coleridge's 1837 jury instruction (quoted in chapter; *Interstate Circuit v. United States*)
- **Trap:** Students require an explicit meeting of the minds because they confuse conspiracy with contract, missing that coordinated conduct alone can satisfy the agreement element.
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** If the defendants' parallel conduct could plausibly have been independent rather than coordinated, the inference of agreement collapses.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal
- **Practiced:** Slides 12–20 — extended treatment including Coleridge instruction (Slide 17), Dan Markel case study (Slides 18–20), and the "inference" frame.

#### agreement-interstate-mutual-awareness: Mutual Awareness of Concerted Scheme Supports Inference
- **Rule:** When multiple parties each know that the others have been invited to join a coordinated scheme, their individual participation is sufficient to infer agreement among themselves, even without direct communication between them.
- **Source:** *Interstate Circuit v. United States* (1939)
- **Trap:** Students treat *Interstate Circuit* as requiring proof that each distributor explicitly agreed with every other distributor, when the court held that knowing participation in a scheme where concerted action was contemplated suffices.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If the distributors had no reason to know the others were receiving identical proposals, the inference of mutual agreement would be unavailable.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal
- **Practiced:** Slide 28 (movie theater image as case introduction).

#### agreement-wheel-without-rim: Wheel-Without-Rim Conspiracy vs. Single Conspiracy
- **Rule:** A "wheel" structure in which multiple parties each deal separately with one central figure, but have no knowledge of or dealings with each other, constitutes multiple separate conspiracies, not one. The government cannot try them as a single conspiracy without a "rim" connecting the spokes.
- **Source:** *Kotteakos v. United States* (1946)
- **Trap:** Students assume that any conspiracy with a common actor at the center is a single conspiracy, missing that the spokes must know of and depend on each other for there to be a rim.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If the borrowers in *Kotteakos* had known of each other and depended on collective fraud to sustain the scheme, a single conspiracy would be supported.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal
- **Practiced:** Slide 29 — "wheel without a rim" diagram with *Kotteakos* holding.

#### agreement-alvarez-minimal-conduct: Agreement Inferred from Minimal Conduct
- **Rule:** A nod, a smile, and an ambiguous statement in circumstances that imply criminal coordination can be sufficient for a jury to infer agreement to join a conspiracy, though this is the most aggressive application of the inference doctrine.
- **Source:** *United States v. Alvarez* (5th Cir. 1980) (note case)
- **Trap:** Students think the prosecution needs substantial affirmative conduct to prove agreement, not realizing that contextually loaded minimal conduct can satisfy the element.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If Alvarez's nod and smile had occurred outside any criminal context — rather than at an airport where others were arranging an illegal flight — there would be no basis for the inference.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority (aggressive inference) vs. minority (stricter sufficiency review)
- **Practiced:** Slide 30 — "A nod and a smile?" with the Alvarez image.

#### agreement-bilateral-vs-unilateral: Bilateral vs. Unilateral Conspiracy (Undercover Agent)
- **Rule:** Traditional (bilateral) jurisdictions require two genuinely agreeing minds; a defendant whose only co-conspirator is a government agent has no conspiracy partner. The MPC (unilateral) approach asks only whether the *defendant* believed she was entering a genuine agreement and acted with purpose, making the agent's true intentions irrelevant.
- **Source:** *State v. Pacheco* (Wash. 1994) (bilateral); *Garcia v. State* (Ind. 1979) (unilateral); MPC § 5.03
- **Trap:** Students apply the bilateral rule mechanically in MPC jurisdictions, concluding no conspiracy exists when an undercover officer is the only co-conspirator.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** In a bilateral jurisdiction, if the undercover agent had genuinely intended to participate, the bilateral requirement would be satisfied and the conspiracy conviction would stand.
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC) — majority of reformed codes follow unilateral (MPC); traditional common law is bilateral.
- **Practiced:** Slide 31 — "One-Sided Agreements?" with the bilateral/unilateral distinction and undercover agent example.

#### agreement-buyer-seller: Buyer-Seller Relationship Is Not Conspiracy
- **Rule:** A bare agreement to purchase drugs does not establish a drug distribution conspiracy. The prosecution must show a shared purpose to further ongoing distribution — evidenced by a prolonged course of dealing, coded language, or "fronting" arrangements.
- **Source:** *United States v. Pauling* (2d Cir. 2019) (note case)
- **Trap:** Students assume any drug transaction between two parties creates a conspiracy, missing the "shared distribution purpose" requirement.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If the buyer was receiving product on credit (fronting) and both parties expected continued distribution, the buyer-seller relationship would cross into conspiracy.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority (federal and most states)

### Mens Rea

#### mens-rea-purpose-required: Conspiracy Requires Purpose, Not Merely Knowledge
- **Rule:** Conspiracy requires the mental state of *purpose* — intent to promote and facilitate the crime — not merely knowledge that one's conduct assists criminal activity. A supplier who knows customers are engaged in crime but lacks a stake in or purpose toward that crime is not a conspirator.
- **Source:** *People v. Lauria* (Cal. 1967)
- **Trap:** Students treat knowledge of criminal use as equivalent to conspiratorial intent, failing to analyze the "purpose" component separately.
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** If Lauria had charged inflated prices to his prostitute clients (reflecting a premium for criminal dealing), that stake in the venture would support an inference of purpose.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal
- **Practiced:** Slides 21, 23 — "Conspiracy requires purpose — even when the underlying crime requires less"; *Lauria* holding stated.

#### mens-rea-lauria-inferences: Three Circumstances Where Knowledge Supports Purpose
- **Rule:** A supplier's knowledge of criminal use can support an inference of purpose to participate when: (1) the supplier has a *stake in the venture* (inflated prices, profit-sharing); (2) the goods or services have *no substantial legitimate use*; or (3) the crime is of *grave/aggravated character* such that knowledge alone supports intent.
- **Source:** *People v. Lauria* (Cal. 1967)
- **Trap:** Students apply only one of the three *Lauria* factors and miss that any of the three independently suffices, or miss that all three failed on Lauria's facts.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If the crime had been a felony (rather than a misdemeanor), the aggravated-crime inference could supply purpose even without a stake or exclusive criminal use.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal (widely followed)
- **Practiced:** Slide 24 — "Lauria's Three Inferences" with numbered red boxes; Slide 25 — Silk Road vs. Social Media table.

#### mens-rea-purpose-every-element: Purpose Must Encompass Every Element of the Target Offense
- **Rule:** Because conspiracy is an inchoate offense defined by the agreement and the intent to perform the conduct constituting the crime, the conspirator must specifically intend that *every element* of the target crime be accomplished — including elements that require no specific intent for the completed offense.
- **Source:** *State v. Pond* (Conn. 2015)
- **Trap:** Students conclude that if the underlying robbery requires no specific intent as to the weapon element, the conspiracy charge likewise requires none — the majority's holding rejects this symmetry.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If the three men had discussed using a weapon during their planning meeting, Pond's specific intent as to the weapon element would be established and the conviction would stand.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority (follows MPC/Pond principle); split with minority following the dissent's symmetry approach.
- **Practiced:** Slide 22 — *State v. Pond* case box with holding in red.

#### mens-rea-general-intent-crimes: General Intent Crimes Can Support Conspiracy
- **Rule:** Conspiracy is not limited to specific intent crimes; a defendant can conspire to commit a general intent crime if the parties agree to engage in the prohibited conduct. What conspiracy *cannot* accommodate is a negligence crime, because one cannot agree to accomplish an unintended result.
- **Source:** *People v. Koenig* (Cal. 2020) (note case, following *Pond*)
- **Trap:** Students over-read *Pond* to hold that all conspiracy targets must be specific intent crimes, missing that the principle cuts only against negligence-based offenses.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If the target crime were an offense requiring only negligence (e.g., negligent homicide), conspiracy to commit it would be unavailable under *Koenig*.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority

### Overt Act

#### overt-act-minimal: The Overt Act Requirement Is Extremely Low
- **Rule:** Most jurisdictions require an overt act in furtherance of the conspiracy, but the act need only be any step toward the goal — a phone call, a plane ticket, a library book checkout. It need not be substantial, illegal, or close to completion of the crime.
- **Source:** Chapter text (citing *Hyde v. United States*, Holmes, J., dissenting); MPC § 5.03(5)
- **Trap:** Students conflate the overt-act standard with the "substantial step" required for attempt, applying a much higher bar than conspiracy law imposes.
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** The MPC limits the overt act requirement to lesser felonies; first- and second-degree felonies do not require an overt act at all.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority (requires overt act); federal statute (§ 371) requires "any act"; MPC creates partial exemption for serious felonies.
- **Practiced:** Slides 7–8 — "Combine: 1 agreement, 1 overt act. You're Done!" and formal doctrine summary.

### Pinkerton Liability (Extension)

#### pinkerton-vicarious-scope: Each Conspirator Liable for All Foreseeable Co-Conspirator Crimes
- **Rule:** Under *Pinkerton*, each member of a conspiracy is liable for the substantive offenses committed by any co-conspirator in furtherance of the conspiracy, provided those offenses were reasonably foreseeable as necessary or natural consequences of the agreement — even if the defendant had no direct knowledge of or participation in the specific act.
- **Source:** *Pinkerton v. United States* (1946)
- **Trap:** Students apply *Pinkerton* only to crimes the defendant personally knew about, missing that the standard is foreseeability as a natural consequence of the agreement, not actual knowledge.
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** If one conspirator commits a crime that falls entirely outside the scope of the conspiracy (e.g., a personal assault unrelated to the criminal enterprise), *Pinkerton* does not impute that crime to the others.
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC) — *Pinkerton* is federal law and most traditional states; MPC and most reformed codes reject it.
- **Practiced:** Slides 33–34, 37 — *Pinkerton* definition, Daniel-in-jail image, and the bank robbery comparison table.

#### pinkerton-mpc-rejection: MPC Rejects Pinkerton — Accomplice Elements Still Required
- **Rule:** Under the MPC, a conspirator is NOT automatically liable for co-conspirators' crimes merely by virtue of membership in the conspiracy; the traditional elements of accomplice liability (purpose to promote or facilitate the specific offense) must independently be satisfied.
- **Source:** MPC § 2.06; chapter note
- **Trap:** Students apply *Pinkerton* automatically in all jurisdictions, failing to check whether the jurisdiction has adopted the MPC's rejection of vicarious conspiracy liability.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** In a reformed-code state, a conspirator who had no knowledge of and did not facilitate a co-conspirator's specific crime would not be liable for it — the opposite of the *Pinkerton* result.
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC) — most reformed states follow MPC.
- **Practiced:** Slide 37 — Pinkerton vs. accomplice liability comparison table (implicit in the contrast shown).

#### pinkerton-girlfriend-problem: Quantity Attribution and Mandatory Minimums
- **Rule:** Because *Pinkerton* attributes the *total quantity* of drugs handled by any co-conspirator to every member of the conspiracy, and federal mandatory minimums are triggered by quantity, a peripheral participant can face the same sentence as the organizer.
- **Source:** Chapter text (drug sting section — "girlfriend problem")
- **Trap:** Students assume that peripheral role reduces criminal exposure in drug conspiracies; under *Pinkerton* + mandatory minimums, it may not.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** In an MPC or reformed-code jurisdiction that has abolished mandatory minimums, peripheral participants would be sentenced based on their individual conduct rather than total conspiracy quantity.
- **Jurisdiction:** federal law (most severe application); some states follow similar attribution rules.

### Withdrawal (Limit)

#### withdrawal-requirements: Withdrawal Requires Affirmative Steps and Communication
- **Rule:** To effectively withdraw from a conspiracy, a defendant must: (1) take affirmative steps to disavow or defeat the objectives of the conspiracy, and (2) communicate those steps to co-conspirators or disclose the scheme to law enforcement. Mere abandonment — ceasing participation without affirmative action — is not sufficient.
- **Source:** Chapter text (Pinkerton excerpt: "no evidence of affirmative action... necessary to establish withdrawal"); slides presenting two-prong test
- **Trap:** Students treat walking away from a conspiracy as withdrawal, missing that silence does not satisfy the communication requirement.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** Under the MPC, withdrawal terminates future liability for subsequent crimes; at common law, withdrawal does not erase liability for the conspiracy itself but may bar liability for subsequent substantive offenses.
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC) — traditional: withdrawal is demanding and cuts off future liability only; MPC: withdrawal terminates all liability going forward.
- **Practiced:** Slides 9–11 — Brad-gets-cold-feet hypothetical, two-prong test (Slide 10), "withdrawal: allowed but demanding" (Slide 11).

### Wharton's Rule (Limit)

#### whartons-rule: Two-Party Crimes Cannot Be Prosecuted as Conspiracy
- **Rule:** When a crime by definition requires the participation of two people, an agreement between only those two is not separately punishable as conspiracy — the agreement is already embedded in the offense. But if more parties participate than the crime logically requires, a conspiracy charge may lie. Wharton's Rule is a judicial presumption that the legislature can override.
- **Source:** Chapter text; *Iannelli v. United States* (1975) (illustrative)
- **Trap:** Students apply Wharton's Rule categorically to all two-party crimes without checking whether the legislature has expressly overridden it or whether more than the minimum number of parties are involved.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If a third party joins the scheme (e.g., a facilitator in a bribery scheme), the Rule no longer applies and conspiracy may be charged.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority (traditional and many reformed states); subject to legislative override.

### Legislative Exemption (Limit)

#### legislative-exemption-gebardi: Legislature's Choice Not to Punish One Party Bars Conspiracy
- **Rule:** When the legislature deliberately excluded one class of participants from criminal liability under a statute, that class cannot be charged with conspiracy to commit the offense — Congress's protective silence would be circumvented if conspiracy supplied liability.
- **Source:** *Gebardi v. United States* (1932) (note case)
- **Trap:** Students charge the transported woman under the conspiracy statute without asking whether Congress's failure to criminalize her voluntary participation signals a protective legislative choice.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If the statute had explicitly punished both the transporter and the transported person, the legislative exemption would not apply and conspiracy could be charged against both.
- **Jurisdiction:** federal law; principle broadly followed.

### Scope of Conspiracy

#### scope-single-vs-multiple: Single vs. Multiple Conspiracies (Wheel Without a Rim)
- **Rule:** Whether defendants are part of one conspiracy or many turns on whether a common thread runs through all participants' agreements — not just a common actor at the center. Without a "rim" (mutual awareness and interdependence among spokes), there are multiple separate conspiracies. The distinction matters for joinder, hearsay admissibility, *Pinkerton* reach, and statutes of limitations.
- **Source:** *Kotteakos v. United States* (1946)
- **Trap:** Students assume the presence of a common organizer proves a single conspiracy, missing the rim requirement and its procedural consequences.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If the borrowers in *Kotteakos* had shared a common stake in maintaining the overall fraud program (e.g., each borrower needed the others' fraud to escape detection), a single conspiracy would be more defensible.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal
- **Practiced:** Slide 29 — wheel-without-rim diagram and *Kotteakos* holding.

### Conspiracy as System Architecture (Policy)

#### system-architecture-manufactured: Unilateral Rule + Substantial Step + Forfeiture as System
- **Rule:** The unilateral conspiracy rule, the substantial step test for attempt, mandatory minimums, and civil asset forfeiture interact to create a system that enables government-manufactured conspiracies, incentivizes sting operations regardless of crime-reduction value, and produces sentencing disparities between organizers (who can cooperate) and peripheral participants (who have nothing to trade).
- **Source:** Chapter text (Conspiracy as System Architecture section); Slide 39 ("The Machine") and Slide 40 (empirical evidence)
- **Trap:** Students analyze each doctrine in isolation and miss that the doctrinal interaction — not any single feature — produces the systemic outcome the chapter critiques.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If forfeiture proceeds were prohibited from flowing back to investigating agencies (as in New Mexico's reform), the financial incentive for reverse sting operations would disappear, even though the substantive conspiracy doctrine remained unchanged.
- **Jurisdiction:** federal law (most severe application); state variation is significant.
- **Practiced:** Slides 39–40 — "The Machine" system architecture diagram; empirical data on drug stings and forfeiture.

#### system-architecture-racial-disparities: Enforcement Asymmetry and Racial Disparities
- **Rule:** Conspiracy's procedural advantages (relaxed venue, co-conspirator hearsay, joint trial) and mandatory minimums are deployed asymmetrically: aggressively against marginalized defendants in drug cases, rarely against corporate actors. At least 91% of ATF reverse-sting defendants were racial or ethnic minorities; Black defendants face mandatory minimum charges at 75% higher rates than comparable white defendants.
- **Source:** Chapter text (Drug Stings and Manufactured Conspiracy section; Corporate Conspiracy section)
- **Trap:** Students treat prosecutorial discretion as a neutral application of doctrine rather than recognizing it as the mechanism through which systemic racial disparities are produced.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If the same inference-of-agreement and foreseeability standards applied to Purdue Pharma executives were applied with equal rigor, the chapter suggests executives would face conspiracy charges — the asymmetry is a product of prosecutorial choice, not doctrinal limits.
- **Jurisdiction:** federal law (most documented); some state-level variation.

## Clusters

### cluster-agreement-inference-progression
- **Refinements:** agreement-explicit-vs-inferred, agreement-interstate-mutual-awareness, agreement-wheel-without-rim, agreement-alvarez-minimal-conduct
- **Why they cluster:** All four refinements trace the arc of how agreement can be inferred — from sophisticated mutual awareness (*Interstate*) to wheel-without-rim fragmentation (*Kotteakos*) to a day laborer's nod (*Alvarez*) — and exam questions naturally test whether a given fact pattern crosses the threshold.
- **Scenario hook:** Several defendants, unknown to each other, each receive instructions from the same organizer and execute parallel tasks; did they conspire with each other or only with the organizer?

### cluster-mens-rea-supplier
- **Refinements:** mens-rea-purpose-required, mens-rea-lauria-inferences, mens-rea-purpose-every-element
- **Why they cluster:** *Lauria* and *Pond* both turn on the granularity of purpose — *Lauria* on purpose toward the agreement itself, *Pond* on purpose as to each element of the target crime — and a fact pattern about a supplier in a multi-party scheme can require both analyses.
- **Scenario hook:** A warehouse operator knows that a tenant uses his facilities to store stolen electronics, receives above-market rent, and agrees when the tenant asks him to "keep things organized" during an upcoming shipment; is he a conspirator to robbery?

### cluster-undercover-agent
- **Refinements:** agreement-bilateral-vs-unilateral, withdrawal-requirements, system-architecture-manufactured
- **Why they cluster:** Government-manufactured conspiracies implicate the bilateral/unilateral split (was there a real agreement?), withdrawal doctrine (can a defendant who backs out after an undercover contact escape liability?), and the systemic critique of sting operations.
- **Scenario hook:** An undercover DEA agent proposes a drug deal, the defendant agrees and makes one phone call to line up a driver, then tells the agent "I'm out" the next day; analyze liability in a traditional vs. MPC jurisdiction.

### cluster-pinkerton-scope
- **Refinements:** pinkerton-vicarious-scope, pinkerton-mpc-rejection, pinkerton-girlfriend-problem, scope-single-vs-multiple
- **Why they cluster:** The scope of a conspiracy determines who is subject to *Pinkerton* liability, and the quantity-attribution problem only arises once the defendant is inside a single conspiracy — these four points must be analyzed together in any drug trafficking or organized-crime fact pattern.
- **Scenario hook:** A girlfriend drives her partner to one drug deal and is unaware of three others; the partnership is charged as a single conspiracy involving 10 kilos; analyze her liability in a federal court vs. an MPC jurisdiction.

### cluster-structural-limits
- **Refinements:** whartons-rule, legislative-exemption-gebardi, buyer-seller
- **Why they cluster:** All three are categorical bars to conspiracy liability that students must check after establishing prima facie elements; they arise from legislative structure rather than factual insufficiency and can terminate liability at the outset.
- **Scenario hook:** A woman and her married lover agree to engage in adultery and arrange a meeting; additionally, she agrees to pay him $500 "for his time"; analyze conspiracy charges under Wharton's Rule and the legislative exemption.

### cluster-corporate-vs-drug-enforcement
- **Refinements:** mens-rea-lauria-inferences, system-architecture-manufactured, system-architecture-racial-disparities
- **Why they cluster:** The chapter's central policy critique pairs *Lauria* (what inference of purpose from corporate knowledge?) with the enforcement asymmetry between Purdue Pharma (no executive convicted) and drug sting defendants (peripheral participants sentenced on organizer quantities).
- **Scenario hook:** A pharmaceutical company's regional sales director knew that certain prescribers were writing diverted scripts and continued calling on them; compare the *Lauria* analysis for the director with the analysis for a low-level drug courier in a reverse-sting case.

## Cases

### *Interstate Circuit v. United States* (1939)
- **Holding:** Film distributors who each knew the others were being asked to join a coordinated restraint-of-trade scheme were guilty of conspiracy, even without direct communication among themselves.
- **Reasoning:** Knowing participation in a plan where concerted action was both contemplated and essential suffices for a conspiratorial agreement; each party's adherence made the scheme possible.
- **Facts:** O'Donnell sent identical letters to all eight distributors, each letter naming the others as addressees, requesting uniform pricing restrictions on subsequent-run theaters as a condition of continued exhibition rights.
- **Teaches:** agreement-explicit-vs-inferred, agreement-interstate-mutual-awareness
- **Source type:** main case
- **Discussion questions:** no

### *Kotteakos v. United States* (1946)
- **Holding:** Evidence establishing eight or more separate conspiracies — each involving only Brown and individual borrowers, with no cross-awareness among borrowers — could not support a conviction for a single general conspiracy; the variance was prejudicial.
- **Reasoning:** The gist of conspiracy is agreement; one who conspires separately with a common figure is not thereby a party to all of that figure's other conspiracies, and trying all together was prejudicial because evidence from each conspiracy was admitted against all.
- **Facts:** Brown helped numerous borrowers submit fraudulent FHA loan applications; borrowers had no knowledge of or dealings with each other.
- **Teaches:** agreement-wheel-without-rim, scope-single-vs-multiple
- **Source type:** main case
- **Discussion questions:** yes

### *State v. Pond* (Conn. 2015)
- **Holding:** A defendant convicted of conspiracy to commit second-degree robbery must specifically intend that every element of the target offense — including the weapon display element — be accomplished, even though the weapon element carries no specific intent requirement for the completed crime.
- **Reasoning:** Conspiracy is an inchoate specific intent offense; the intent to commit "the offense" means intent to bring about every element, so the conspirator's purpose must encompass even strict-liability or general-intent elements of the target crime.
- **Facts:** Pond waited outside as lookout while co-conspirators robbed a store; one made a threatening gesture implying a weapon during the robbery; the men had discussed a robbery in general terms but not weapon use.
- **Teaches:** mens-rea-purpose-every-element
- **Source type:** main case
- **Discussion questions:** yes

### *People v. Lauria* (Cal. 1967)
- **Holding:** A telephone answering service operator who knew prostitutes used his service for business but had no inflated charges, no financial stake, and whose crime involved only a misdemeanor was not guilty of conspiracy to commit prostitution.
- **Reasoning:** Knowledge of criminal use is not equivalent to purpose to promote it; purpose can be inferred from (1) a stake in the venture, (2) absence of legitimate use, or (3) aggravated crime gravity — none of which was present.
- **Facts:** Police monitored prostitutes' phones; Lauria acknowledged knowing one customer was a prostitute but denied any involvement; his service was used by legitimate customers too and his fees were not inflated.
- **Teaches:** mens-rea-purpose-required, mens-rea-lauria-inferences
- **Source type:** main case
- **Discussion questions:** yes (three discussion questions, including Purdue Pharma and social media platforms)

### *Pinkerton v. United States* (1946)
- **Holding:** Daniel Pinkerton was properly convicted of substantive tax violations committed by his brother Walter, even though Daniel was incarcerated for other crimes at the time and did not directly participate in those offenses.
- **Reasoning:** So long as a conspiracy continues, each conspirator acts for the others in carrying it forward; crimes committed in furtherance of the conspiracy that could reasonably be foreseen as natural consequences of the unlawful agreement are attributable to all members.
- **Facts:** Daniel and Walter Pinkerton were brothers convicted of tax violations; Walter committed several of the charged offenses while Daniel was in prison; they had a continuous conspiratorial relationship.
- **Teaches:** pinkerton-vicarious-scope, pinkerton-mpc-rejection
- **Source type:** main case
- **Discussion questions:** yes

### *Garcia v. State* (Ind. 1979)
- **Holding:** A conspiracy conviction stands where the defendant believed she was entering a genuine agreement and acted with purpose to commit the crime, even though her only co-conspirator was an undercover officer.
- **Reasoning:** Under the unilateral approach, criminal culpability depends on the defendant's own mental state and agreement, not on whether the other party genuinely intended to carry out the crime.
- **Facts:** A woman asked a police informant to kill her husband, then paid an undercover detective $200 along with a photograph and her husband's daily schedule.
- **Teaches:** agreement-bilateral-vs-unilateral
- **Source type:** note case (illustrative)
- **Discussion questions:** no

### *State v. Pacheco* (Wash. 1994)
- **Holding:** A conspiracy conviction cannot stand when the defendant's only alleged co-conspirator was a government agent who never genuinely agreed to commit the crime.
- **Reasoning:** The bilateral approach requires at least two genuinely agreeing minds; the group danger that conspiracy law is designed to address does not arise when no real criminal group exists.
- **Facts:** Defendant conspired with what he believed was a willing partner, who was actually an undercover officer.
- **Teaches:** agreement-bilateral-vs-unilateral
- **Source type:** note case (illustrative)
- **Discussion questions:** no

### *United States v. Alvarez* (5th Cir. 1980) (en banc)
- **Holding:** Alvarez's nod, smile, and promise to be at the unloading site — viewed in context of the criminal operation — was sufficient for a jury to infer he had agreed to join the importation conspiracy.
- **Reasoning:** Contextually loaded minimal conduct, taken together with surrounding circumstances, can cross the threshold for inferring agreement even if no single act is unambiguously criminal.
- **Facts:** Alvarez drove a truck to an airport where others arranged a marijuana import flight; when asked if he'd be at the unloading site, he nodded and smiled; he unloaded his truck's appliances.
- **Teaches:** agreement-alvarez-minimal-conduct, agreement-explicit-vs-inferred
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** no

### *United States v. Pauling* (2d Cir. 2019)
- **Holding:** A mere agreement to purchase drugs for personal use or resale does not by itself establish a conspiracy to distribute; the prosecution must show shared purpose to further ongoing distribution.
- **Reasoning:** Without evidence of mutual investment in distribution beyond the immediate transaction, the parties are buyer and seller, not co-conspirators.
- **Facts:** Defendant purchased drugs; the question was whether the buyer-seller relationship rose to conspiracy.
- **Teaches:** agreement-buyer-seller
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** no

### *Gebardi v. United States* (1932)
- **Holding:** A woman who voluntarily traveled with a man across state lines for immoral purposes could not be convicted of conspiracy to violate the Mann Act, because Congress deliberately excluded the transported woman from criminal liability.
- **Reasoning:** Congress's protective silence regarding the transported woman's participation must carry over to the conspiracy statute; otherwise, the conspiracy charge would circumvent the legislative choice.
- **Facts:** A man and woman traveled together across state lines; the woman was charged with conspiracy to violate the Mann Act.
- **Teaches:** legislative-exemption-gebardi
- **Source type:** note case (illustrative)
- **Discussion questions:** no

### *Iannelli v. United States* (1975)
- **Holding:** Wharton's Rule is a judicial presumption rather than a constitutional command, so Congress can override it by expressly providing for conspiracy charges even for two-party crimes.
- **Reasoning:** Because the Rule is a presumption about legislative intent, explicit statutory evidence of Congress's contrary intent defeats the Rule.
- **Facts:** Defendants charged with conspiracy to violate a gambling statute where the underlying offense required five or more participants.
- **Teaches:** whartons-rule
- **Source type:** note case (illustrative)
- **Discussion questions:** no

### *People v. Koenig* (Cal. 2020)
- **Holding:** A defendant can conspire to commit a general intent crime (securities fraud); conspiracy is unavailable only for negligence-based offenses, not general intent crimes.
- **Reasoning:** Conspiracy's specific intent requirement is satisfied when the parties agree to engage in the prohibited conduct; the incompatibility arises only when the target crime is defined by an unintended result (negligence), not when it merely requires general intent.
- **Facts:** Defendant charged with conspiracy to commit securities fraud, a general intent crime.
- **Teaches:** mens-rea-general-intent-crimes
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** no
