## Doctrine Clusters

### Cluster: agreement-inference-and-scope
- **Doctrines:** inferring-agreement, single-vs-multiple-conspiracies, buyer-seller-exception, bilateral-vs-unilateral
- **Why they cluster:** All four doctrines govern whether a legally cognizable agreement exists — the threshold question before any other conspiracy analysis proceeds.
- **Sample scenario hook:** A drug broker deals separately with six buyers who never meet each other; the government charges all in one conspiracy; one buyer only purchased small quantities and never spoke to any other buyer.

### Cluster: mens-rea-and-supplier-liability
- **Doctrines:** conspiracy-mens-rea-purpose, conspiracy-specific-intent-target-elements, knowledge-vs-purpose-supplier
- **Why they cluster:** Together they define the mental state threshold — a question that recurs whenever a defendant claims he agreed to something less than what eventually happened, or that he merely knew about criminal activity without endorsing it.
- **Sample scenario hook:** A logistics company knowingly ships packages that turn out to contain opioids; its employees knew buyers were diverting pills but received standard market rates and had many legitimate customers.

### Cluster: vicarious-liability-and-scope-of-conspiracy
- **Doctrines:** pinkerton-vicarious-liability, mpc-rejects-pinkerton, conspiracy-foreseeable-crimes-limit
- **Why they cluster:** These doctrines define how far a conspiracy member's liability extends to crimes committed by others, and which jurisdiction's rule applies — a critical sentencing and charging question.
- **Sample scenario hook:** A minor courier in a drug network — she transported one package — faces sentencing based on the total weight handled by the entire organization; the question is whether every co-conspirator's act was a "foreseeable" consequence of her agreement.

### Cluster: structural-limits-on-conspiracy
- **Doctrines:** whartons-rule, legislative-exemption-gebardi, cumulative-punishment-merger
- **Why they cluster:** All three impose categorical bars on conspiracy liability that operate independently of agreement, mens rea, or vicarious liability analysis.
- **Sample scenario hook:** A man and woman travel voluntarily across state lines for an illicit liaison; both are charged with conspiracy to violate a statute that punishes only the man; the government argues that because two people agreed, a conspiracy charge lies.

### Cluster: system-architecture-and-manufactured-conspiracy
- **Doctrines:** bilateral-vs-unilateral, drug-sting-manufactured-conspiracy, civil-asset-forfeiture-incentive, overt-act-requirement
- **Why they cluster:** These doctrines interact to explain how undercover sting operations are legally possible, why agencies deploy them, and what doctrinal limit (if any) exists.
- **Sample scenario hook:** A DEA informant approaches a financially desperate man, proposes a drug deal, supplies the drugs, arranges the buyer, and then arrests the man; the informant was paid $15,000; the man had no prior drug history.

### Cluster: conspiracy-basics
- **Doctrines:** conspiracy-elements, overt-act-requirement, conspiracy-mens-rea-purpose
- **Why they cluster:** These are the foundational building blocks taught first; every conspiracy problem requires establishing each element before reaching any doctrinal refinement.
- **Sample scenario hook:** Two classmates talk about robbing a convenience store; one later purchases ski masks but neither takes any further action; the question is whether the agreement plus the purchase suffices for conviction.

## Doctrines

### 1. conspiracy-elements
- **Rule:** Conspiracy consists of (1) an agreement between two or more persons (2) to commit a crime, (3) with intent to agree and intent that the crime be committed, and (4) in most jurisdictions, at least one overt act in furtherance of the agreement.
- **Split:** MPC § 5.03 codifies these elements; 18 U.S.C. § 371 imposes them for federal offenses. The overt-act requirement is absent for common-law felonies in some jurisdictions.
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Levers:** whether parties explicitly agreed vs. merely acted in parallel; whether the overt act was completed before arrest; whether the target offense was a felony or misdemeanor under the MPC overt-act threshold.
- **Traps:** Students [conclude that conspiracy requires a substantial step like attempt] because [they confuse the overt-act standard for conspiracy with the substantial-step standard for attempt]; Students [find no agreement because the parties never used the word "agree"] because [they confuse the legal standard — inferred from coordinated conduct — with an explicit verbal commitment].
- **Cases:** Pinkerton v. United States, State v. Pond, Kotteakos v. United States
- **Lines:** [[33, 61]]
- **Block count:** 2
- **Cross-chapter:** attempt-elements (Ch. 17)
- **Exam lens:** prosecution
- **Hypo seeds:**

### 2. overt-act-requirement
- **Rule:** Most jurisdictions require that at least one conspirator commit an overt act in furtherance of the conspiracy; unlike the substantial-step test for attempt, the overt act may be entirely legal and seemingly trivial.
- **Split:** MPC § 5.03(5) limits the overt-act requirement to felonies below first or second degree; federal conspiracy under § 371 requires an "act to effect the object."
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Levers:** how trivial the act is (buying a plane ticket vs. casing a target); whether the act was done by any conspirator vs. only by the defendant; whether the jurisdiction is MPC or traditional.
- **Traps:** Students [require a substantial step before agreeing a conspiracy exists] because [they import the attempt standard into conspiracy]; Students [think only the defendant's acts count] because [they overlook that any conspirator's overt act satisfies the requirement].
- **Cases:** Hyde v. United States (Holmes, J., dissenting)
- **Lines:** [[58, 61]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** attempt-substantial-step (Ch. 17)
- **Exam lens:** both
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - A and B agree to rob a store; B drives past the store once to note its hours — is that a sufficient overt act? — **[novel]**

### 3. inferring-agreement
- **Rule:** Because conspirators rarely announce their agreements, agreement may be inferred from coordinated conduct — circumstantial evidence showing that parties pursued the same criminal object by complementary means.
- **Split:** null
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Levers:** directness of communication between parties; whether each party knew the others were being asked to join; whether there is a plausible innocent explanation; sufficiency of each individual piece of evidence vs. the totality.
- **Traps:** Students [require direct evidence of an explicit verbal or written agreement] because [they confuse the everyday meaning of "agreement" with the legal standard, which permits inference from conduct]; Students [treat each piece of evidence as independently insufficient and stop there] because [they fail to apply the totality-of-the-circumstances standard].
- **Cases:** Interstate Circuit v. United States, Kotteakos v. United States, United States v. Alvarez, Pinkerton v. United States
- **Lines:** [[18, 27], [315, 391]]
- **Block count:** 4
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** prosecution

### 4. conspiracy-mens-rea-purpose
- **Rule:** The defendant must intend to agree (knowingly and voluntarily enter the conspiratorial relationship) and must intend that the object crime be committed; most jurisdictions require *purpose*, even if the underlying crime requires only knowledge or recklessness.
- **Split:** Traditional/MPC: both require purpose as to the conspiracy's object; the split is between jurisdictions that carry purpose through to each element of the target offense vs. those that apply the mens rea required by the substantive crime.
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Levers:** whether the defendant's mental state as to the criminal objective was purpose vs. knowledge; whether the underlying offense requires specific or general intent; whether the MPC or common law applies.
- **Traps:** Students [find conspiracy whenever a defendant knew about criminal activity] because [they conflate the knowledge element with the purpose element]; Students [think conspiracy can be established by recklessness because the target offense requires only recklessness] because [they apply the substantive offense's mens rea standard to the conspiracy charge itself].
- **Cases:** State v. Pond, People v. Lauria
- **Lines:** [[56, 59], [74, 77]]
- **Block count:** 2
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** both
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - A defendant agrees to join a group he knows is planning a "prank" that turns out to be criminal assault — does he have the mens rea for conspiracy to commit assault? — **[novel]**
  - A taxi driver knowingly drives a passenger to a drug deal location every week but charges only his standard fare — conspiracy? — **[practiced: slide 20]**

### 5. conspiracy-specific-intent-target-elements
- **Rule:** A conspirator must specifically intend that every element of the target offense be accomplished, including elements that carry no specific-intent requirement for the principal offender.
- **Split:** *State v. Pond* (Connecticut) requires specific intent as to every element even if the element is not a specific-intent element for the principal; the *Pond* dissent argues this creates an irrational gap where conspiracy requires a higher mens rea than the completed offense; other jurisdictions apply the principal's mens rea standard.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Levers:** which element of the target offense is at issue; whether that element requires specific intent for the principal; the jurisdiction's approach to conspiracy mens rea; whether the element arose contingently during commission vs. being part of the plan.
- **Traps:** Students [convict a conspiracy defendant because the completed crime is established] because [they fail to check whether the conspirator specifically intended every element, not just the general plan]; Students [apply the principal's lower mens rea to the conspiracy charge] because [they overlook the majority's rule that conspiracy is a specific-intent crime as to all elements].
- **Cases:** State v. Pond, People v. Koenig
- **Lines:** [[78, 135]]
- **Block count:** 3
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** defense
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - Three people plan a robbery; two discuss a knife but the third hears only "get money from the clerk"; during the robbery the second person uses the knife — is the third guilty of conspiracy to commit robbery with a weapon? — **[practiced: slide 19]**

### 6. knowledge-vs-purpose-supplier
- **Rule:** A supplier of goods or services who knows that a customer is using them for criminal purposes is not a conspirator unless the evidence establishes purpose to promote the criminal enterprise — shown by direct evidence, a stake in the venture, or the aggravated nature of the crime.
- **Split:** *Lauria* (California) requires purpose; the MPC adopts the same standard; some jurisdictions permit an inference of purpose from mere knowledge combined with the gravity of the crime.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Levers:** whether the supplier charges inflated prices or receives a commission; whether the goods/services have substantial legitimate uses; the severity of the underlying crime (misdemeanor vs. grave felony); whether the supplier advertises or tailors services to criminal customers.
- **Traps:** Students [find conspiracy whenever the supplier knows of criminal use] because [they equate knowledge with purpose and ignore the stake/gravity framework]; Students [automatically find conspiracy when the crime is serious] because [they misread the aggravated-crime inference as eliminating the need for any additional purpose evidence rather than as one factor that shifts the inference].
- **Cases:** People v. Lauria, United States v. Falcone
- **Lines:** [[145, 240]]
- **Block count:** 4
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** both
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - A web-hosting company learns that one client is running a child-trafficking site but continues to provide service at standard rates — conspiracy? — **[practiced: slide 22]**
  - A fertilizer seller learns a customer has bought large quantities consistent only with bomb-making; the seller continues sales at market price — does the gravity of the potential crime change the analysis? — **[novel]**

### 7. bilateral-vs-unilateral
- **Rule:** Traditional (bilateral) jurisdictions require at least two genuinely agreeing minds; the MPC and a majority of reformed states adopt the unilateral approach, under which a defendant can be guilty of conspiracy even if the only "co-conspirator" was an undercover agent feigning agreement.
- **Split:** Bilateral: *State v. Pacheco* (Washington) — no conspiracy when co-conspirator is a government agent. Unilateral (MPC § 5.03): *Garcia v. State* (Indiana) — agent's secret intent irrelevant; defendant's culpability governs.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Levers:** whether the jurisdiction is traditional or MPC; whether the co-conspirator was an undercover agent or merely an unreliable ally; entrapment defense availability; interaction with sting operations.
- **Traps:** Students [find no conspiracy whenever one party was an undercover agent] because [they apply the bilateral rule without checking whether the jurisdiction has adopted the unilateral approach]; Students [convict under the bilateral approach because the defendant genuinely believed the agent was real] because [they confuse the defendant's subjective belief — which is the unilateral test — with the bilateral test's requirement of two genuine agreeing minds].
- **Cases:** State v. Pacheco, Garcia v. State, United States v. Valigura
- **Lines:** [[62, 73]]
- **Block count:** 2
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** both
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - A defendant hires an undercover detective to kill her husband, pays $200, and provides a photo — can she be convicted of conspiracy in a unilateral state? — **[practiced: slide 26]**
  - In a bilateral jurisdiction, a defendant negotiates a drug deal exclusively with an undercover agent; the agent expresses genuine-seeming enthusiasm — is there a conspiracy? — **[novel]**

### 8. single-vs-multiple-conspiracies
- **Rule:** The existence of a common figure dealing separately with independent groups who have no knowledge of or dealings with each other constitutes multiple separate conspiracies (a "wheel without a rim"), not a single conspiracy; prejudice results when evidence of each separate conspiracy is admitted against defendants in the others.
- **Split:** null — both traditional and MPC analysis require that parties to one conspiracy know of or share purpose with the overall scheme for a single conspiracy to exist.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Levers:** whether participants knew of each other; whether there was a shared overarching purpose vs. merely parallel dealings with a common actor; number of transactions; joinder and hearsay admissibility consequences.
- **Traps:** Students [find one conspiracy whenever a common organizer links all parties] because [they conflate the hub's single criminal purpose with a shared agreement among all the spokes]; Students [ignore the prejudice question] because [they focus only on whether a conspiracy exists and not on what evidence was improperly admitted against all defendants].
- **Cases:** Kotteakos v. United States, Interstate Circuit v. United States
- **Lines:** [[315, 391]]
- **Block count:** 3
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** defense
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - A loan broker helps six clients fraudulently obtain FHA loans; each client knows only the broker; they are all indicted in one conspiracy — proper? — **[practiced: slide 24]**
  - A cartel kingpin supplies drugs to three independent street-level dealers who have never met; are they in a single conspiracy? — **[novel]**

### 9. pinkerton-vicarious-liability
- **Rule:** Each member of a conspiracy is criminally liable for the substantive crimes committed by any co-conspirator in furtherance of the conspiracy, even without direct participation, as long as the crimes were foreseeable consequences of the unlawful agreement.
- **Split:** *Pinkerton* (federal and many states): broad vicarious liability. MPC and majority of reformed states: reject *Pinkerton*; require traditional accomplice-liability elements.
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Levers:** whether the co-conspirator's crime was in furtherance of the agreement; whether it was reasonably foreseeable; whether the defendant had withdrawn from the conspiracy; whether the jurisdiction follows *Pinkerton* or MPC.
- **Traps:** Students [limit liability to the crimes a defendant personally committed or directed] because [they apply accomplice-liability intuitions and forget that *Pinkerton* extends liability to all foreseeable co-conspirator acts]; Students [extend *Pinkerton* to any crime committed by any conspirator] because [they ignore the limiting principle that the crime must be in furtherance of the conspiracy and reasonably foreseeable].
- **Cases:** Pinkerton v. United States
- **Lines:** [[243, 305]]
- **Block count:** 3
- **Cross-chapter:** accomplice-liability (Ch. 18)
- **Exam lens:** prosecution
- **Hypo seeds:**
  - A is the organizer; B robs bank 1; C robs bank 2; D steals a car; none personally directs the others — who is liable for what under *Pinkerton*? — **[practiced: slide 31]**
  - A member of a drug conspiracy is in prison when a co-conspirator commits a murder to protect the operation — is the imprisoned member liable for the murder under *Pinkerton*? — **[novel]**
  - Two brothers conspire to evade liquor taxes; one later commits additional tax offenses while the other is incarcerated — *Pinkerton* holding applied? — **[practiced: slide 31]**

### 10. mpc-rejects-pinkerton
- **Rule:** The MPC rejects the *Pinkerton* doctrine; under MPC § 2.06, a conspirator can only be held liable for a co-conspirator's substantive offense if the traditional elements of accomplice liability are independently satisfied — i.e., the defendant actually aided, abetted, or encouraged that specific offense with purpose to promote it.
- **Split:** Most reformed state codes follow MPC and reject *Pinkerton*; federal courts and many unreformed states retain *Pinkerton*.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Levers:** whether the jurisdiction has adopted the MPC; whether the defendant did anything to aid the specific offense beyond being a conspirator; the scope of the conspiracy agreement.
- **Traps:** Students [apply *Pinkerton* in an MPC jurisdiction] because [they learn the federal rule and fail to check whether the state has rejected vicarious conspiracy liability]; Students [think MPC membership in the conspiracy is enough for accomplice liability] because [they conflate conspiring to commit a crime with aiding or abetting each crime committed in furtherance of it].
- **Cases:** Pinkerton v. United States
- **Lines:** [[299, 305]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** accomplice-liability (Ch. 18)
- **Exam lens:** defense
- **Hypo seeds:**

  - A defendant is a low-level courier in a drug conspiracy in a federal court; can she be held liable under *Pinkerton* for the entire organization's drug quantities? — **[novel]**

### 11. conspiracy-foreseeable-crimes-limit
- **Rule:** *Pinkerton* liability does not extend to a substantive offense committed by a co-conspirator that was not in furtherance of the conspiracy, did not fall within the scope of the unlawful project, or could not reasonably be foreseen as a natural consequence of the unlawful agreement.
- **Split:** null — the majority in *Pinkerton* itself recognized this limit, though its practical scope is disputed.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Levers:** how closely the crime relates to the conspiracy's stated purpose; whether the crime was improvised vs. planned; foreseeability in the context of the type of criminal enterprise (e.g., drug trafficking makes violence foreseeable).
- **Traps:** Students [extend *Pinkerton* without limit because once a conspiracy exists everything a member does is foreseeable] because [they read the rule too broadly and forget the limiting principle]; Students [use the limiting principle to exonerate defendants in drug conspiracies from violence liability] because [they fail to recognize that violence is routinely foreseeable in drug conspiracies].
- **Cases:** Pinkerton v. United States
- **Lines:** [[263, 268], [284, 297]]
- **Block count:** 2
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** defense
- **Hypo seeds:**

### 12. buyer-seller-exception
- **Rule:** A mere buyer-seller relationship in drug transactions does not constitute a conspiracy to distribute; a conspiracy exists only where evidence shows a shared purpose between seller and buyer to further the distribution — hallmarks include prolonged dealings, coded language, and credit/fronting arrangements.
- **Split:** null — *United States v. Pauling* (2d Cir.) states the majority rule; most federal circuits agree.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Levers:** single purchase vs. ongoing dealings; whether drugs were fronted on credit; use of coded language; whether buyer resells vs. uses personally; degree of coordination on downstream distribution.
- **Traps:** Students [find conspiracy whenever a buyer and seller exchange drugs] because [they equate a sales transaction with an agreement to further distribution]; Students [require proof the buyer actually resold] because [they confuse evidence relevant to the shared-purpose inference with a required element].
- **Cases:** United States v. Pauling
- **Lines:** [[393, 396]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** defense

### 13. whartons-rule
- **Rule:** An agreement between two persons to commit a crime that by definition requires the participation of two persons cannot be prosecuted as a conspiracy; if more persons participate than are logically required, a conspiracy charge may lie; the rule is a rebuttable judicial presumption, not a constitutional command.
- **Split:** *Iannelli v. United States* characterizes Wharton's Rule as a judicial presumption that legislatures can override; some jurisdictions treat it more categorically.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Levers:** how many people the substantive offense requires vs. how many participated; whether the legislature has explicitly authorized conspiracy charges; the classic examples (dueling, bigamy, incest, adultery, bribery) vs. novel applications.
- **Traps:** Students [apply Wharton's Rule to any crime requiring two participants] because [they extend the rule beyond its classic examples without asking whether the legislature has indicated a contrary intent]; Students [conclude Wharton's Rule is absolute] because [they miss the "more parties than necessary" exception and *Iannelli*'s characterization as a rebuttable presumption].
- **Cases:** Iannelli v. United States
- **Lines:** [[397, 401]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** defense

### 14. legislative-exemption-gebardi
- **Rule:** When the legislature defines a crime in terms that protect or exempt one class of participants, that class cannot be prosecuted for conspiring to commit the offense; the exemption reflects a legislative determination that their participation should go unpunished.
- **Split:** null — *Gebardi v. United States* states the principle; the dispute is over its extension to novel statutory contexts.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Levers:** whether the legislature expressly or impliedly excluded the class; whether the exemption is protective vs. merely a drafting artifact; whether additional participants bring the excluded party back within conspiracy liability.
- **Traps:** Students [convict the exempt party of conspiracy because she voluntarily participated and knew of the crime] because [they apply general conspiracy principles without checking whether the legislature implicitly excluded her class from liability]; Students [limit *Gebardi* to the Mann Act] because [they treat it as a narrow statutory holding rather than a general principle].
- **Cases:** Gebardi v. United States
- **Lines:** [[402, 408]]
- **Block count:** 1
- **Cross-chapter:** null
- **Exam lens:** defense

### 15. cumulative-punishment-merger
- **Rule:** Under the MPC, the conspiracy merges with the completed substantive offense — a defendant cannot be punished for both the conspiracy and the crime it produced; traditional jurisdictions permit cumulative punishment.

- **Traps:** Students [allow cumulative punishment in all jurisdictions] because [they learn the federal/traditional rule and forget that MPC jurisdictions merge conspiracy into the completed offense]; Students [merge conspiracy and crime even in traditional jurisdictions] because [they assume the MPC rule is universal].

### 16. drug-sting-manufactured-conspiracy
- **Rule:** In a reverse-sting operation, the government itself supplies the drugs and then charges defendants with conspiracy to distribute; under the unilateral approach, agreement with an undercover agent satisfies the conspiracy requirement; the bilateral approach and entrapment doctrine are the primary defenses.
- **Split:** Federal circuits uniformly permit reverse-sting conspiracy charges under the unilateral rule; some scholars argue such prosecutions raise due process concerns when the government was the sole source of criminal opportunity, motive, and means.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Levers:** whether the defendant had predisposition to commit the crime; the government's role (proposer vs. mere provider); whether the bilateral or unilateral rule applies; entrapment doctrine; racial disparities in targeting.
- **Traps:** Students [find no conspiracy because the government supplied the drugs and was the "co-conspirator"] because [they apply the bilateral rule in a unilateral jurisdiction]; Students [dismiss entrapment as a defense because the defendant agreed to the deal] because [they conflate agreement with predisposition and ignore the inducement element of entrapment].
- **Cases:** Garcia v. State

### 17. civil-asset-forfeiture-incentive
- **Rule:** Civil asset forfeiture allows law enforcement to seize and retain property connected to criminal activity, often without a conviction; the federal Equitable Sharing Program permits local agencies to bypass state restrictions by routing seizures through federal proceedings; this creates a financial incentive to conduct reverse stings that generate large cash seizures.

- **Cases:** null (policy material)

## Cases

### State v. Pond
- **Lines:** [78, 131]
- **Holding:** To be convicted of conspiracy to commit robbery in the second degree, a defendant must specifically intend that every element of the planned offense be accomplished, including an element (weapon display) that carries no specific-intent requirement for the principal.
- **Reasoning:** Because conspiracy is defined by agreement and the intent to perform the target crime's conduct, the conspirator's intent must encompass all essential elements of the object crime — not merely the general plan — since the inchoate offense exists independently of whether the principal satisfies the mens rea required for the completed offense.
- **Facts:** Pond was a lookout in a robbery planned without any mention of a weapon; a co-perpetrator gestured as if armed during the robbery; Pond was convicted of conspiracy to commit robbery in the second degree.
- **Doctrines:** conspiracy-specific-intent-target-elements, conspiracy-mens-rea-purpose

### People v. Lauria
- **Lines:** [149, 192]
- **Holding:** A supplier who knows his customers use his services for criminal purposes is not a conspirator absent evidence of purpose to promote the criminal enterprise — shown by direct evidence, a stake in the venture, or the aggravated nature of the crime.
- **Reasoning:** Knowledge of criminal use satisfies only one component of intent; because intent requires both a knowledge component and a purpose component, a supplier who lacks any financial stake, provides services to legitimate customers as well, and whose customer's crime is a misdemeanor has not demonstrated the purpose necessary for conspiracy.
- **Facts:** Lauria ran a telephone answering service knowing clients were prostitutes; his charges were standard, he had no stake in their earnings, and prostitution was a misdemeanor.
- **Doctrines:** knowledge-vs-purpose-supplier, conspiracy-mens-rea-purpose

### Pinkerton v. United States
- **Lines:** [247, 282]
- **Holding:** Each member of a conspiracy is liable for the substantive offenses committed by a co-conspirator in furtherance of the conspiracy, even without direct participation, as long as the offense was within the scope of the unlawful project and could be reasonably foreseen as a natural consequence of the agreement.
- **Reasoning:** The unlawful agreement contemplates the criminal acts performed in its execution; if an overt act of one partner can be attributed to all conspirators for purposes of the conspiracy charge, acts in furtherance of the conspiracy can likewise be attributed to all for purposes of substantive liability — the partnership-in-crime continues and partners act for each other.
- **Facts:** Daniel and Walter Pinkerton conspired to evade liquor taxes; Daniel had no direct role in Walter's substantive offenses, was in prison during some of them, yet was convicted of the substantive counts because Walter committed them in furtherance of their continuing conspiracy.
- **Doctrines:** pinkerton-vicarious-liability, conspiracy-foreseeable-crimes-limit, mpc-rejects-pinkerton

### Interstate Circuit v. United States
- **Lines:** [319, 344]
- **Holding:** When parties know that concerted action is contemplated and invited, give their adherence to a scheme, and participate in it knowing that cooperation is essential to success and that all others are participating, an unlawful agreement may be found even without proof of direct communication between the parties or explicit agreement among themselves.
- **Reasoning:** It taxes credulity to believe that sophisticated distributors would, in the circumstances — each knowing the others were being asked to join and that without substantially unanimous action they risked loss of business — have adopted identical far-reaching changes in business practice through mere chance; the inference of agreement is overwhelmingly supported even without direct evidence of inter-distributor communication.
- **Facts:** O'Donnell sent a single letter to eight film distributors, naming all as addressees, demanding pricing and double-feature restrictions; all eight complied with substantial unanimity.
- **Doctrines:** inferring-agreement, single-vs-multiple-conspiracies

### Kotteakos v. United States
- **Lines:** [346, 377]
- **Holding:** When the evidence proves not one conspiracy but multiple separate ones sharing only a common figure, the government cannot charge all defendants as a single conspiracy; trying them together on a single-conspiracy theory and admitting each conspiracy's evidence against all defendants is prejudicial error requiring reversal.
- **Reasoning:** The gist of conspiracy is agreement, and agreement requires a shared overarching purpose known to and embraced by all parties; without a "rim to the wheel" — knowledge of and dealings with each other — each spoke represents only a separate bilateral conspiracy with the hub, not a single multilateral agreement.
- **Facts:** Loan broker Brown helped numerous borrowers independently obtain fraudulent FHA loans; the borrowers had no knowledge of or dealings with each other.
- **Doctrines:** single-vs-multiple-conspiracies, inferring-agreement

### United States v. Alvarez
- **Lines:** [379, 386]
- **Holding:** (En banc Fifth Circuit) A defendant's promise, in context, to be present at an unloading site for a marijuana importation, combined with surrounding circumstances, is sufficient for a jury to infer that he agreed to participate in the importation conspiracy.
- **Reasoning:** The en banc majority concluded that Alvarez's nod and question in the context of an ongoing criminal operation — not a casual encounter — supplied sufficient circumstantial evidence for a jury to infer agreement; the dissent, joined by nine judges, contended this eviscerated the agreement requirement.
- **Facts:** Alvarez drove a pickup to an airport where others were arranging a marijuana flight; an undercover agent asked if he planned to be at the unloading site; Alvarez nodded, smiled, and asked if the agent was going on the plane.
- **Doctrines:** inferring-agreement

### State v. Pacheco
- **Lines:** [62, 67]
- **Holding:** (Washington) Under the bilateral approach, no conspiracy exists when the defendant's only co-conspirator was a government agent who never genuinely intended to commit the crime.
- **Reasoning:** Conspiracy doctrine addresses the special danger of group criminal activity; when only one person genuinely agrees, there is no group and no group danger — the bilateral rule reflects this purpose and cannot be satisfied by a feigned agreement.
- **Facts:** Defendant attempted to conspire with a government informant; the informant never intended to follow through.
- **Doctrines:** bilateral-vs-unilateral

### Garcia v. State
- **Lines:** [68, 71]
- **Holding:** (Indiana, applying MPC unilateral approach) A defendant who asks an informant to kill her husband, then pays an undercover officer and provides identifying information, can be convicted of conspiracy notwithstanding the officer's secret intent not to commit the crime.
- **Reasoning:** Under the MPC's unilateral approach, the culpable party's guilt is not affected by the other party's feigned agreement; the defendant's belief that she was entering a genuine agreement and her purpose to have the crime committed are sufficient — the project's "doom" from the agent's involvement does not diminish her culpability.
- **Facts:** Woman asked an informant to kill her husband; paid an undercover detective $200 along with a photograph and daily schedule.
- **Doctrines:** bilateral-vs-unilateral

### Gebardi v. United States
- **Lines:** [402, 407]
- **Holding:** A woman who voluntarily transported herself across state lines for immoral purposes cannot be convicted of conspiring to violate the Mann Act because Congress's decision not to criminalize the woman's participation in the transportation implies that her participation was not to be made punishable through the conspiracy statute.
- **Reasoning:** The Mann Act's failure to condemn the voluntary transported woman's participation — even though it contemplates her as an inseparable incident of all cases — has significance for the conspiracy statute: the same participation Congress declined to punish directly cannot automatically become punishable as conspiracy.
- **Facts:** A man and woman voluntarily traveled across state lines together; both were charged with conspiracy to violate the Mann Act.
- **Doctrines:** legislative-exemption-gebardi

### Iannelli v. United States
- **Lines:** [400, 401]
- **Holding:** Wharton's Rule is a judicial presumption, not a constitutional command, and legislatures may override it; it does not bar conspiracy charges when more persons participate than are logically necessary for the underlying offense.
- **Reasoning:** Because Wharton's Rule reflects a policy judgment about redundant punishment rather than a structural feature of conspiracy, Congress retains power to authorize separate conspiracy charges for offenses that inherently require two participants.
- **Facts:** (Cited as authority for characterizing Wharton's Rule as rebuttable; specific facts of the gambling case not detailed in chapter.)
- **Doctrines:** whartons-rule
