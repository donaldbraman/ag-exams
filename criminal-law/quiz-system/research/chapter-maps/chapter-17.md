## Elements

### Prima Facie Elements

1. **Mens rea — predicate crime elements:** The defendant must possess whatever mental state the underlying offense requires (knowledge, recklessness, negligence, etc.) for all non-result elements.
2. **Mens rea — specific intent as to result:** If the target offense requires a particular *result*, the defendant must have acted with *purpose* to cause that result (or in some jurisdictions, *belief* that it would occur). This is higher than what the completed crime may require.
3. **Actus reus — beyond mere preparation:** The defendant must have taken action beyond mere preparation. Courts apply one of two tests: (a) the defendant must have come "dangerously near" or "dangerously close" to completing the crime (proximity test, minority), or (b) the defendant must have taken a "substantial step" that is "strongly corroborative" of criminal intent (MPC majority test).

### Liability Extensions

*(None specific to attempt itself — attempt is an inchoate form of liability rather than a source of extensions. Note, however, that the felony murder rule can attach death liability to an attempted predicate felony, effectively extending punishment upward beyond what attempt grading would suggest.)*

### Liability Limits

1. **Abandonment / Renunciation:** Under the MPC (and roughly half of states), a defendant who voluntarily and completely renounces criminal purpose and actually prevents the crime has an affirmative defense. Renunciation is not voluntary if motivated by increased detection risk; it is not complete if the defendant merely postpones or redirects.
2. **Impossibility (traditional common law rule):** "Legal impossibility" — where the defendant's actions, even if the facts were as believed, would not constitute a crime — is a defense at common law. "Factual impossibility" is not a defense. The distinction is notoriously unstable.
3. **Impossibility abolished (MPC majority):** Under the MPC and most states, impossibility is irrelevant. The focus is on what the defendant believed, not what actually existed.

## Refinements

### Prima Facie Element 1 & 2: Mens Rea

#### mens-rea-specific-intent: The Specific Intent Requirement
- **Rule:** Attempt requires *purpose* to cause any result element of the target offense, even when the completed crime can be committed with a lower mental state (e.g., recklessness or knowledge). This creates an asymmetry: attempted depraved-heart murder is a logical impossibility because depraved-heart murder requires only recklessness, not purpose to kill.
- **Source:** Chapter text (Introduction and Mens Rea section); MPC § 5.01(1)(b)
- **Trap:** Students apply the predicate crime's mens rea to the result element of an attempt charge, concluding that recklessness suffices for attempted murder. They confuse the predicate crime's mens rea with the attempt's additional specific-intent requirement.
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** If the same defendant who fired recklessly into a crowd *also* had a specific target he intended to kill, the specific intent requirement is satisfied for that target.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal
- **Practiced:** Slide 5 — Attempt Requirements table distinguishes mens rea for attempt vs. predicate crime; discussed again in Slide 22 re: corporate actors

#### mens-rea-purpose-vs-knowledge: Purpose vs. Belief as to Result
- **Rule:** The MPC allows conviction where the defendant acts with "belief that [the result] will cause such result without further conduct on his part" (§ 5.01(1)(b)), not only with purpose. Most common law courts require purpose. This matters at the margins for actors who are nearly certain harm will result but whose primary goal is something else (e.g., profit).
- **Source:** MPC § 5.01(1)(b); chapter Introduction discussion of Kapoor/Insys
- **Trap:** Students assume "belief" is automatically sufficient under the MPC for all attempt charges. They overlook that § 5.01(1)(b) applies only when "causing a particular result is an element of the crime."
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If Kapoor's scheme was designed to *cause* patient deaths (rather than merely risking them for profit), the specific intent gap closes.
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC)

#### mens-rea-state-v-white: Specific Intent Across Offense Categories
- **Rule:** The specific intent requirement is not limited to result crimes like murder. For possession offenses, the prosecution must prove intent to exercise dominion or control, not merely awareness of the item's presence.
- **Source:** *State v. White* (cross-reference from Chapter 15, discussed in Mens Rea section)
- **Trap:** Students assume the specific intent requirement is unique to homicide and miss that it applies to any offense where the actus reus requires a volitional element (possession, use, etc.).
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If White had reached for the gun or asked his wife to hand it to him, the intent to exercise dominion would be established.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal

#### kill-zone-theory: Kill Zone / Transferred Intent Limits
- **Rule:** The "kill zone" theory permits conviction for attempted murder of bystanders within a defendant's intended zone of fatal harm, but only when the evidence supports the "only reasonable inference" that the defendant intended to kill *everyone* in the zone to ensure the primary target's death. Conscious disregard of death risk is not enough; specific intent is required for each attempted murder. Transferred intent does not apply to attempt: a defendant cannot be convicted of attempting to kill Person B simply because he intended to kill Person A.
- **Source:** *People v. Canizales*, 7 Cal.5th 591 (2019) — main case with discussion questions
- **Trap:** Students apply the completed murder "transferred intent" doctrine to attempt, concluding that intent to kill the primary target transfers to nearby bystanders. They confuse transferred intent (completed murder) with kill zone theory (attempted murder), which requires specific proof of intent as to each victim.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If the defendant had used a pipe bomb rather than a handgun, creating force designed to kill all persons within a defined radius, the kill zone inference becomes more reasonable.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (California-developed; adopted with variations in some states, rejected or modified in others)
- **Practiced:** Slide 22 — corporate actors/Garcia discussion implicitly raises specific intent limits

### Prima Facie Element 3: Actus Reus

#### actus-reus-proximity-test: The Dangerous Proximity Test (Common Law)
- **Rule:** Under the dangerous proximity (proximity) test, a defendant has attempted a crime only when the conduct has come "dangerously near" or "dangerously close" to completion — when, in all reasonable probability, the crime itself would have been committed but for timely interference. The test focuses on how much remains to be done, not what has already been accomplished. Mere preparation, even extensive preparation, is not enough.
- **Source:** *Commonwealth v. Peaslee* (1901) — main case; *People v. Rizzo* (1927) — main case with discussion questions
- **Trap:** Students conclude that any purposeful preparatory act satisfies the proximity test because the defendant "tended" toward the crime. They confuse tendency toward the crime with being "dangerously near" completion.
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** In *Rizzo*, if the payroll carrier had been identified and approached, proximity would likely be established.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (minority: ~8–15 states; New York is the prominent modern holdout)
- **Practiced:** Slides 6, 7, 8 — Peaslee/Rizzo comparison table; proximity test limits; Slide 12 — applying tests to cases

#### actus-reus-proximity-modern: Modern Proximity Test — Murder-for-Hire
- **Rule:** Under New York's modern application of the dangerous proximity test, notes and conversations providing detailed kill instructions do not satisfy the proximity requirement when no weapons are acquired, no one travels to the scene, and no dry run is conducted. The plan's progress must be measured by the defendant's own physical steps, not by whether the defendant believes the crime has been carried out.
- **Source:** *People v. Lendof-Gonzalez*, 36 N.Y.3d 87 (2020) — main case with discussion questions
- **Trap:** Students think a defendant who believes the crime has been completed must have crossed the attempt threshold. They confuse the defendant's belief about completion with the actus reus requirement, which depends on the defendant's own acts, not their mental state about results.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If Lendof-Gonzalez had arranged and paid a deposit to a (real or pseudo) hitman who had traveled to the targets, proximity would be established — analogous to *Trepanier*.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (New York strict proximity; most states would convict under substantial step)

#### actus-reus-substantial-step: The Substantial Step Test (MPC)
- **Rule:** Under the MPC substantial step test, a defendant has attempted a crime if they have taken a "substantial step" in a course of conduct planned to culminate in the crime, where that step is "strongly corroborative" of criminal purpose. The test focuses on what the defendant has already done. MPC § 5.01(2) provides a non-exhaustive list: lying in wait, searching for the victim, reconnoitering the location, possession of materials with no lawful purpose, soliciting an innocent agent.
- **Source:** MPC § 5.01(1)(c) and § 5.01(2) — statutory text; chapter discussion of Rizzo and Lendof-Gonzalez under MPC; *Routh* and *Garner* note cases
- **Trap:** Students read "substantial step" as requiring a large or significant act and miss that even reconnaissance, searching for a victim, or possessing purpose-built materials qualifies. They underestimate how early the MPC line is drawn.
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** In *Rizzo*, under the MPC the defendants' driving around searching for the payroll carrier while armed satisfies § 5.01(2)(a) — the MPC explicitly lists "searching for the contemplated victim."
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (majority: ~30–34 states + all federal circuits)
- **Practiced:** Slides 9, 10, 11, 12 — MPC's bargain; substantial step test; comparison table; applying to Peaslee and Rizzo

#### actus-reus-substantial-step-taylor: Substantial Step and Crime-of-Violence Categorization
- **Rule:** Because the substantial step test can be satisfied by purely preparatory conduct involving no force (e.g., reconnoitering, purchasing equipment), attempted Hobbs Act robbery does not categorically qualify as a "crime of violence" under 18 U.S.C. § 924(c). Under the "categorical approach," courts ask what the statute's elements require at minimum, not what the defendant actually did.
- **Source:** *United States v. Taylor*, 596 U.S. 845 (2022) — note case (no formal excerpt)
- **Trap:** Students assume that if robbery is a crime of violence, attempted robbery must be too. They miss that the substantial step test's breadth — reaching purely non-forceful preparatory acts — breaks the categorical equivalence.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If Congress had defined attempted Hobbs Act robbery to require proof that the defendant used, attempted to use, or threatened force (rather than using the general attempt statute's substantial step test), the § 924(c) enhancement would apply.
- **Jurisdiction:** federal only

#### actus-reus-gray-zone: Gray Zone — Proximity vs. Substantial Step in Practice
- **Rule:** The choice of test determines outcomes in cases that fall between clear preparation and clear attempt. Under the substantial step test, Ryan Routh (multiple reconnaissance trips, loaded rifle hidden in shrubbery, written plan describing a "failed attempt") and Samson Garner (armed, equipped, en route to the target event, still 20 miles away) are both guilty of attempt. Under the proximity test, Garner's conviction is considerably more doubtful.
- **Source:** *Routh* (note case, 2024) and *Garner* (note case, 2023)
- **Trap:** Students apply the proximity test's "dangerously near" language but use MPC intuitions about what "matters" as preparation, reaching inconsistent hybrid outcomes.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** For Garner, if he had been arrested while approaching the festival gates rather than 20 miles away, even a proximity court would likely find attempt.
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC)
- **Practiced:** Slides 14, 18, 19 — Roske/Routh comparison; Fox/Harris comparison in Whitmer plot

### Liability Limit 1: Abandonment

#### abandonment-common-law: No Abandonment Defense at Common Law
- **Rule:** Once a defendant crosses the line from preparation to attempt under the proximity test, withdrawal is not a defense. The crime is complete. A defendant who turns away a quarter-mile from the target is either guilty (having crossed the line) or innocent (having never crossed it) — there is no credit for quitting.
- **Source:** Chapter comparison table; *Peaslee* discussion; Slide 8
- **Trap:** Students import MPC abandonment analysis into a common law proximity problem, crediting a defendant's change of heart. They forget that common law attempt is complete upon crossing the proximity threshold; there is nothing to "abandon" afterward.
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** If Peaslee had returned to the building and completed the arson after turning away, no abandonment defense would be at issue — but the turn itself cannot undo liability already attached.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (roughly half of states follow common law no-abandonment rule)
- **Practiced:** Slides 7, 8, 11, 13 — abandonment discussion; incentive structure comparison

#### abandonment-mpc: Abandonment as Affirmative Defense (MPC)
- **Rule:** Under MPC § 5.01(4), a defendant who voluntarily and completely abandons the criminal effort and actually prevents the crime has an affirmative defense. Renunciation is not voluntary if motivated by increased detection risk or apprehension risk. It is not complete if the defendant merely postpones or redirects to a similar objective. The burden of proof on the affirmative defense rests with the defendant.
- **Source:** MPC § 5.01(4); *Roske* discussion; Slides 10, 13, 14
- **Trap:** Students think any change of mind constitutes abandonment under the MPC. They miss the "complete and voluntary" requirement, particularly the rule that fear of detection disqualifies the defense — Routh fled when spotted; Roske called 911 voluntarily before law enforcement knew about her.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If Roske had been spotted by marshals and only then walked away and called 911, the fear-of-detection disqualifier might apply, and the abandonment defense would fail.
- **Jurisdiction:** split (traditional/MPC) — roughly half of states recognize abandonment
- **Practiced:** Slides 10, 13, 14 — MPC bargain; incentive structures; Roske/Routh comparison

#### abandonment-incentive-structure: Incentive Structure Comparison
- **Rule:** The MPC's "bargain" creates a different behavioral incentive than the common law. Common law: liability attaches late (near completion), but abandonment never matters. MPC: liability attaches early (any substantial step), but genuine voluntary renunciation escapes punishment entirely. The MPC produces stronger ex ante incentives to stop once the criminal project is underway; the common law produces a long window of impunity during preparation followed by inescapable liability.
- **Source:** Chapter text (substantial step section); Slides 9, 13
- **Trap:** Students evaluate the tests only by their outcomes in decided cases, missing the policy comparison about behavioral incentives for potential offenders who have not yet been caught.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** A defendant who has taken a substantial step (MPC liability) but not yet reached dangerous proximity (common law no liability) has a MPC-created incentive to stop that common law does not create.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal (policy analysis applies regardless of jurisdiction)
- **Practiced:** Slides 9, 10, 13 — incentive structure comparison; MPC's bargain framing

### Liability Limit 2 & 3: Impossibility

#### impossibility-traditional: Traditional Impossibility Doctrine (Common Law)
- **Rule:** At traditional common law, "legal impossibility" is a defense to attempt; "factual impossibility" is not. Legal impossibility: even if the facts were as the defendant believed, no crime would be committed (e.g., the statute was repealed). Factual impossibility: circumstances unknown to the defendant made the crime impossible (e.g., the victim's pocket was empty). But the line between legal and factual impossibility is unstable — the same facts can be characterized either way depending on how the target offense is defined.
- **Source:** Lady Eldon's Lace problem; chapter Impossibility section; Slide 15
- **Trap:** Students try to apply the factual/legal distinction as if it is stable, reaching different conclusions depending on how they frame the crime. They miss that the distinction is notoriously manipulation-susceptible — the same problem can be "legal" or "factual" depending on framing.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** In Lady Eldon Version A: if the question is whether importing "French lace" is a crime, it is legal impossibility; if the question is whether importing "items she believed were contraband" is a crime, it looks factual.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (minority: ~10–13 states retain legal impossibility defense)
- **Practiced:** Slide 15 — Lady Eldon's lace hypothetical with both versions

#### impossibility-mpc: MPC Abolition of Impossibility Defense
- **Rule:** Under MPC § 5.01(1)(a), a defendant is guilty of attempt if they "purposely engage in conduct which would constitute the crime if the attendant circumstances were as he believes them to be." Impossibility — whether factual or "hybrid legal" — is irrelevant. However, pure legal impossibility survives even under the MPC: you cannot attempt to violate a statute that doesn't exist. If the defendant's conduct, even under the defendant's own beliefs, does not constitute any offense, there is no attempt.
- **Source:** MPC § 5.01(1)(a); Lady Eldon's Lace; voodoo hypothetical; *People v. Jade* (note case); Slide 15
- **Trap:** Students overread the MPC abolition and conclude that *all* impossibility is irrelevant, including pure legal impossibility. They charge attempt for conduct that would not be criminal even if the defendant's beliefs were accurate.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** In Version A (lace legalized), if the defendant believed importing *any* foreign textile was illegal (not just French lace), she might have attempt liability under the MPC — the question is whether her believed circumstances constitute a crime.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (majority: ~37–40 states have abolished impossibility defense)
- **Practiced:** Slide 15 — Lady Eldon with both versions; pure legal impossibility exception noted

#### impossibility-sting-nexus: Impossibility and Government Sting Operations
- **Rule:** When impossibility arises because the government created both the criminal opportunity and the factual impossibility (e.g., posting an escort ad, then revealing the "victim" is underage), the MPC's abolition of impossibility effectively means the government can manufacture attempt liability. The entrapment defense — not impossibility — is the primary doctrinal check, but predisposition-based entrapment has proven difficult to establish.
- **Source:** *People v. Jade* (pending Michigan S.Ct.); chapter Sting Operations section; Slide 15 (note on impossibility/entrapment nexus)
- **Trap:** Students treat impossibility and entrapment as alternative defenses that cover the same ground, and assume that abolishing impossibility is no big deal because entrapment fills the gap. They miss that entrapment's predisposition test is nearly impossible to satisfy in sting cases.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If the Michigan Supreme Court shifts entrapment to an objective "government overreach" standard, the practical gap left by abolishing impossibility would narrow significantly.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (entrapment standards vary; objective standard in some states)

### Punishment of Attempts

#### grading-general-reduction: General Rule — Attempt Graded Below Completed Offense
- **Rule:** Most states punish attempts at one grade below the completed offense or at half the maximum sentence. The common law treated attempts as misdemeanors regardless of the underlying offense. Today's reduction reflects the view that harm is morally relevant: a failed attempt is treated as less culpable than a completed crime, even when the defendant's conduct and intent were identical.
- **Source:** Chapter Punishment section; comparison table; Kneer/Skoczeń (2022); Robinson and Darley (1995)
- **Trap:** Students cite the MPC's equal-grading rule as the majority approach. They confuse the MPC's *influence* on actus reus and impossibility (where it achieved majority adoption) with its grading rule (where it remains minority).
- **Difficulty:** foundational
- **Pivot fact:** If outcomes were morally irrelevant and legislatures followed through on the MPC's reasoning, sentencing would be identical for attempting and completing the same offense — but experimental psychology research shows that laypeople resist this regardless of stated principle.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (majority: ~28–35 states reduce; MPC same-grade: ~9–13 states)
- **Practiced:** Slide 11 — comparison table includes punishment row; Slide 14 — Roske (97 months) vs. Routh (life)

#### grading-mpc-equal: MPC Equal Grading
- **Rule:** Under the MPC, attempts are graded the same as the completed offense (except for capital crimes and first-degree felonies). The drafters argued that same culpability means the same "antisocial disposition" is revealed regardless of whether luck favors the defendant.
- **Source:** Chapter Punishment section; MPC § 5.05; comparison table
- **Trap:** Students treat the MPC rule as if it means defendants always receive the same sentence as completers. They miss that judges retain sentencing discretion, and that even in MPC jurisdictions, actual sentences often diverge.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** A defendant who fires at a victim and misses is equally culpable under MPC theory; under common law grading theory, the victim's survival reduces the offense grade.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (minority: ~9–13 states)

#### grading-path2-offense-specific: Path 2 — Offense-Specific Attempt Riders
- **Rule:** Congress and state legislatures increasingly write "attempts or conspires to" directly into offense definitions, imposing the same penalties as the completed crime. The most consequential example is 21 U.S.C. § 846 (federal drug attempt), which applies mandatory minimums in full to defendants arrested before any drugs change hands. Other major examples: 18 U.S.C. § 1349 (fraud), § 1951 (Hobbs Act), § 2241(c) (aggravated sexual abuse of children under 12, 30-year mandatory minimum), and major terrorism statutes. When Congress builds attempt into the offense definition, the federal guidelines' three-level reduction for attempt (USSG § 2X1.1) does not apply.
- **Source:** Chapter "Three Ways Legislatures Punish Attempts" section (Path 2)
- **Trap:** Students apply the general attempt grading reduction to drug or terrorism charges, not realizing that offense-specific attempt statutes eliminate that reduction. They fail to check whether the charged statute incorporates attempt directly.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** A defendant arrested in a sting before any drugs change hands faces the same mandatory minimum as a completed trafficker because § 846 incorporates attempt with same-grade penalties.
- **Jurisdiction:** federal only (but state equivalents exist for certain offenses)

#### grading-path3-structurally-inchoate: Path 3 — Structurally Inchoate Offenses
- **Rule:** Some offenses are completed attempts by statutory definition — burglary (entry with intent, felony need not occur), assault-as-attempted-battery, forgery (false instrument with intent to defraud, no actual deception needed). These carry full penalties because the "attempt" is the actus reus of the completed crime. "Attempted burglary" — arrested while picking the lock — is logically an attempt to commit a completed attempt.
- **Source:** Chapter "Three Ways Legislatures Punish Attempts" section (Path 3)
- **Trap:** Students try to apply attempt grading to structurally inchoate offenses, reducing burglary or forgery by one grade because the underlying "target crime" was never completed. They fail to recognize that the statutory offense is complete upon entry with intent; there is no separate "completed" version to reduce from.
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Pivot fact:** If a jurisdiction defines assault as threatened battery (not attempted battery), the assault is not a structurally inchoate offense and is graded in its own right.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal (but specific structural definitions vary by state)

#### grading-felony-murder-attempt: Felony Murder and Attempted Predicate Felonies
- **Rule:** Under the felony murder rule, a death caused during an *attempt* to perpetrate a predicate felony (robbery, burglary, kidnapping) triggers first-degree murder liability. This means a botched robbery that results in a bystander's death carries a higher penalty than the robber's attempt alone — the attempt is graded upward, not downward, by the unintended death.
- **Source:** Chapter Punishment section; cross-reference to Chapter 14 (felony murder)
- **Trap:** Students apply the attempt grading reduction to the predicate felony in a felony murder case, concluding the defendant gets a lighter sentence because the robbery was incomplete. They fail to recognize that the death liability is attached to the attempted predicate, eliminating any reduction.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If no one dies during the botched robbery, the defendant faces only attempted robbery (reduced grade); the moment a bystander dies, the predicate's incomplete nature becomes irrelevant to the murder charge.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal (in jurisdictions with felony murder rule; varies on scope of predicate offenses)

### Attempt in Practice: Who Attempt Misses

#### specific-intent-gap: The Specific Intent Gap — Corporate Actors
- **Rule:** Because attempt requires *purpose* as to the result, it cannot reach actors whose purpose is profit even when they knowingly cause deaths. Corporate executives who market a product knowing it will cause deaths are not guilty of attempted murder because their purpose is money, not death. Attempt doctrine is calibrated to catch individuals who plan discrete crimes against identifiable victims; it provides no traction against systemic harms caused by institutional actors.
- **Source:** *United States v. Simon* (Insys/Kapoor) — note case; Purdue Pharma discussion; Slide 22
- **Trap:** Students apply a "substantial risk of death" standard to attempt, treating *knowledge* that deaths will occur as equivalent to *purpose* to cause death. They conflate recklessness/knowledge with the specific intent requirement.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If an Insys executive had communicated a desire that patients die to eliminate potential witnesses, the specific intent threshold would be crossed and attempted murder would be viable.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal
- **Practiced:** Slide 22 — Garcia/Kapoor comparison; Slide 5 — purpose requirement

#### sting-operations-manufactured-crime: Sting Operations and Manufactured Crime
- **Rule:** The substantial step test, by moving the attempt line earlier, enables the government to intervene before genuine harm is imminent and creates the predicate for sting operations. Sting operations generate attempt liability against defendants whose "plots" may be substantially government-created. Empirical data suggests ~54.7% of federal terrorism cases involved government informants, with ~9% appearing to thwart genuinely threatening plots. The entrapment defense, as currently structured around "predisposition," has proven largely ineffective in sting cases.
- **Source:** *United States v. Croft* (Whitmer kidnapping plot) — note case with discussion questions; Norris & Grol-Prokopczyk (2015) data; Human Rights Watch documentation; Slides 17, 18, 19, 20
- **Trap:** Students treat the sting operation problem as purely an entrapment issue and miss that the choice of actus reus standard (substantial step vs. proximity) is what enables sting operations structurally. They analyze the doctrine without asking what it produces at scale.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If the proximity test governed federally, government infiltrators would need to wait until the plot came "dangerously near" completion before arrest — reducing the viability of early-stage stings.
- **Jurisdiction:** majority/minority (substantial step majority enables stings; proximity minority limits them)
- **Practiced:** Slides 17, 18, 19, 20 — Whitmer plot; sting data; resource question

#### resource-allocation-policy: Resource Allocation and System Design
- **Rule:** Attempt doctrine shapes prosecutorial resource allocation. Sting operations (resource-intensive, manufactured crime risk) produce attempt prosecutions. Corporate investigations (complex, slow) rarely do. Focused deterrence (direct warnings + social services) has the strongest evidence base for reducing violence but produces no attempt prosecutions. Because certainty of apprehension deters more effectively than severity of punishment, the doctrine's incentives may systematically favor less effective enforcement strategies.
- **Source:** Chapter "Attempt and System Design" section; Nagin (2013) deterrence review; Slide 23
- **Trap:** Students evaluate attempt doctrine solely on its doctrinal logic (fair notice, proximity, intent), missing the aggregate enforcement pattern it creates. They treat each case as an individual liability question rather than examining what the doctrine produces when applied at scale.
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Pivot fact:** If focused deterrence programs produced prosecutable attempt charges, prosecutorial resource allocation would likely shift significantly toward community-based approaches.
- **Jurisdiction:** universal (policy analysis)
- **Practiced:** Slide 23 — resource allocation hypothetical with three options

## Clusters

### cluster-proximity-vs-substantial-step
- **Refinements:** actus-reus-proximity-test, actus-reus-proximity-modern, actus-reus-substantial-step, actus-reus-gray-zone
- **Why they cluster:** The two tests use different focal points (what remains vs. what has been done) and produce different outcomes in the same gray-zone facts.
- **Scenario hook:** A defendant drives from out of state to the victim's neighborhood, parks outside her house with a loaded weapon, but the victim is not home; he is arrested three blocks away when police stop him for a broken taillight.

### cluster-mens-rea-gap
- **Refinements:** mens-rea-specific-intent, mens-rea-purpose-vs-knowledge, kill-zone-theory, specific-intent-gap
- **Why they cluster:** All turn on the gap between purposeful conduct (reaching attempt) and knowing or reckless conduct (falling short), and illustrate how the same rule shapes liability very differently depending on who is charged.
- **Scenario hook:** A pharmaceutical company knowingly ships contaminated insulin to hospitals in multiple states; an executive who authorized the shipment knew at least three patients would likely die but hoped no one would trace it back to him.

### cluster-abandonment-incentives
- **Refinements:** abandonment-common-law, abandonment-mpc, abandonment-incentive-structure
- **Why they cluster:** The policy tradeoff between the proximity test's no-abandonment rule and the MPC's abandonment bargain is the central debate in the chapter's middle section.
- **Scenario hook:** A defendant who has reconnoitered the target, assembled materials, and driven to the scene receives a text message from his sister begging him to stop and calls 911 — but only after noticing a police cruiser nearby.

### cluster-impossibility
- **Refinements:** impossibility-traditional, impossibility-mpc, impossibility-sting-nexus
- **Why they cluster:** Lady Eldon and sting operations both involve defendants whose conduct could not produce the actual crime they intended — but the legal treatment differs under common law, the MPC, and when the government has manufactured the impossibility.
- **Scenario hook:** An undercover agent posts a profile on an adults-only website offering escort services; when a defendant responds and negotiates, the agent reveals the escort is "actually 15." No minor ever existed.

### cluster-grading
- **Refinements:** grading-general-reduction, grading-mpc-equal, grading-path2-offense-specific, grading-path3-structurally-inchoate, grading-felony-murder-attempt
- **Why they cluster:** The formal/functional gap in attempt grading — where legislatures say "one grade below" but deliver same-grade punishment through paths 2 and 3 — is itself a doctrinal puzzle worth testing.
- **Scenario hook:** A defendant arrested in a sting before any drugs change hands, and a defendant who completes the same transaction, are both prosecuted under § 846; a third defendant botches a robbery and a bystander is killed.

### cluster-specific-intent-limits
- **Refinements:** mens-rea-specific-intent, mens-rea-state-v-white, actus-reus-substantial-step-taylor, specific-intent-gap
- **Why they cluster:** All four refinements turn on how the specific intent requirement and the substantial step test interact with non-homicide offenses — possession crimes, federal sentencing, and corporate conduct — illustrating that intent rules produce unexpected results outside the murder context.
- **Scenario hook:** A defendant who knew a gun was in his house is charged with attempted possession; a co-defendant who planned an armed robbery that fell apart before reaching the target is charged under § 924(c); a third defendant, a pharmaceutical executive, knew his drug would cause deaths but proceeded for profit.

### cluster-sting-operations
- **Refinements:** sting-operations-manufactured-crime, impossibility-sting-nexus, abandonment-mpc, resource-allocation-policy, actus-reus-substantial-step
- **Why they cluster:** Sting operations raise the substantial step threshold, impossibility abolition, abandonment doctrine, and system-level enforcement choices simultaneously.
- **Scenario hook:** An FBI informant becomes the operational leader of a kidnapping plot, drives the defendant to reconnaissance trips, and arranges the weapons purchase that triggers arrest — five of fourteen defendants are acquitted at trial.

## Cases

### Commonwealth v. Peaslee (1901)
- **Holding:** The defendant's conduct (arranging combustibles and driving toward the building before turning away) was arguably close to the attempt threshold but the court declined to decide definitively; the framework establishes that some preparations can amount to attempt if they come "very near" to accomplishment.
- **Reasoning:** Holmes reasoned that when further acts are needed to complete the crime, the defendant retains a *locus poenitentiae*; liability attaches when the probability of completion is so high that the remaining act of will is a near-certainty.
- **Facts:** Defendant arranged combustibles in a building for insurance fraud arson; offered to pay an employee to complete the plan; drove within a quarter mile, then turned around.
- **Teaches:** actus-reus-proximity-test
- **Source type:** main case
- **Discussion questions:** no (discussed in *Rizzo* discussion questions)

### People v. Rizzo (1927)
- **Holding:** Defendants who drove around the city searching for the payroll carrier but never found him were not guilty of attempted robbery under the dangerous proximity test.
- **Reasoning:** The crime could not commence until the defendants found the victim; no act that "dangerously near" completion had occurred because the necessary precondition — locating the target — never materialized.
- **Facts:** Four defendants, two armed, drove around looking for a payroll carrier to rob; police followed and arrested them when they had not yet found or approached the target.
- **Teaches:** actus-reus-proximity-test
- **Source type:** main case
- **Discussion questions:** yes

### People v. Lendof-Gonzalez (2020)
- **Holding:** Detailed notes providing murder instructions to a fellow inmate, without acquiring weapons, traveling to the scene, or conducting reconnaissance, did not satisfy New York's "dangerously close" test; defendant was convicted of criminal solicitation only.
- **Reasoning:** Proximity requires physical steps toward completion measured by the defendant's own conduct; the fact that the defendant believed the crime was carried out does not substitute for proximity in the defendant's own acts.
- **Facts:** Jailed defendant passed detailed murder-for-hire instructions to an adjacent inmate (who was actually an informant); inmate alerted authorities; defendant was "happy" when told murders were complete.
- **Teaches:** actus-reus-proximity-modern
- **Source type:** main case
- **Discussion questions:** yes

### People v. Canizales (2019)
- **Holding:** The kill zone theory requires proof that the defendant intended to kill *everyone* within a zone of fatal harm to ensure the primary target's death; five shots from a handgun at 100–160 feet in an open outdoor area do not satisfy this standard.
- **Reasoning:** The kill zone is defined by intent to create a zone of fatal harm, not by the reach of the defendant's bullets; conscious disregard of death is insufficient — the prosecution must prove specific intent to kill each alleged victim.
- **Facts:** Defendants fired five shots from ~100–160 feet at two rival gang members at a block party; neither intended target was hit but a bystander was killed; defendants were charged with attempted murder of the intended targets.
- **Teaches:** kill-zone-theory
- **Source type:** main case
- **Discussion questions:** yes

### United States v. Taylor (2022)
- **Holding:** Attempted Hobbs Act robbery is not a "crime of violence" under 18 U.S.C. § 924(c) because the substantial step test can be satisfied by purely preparatory conduct involving no force.
- **Reasoning:** Under the categorical approach, the court asks what the statute requires at minimum; because a substantial step can involve research, reconnaissance, or equipment purchases with no use or threat of force, attempted Hobbs Act robbery does not categorically require force.
- **Facts:** Defendant charged with using a firearm during a "crime of violence" (§ 924(c)) in connection with an attempted Hobbs Act robbery; government argued attempted robbery is categorically a crime of violence.
- **Teaches:** actus-reus-substantial-step-taylor
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** no

### Ryan Wesley Routh (2024)
- **Holding:** Convicted of attempted assassination after hiding with a loaded rifle at Trump International Golf Club during a golf outing; sentenced to life.
- **Reasoning:** Multiple reconnaissance trips, written plan describing a "failed attempt," and lying in wait with a loaded rifle satisfied the substantial step test; the MPC's § 5.01(2)(a) lists "lying in wait" and "reconnoitering" explicitly.
- **Facts:** Routh hid a rifle in shrubbery at the golf course while the former president golfed; fled without firing when spotted by Secret Service; convicted on overwhelming physical and documentary evidence.
- **Teaches:** actus-reus-gray-zone, actus-reus-substantial-step
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** yes (shared with *Roske*)

### Sophie Roske (2022/2025)
- **Holding:** Pleaded guilty to federal attempted murder of Justice Kavanaugh; sentenced to 97 months after judge credited voluntary abandonment as a mitigating factor under 18 U.S.C. § 3553(a).
- **Reasoning:** Federal law does not formally recognize the MPC abandonment defense, but the judge treated Roske's voluntary 911 call — before law enforcement knew of the plot — as substantial mitigation; abandonment principles shaped the outcome even without formal doctrinal status.
- **Facts:** Roske arrived by taxi at Justice Kavanaugh's home after midnight, armed with pistol, knife, and zip ties; saw marshals; walked away; called 911 33 minutes later after her sister urged her to stop.
- **Teaches:** abandonment-mpc, actus-reus-gray-zone, grading-general-reduction
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** yes (shared with *Routh*)

### Samson Garner / Smith Rock Plot (2023)
- **Holding:** Convicted of 26 felonies including attempted first-degree murder; sentenced to 30 years.
- **Reasoning:** Armed, equipped with tactical gear, and traveling toward the festival site with written evidence of intent to kill constituted a substantial step; the fact that Garner had not arrived at Smith Rock did not prevent conviction.
- **Facts:** Garner left Portland with an AR-15, two handguns, and tactical gear heading toward a climbing festival he had emailed he planned to attack; arrested 20 miles south of Smith Rock.
- **Teaches:** actus-reus-gray-zone, actus-reus-substantial-step
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** yes (shared Routh/Roske discussion)

### United States v. Croft (Whitmer Kidnapping Plot, 6th Cir. 2025)
- **Holding:** Adam Fox (192 months) and Barry Croft (235 months) convicted; Sixth Circuit affirmed, finding "insufficient evidence" that government planted the kidnapping idea; two defendants at the same trial were acquitted.
- **Reasoning:** Fox's surveillance trips, maps, and kidnapping kit demonstrated a substantial step independent of government facilitation; Daniel Harris and Brandon Caserta lacked comparable independent steps and jury found government orchestration inseparable from their conduct.
- **Facts:** FBI embedded 12 confidential informants and 2 undercover agents in a 14-person conspiracy to kidnap Michigan Governor Whitmer; lead informant became second-in-command; 5 of 14 defendants ultimately acquitted.
- **Teaches:** sting-operations-manufactured-crime, actus-reus-substantial-step, abandonment-mpc
- **Source type:** main case (with extended note discussion)
- **Discussion questions:** yes

### United States v. Simon / Insys (1st Cir. 2021)
- **Holding:** John Kapoor and other Insys executives convicted of RICO conspiracy and fraud for a scheme to bribe doctors to overprescribe fentanyl; not charged with attempted murder.
- **Reasoning:** Their purpose was profit, not death; attempt doctrine requires purpose as to the result, not merely knowledge that deaths would occur.
- **Facts:** Insys ran a kickback scheme to increase off-label fentanyl prescriptions through a sham Speaker Program and false insurance authorizations; the scheme caused deaths. Kapoor received 66 months.
- **Teaches:** specific-intent-gap, mens-rea-specific-intent
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** no (discussed in system design section)

### People v. Jade (Michigan S.Ct., argued January 2026)
- **Holding:** Pending — Michigan Supreme Court considering whether "bait-and-switch" sting technique constitutes entrapment.
- **Reasoning:** GHOST Task Force posted on adults-only website, then revealed the "escort" was underage; no minor ever existed; defendant charged with attempt under MPC-style abolition of impossibility.
- **Facts:** Police posted escort ad on adults-only site; when defendant responded, decoy revealed age as 15; defendant charged with attempted crime against a minor despite no minor's existence.
- **Teaches:** impossibility-sting-nexus, impossibility-mpc
- **Source type:** note case
- **Discussion questions:** yes (in impossibility section)
