## Finding-by-Finding Review

### Refinement: criminal-law-vs-system
- **Map claims:** "Criminal law" = agreed-upon norms; "criminal legal system" = people, practices, and resources that enforce them. Both are aspirational and imperfect.
- **Source says:** "The criminal law is, at least in theory, focused on the public response to serious public harms and wrongs. The criminal legal system is a group of people, practices, and resources that, at least in theory, enforce the criminal law. Both are aspirational and deeply imperfect." (line 3)
- **Verdict:** ACCURATE

### Refinement: classical-model-exception
- **Map claims:** The classical model (crime → report → investigate → arrest → charge → trial → verdict → sentence → appeal) is "an increasingly rare exception rather than the rule" in contemporary American practice.
- **Source says:** "this classical model is, in contemporary American practice, an increasingly rare exception rather than the rule." (line 7)
- **Verdict:** ACCURATE — direct quotation paraphrased correctly.

### Refinement: procedural-justice-four-principles
- **Map claims:** Four principles: voice, neutrality, respect, trustworthiness. Cites "annualreviews.org" procedural justice research.
- **Source says:** "(1) ensuring every person has a voice … (2) practicing neutrality … (3) conveying respect … (4) embodying trustworthiness by acting with genuine concern for people's well-being." (line 17)
- **Verdict:** ACCURATE

### Refinement: status-competition-and-criminal-law
- **Map claims:** Criminal law is a site of "status competition between groups." Value conflicts cause people to perceive conduct as harmful irrespective of evidence.
- **Source says:** "the criminal law is often the site of intense status competition between groups … value conflicts can cause people to believe that conduct inconsistent with their values is harmful, irrespective of any evidence about harm." (lines 11–13)
- **Verdict:** ACCURATE

### Refinement: anticitizenship-and-trust
- **Map claims:** Repeated encounters with coercive, disrespectful enforcement produce "anticitizenry." Cites Justice & Meares.
- **Source says:** "As Professors Benjamin Justice and Tracey Meares have argued, the criminal justice system 'educates' citizens about their relationship to the state … When that education consists of repeated encounters characterized by suspicion, coercion, and disrespect, it produces what she terms 'anticitizenry.'" (line 23)
- **Note:** The source uses "she" referring to Meares (the pronoun "she" refers to Meares, though the chapter first introduces both Justice and Meares as "Professors"). The map attributes the concept to "Justice & Meares" which is accurate as the chapter names both. The pronoun error is in the source, not the map.
- **Verdict:** ACCURATE

### Refinement: purpose-defined
- **Map claims:** Purpose = "conscious object to cause a particular result." Highest culpability. Illustrated by Garcia.
- **Source says:** "A jury convicted Garcia of first degree murder … finding that he acted with purpose—the highest level of criminal intent, meaning it was his specific conscious object to cause Markel's death." (line 43); "Sigfredo Garcia acted with the purpose of causing the death of Dan Markel." (line 101)
- **Verdict:** ACCURATE

### Refinement: knowledge-defined
- **Map claims:** Knowledge = awareness that a particular result is "practically certain to follow."
- **Source says:** "When a defendant does not intend a death but is nearly certain their conduct will cause it occur, we typically call this knowledge." (line 99)
- **Note:** The map uses "practically certain" while the source uses "nearly certain." These are functionally equivalent; neither the source nor the map uses the MPC formulation exactly. This is a minor terminological imprecision but not an error.
- **Verdict:** IMPRECISE — the source uses "nearly certain," not "practically certain." The distinction may matter when the MPC formulation ("practically certain") is introduced later in the course. Recommended fix: use "nearly certain" to track the source's language.

### Refinement: recklessness-defined
- **Map claims:** Recklessness = "consciously disregards a substantial and unjustifiable risk." Distinguished from negligence by subjective awareness requirement.
- **Source says:** "When a defendant consciously disregards a substantial and unjustifiable risk that death will result from their conduct, we call this recklessness." (line 99–100)
- **Verdict:** ACCURATE

### Refinement: negligence-defined
- **Map claims:** Negligence = failure to perceive a risk a reasonable person would have recognized. Criminal negligence requires "gross deviation."
- **Source says:** "if a defendant was unaware that their conduct would cause death but a reasonable person would have been aware, we call this negligence." (line 100)
- **Note:** The map adds "Criminal negligence typically requires a gross deviation from the reasonable person standard" — this is standard criminal law doctrine but is NOT stated in Chapter 1. The chapter provides only the basic definition. The gross-deviation gloss is anticipatory doctrine from a later chapter.
- **Verdict:** ERROR — the gross deviation gloss is not sourced in Chapter 1. It should either be removed or marked as "(see later chapters)" to avoid confusion. Students using this map to study Chapter 1 may believe the chapter established this distinction when it did not.

### Refinement: mens-rea-hierarchy
- **Map claims:** Purpose > knowledge > recklessness > negligence. Higher culpability = more serious charges. Illustrated by Garcia vs. Purdue executives.
- **Source says:** The hierarchy is implied by the order of introduction (lines 99–101) and explicitly illustrated by the contrast between Garcia's life sentence and the Purdue executives' probation. (lines 87–89, 101)
- **Verdict:** ACCURATE

### Refinement: actus-reus-prerequisite
- **Map claims:** A crime requires conduct (actus reus). No mental state suffices without a prohibited act.
- **Source says:** "a crime requires some form of conduct, sometimes referred to as the actus reus or bad act. In homicide, that conduct is causing the death of a human." (line 99)
- **Note:** The map's claim that "thinking about, planning, or desiring harm is not punishable without a prohibited act" is a correct general principle but goes beyond what Chapter 1 says explicitly. The map does not source this claim to the chapter. It is standard doctrine but introduces a nuance (inchoate offenses, conspiracy) that the chapter does not address.
- **Verdict:** IMPRECISE — the elaboration (thoughts alone are not punishable) is accurate doctrine but not stated in Chapter 1. Should be noted as general principle, not chapter-specific teaching.

### Refinement: park-doctrine-strict-liability
- **Map claims:** Park Doctrine = strict liability misdemeanor for corporate officers. No proof of mental state required. Executives can plead guilty without admitting knowledge or recklessness.
- **Source says:** "prosecutors utilized the 'Park Doctrine' to charge them with 'misbranding,' a strict liability misdemeanor. This legal mechanism was a strategic off-ramp: it allowed the executives to plead guilty without the government having to prove they acted with knowledge of the fraud or recklessness toward the substantial risk of addiction and death." (lines 87–88)
- **Verdict:** ACCURATE — the map accurately summarizes the Park Doctrine as characterized in the chapter.

### Refinement: over-enforcement-minor-offenses
- **Map claims:** Ferguson issued 32,000 warrants for a population of 21,000.
- **Source says:** "in 2013, the City of Ferguson had issued over 32,000 warrants for a population of just 21,000 people." (line 67)
- **Verdict:** ACCURATE

### Refinement: under-enforcement-corporate-harm
- **Map claims:** Purdue's $600 million fine was "readily absorbed" by a company generating billions in revenue from the same product.
- **Source says:** "By that time, the opioid crisis had already claimed an estimated 200,000 lives and was costing the U.S. economy over $50 billion per year. For Purdue Pharma, though, the fine was easily absorbed by a company generating billions in revenue." (lines 85–86)
- **Verdict:** ACCURATE

### Refinement: pretextual-stops-logic-and-reality
- **Map claims:** Pretextual stops are justified by a "highly disputed" theory of public safety.
- **Source says:** "Pretextual stops are justified with a highly-disputed theory of public safety." (line 54)
- **Verdict:** ACCURATE — direct quotation.

### Refinement: case-method-blind-spot
- **Map claims:** The case method creates a blind spot: excludes mass processing of the poor and immunity of the powerful. Future prosecutors trained only on canonical law may replicate historical enforcement patterns.
- **Source says:** "this method creates a dangerous blind spot in criminal law. Because low-level cases … are easy to prove and usually result in pleas, they rarely produce the complex appellate discussions that populate textbooks." (lines 116–117)
- **Verdict:** ACCURATE

### Refinement: human-centered-design-reform
- **Map claims:** HCD = understand → imagine → pilot → evaluate → adopt/adapt/abandon. Increasingly adopted by prosecutors.
- **Source says:** "HCD is a cyclical process increasingly adopted by modern government officials—including prosecutors—across the ideological spectrum. It begins with … understand … imagine … pilot … evaluate … adopt … adapt … or abandon." (lines 128–131)
- **Verdict:** ACCURATE
