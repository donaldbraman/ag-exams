**Q1.** Is Wallace guilty of arson or a related conduct crime for sparking the chemical fire?

(a) Guilty of a conduct crime, because he voluntarily inhaled the chemical fumes prior to the convulsion.
(b) Not guilty of a conduct crime, because his bodily movements during the neurological convulsion were not voluntary. <!-- correct -->
(c) Guilty of a conduct crime, because he was physically capable of avoiding the vat before he lost consciousness.
(d) Not guilty of a conduct crime, because he did not possess the specific intent to ignite the chemical fire.
(e) Not guilty of a conduct crime, because he abandoned his actions when he passed out from inhaling the fumes.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** (b) is correct because under MPC § 2.01, reflexes and convulsions do not constitute voluntary acts, and criminal liability must be based on conduct that includes a voluntary act. (a) fails because the time-framing doctrine requires the prior voluntary act to foreseeably create the risk of the harm, which is not established here. (c) fails because being capable of an act prior to the event does not substitute for a voluntary act directly causing the harm. (d) fails because actus reus must be established before analyzing mens rea. (e) fails because abandonment is a defense to inchoate crimes, not a negation of actus reus.
