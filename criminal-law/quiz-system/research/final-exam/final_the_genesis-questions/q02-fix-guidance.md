## Issue 1 — grounding

**Q2.** Brenda claims she cannot be convicted under Statute 101 because she relied on the Zoning Commissioner's letter. Is this a valid defense?

(a) No, because the traditional rule that ignorance of the law is no excuse applies strictly and without exception to all public welfare misdemeanors.
(b) No, because the Zoning Commissioner's letter constitutes a personal misreading of the law rather than a formally published appellate court decision.
(c) Yes, because any subjective mistake regarding the applicability of a penal statute automatically negates the mens rea required for a regulatory offense.
(d) Yes, because she reasonably relied on a written statement from an official government entity formally charged with administering the relevant law. <!-- correct -->
(e) No, because advice from a government official only provides a valid defense if the defendant also consults private counsel to verify the interpretation.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** Option (d) is correct because reasonable reliance on an official statement of the law from a public officer or body charged with interpretation or administration of that law (like a Zoning Commissioner granting a variance) is a well-established exception to the "ignorance of the law is no excuse" rule. Option (a) is incorrect because the official statement exception is a specific carve-out to the traditional rule. Option (b) is incorrect because an official agency letter qualifies as an official statement; published appellate decisions are not the sole source of official reliance. Option (c) is incorrect because ordinary subjective mistakes of law do not negate mens rea unless the statute explicitly requires knowledge of illegality. Option (e) is incorrect because there is no requirement to independently verify an official government statement with private counsel.

**Tags:** chapters: [10], topics: [mistake of law, official statement exception], difficulty: easy, cognitive: application

**Grounding:** Chapter 10, Mistake of Law - Reasonable Reliance on Official Statements (Cox v. Louisiana)
