**Q4.** Barksdale Logistics is charged under the municipal ordinance for operating without a safety inspection certificate. Does this qualify as a strict-liability public welfare offense?

(a) No, because strict liability is constitutionally prohibited for any offense that carries a financial penalty.
(b) No, because the ordinance fails to require inherently wrongful conduct, which is necessary for strict liability.
(c) Yes, because it regulates commercial conduct for public safety and carries a minor penalty of a $500 fine. <!-- correct -->
(d) Yes, because municipal governments lack the authority to include mens rea requirements in their local ordinances.
(e) Yes, because the operation of a commercial warehouse is considered an abnormally dangerous activity under the law.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** (c) is correct because the ordinance fits the classic profile of a public welfare offense under *Morissette*: it regulates public health/safety, does not require inherently wrongful conduct, and carries a minor penalty. (a) fails because minor fines are the standard, constitutionally permissible penalty for public welfare offenses. (b) fails because the *absence* of inherently wrongful conduct (malum in se) actually supports applying strict liability. (d) fails because municipalities certainly can and do include mens rea in ordinances. (e) fails because standard warehouses are not inherently dangerous like hand grenades; strict liability applies here due to the regulatory structure.
