**Q4.** Assume Marlowe is charged under the federal statute for "knowingly stores hazardous chemicals without a permit." He raises a mistake of fact defense based on his genuine belief that Acetone-FX was a harmless water-based cleaner. How should the court rule?

(a) The defense fails, because public welfare offenses never require the prosecution to prove the defendant knew the specific dangerous characteristics of the regulated item.
(b) The defense fails, because his mistake was objectively unreasonable, and unreasonable mistakes never provide a defense to any federal regulatory or general intent crimes.
(c) The defense succeeds, because he did not have the specific intent to violate the federal regulatory statute's administrative permitting requirement when he operated the warehouse.
(d) The defense succeeds, because the statutory term "knowingly" is presumed to apply to the attendant circumstance that the stored chemicals were hazardous. <!-- correct -->
(e) The defense succeeds, because under the *Prince* principle, a defendant's genuine mistake regarding an attendant circumstance is always an absolute bar to a criminal conviction.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** (d) is correct. Under the *Morissette* presumption and MPC § 2.02(4) "one for all" rule, a mens rea term like "knowingly" typically applies to all material elements, including attendant circumstances (that the chemicals are hazardous). Furthermore, serious felony penalties defeat the strict liability presumption (*Staples*). (a) is wrong because the 5-year felony penalty removes this from the realm of strict liability public welfare offenses. (b) is wrong because "knowingly" requires actual subjective knowledge; an honest, even if unreasonable, mistake negates knowledge. (c) is wrong because knowledge of the facts that make conduct illegal is required, not specific intent to violate the law. (e) is wrong because the *Prince* principle applies only when the imagined conduct is morally wrong in itself, unlike storing harmless cleaner.
