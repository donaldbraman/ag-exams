**Q4.** Marlowe is charged with felony murder for Greggs's death, predicated on his unpermitted chemical storage (a strict-liability regulatory felony). The jurisdiction applies the traditional common law limitations on the felony-murder rule. Will the felony murder charge likely succeed?

(a) Yes, because Greggs died during the commission of the underlying felony, and the storage violation was ongoing at the time of the fire.
(b) Yes, because strict-liability regulatory offenses can serve as predicates for felony murder without further restriction.
(c) No, because strict-liability regulatory felonies are considered *malum prohibitum* and cannot serve as the predicate for felony murder. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because Marlowe was not personally present at the warehouse when the fire started or when Greggs died.
(e) No, because the termite rot was a superseding intervening cause that breaks the chain of liability for felony murder.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** (c) is correct. Traditional common law jurisdictions limit the misdemeanor-manslaughter and felony-murder rules by requiring the predicate offense to be *malum in se* (inherently wrong) or inherently dangerous. Strict-liability regulatory violations are *malum prohibitum* and generally cannot support a felony murder charge. (a) ignores the predicate offense limitation. (b) directly contradicts the *malum prohibitum* limitation. (d) is wrong because physical presence is not required for felony murder liability if the defendant is responsible for the predicate crime. (e) is incorrect because foreseeability limitations are distinct from the strict structural limitations on predicate offenses.
