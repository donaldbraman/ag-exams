**Q7.** Assume Marlowe is prosecuted for felony murder regarding Inspector Greggs's death, predicated on the underlying offense of manufacturing illicit drugs. How should the court rule on the felony-murder charge?

(a) Guilty, because Greggs died as a direct result of a fire caused during Marlowe's commission of an enumerated, inherently dangerous felony in the warehouse. <!-- correct -->
(b) Guilty, because all strict-liability regulatory storage violations automatically serve as predicates for the felony-murder rule if a rescuer's death ultimately results from the violation.
(c) Not guilty, because felony murder explicitly requires the prosecution to prove that the defendant subjectively acted with extreme indifference to human life during the offense.
(d) Not guilty, because the traditional merger doctrine prohibits using drug manufacturing as a predicate felony since it is not factually independent of the resulting homicide.
(e) Not guilty, because Inspector Greggs was acting in an official capacity, which affords the defendant absolute governmental immunity from liability for the resulting rescue death.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Felony murder holds a defendant strictly liable for a death that occurs during the commission or attempted commission of an enumerated inherently dangerous felony (drug manufacturing). (b) is wrong because felony murder applies only to inherently dangerous felonies or explicitly enumerated felonies, not all strict-liability regulatory violations. (c) is wrong because felony murder explicitly acts as a substitute for malice; the prosecution does not need to separately prove extreme indifference. (d) is wrong because the merger doctrine prohibits predicates that are assaultive in nature and not independent of the homicide; drug manufacturing has an independent felonious purpose. (e) is wrong because there is no blanket immunity rule that exempts a felon from liability when a first responder dies while mitigating the emergency caused by the felony.
