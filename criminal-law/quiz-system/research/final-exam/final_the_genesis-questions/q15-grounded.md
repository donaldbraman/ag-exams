**Q15.** Assume the jurisdiction applies the traditional common law "dangerous proximity" test instead of the MPC. Does the Courier's conduct satisfy the actus reus for attempt?

(a) Yes, because driving to the neighborhood clearly demonstrates his specific criminal intent to commit the possession offense beyond a reasonable doubt.
(b) Yes, because he had crossed the line from mere preparation to actual perpetration the exact moment he received the text from Avon.
(c) No, because being a block away and not reaching the property line means he was not in dangerous proximity to completing the crime. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because the police intervention for the traffic warrant was an independent intervening cause that legally negated his specific criminal intent.
(e) No, because walking with an empty duffel bag is an inherently dangerous act that merges entirely into the underlying drug conspiracy charge.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** The traditional common law "dangerous proximity" test requires the defendant's conduct to be physically and temporally close to the completed crime, leaving no time for abandonment. Because the Courier was still a block away, had not reached the property line, and had not entered the warehouse, he was not in dangerous proximity to successfully possessing the drugs. (a) is wrong because attempt requires both specific intent and a sufficient actus reus; intent alone is not enough. (b) is wrong because receiving the text is mere preparation under the dangerous proximity test. (d) is wrong because police intervention stops the crime but does not negate the original intent. (e) is wrong because walking with a duffel bag is not an inherently dangerous act and does not merge into conspiracy.
