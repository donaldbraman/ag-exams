**Q6.** Assume Stringer is charged as an accomplice to the illicit drug manufacturing. The prosecution introduces evidence that Stringer knew Marlowe would use the Acetone-FX for illegal drug production. Is Stringer guilty as an accomplice?

(a) Yes, because under the *Lauria* standard, mere knowledge of a buyer's illicit purpose is always sufficient for accomplice liability when selling any commercial chemical.
(b) Yes, because an independent contractor assumes absolute strict liability for all the foreseeable downstream criminal uses of the standard products they sell in commerce.
(c) No, because Stringer only sold the chemicals at normal market rates and lacked a financial stake or the purpose to facilitate the illicit manufacturing. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because an accomplice must physically participate in the commission of the actus reus alongside the principal to be held liable for a target offense.
(e) No, because the *Pinkerton* doctrine strictly requires an explicit, written agreement to participate before accomplice liability can attach to a third-party commercial vendor.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** (c) is correct. Under *Lauria*, a vendor who merely sells ordinary goods at market rates without a financial stake in the buyer's criminal venture lacks the necessary "purpose" to facilitate the crime, even if they possess knowledge of the illicit use. (a) is wrong because mere knowledge is generally insufficient for accomplice liability without a stake in the venture or special circumstances. (b) is wrong because there is no strict liability for independent contractors regarding the downstream criminal acts of their buyers. (d) is wrong because accomplice liability can attach through aiding or supplying materials; physical participation in the actus reus is not required. (e) is wrong because the *Pinkerton* doctrine applies to conspiracy liability, not the mens rea requirements for accomplice liability, and no written agreement is necessary.
