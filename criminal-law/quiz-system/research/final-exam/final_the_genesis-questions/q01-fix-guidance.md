**Q1.** Assuming federal common law applies, how will a court interpret the mens rea requirement of State Statute 101 regarding Brenda's storage of the hazardous chemicals?

(a) A mens rea of "knowingly" is presumed because the *Morissette* doctrine strictly forbids criminal convictions without proof of a culpable mental state.
(b) Strict liability applies because the statute regulates dangerous items, serves a public welfare purpose, and imposes only a minor $500 fine. <!-- correct -->
(c) A mens rea of "recklessly" is presumed because the Model Penal Code default rule strictly governs all regulatory statutes omitting an explicit mental state.
(d) Strict liability applies because the Supreme Court in *Staples* ruled that any offense involving hazardous materials automatically dispenses with mens rea.
(e) A mens rea of "purposely" is presumed because the statute involves residential property, which triggers a constitutionally mandated higher standard of proof.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** Option (b) is correct because under the public welfare offense doctrine, regulatory statutes omitting mens rea impose strict liability when they deal with dangerous items, serve a public safety purpose, and carry minor penalties (like a $500 fine). This combination overrides the general presumption against strict liability. Option (a) is incorrect because *Morissette* explicitly recognizes the public welfare exception to its mens rea presumption. Option (c) is incorrect because MPC default rules do not govern federal or common law public welfare analysis. Option (d) is incorrect because *Staples* specifically limited strict liability by holding that widely owned, ordinarily safe items do require mens rea, unlike truly dangerous items. Option (e) is incorrect because residential property involvement does not trigger a "purposely" mens rea requirement.
