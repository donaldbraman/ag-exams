**Stem 2: Homicide Charges**
Tenant Victor just died in the hospital from smoke inhalation, and the State is upgrading charges to homicide. 

**Q8.** The State charges Arthur with the homicide of Victor under the *Pinkerton* doctrine. Arthur argues that he explicitly banned flammable agents, making the fatal fire unforeseeable. Is Arthur's argument likely to result in a directed verdict of acquittal?

(a) Yes, because a co-conspirator's explicit prohibition against a specific act permanently severs *Pinkerton* liability. This prevents any conviction for consequences flowing from that banned act.
(b) Yes, because *Pinkerton* liability requires the prosecution to prove Arthur possessed specific intent. The state must show he specifically intended to cause the exact type of harm that occurred.
(c) No, because *Pinkerton* liability operates as a strict liability doctrine for all homicides. Any death that occurs during an active conspiracy automatically results in murder charges for all members.
(d) No, because Arthur's failure to physically inspect the premises constituted gross negligence. This omission independently satisfies the actus reus required to establish accomplice liability for murder.
(e) No, because operating an illicit chemical lab carries inherent risks of fire and explosion. A jury could reasonably find the fatal fire was a foreseeable consequence of the conspiracy despite Arthur's instruction. <!-- correct -->

**Answer:** (e)

**Explanation:** Option (e) is correct because *Pinkerton* liability holds conspirators responsible for the substantive crimes of their co-conspirators if those crimes were in furtherance of the conspiracy and were reasonably foreseeable. A fire is an inherently foreseeable risk of operating an illicit chemical lab, and a jury could find that Brenda's actions were foreseeable despite Arthur's contrary instruction. Option (a) is incorrect because a private instruction does not automatically defeat objective foreseeability of the risks inherent to the criminal enterprise. Option (b) is incorrect because *Pinkerton* replaces the specific intent requirement with reasonable foreseeability. Option (c) is incorrect because *Pinkerton* is not a strict liability doctrine; it requires foreseeability. Option (d) is incorrect because omission liability requires a legal duty to act, which is not the basis for liability here.
