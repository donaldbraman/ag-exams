**Q5.** Assume Marlowe is charged under the city ordinance making it a strict-liability misdemeanor to operate an unregistered storage facility. He argues that dispensing with mens rea is unconstitutional. Under the *Morissette* framework, is this likely a valid public welfare offense?

(a) No, because operating a commercial storage facility is an inherently innocent activity that cannot be regulated criminally without proof of a guilty mind.
(b) No, because a strict liability regulatory offense must exclusively involve inherently dangerous instrumentalities like hand grenades, machine guns, or highly classified narcotics.
(c) Yes, because the ordinance carries a minor penalty and targets a regulated commercial activity where the regional manager is positioned to prevent the violation. <!-- correct -->
(d) Yes, because under the Model Penal Code framework, all local municipal ordinances are automatically classified as strict liability violations regardless of their penalty structure.
(e) Yes, because corporate officers and regional managers categorically forfeit their constitutional due process right to demand proof of mens rea when operating a business.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** (c) is correct. Under *Morissette*, a valid public welfare offense dispenses with mens rea when it has a regulatory purpose, involves items putting possessors on notice, carries minor penalties (like a misdemeanor), and the defendant has the capacity to prevent the harm. (a) is wrong because public welfare offenses specifically target otherwise innocent conduct to enforce regulatory compliance. (b) is wrong because strict liability extends broadly to commercial regulatory activities like operating storage facilities, not just inherently dangerous instrumentalities. (d) is wrong because the MPC restricts absolute liability only to "violations" punishable by fines, not misdemeanors carrying potential imprisonment. (e) is wrong because corporate officers do not categorically forfeit due process rights; strict liability is bounded by statutory intent and constitutional minor-penalty constraints.
