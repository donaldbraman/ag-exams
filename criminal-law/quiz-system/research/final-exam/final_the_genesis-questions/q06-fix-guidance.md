## Issue 1 — grounding

**Q6.** The State charges David as an accomplice to Brenda's illicit lab operation. David argues he was merely a merchant who knew about the crime but did not share her criminal purpose. Will David's defense succeed?

(a) Yes, because merchants who sell ordinary commodities in the regular course of business can never be held as accomplices. This immunity applies regardless of what they know about the buyer's intentions.
(b) Yes, because David's motivation was purely financial profit rather than a personal desire to see the illicit pharmaceutical laboratory succeed. Accomplice liability requires a shared ideological or personal stake.
(c) No, because David's sale of industrial chemicals constituted a strict liability regulatory offense. Accomplice liability automatically attaches to any regulatory violation that eventually results in a felony.
(d) No, because David charged a significant premium and actively altered product labels to help Brenda evade law enforcement. These actions objectively demonstrate a purpose to further the criminal venture. <!-- correct -->
(e) No, because under modern statutes, any individual who possesses actual knowledge of an impending felony is strictly liable as an accomplice. This liability attaches if the merchant fails to report the activity.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** Option (d) is correct because while mere knowledge of a buyer's criminal intent is often insufficient for merchant accomplice liability, charging a disproportionate premium or providing customized assistance (like mislabeling products to evade law enforcement) demonstrates a specific purpose or stake in the venture's success. Option (a) is incorrect because merchant immunity is not absolute and dissolves when the merchant takes affirmative steps to assist the crime. Option (b) is incorrect because financial profit derived from charging an illegal premium qualifies as a stake in the venture. Option (c) is incorrect because accomplice liability for a specific intent felony requires shared purpose, not strict liability. Option (e) is incorrect because mere failure to report a known felony does not generally trigger accomplice liability without an affirmative duty to act.

**Grounding:** Doctrinal limits on merchant accomplice liability (purpose vs knowledge standard)
