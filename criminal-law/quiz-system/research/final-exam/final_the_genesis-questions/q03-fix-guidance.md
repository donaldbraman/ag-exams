## Issue 1 — grounding

**Q3.** Assume Charles is charged with recklessly causing a fire. Does his sleepwalking at the time of the spill defeat the voluntary act requirement?

(a) No, because Charles made a prior voluntary choice to consume a medication he knew triggered sleepwalking, which foreseeably created the subsequent risk. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because a defendant who is clinically asleep at the exact moment of the actus reus categorically cannot be held criminally liable for any resulting harm.
(c) Yes, because complex sleepwalking is classified as a legally involuntary reflex rather than a product of conscious or habitual determination.
(d) No, because the voluntary act requirement only applies to specific intent offenses, whereas recklessness is satisfied by mere gross negligence.
(e) No, because modern jurisdictions have completely abolished the unconsciousness defense in favor of the insanity defense for all parasomnia cases.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Option (a) is correct under the time-framing doctrine. When a defendant makes a voluntary choice that foreseeably creates the risk of later involuntary harmful conduct (like voluntarily taking a pill known to trigger sleepwalking), courts trace liability back to that prior voluntary act. Option (b) is incorrect because the time-framing doctrine explicitly allows conviction even if the defendant was unconscious at the exact moment of harm. Option (c) is incorrect because, while sleepwalking is generally involuntary, the prior conscious act of taking the medication satisfies the voluntary act requirement. Option (d) is incorrect because the voluntary act requirement applies to all offenses, regardless of the required mental state. Option (e) is incorrect because unconsciousness (automatism) remains a distinct and valid complete defense, separate from insanity.

**Grounding:** Chapter 7, Time-Framing: Prior Voluntary Choice Creates Liability for Later Involuntary Conduct
