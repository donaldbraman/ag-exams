**Q4.** Assume Brenda is charged with recklessly causing the fire. Does Charles's act of knocking over the drum serve as a superseding intervening cause that relieves Brenda of liability?

(a) Yes, because Charles's physical motion was the most immediate factual cause of the chemical spill. This immediate intervention legally severs any prior chain of causation initiated by Brenda.
(b) Yes, because the intervening act of a third party automatically breaks the proximate causal chain. A defendant is only liable if they specifically directed the third party's hazardous conduct.
(c) No, because Charles acted as a dependent intervening cause by attempting to rescue the chemicals. A dependent rescue attempt is legally attributed to the original wrongdoer regardless of foreseeability.
(d) No, because proximate causation only requires establishing that the defendant's conduct was the sole but-for cause of the harm. The presence of concurrent sufficient causes is entirely irrelevant to the legal analysis.
(e) No, because Brenda's act of placing a faulty heater near flammable chemicals made the fire a reasonably foreseeable consequence. The precise mechanism of the spill does not make the result highly extraordinary. <!-- correct -->

**Answer:** (e)

**Explanation:** Option (e) is correct because under common law proximate cause standards, a result is attributed to the defendant if it was a reasonably foreseeable consequence of their actions (or not "highly extraordinary"). Placing a sparking heater next to volatile chemicals makes a fire highly foreseeable, and the exact mechanical trigger (a clumsy or sleepwalking employee) does not sever the chain. Option (a) is incorrect because the most immediate factual cause does not automatically sever proximate causation if the result was foreseeable. Option (b) is incorrect because foreseeable third-party acts do not automatically break the causal chain. Option (c) is incorrect factually (Charles was not rescuing chemicals) and legally (dependent causes still require foreseeability analysis). Option (d) is incorrect because proximate cause requires more than just but-for causation; it requires a normative determination of foreseeability.
