Scenario 1: The Genesis

Facts

Assume the following facts can be proven beyond a reasonable doubt.

  1. Albert Vance, a tenured chemistry professor, was recently diagnosed with terminal cancer and faced mounting medical debts. Desperate for money, Albert decided to secretly manufacture ‘Blue-X,’ a Schedule I narcotic.

  2. Albert approached Benny Cole, a former student involved in the local drug trade. They verbally agreed that Albert would synthesize the Blue-X and Benny would manage street distribution, splitting all profits equally.

  3. To procure precursors, Albert contacted Dan, a commercial chemical supplier. Dan knew Albert intended to use the chemicals to manufacture illicit drugs. Citing the legal risk, Dan charged Albert a 500% markup on 10 gallons of highly volatile ether, explicitly stating, ‘I don’t want any share of whatever you’re selling; just pay my invoice.’

  4. Albert needed a high-speed centrifuge. He walked into an adjacent university lab and took a commercial centrifuge to keep in his own workspace, genuinely believing it was the exact machine assigned to his own university grant, when in fact it belonged to Dr. Smith.

  5. The State Public Welfare Chemical Act states: ‘It shall be unlawful to store more than 5 gallons of ether in a single facility without a fire safety permit.’ The statute is silent on mental state and carries a maximum penalty of 30 days in jail.

  6. Albert read the Act online but did not apply for a permit. He personally concluded, based on his own reading of the statute’s preamble, that university laboratories were implicitly exempt from the permit requirement.

  7. Albert had recently started experimental cancer medication. The warning label explicitly cautioned that it causes ‘sudden, severe, and uncontrollable muscle spasms.’ Over the prior week, Albert experienced three such spasms, causing him to drop heavy objects, yet he decided to proceed with the volatile chemical synthesis anyway.

  8. Benny delivered the final pseudoephedrine precursors to the lab. Immediately after, Albert began synthesizing the Blue-X—a process the jurisdiction explicitly classifies as an enumerated felony for purposes of felony murder. He lifted a flask of highly reactive catalyst to pour into the ether vat.

  9. As Albert held the flask over the vat, a severe muscle spasm caused his hand to jerk violently, dumping the entire flask into the ether. The sudden over-concentration triggered an immediate, massive explosion that blew out the laboratory’s glass walls.

  10. Chloe Davis, an adult lab assistant who had no knowledge of the illicit manufacturing, was walking past the lab door at that exact moment. The explosion shattered the glass, inflicting deep, life-threatening lacerations to her neck and chest.

  11. Paramedics stabilized Chloe and transported her to the hospital. In the emergency room, a doctor committed an act of ordinary medical negligence by misreading Chloe’s chart and administering a massive dose of a blood thinner instead of a coagulant. The blood thinner caused her stabilized wounds to hemorrhage violently, resulting in her death.

  12. Following the explosion, Benny fled the scene with the one intact case of Blue-X product. He took the case to a stash house rented under a fake name by his associate, Kev.

  13. Kev placed the Blue-X in a floor safe. Benny did not live at the stash house, but Kev gave Benny the safe’s combination so Benny could independently retrieve the drugs for street sales whenever he wanted.

  14. One evening, while Benny was at the stash house auditing the product, a rival dealer named Slash kicked down the front door wielding a machete, screaming, ‘Give me the Blue-X or I’m chopping your head off right now!’

  15. Slash cornered Benny in a back room. The room had an open, ground-floor window immediately behind Benny. In this jurisdiction, there is a statutory duty to retreat before using deadly force if a safe retreat is available. Benny did not attempt to exit the window; instead, he drew a concealed handgun and shot Slash dead.

  16. Hearing the gunshot, Kev emerged from the basement, furious that Benny had brought violence to his stash house. Kev racked a shotgun, pointed it at Benny’s head, and screamed, ‘Take this duffel bag of Blue-X to the drop point right now or I’ll blow a hole in your chest!’

  17. Terrified of Kev’s immediate threat, Benny grabbed the bag, fled the stash house, and delivered the drugs to the specified location, where he was immediately arrested by undercover police.

Q1. Assume Albert and Benny are charged with conspiracy to manufacture Blue-X. Benny argues that because he was only handling street distribution while Albert was solely responsible for synthesis, he did not agree to manufacture the drug. Which is the most likely legal conclusion regarding the agreement element?

  1. Sufficient to establish the agreement element, because their verbal agreement to split the profits from the joint enterprise satisfies the bilateral agreement requirement for the entire manufacturing scheme.
  2. Insufficient to establish the agreement element, because Benny lacked the technical capacity to manufacture the drug, rendering his participation in any agreement legally impossible to execute.
  3. Insufficient to establish the agreement element, because a valid conspiracy agreement requires each member to personally commit to executing the actus reus of the target offense.
  4. Sufficient to establish the agreement element, because Benny’s physical presence at the laboratory during the precursor delivery constitutes a unilateral agreement under modern conspiracy doctrine.
  5. Insufficient to establish the agreement element, because Wharton’s Rule precludes an independent conspiracy agreement when the underlying crime necessarily requires two parties to complete the transaction.

Q2. Assume Dan is charged with conspiracy to manufacture Blue-X. He argues that he explicitly disclaimed any share of the profits and merely sold a legal chemical at a high price. Which is the most likely legal conclusion regarding Dan’s liability?

  1. Guilty of conspiracy because charging a 500% markup gives him a financial stake in the venture, allowing an inference of his purpose to facilitate it.
  2. Guilty of conspiracy because any sale of goods to a known criminal enterprise automatically establishes purpose under the common law.
  3. Not guilty of conspiracy because his explicit statement disclaiming a share of the profits completely immunizes him from any inferred intent.
  4. Not guilty of conspiracy because Dan did not participate in the actual synthesis of the Blue-X or the downstream street distribution.
  5. Not guilty of conspiracy because ether is a legally available chemical with widespread innocent uses, barring any strict liability application.

Q3. Assume Albert is charged with theft for taking the centrifuge, an offense requiring the specific intent to permanently deprive another of their property. He argues he genuinely believed the centrifuge was his own. If he proves his mistaken belief is genuine, which is the most likely legal conclusion?

  1. Guilty of theft because his broader illicit intent to manufacture narcotics transfers to any property crimes committed during the preparation phase.
  2. Not guilty of theft because his actual, honest belief that he owned the centrifuge directly negates the mens rea required for the element of taking the property of another.
  3. Guilty of theft because mistakes of fact must be both honest and objectively reasonable, and taking an item from a different lab is per se unreasonable.
  4. Not guilty of theft because the university environment implies a general consent to share equipment among tenured professors conducting official research.
  5. Guilty of theft because the taking of scientific equipment without authorization is a strict liability offense where subjective mental state is irrelevant.

Q4. Assume Albert is charged with the unpermitted storage of ether under the State Public Welfare Chemical Act. He argues the prosecution must prove he knew he exceeded the 5-gallon limit, as the statute is silent on mental state. How will the court likely interpret the statute?

  1. The statute is a strict liability offense because the legislature’s silence on mental state operates as an absolute bar to any mistake of fact defense.
  2. The statute requires proof of knowledge because ether has widespread innocent uses in university laboratories, triggering the Staples exception.
  3. The statute is a strict liability offense because it regulates a dangerous item, carries a minor penalty, and Albert was in a position to prevent the harm.
  4. The statute is a strict liability offense because environmental and chemical regulations automatically dispense with the voluntary act requirement entirely.
  5. The statute requires proof of knowledge because the Morissette presumption mandates reading a mens rea requirement into all criminal statutes regardless of penalty.

Q5. Assume that, regardless of the required mens rea, Albert argues his personal reading of the statute’s preamble—that universities were implicitly exempt—constitutes a valid mistake of law defense for the ether storage charge. Which is the most likely legal conclusion?

  1. Valid defense because his reading was based on the actual text of the statute rather than general ignorance of the law’s existence.
  2. Invalid defense because mistake of law defenses are only available for malum in se crimes where moral blameworthiness must be firmly established.
  3. Invalid defense because the Cheek exception specifically explicitly prohibits mistake of law defenses for any regulatory or public welfare offense.
  4. Invalid defense because a personal misinterpretation of an ambiguous statute does not qualify as reasonable reliance on an official statement.
  5. Valid defense because professors are considered agents of the state, transforming his interpretation into an official statement of the law.

Q6. Assume Albert argues that he cannot be held liable for the explosion because the catastrophic event was triggered by an involuntary muscle spasm, which negates the voluntary act requirement. Which is the most likely legal conclusion?

  1. Not liable because the immediate physical movement causing the explosion was involuntary, severing any connection to his prior acts.
  2. Liable because liability traces back to his prior voluntary choice to proceed with the synthesis despite knowing the risk of spasms.
  3. Liable because a muscle spasm is legally classified as a reflex that fulfills the voluntary act requirement if the defendant is awake.
  4. Not liable because the state must prove that every single movement in the sequence of a crime was fully voluntary and conscious.
  5. Not liable because the medical side effects of a prescription drug constitute an external physical compulsion that absolutely bars prosecution.

Q7. Assume Albert is charged with homicide for Chloe’s death. He argues that the doctor’s administration of a massive dose of the wrong medication was the actual cause of her death and relieves him of liability. Which is the most likely legal conclusion?

  1. Proximate cause is established because under the concurrent sufficient causes doctrine, both Albert and the doctor acted with identical mens rea.
  2. Proximate cause fails because the doctor’s error constituted an independent, superseding intervening act that completely severed the causal chain.
  3. Proximate cause is established because medical professionals are considered strict liability actors, transferring any negligence directly to the initial aggressor.
  4. Proximate cause is established because ordinary medical negligence in treating an inflicted wound is a dependent intervening cause that does not sever liability.
  5. Proximate cause fails because the explosion only caused lacerations, meaning the death was a highly extraordinary result not foreseeable from Albert’s conduct.

Q8. Assume the prosecution must choose the most viable homicide theory against Albert for Chloe’s death. They are debating between felony murder and extreme indifference murder. Which theory is more viable?

  1. Felony murder is more viable because the extreme indifference standard requires the prosecution to prove Albert actually intended to kill bystanders.
  2. Extreme indifference murder is more viable because the inherently dangerous felony limitation strictly bars drug offenses from serving as predicates.
  3. Felony murder is more viable because drug manufacturing is an enumerated felony, allowing a murder conviction without needing to prove Albert’s subjective awareness of the risk to Chloe.
  4. Both are equally viable because strict liability offenses automatically fulfill the mens rea requirements for both felony murder and extreme indifference murder.
  5. Extreme indifference murder is more viable because felony murder requires the victim to be a participant in the underlying criminal conspiracy.

Q9. Assume Albert argues that the drug manufacturing felony cannot serve as the predicate for felony murder because the act of manufacturing the drugs merges with the explosion that caused Chloe’s death. Which is the most likely legal conclusion?

  1. The felony does not merge because the death of a bystander automatically negates the application of the merger doctrine.
  2. The felony merges because all non-violent felonies must be independently completed before any homicide liability can attach.
  3. The felony does not merge because Albert’s independent felonious purpose was to synthesize drugs, not to assault Chloe.
  4. The felony does not merge because the merger doctrine only applies to inherently dangerous felonies committed with weapons.
  5. The felony merges because the explosion and the drug manufacturing occurred simultaneously in the same laboratory setting.

Q10. Assume that, hypothetically, the explosion had not occurred and police raided the lab just as Albert lifted the flask of reactive catalyst over the ether vat. He is charged with attempted manufacture of Blue-X in a jurisdiction applying the Model Penal Code. Which is the most likely legal conclusion?

  1. Guilty of attempt because his prior act of verbally agreeing with Benny satisfied the strict dangerous proximity test for attempt.
  2. Not guilty of attempt because the police raid constitutes an involuntary abandonment of the criminal enterprise, which serves as a complete defense.
  3. Not guilty of attempt because he had not yet mixed the chemicals, meaning he had not crossed the threshold from mere preparation.
  4. Not guilty of attempt because attempt liability requires the actual production of a usable quantity of the prohibited substance.
  5. Guilty of attempt because lifting the catalyst to pour it into the vat constituted a substantial step that was strongly corroborative of his criminal purpose.

Q11. Assume Benny is charged with the unpermitted ether storage violation committed by Albert in a jurisdiction that applies the Pinkerton doctrine. If Benny establishes he had no knowledge of how much ether Albert was storing and never visited the lab, which is the most likely legal conclusion regarding Benny’s liability?

  1. Pinkerton liability is not established because the doctrine requires each co-conspirator to directly participate in the specific actus reus of the substantive offense.
  2. Pinkerton liability is established because strict liability public welfare offenses are the only crimes that automatically extend to all unindicted co-conspirators.
  3. Pinkerton liability is not established because Benny’s role was strictly limited to downstream street distribution rather than upstream chemical procurement.
  4. Pinkerton liability is established because the unpermitted ether storage was a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the Blue-X manufacturing conspiracy and in furtherance of it.
  5. Pinkerton liability is not established because the offense was merely a misdemeanor regulatory violation, which cannot serve as a conspiratorial predicate.

Q12. Assume we are prosecuting Benny regarding the stash house incident. Benny is charged with possession of the Blue-X found in Kev’s floor safe. Benny argues he did not live there and the drugs were not on his person. Which is the most likely legal conclusion?

  1. Constructive possession is established because Benny had the combination to the safe in order to exercise independent control over the drugs.

  4. Constructive possession is not established because the drugs were placed in the safe by Kev, severing Benny’s direct physical nexus to the contraband.
  5. Constructive possession is established because Benny was physically present in the stash house when the rival dealer kicked down the door.

Q13. Assume Benny asserts a self-defense claim for shooting Slash. The jurisdiction enforces a duty to retreat before using deadly force if a safe retreat is available. If Benny successfully proves Slash threatened him with a machete, which is the most likely legal conclusion?

  1. Valid defense because Slash’s threat to chop off Benny’s head created a subjective fear of imminent death, which automatically overrides retreat requirements.
  2. Valid defense because the Castle Doctrine universally abolishes the duty to retreat for anyone physically inside a residential structure regardless of ownership.
  3. Invalid defense because a handgun constitutes disproportionate and excessive force when defending against a bladed weapon like a machete.
  4. Invalid defense because a person actively engaged in an illegal drug enterprise is absolutely barred from asserting self-defense under any circumstances.
  5. Invalid defense because Benny panicked and failed to utilize the open back door, violating his statutory duty to retreat before employing lethal force.

Q14. Assume Benny is charged with drug distribution for delivering the duffel bag of Blue-X in a traditional common-law jurisdiction. He raises a duress defense, pointing to Kev’s threat. Given his pre-existing role in the drug ring, which is the most likely legal conclusion?

  3. The defense is valid because Kev’s use of a racked shotgun escalated the situation beyond the ordinary risks Benny accepted when joining the enterprise.
  4. The defense is valid because Benny’s prior agreement to distribute only applied to transactions negotiated by Albert, not those forced by Kev.
  5. The defense is valid because the common law strictly limits fault-based exclusions to cases where the defendant was the initial physical aggressor in a violent confrontation.

Q15. Assume Benny argues in a common-law jurisdiction that his sheer terror of Kev’s shotgun proves he no longer wanted to be part of the drug enterprise, effectively terminating his involvement. Under traditional common-law conspiracy doctrine, is this a legally valid withdrawal?

  1. Invalid, because a legally effective withdrawal requires an affirmative, voluntary act communicating renunciation to co-conspirators or working to defeat the conspiracy, not merely committing the target offense under emotional distress.

  3. Valid, because an extreme emotional state triggered by a deadly weapon automatically negates the mens rea required to maintain an ongoing illegal agreement.
  4. Valid, because his subjective unwillingness to complete the task operates as an implied withdrawal from his original partnership.
  5. Valid, because the intervention of Kev’s independent threat legally frustrated the original conspiratorial purpose, automatically dissolving the agreement.

Scenario 2: The Blowback

Facts

Assume the following facts can be proven beyond a reasonable doubt.

  1. For over two years, Deacon continuously operated a fentanyl distribution network with a defined hierarchy, weekly operations meetings, and distinct roles for his chemist, enforcers, and street-level sellers.

  2. Vance approached Deacon at a diner, stated ‘Your time is over, old man,’ and spat directly in Deacon’s face.

  3. Vance, a rival distributor, walked up to Deacon’s table at a diner. Vance leaned over, stated ‘Your time is over, old man,’ and spat directly in Deacon’s face. As Vance smugly turned away to walk to the counter, Deacon immediately retrieved a vial…

  4. The next day, Deacon instructed his chemist, Sloane, to formulate a new drug batch. Deacon ordered: ‘Double the fentanyl concentration. I want the street to know our product is the strongest.’

  5. Sloane warned Deacon: ‘This concentration is extremely dangerous. Given normal injection habits, it is highly likely to cause fatal overdoses.’ Deacon replied: ‘That’s exactly the reputation we need. Bag it up.’ Sloane packaged the product and gave it to Deacon’s sellers.

  6. Chloe, a 30-year-old customer, purchased the new batch. She injected her standard volume and died of a fentanyl overdose within an hour.

  7. With Vance dead, Deacon directed his enforcer, Trey, to break into Vance’s commercial storage unit to steal Vance’s remaining drug proceeds. Deacon provided Trey with a crowbar but no weapons, and remained at his own home.

  8. Trey drove to a location five blocks from the storage facility and parked. He put on a ski mask and was about to grab his crowbar when spotted a security officer who appeared to be looking at him and calling the police, then heard sirens. Trey drove away.

  9. To escape, Trey committed the state-law predicate offense of ‘Felony Evasion’ (statutorily defined strictly as ‘willfully fleeing a pursuing police vehicle in a motor vehicle’). During the chase, Trey drove 80 mph through a residential area while his driver’s license was suspended. He struck a pedestrian, Omar, killing him instantly.

  10. Police arrested Trey immediately after the crash. A search of the van’s center console revealed 50 individual baggies of fentanyl. Trey told police, ‘The van is Deacon’s, I just drive it. I don’t know what’s in the console.’

  11. A review of the network’s financial ledgers recovered from Sloane’s apartment showed that she did not receive a flat salary; instead, she received a 20% cut of all profits from the network’s drug sales.

  4. No, because the lethal response was the immediate offspring of a rash and impetuous temper.
  5. No, because the Model Penal Code rejects the premeditation framework for homicide offenses entirely.

  1. Not mitigated to manslaughter, because an objectively unreasonable amount of time elapsed between the provocation and the retaliation.
  2. Mitigated to manslaughter, because the combination of a physical insult and opprobrious words satisfies the extreme emotional disturbance test.
  3. Not mitigated to manslaughter, because insulting gestures and opprobrious words alone can never constitute legally adequate provocation.
  4. Not mitigated to manslaughter, because his immediate retrieval of the poison demonstrates prior malice rather than a sudden heat of passion.
  5. Mitigated to manslaughter, because Vance spitting directly in his face qualifies under the traditional assault and battery category.

Q19. Assume Sloane is charged with manslaughter under the Model Penal Code for Chloe’s death. How does Sloane’s statement to Deacon affect her liability?

  1. Not guilty of manslaughter, because her actions as a subordinate employee legally shield her from liability for the ultimate distribution.
  2. Guilty of manslaughter, because she failed to exercise the ordinary caution that a reasonable chemist would observe in formulating drugs.

  4. Not guilty of manslaughter, because Deacon’s subsequent order to distribute the drugs was an independent intervening cause of the death.

Q20. Assume Trey is charged with felony murder for Omar’s death, using “Felony Evasion” as the predicate. The jurisdiction applies California’s abstract-elements test to determine if a felony is inherently dangerous. Based strictly on the statutory definition provided in the facts, does the predicate qualify?

  2. It does not qualify as a predicate, because the prosecution cannot prove Trey possessed the specific intent to kill the pedestrian.
  3. It does not qualify as a predicate, because the evasion statute can theoretically be violated safely by fleeing at very low speeds.
  4. It does not qualify as a predicate, because the crime of evasion merges with the homicide under the independent-purpose doctrine limit.
  5. It qualifies as a predicate, because Trey actually drove 80 mph through a residential area, which is highly dangerous to human life.

Q21. Assume Deacon is charged with felony murder for Omar’s death under an accomplice theory. The jurisdiction strictly follows the Tison standard for accomplice felony murder (as adapted for state non-capital cases like California’s SB 1437). Is Deacon guilty of Omar’s murder?

  1. Not guilty, because the Tison standard requires the defendant to personally inflict the fatal injury to support a murder conviction.
  2. Not guilty, because the agency theory of felony murder completely shields any accomplices who are not physically present at the scene.
  3. Guilty, because Omar’s death was a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the planned storage unit burglary and vehicular escape.
  4. Not guilty, because he was absent from the scene and his peripheral involvement lacked a reckless indifference to human life.
  5. Guilty, because providing the crowbar for the planned burglary makes him a principal in the underlying predicate felony offense.

Q22. Under the common law, assume Trey is charged with misdemeanor manslaughter based specifically on driving with a suspended license when he struck Omar. The jurisdiction applies the proximate-cause limitation. Is Trey guilty under this specific theory?

  1. Guilty, because the lack of a valid driver’s license constructively supplies the necessary malice to establish criminal homicide.
  2. Not guilty, because driving with a suspended license is merely a malum prohibitum regulatory offense rather than a malum in se crime.
  3. Not guilty, because the specific unlawful aspect of lacking a valid license did not causally produce the fatal vehicular collision.
  4. Not guilty, because the common law famously rejects the misdemeanor manslaughter doctrine entirely in favor of proving actual recklessness.
  5. Guilty, because striking Omar occurred continuously during the exact same timeframe that Trey was unlawfully driving without his license.
