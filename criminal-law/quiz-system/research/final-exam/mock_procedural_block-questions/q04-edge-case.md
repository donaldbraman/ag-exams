**Q4.** Assume Sal claims duress as a defense to the drug transport charges. Will his defense succeed?

(a) Valid because Leo's position in a dangerous cartel makes his threats inherently and continuously imminent to his subordinates.
(b) Valid because Sal subjectively believed his family was in grave danger if he failed to deliver the drugs to the safehouse.
(c) Invalid because the threat that his family would pay "tomorrow" fails the strict imminence requirement for a duress defense. <!-- correct -->
(d) Invalid because duress can never be raised as a defense to any felony charge involving commercial drug distribution.
(e) Invalid because Sal voluntarily joined the cartel, which automatically waives the duress defense for any subsequent criminal acts.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** The common law duress defense strictly requires an imminent threat of death or serious bodily injury. A threat of future harm—even harm promised for "tomorrow"—fails the imminence requirement because it provides the defendant with a window of time to seek help from authorities or escape. (a) is wrong because the law evaluates the specific threat's timing, not the general dangerousness of the threatener. (b) is wrong because duress requires an objectively imminent threat, not just a subjective fear of grave danger. (d) is wrong because duress is generally available for drug felonies (unlike intentional murder). (e) is wrong because while creating one's own peril limits the defense, joining an organization does not absolutely waive the defense absent specific foreseeability of coercion, and the "tomorrow" fact is the direct, dispositive doctrinal bar here.
