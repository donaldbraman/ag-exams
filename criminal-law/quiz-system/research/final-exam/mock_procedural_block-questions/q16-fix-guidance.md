**Q16.** Assume the council members' transcripts are public. How does the "numeracy paradox" from the study of motivated cognition explain the council members' behavior?

(a) It demonstrates that higher analytical ability increases polarization, as numerate individuals use their skills to selectively interpret data to support their prior moral commitments. <!-- correct -->
(b) It shows that advanced statistical training reliably overrides political bias, allowing highly numerate individuals to reach objective conclusions regardless of their personal beliefs.
(c) It proves that individuals with lower numeracy are more susceptible to political polarization because they lack the ability to comprehend complex empirical evidence.
(d) It indicates that science curiosity completely neutralizing political bias is only possible when policy debates involve minor municipal offenses rather than serious felonies.
(e) It reveals that numerical data is inherently subjective, meaning that all statistical interpretations are equally valid forms of culturally motivated legal reasoning.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because the numeracy paradox shows that higher analytical ability increases polarization, as numerate individuals use their skills to selectively interpret data to protect their existing cultural or moral commitments. (b) is incorrect because the paradox explicitly proves that statistical training does not override political bias, but rather amplifies it. (c) is incorrect because the research demonstrates that individuals with higher numeracy are actually more susceptible to this specific form of polarization. (d) is incorrect because the neutralizing effect of science curiosity applies generally to contested scientific questions, regardless of the severity of the offense being debated. (e) is incorrect because the paradox highlights motivated reasoning in interpreting objective data, not that the mathematical data itself is inherently subjective.
