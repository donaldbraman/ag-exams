**Q13.** Senator Vance is investigated under federal anti-corruption statutes for accepting the $20,000 cash payment. If Vance proves the money was given purely as a "gratuity" for his general support of criminal justice reform after the fact, under the federal framework articulated in *Snyder v. United States*, does this transaction constitute a bribe rather than a gratuity?

(a) Yes, because any substantial cash payment to a public official automatically qualifies as a criminal bribe under federal anti-corruption law.
(b) Yes, because the payment was made pursuant to an explicit agreement to influence a specific official act before that act occurred. <!-- correct -->
(c) No, because federal corruption statutes solely regulate state and local executive officials, making state senators inherently immune from prosecution.
(d) No, because the funds were explicitly labeled as a campaign contribution, which creates an irrebuttable statutory presumption of legality.
(e) No, because a gratuity is defined as any payment over $10,000, while bribes only apply to smaller, less visible financial transactions.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** Under *Snyder v. United States* and 18 U.S.C. § 666, a bribe is a payment made or agreed to *before* an official act with the intent to influence it (a quid pro quo), whereas a gratuity is a payment made *after* an act as appreciation. Because Vance and Thorne explicitly agreed to exchange the money for the sponsorship of a specific bill before the bill was introduced, it is a bribe. (a) is wrong because not all cash payments are bribes; they must involve a corrupt quid pro quo. (c) is wrong because § 666 explicitly covers state and local officials whose agencies receive federal funds. (d) is wrong because labeling a bribe as a "campaign contribution" does not legally immunize a corrupt quid pro quo. (e) is wrong because the distinction between bribe and gratuity is based on timing and intent, not the dollar amount.
