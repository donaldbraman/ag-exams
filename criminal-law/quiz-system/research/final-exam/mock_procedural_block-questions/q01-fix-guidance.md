**Q1.** Based on the structure of the logistics operation, does Vance legally possess the fentanyl crates?

(a) Yes, because he had the power and intention to exercise dominion and control over the fentanyl despite lacking physical custody. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because under the strict liability doctrine of corporate responsibility, a CEO is deemed to possess any contraband located on company premises.
(c) No, because constructive possession requires an individual to be physically present at the location where the contraband is stored or transported.
(d) No, because he delegated the physical loading of the crates to warehouse managers, which severs his legal chain of custody over the items.
(e) No, because constructive possession cannot be established without proof of an explicit agreement with the warehouse managers regarding the specific crates.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Constructive possession requires the power and intent to exercise dominion and control over an item, which Vance established through his ability to remotely reroute or stop the trucks (Fact 1). (a) is correct. (b) is wrong because constructive possession is an intentional state, not a strict liability offense tied merely to corporate title. (c) is wrong because physical presence is precisely what constructive possession does not require. (d) is wrong because delegating physical tasks to an employee does not surrender one's ultimate dominion and control over the items. (e) is wrong because constructive possession analyzes an individual's control; it does not require a formal conspiratorial agreement with others to be proven.

**Grounding:** Common law doctrine of constructive possession
