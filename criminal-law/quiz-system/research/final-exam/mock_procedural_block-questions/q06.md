**Q6.** Assume the jurisdiction has abolished the felony-murder rule. The prosecution charges Silas with "depraved heart" (extreme indifference) murder for the pedestrian's death during the 90 mph evasion. Can Silas be convicted of this grade of homicide?

(a) Yes, because driving 90 mph erratically through a crowded residential neighborhood demonstrates an extreme indifference to the value of human life. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because any violation of a municipal traffic law that results in a pedestrian death is automatically classified as depraved heart murder.
(c) Yes, because his subsequent flight to the stash house conclusively proves that he acted with premeditation and deliberation before the crash.
(d) No, because depraved heart murder requires proof that the defendant harbored actual ill will or hatred toward the specific victim who died.
(e) No, because a standard motor vehicle cannot constitute a deadly weapon under the traditional common law definitions of intentional murder.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Depraved heart murder requires conduct creating a grave risk of death under circumstances manifesting an extreme indifference to human life, which is satisfied by driving 90 mph erratically through a crowded neighborhood. (b) is incorrect because ordinary traffic violations resulting in death typically constitute vehicular manslaughter or negligent homicide, not depraved heart murder. (c) is incorrect because premeditation applies to first-degree intentional murder, not depraved heart murder, and subsequent flight does not prove prior reflection. (d) is incorrect because depraved heart murder is defined by generalized extreme recklessness, not specific ill will toward a particular victim. (e) is incorrect because vehicles are routinely classified as deadly weapons when used recklessly.
