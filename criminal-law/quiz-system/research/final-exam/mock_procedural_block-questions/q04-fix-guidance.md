## Issue 1 — grounding

**Q4.** Assume that, whether or not Willis is guilty of homicide, it is established that Vance and Willis were co-conspirators in the fentanyl distribution ring. Can Vance be held liable for the journalist's death under the *Pinkerton* doctrine?

(a) Yes, because Willis's act of chasing the journalist was in furtherance of the conspiracy to distribute fentanyl and was a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the drug operation. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because the *Pinkerton* doctrine imposes strict liability on all co-conspirators for any crime committed by a partner, regardless of whether the act was reasonably foreseeable.
(c) No, because Vance was located remotely in his corporate office and did not directly participate in or expressly authorize the confrontation with the investigative journalist.
(d) No, because the *Pinkerton* doctrine only applies to the substantive target offense of the conspiracy and cannot extend to collateral crimes like homicide committed by partners.
(e) No, because Vance did not share the requisite mens rea for homicide, which is a fundamental constitutional prerequisite for holding him liable for his co-conspirator's actions.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** The *Pinkerton* doctrine holds co-conspirators liable for substantive offenses committed by their partners if those offenses are both in furtherance of the conspiracy and reasonably foreseeable. Protecting the drug operation from a witness is in furtherance, and violent confrontations are a foreseeable consequence of major drug trafficking (Facts 1 & 2). (a) is correct. (b) is wrong because *Pinkerton* liability is bounded by foreseeability and the in-furtherance requirement; it is not absolute strict liability. (c) is wrong because physical presence or explicit authorization for the specific act is not required under *Pinkerton*. (d) is wrong because *Pinkerton* explicitly applies to reasonably foreseeable collateral offenses, not just the target offense. (e) is wrong because *Pinkerton* deliberately substitutes the conspiracy agreement for the substantive mens rea, making shared intent for the collateral crime unnecessary.

**Grounding:** Pinkerton v. United States, 328 U.S. 640 (1946)
