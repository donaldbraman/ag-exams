## Issue 1 — audit

**Q4.** Elena is charged as an accomplice to Silas's drug possession and transportation. Does Elena's knowledge of the illegal chemicals satisfy the mens rea requirement for accomplice liability?

(a) Yes, because accepting a payment well above market rate establishes a financial stake in the venture, allowing the jury to infer her true criminal purpose. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because accomplice liability strictly requires explicit verbal agreement to the crime, and mere knowledge of illegal cargo is always legally insufficient.
(c) Yes, because providing transportation inherently makes a driver strictly liable for any contraband the passenger possesses, irrespective of the driver's mental state.
(d) No, because driving three miles is a trivial act of assistance that fails to satisfy the actus reus requirement for establishing accomplice liability.
(e) Yes, because any individual who provides a service to a known criminal automatically shares the principal's specific intent under the natural and probable consequences doctrine.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under traditional accomplice liability (*Lauria*), providing services with mere knowledge of illegal use is generally insufficient unless the provider shares the criminal purpose. However, a jury may infer purpose from knowledge when the provider acquires a stake in the venture, such as charging inflated, above-market rates for the service. (b) is wrong because explicit verbal agreement is not required; purpose can be inferred from conduct. (c) is wrong because there is no strict liability for drivers transporting passengers with contraband. (d) is wrong because any aid, however trivial, satisfies the actus reus of accomplice liability. (e) is wrong because the natural and probable consequences doctrine extends liability for subsequent crimes, but does not automatically impute the specific intent of the underlying crime based on mere service provision.
