**Q5.** Assume that the state charges Silas with felony murder for the death of the pedestrian. Evading police at high speeds is an enumerated, inherently dangerous felony. Is Silas guilty of felony murder?

(a) Guilty, because the pedestrian's death occurred during the commission of an inherently dangerous felony, satisfying the strict liability substitution for malice. <!-- correct -->
(b) Guilty, because Silas intentionally targeted the pedestrian while attempting to aggressively escape from the pursuing police officers in the neighborhood.
(c) Not guilty, because Silas did not act with the specific intent to kill the pedestrian when he jumped the curb and lost control.
(d) Not guilty, because evading the police at high speeds merges with the homicide, preventing the application of the felony-murder rule entirely.
(e) Not guilty, because the misdemeanor manslaughter rule automatically replaces the felony-murder rule whenever a vehicle is involved in the fatality.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** The felony-murder rule holds defendants strictly liable for any death that occurs during the commission of an enumerated or inherently dangerous felony; the intent to commit the felony substitutes for the malice required for murder. (b) is incorrect because the facts state Silas lost control, contradicting the premise that he intentionally targeted the pedestrian. (c) is incorrect because felony murder dispenses with the need to prove specific intent to kill. (d) is incorrect because evading police is not an assaultive offense that merges with the homicide under the independent felonious purpose doctrine. (e) is incorrect because there is no rule automatically preempting felony murder in vehicular cases if the underlying crime is a qualifying felony.
