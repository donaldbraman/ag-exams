**Q1.** Assume that Silas challenges the initial traffic stop, arguing it was unconstitutional under the Fourth Amendment because the cracked taillight was a mere pretext for a drug investigation. How should the court rule?

(a) Constitutional, because officers had objective probable cause of a traffic violation, rendering their subjective motivations irrelevant under the Fourth Amendment. <!-- correct -->
(b) Unconstitutional, because the primary, underlying purpose of the stop was drug interdiction rather than ensuring municipal traffic safety.
(c) Unconstitutional, because the cracked taillight constitutes a minor administrative violation rather than a serious safety hazard requiring intervention.
(d) Constitutional, because the officers were acting in good faith based on their training and experience with similar suspected drug corridors.
(e) Constitutional, because the subsequent discovery of illegal drugs retroactively justifies the initial investigative detention by the police officers.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under *Whren v. United States*, a traffic stop is constitutional under the Fourth Amendment as long as officers have objective probable cause that a traffic violation occurred, regardless of their actual subjective motivation. (b) is incorrect because *Whren* explicitly forecloses inquiring into the officers' primary purpose. (c) is incorrect because any traffic violation, however minor, provides sufficient probable cause for a stop. (d) is incorrect because the standard relies on objective probable cause, not the good faith exception. (e) is incorrect because a stop must be justified at its inception; subsequent discoveries cannot retroactively validate an unconstitutional stop.
