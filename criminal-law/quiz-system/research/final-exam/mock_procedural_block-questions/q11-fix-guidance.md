**Q11.** Assume the State Attorney General's investigation is proceeding. Silas sues DA Miller for civil damages under 42 U.S.C. § 1983 for intentionally deleting the exculpatory wiretap recording. Will Miller's claim of absolute immunity succeed?

(a) Yes, because suppressing or destroying exculpatory evidence is considered intimately associated with the judicial phase of the criminal process, triggering absolute prosecutorial immunity. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because deleting evidence from a database constitutes an administrative act rather than a prosecutorial function, stripping her of all civil immunity protections.
(c) Yes, because prosecutors are constitutionally immune from all civil suits regardless of whether their actions were investigative, administrative, or adjudicative in nature.
(d) No, because intentional violations of the Brady disclosure rule automatically overcome absolute immunity due to the severity of the constitutional deprivation involved.
(e) Yes, because her actions were taken prior to the commencement of the actual trial, meaning she is fully protected by the doctrine of qualified immunity.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under *Imbler* and *Price v. Montgomery County*, prosecutors possess absolute immunity from § 1983 civil liability for conduct "intimately associated with the judicial phase of the criminal process." This absolute shield applies even to ethically reprehensible conduct during trial preparation, such as intentionally suppressing or advising the destruction of exculpatory evidence. (b) is wrong because courts classify handling evidence for trial preparation as a prosecutorial, not administrative, function. (c) is wrong because absolute immunity is strictly functional; investigative and administrative acts receive only qualified immunity. (d) is wrong because the egregiousness of a *Brady* violation does not pierce absolute immunity. (e) is wrong because trial preparation acts trigger absolute immunity, not merely qualified immunity.
