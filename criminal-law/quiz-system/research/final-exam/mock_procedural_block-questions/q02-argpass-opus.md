**Q2.** Assume for this question that the drugs were found exclusively in Sal's physical custody in the vehicle. Is Leo guilty of constructive possession of the fentanyl?

(a) Guilty because he retained the secondary keys and GPS access, demonstrating power and intent to control the drugs. <!-- correct -->
(b) Guilty because any high-level manager in a cartel is strictly liable for contraband held by their direct subordinates.
(c) Not guilty because Sal had exclusive physical custody of the vehicle and the drugs during the actual transportation.
(d) Not guilty because Leo expressly forbade violence, severing his legal responsibility for the subsequent conduct of the courier.
(e) Not guilty because the use of an independent mechanic to build the compartment severed Leo's direct connection to the drugs.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Constructive possession requires that a defendant have the power and intent to exercise dominion and control over the contraband, even if it is not in their physical custody. By retaining secondary keys and active GPS tracking access to the vehicle carrying the fentanyl, Leo maintained both the capability and intent to control the shipment. (b) is wrong because constructive possession requires an individualized showing of power and intent to control, not strict vicarious liability. (c) is wrong because constructive possession was developed precisely to cover situations where the defendant lacks physical custody. (d) is wrong because forbidding violence is irrelevant to the possession elements. (e) is wrong because hiring a mechanic facilitates the concealment but does not defeat Leo's continuing dominion over the vehicle.
