**Q11.** Is DA Paula entitled to absolute civil immunity under § 1983 for instructing the evidence custodian to incinerate the dashcam footage?

(a) She is entitled to absolute immunity because her actions were intimately associated with the judicial phase of the criminal process. <!-- correct -->
(b) She is entitled to absolute immunity because prosecutors are completely shielded from civil liability for any administrative actions taken.
(c) She is not entitled to absolute immunity because intentionally destroying evidence shocks the conscience and violates substantive due process.
(d) She is not entitled to absolute immunity because directing a police evidence custodian constitutes an investigative rather than prosecutorial function.
(e) She is not entitled to absolute immunity because accepting a law firm partnership stripped her of all prosecutorial legal protections.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under *Imbler* and *Price v. Montgomery County*, prosecutors possess absolute civil immunity for actions "intimately associated with the judicial phase of the criminal process." This includes egregious misconduct like intentionally suppressing or directing the destruction of exculpatory evidence during trial preparation. Because Paula acted during the judicial phase of trial prep, absolute immunity attaches. (b) is wrong because prosecutors receive only qualified immunity for administrative or investigative acts. (c) is wrong because the degree of ethical or constitutional outrage (shocking the conscience) does not pierce absolute immunity; the test is functional. (d) is wrong because directing the handling of evidence during trial preparation is considered a prosecutorial function, as seen in *Price*. (e) is wrong because criminal corruption does not retroactively strip functional civil immunity for the procedural acts taken.
