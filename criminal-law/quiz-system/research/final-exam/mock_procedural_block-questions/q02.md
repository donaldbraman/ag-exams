**Q2.** Assume it is established that Silas poured gasoline on the cocaine and held a lighter in his trembling hand before being tackled. Is Silas guilty of attempted destruction of evidence?

(a) Guilty, because pouring the gasoline and holding the lighter constituted a substantial step strongly corroborative of his criminal purpose. <!-- correct -->
(b) Guilty, because his mere physical presence in the stash house with gasoline established a criminal conspiracy to commit the offense.
(c) Not guilty, because he did not actually strike the lighter, so he had not performed the final act necessary to complete the crime.
(d) Not guilty, because the officers intervened, establishing the defense of factual impossibility since he could not have completed the crime.
(e) Not guilty, because his trembling hand clearly indicates that he lacked the requisite specific intent to complete an attempt conviction.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under the MPC's "substantial step" test, an attempt occurs when a defendant's actions strongly corroborate their criminal purpose, which Silas demonstrated by pouring the gasoline and holding the lighter. (b) is incorrect because attempt requires a substantial step toward the crime, not just an agreement or mere preparation. (c) is incorrect because modern attempt law explicitly rejects the restrictive "last act" test. (d) is incorrect because police intervention does not constitute factual impossibility; the crime was factually possible but thwarted. (e) is incorrect because physical trembling does not legally negate the specific intent to commit the crime.
