**Q11.** Assume Silas sues DA Miller for civil damages under 42 U.S.C. § 1983, alleging she intentionally deleted the exculpatory wiretap recording from the evidentiary database to protect her conviction rate. Will DA Miller be shielded by absolute prosecutorial immunity?

(a) Yes, because evaluating and managing evidence in preparation for trial is intimately associated with the judicial phase of the criminal process, shielding her from civil liability. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because prosecutors are constitutionally immune from all civil, criminal, and professional consequences for any official acts performed during their employment.
(c) No, because the intentional destruction of exculpatory evidence automatically strips a prosecutor of absolute immunity and instantly triggers qualified immunity.
(d) No, because absolute immunity only protects prosecutors when they are actively presenting evidence in the courtroom, not during pretrial preparation.
(e) No, because DA Miller's actions flagrantly violated Silas's clearly established constitutional rights under *Brady v. Maryland*, waiving all immunity protections.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under *Price v. Montgomery County*, prosecutors possess absolute immunity from civil liability for actions intimately associated with the judicial phase of the criminal process, which includes pretrial evidence management, even if they intentionally suppress or destroy exculpatory evidence. (b) is incorrect because prosecutors are not immune from criminal prosecution or professional bar discipline, only civil liability. (c) is incorrect because the egregiousness of the misconduct does not pierce absolute immunity if the function is prosecutorial. (d) is incorrect because absolute immunity extends beyond the courtroom to pretrial preparation and evidence evaluation. (e) is incorrect because a *Brady* violation provides grounds for overturning a conviction, but does not pierce civil absolute immunity.
