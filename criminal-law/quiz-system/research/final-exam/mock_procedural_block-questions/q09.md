**Q9.** Assume Thorne explicitly argues during his closing statement that the jury should engage in race-conscious nullification based on Paul Butler's framework. Given that Silas is charged with a violent felony (felony murder), how does Thorne's argument align with Butler's actual proposal?

(a) It conflicts with Butler's proposal, because Butler specifically argued that race-conscious nullification should be reserved for nonviolent, victimless offenses like drug possession, not violent crimes. <!-- correct -->
(b) It aligns perfectly, because Butler argues that Black jurors should nullify in all cases involving Black defendants to protest systemic racism regardless of the charge.
(c) It aligns perfectly, because Butler's framework mandates nullification whenever statistical evidence of disparate prosecutorial impact is presented at a criminal trial.
(d) It conflicts with Butler's proposal, because Butler argued that nullification should only be used by white jurors to protest prosecutorial overreach in specific communities.
(e) It conflicts with Butler's proposal, because Butler strictly maintains that jury nullification is an unconstitutional violation of the rule of law and should be abolished.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Paul Butler's framework for race-conscious nullification explicitly argues that Black jurors should use nullification for nonviolent offenses (like drug crimes) as a democratic protest, but should not nullify in cases involving violent crimes like murder, because those crimes cause direct harm to the community. (b) is incorrect because Butler drew a strict line excluding violent crimes. (c) is incorrect because Butler's proposal is a moral argument for juror discretion, not a legal mandate triggered by statistics. (d) is incorrect because Butler's proposal is specifically addressed to Black jurors as a response to political exclusion. (e) is incorrect because Butler actively defends nullification as a lawful, democratic tool, not an unconstitutional violation.
