**Q14.** Is Silas liable under the *Pinkerton* doctrine for Thorne's specific act of bribing Judge Mercer?

(a) Yes, because bribing the judge was a reasonably foreseeable substantive offense committed by his co-conspirator in furtherance of their broad obstruction conspiracy. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because Silas was only generally aware of aggressive tactics and did not specifically know or agree to the exact plan to bribe the trial judge.
(c) Yes, because under strict liability principles, all members of a conspiracy are automatically guilty of every crime committed by any member of their organization.
(d) No, because bribery is an independent intent crime that requires the prosecution to prove Silas personally harbored the specific intent to corrupt the judiciary.
(e) Yes, because Silas supplied the initial cartel funding, making him directly liable as a principal actor rather than derivatively liable under the conspiracy doctrine.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under the *Pinkerton* doctrine, a conspirator is derivatively liable for any substantive offenses committed by a co-conspirator that are in furtherance of the conspiracy and reasonably foreseeable as a necessary or natural consequence of the unlawful agreement. Silas agreed to illegally obstruct the proceedings using cartel funds; bribing the trial judge is highly foreseeable within a well-funded obstruction conspiracy. (b) is wrong because *Pinkerton* explicitly dispenses with the need for specific knowledge or agreement to the collateral crime. (c) is wrong because liability is not absolute; the crime must still be foreseeable and in furtherance of the conspiracy. (d) is wrong because *Pinkerton* substitutes the conspiracy membership for the mens rea of the substantive offense. (e) is wrong because supplying funds makes him an accomplice or conspirator, not the principal actor in the physical bribe.

**Tags:** chapters: [19], topics: [pinkerton-liability, derivative-liability, conspiracy], difficulty: intermediate, cognitive: application

**Grounding:** Chapter 19, Section: Liability Extensions (*Pinkerton v. United States*)
