## Issue 1 — audit

**Q2.** Assume Silas is charged with attempted destruction of evidence. Under the Model Penal Code's substantial step test, is Silas guilty of the attempt?

(a) Yes, because pouring gasoline on the drugs and holding a lighter strongly corroborates his criminal purpose and extends far beyond mere preparation. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because he was tackled before he could actually strike the lighter, meaning he lacked the final necessary act to complete the offense.
(c) Yes, because his subjective intention to destroy the cocaine was clear, which automatically satisfies the actus reus requirement for any attempt charge.
(d) No, because his trembling hand indicates he might have voluntarily abandoned the effort before the officers physically intervened to stop the ignition.
(e) Yes, because being present in a suspected stash house while possessing illegal narcotics constitutes a substantial step toward the destruction of those narcotics.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under the MPC, an attempt requires a "substantial step" that is strongly corroborative of the actor's criminal purpose. Pouring gasoline and readying a lighter clearly satisfies this standard, as it demonstrates concrete action toward completion. (b) is wrong because it applies the stringent common-law "last proximate act" test, which the MPC rejected. (c) is wrong because mens rea alone is insufficient; a substantial step actus reus is still required. (d) is wrong because voluntary abandonment must be a complete and voluntary renunciation, not merely hesitation, and he was physically interrupted. (e) is wrong because mere presence and possession do not specifically corroborate an intent to *destroy* evidence.
