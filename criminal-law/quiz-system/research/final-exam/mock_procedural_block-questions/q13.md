**Q13.** Are Frank and Senator Vance legally guilty of federal programs bribery under 18 U.S.C. § 666 for the $25,000 check?

(a) They are guilty of bribery because the payment was closely connected in time to the successful passage of the sponsored legislation.
(b) They are guilty of bribery because a twenty-five-thousand-dollar check is presumed to be a corrupt payment regardless of when it was agreed upon.
(c) They are not guilty of bribery under federal law because the payment was made after the official act with no prior agreement, constituting a non-criminal gratuity. <!-- correct -->
(d) They are not guilty of bribery under federal law because state legislators are entirely exempt from prosecution under the federal programs bribery statute.
(e) They are not guilty of bribery under federal law because the federal funding received by the legislative office did not directly subsidize the consulting firm.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** In *Snyder v. United States*, the Supreme Court held that 18 U.S.C. § 666 criminalizes bribes (payments agreed to before the official act to influence it) but does not criminalize gratuities (payments made after the act as a reward, with no prior agreement). Because there was "no prior agreement, understanding, or promise of payment" when Frank met with Vance, the $25,000 check sent after the bill's passage is a gratuity, falling outside the statute. (a) is wrong because temporal proximity does not convert a post-act gratuity into a pre-act bribe. (b) is wrong because the amount of the check does not alter the doctrinal distinction between bribes and gratuities. (d) is wrong because state officials whose agencies receive over $10,000 in federal funds are explicitly covered by § 666. (e) is wrong because the federal funds do not need to trace to the specific consulting firm; they only need to meet the $10,000 threshold for the agency/office.
