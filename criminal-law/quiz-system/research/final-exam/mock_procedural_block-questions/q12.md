**Q12.** Assume that Silas and Thorne are charged with conspiracy to obstruct justice based on their encrypted texts agreeing to use cartel funds to manipulate the proceedings. Are the elements of conspiracy satisfied?

(a) Yes, because they formed a bilateral agreement to commit an unlawful act and possessed the specific intent to illegally obstruct the legal proceedings. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because Thorne's status as Silas's legal attorney automatically establishes a conspiratorial agreement whenever they confidentially communicate about the trial.
(c) Yes, because conspiracy is a strict liability offense that requires only the exchange of encrypted messages regardless of the underlying substantive intent.
(d) No, because conspiracy requires the actual completion of the target offense, and the legal proceedings have not yet fully concluded in this jurisdiction.
(e) No, because Wharton's Rule strictly prohibits charging conspiracy when the underlying crime of obstruction of justice inherently requires exactly two participating people.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Conspiracy requires a bilateral agreement between two or more people to commit an unlawful act, combined with the specific intent to achieve the object of the agreement, which Silas and Thorne explicitly formed via text. (b) is incorrect because the attorney-client privilege protects lawful representation; it does not automatically convert communication into conspiracy absent an illegal agreement. (c) is incorrect because conspiracy is a specific intent crime, not a strict liability offense. (d) is incorrect because conspiracy is an inchoate offense; it is complete upon agreement (and an overt act, in many jurisdictions), regardless of whether the target crime is achieved. (e) is incorrect because obstruction of justice can be committed by a single individual and does not logically require two people, making Wharton's Rule inapplicable.
