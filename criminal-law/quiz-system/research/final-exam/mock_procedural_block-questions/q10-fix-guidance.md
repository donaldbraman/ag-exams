**Q10.** Following Thorne's closing argument, Judge Mercer intends to instruct the jury on nullification. Under *United States v. Kleinman*, which instruction is constitutionally impermissible?

(a) Instructing the jury that if they acquit Silas despite the evidence, they violate their oaths and may be held in contempt of court and punished. <!-- correct -->
(b) Instructing the jury that they have a legal duty to follow the court's instructions on the law, even if they personally disagree with the law's fairness.
(c) Instructing the jury that they may not substitute their own personal sense of justice for their sworn obligation to evaluate the evidence presented.
(d) Instructing the jury that they are tasked solely with determining the facts of the case and applying those facts to the law provided by the judge.
(e) Instructing the jury that sympathetic feelings regarding the defendant's background or the broader justice system must not influence their verdict.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under *United States v. Kleinman*, a trial judge may strongly instruct a jury that they must follow the law and may not substitute their own sense of justice. However, an instruction crosses the line into unconstitutional coercion if it implies that jurors could be punished or held in contempt for exercising their unreviewable power to acquit. (b), (c), (d), and (e) are all standard, constitutionally permissible anti-nullification instructions that correctly describe the jury's formal legal duty without crossing into coercive threats.
