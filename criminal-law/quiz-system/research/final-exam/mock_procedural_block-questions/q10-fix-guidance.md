## Issue 1 — grounding

**Q10.** Willis seeks a new trial, arguing the verdict was tainted by Juror #4's statements. Under the Supreme Court's ruling in *Peña-Rodriguez v. Colorado*, will the court consider the second juror's affidavit?

(a) Yes, because the affidavit contains clear evidence that racial animus was a significant motivating factor in the juror's vote to convict the defendant. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because the no-impeachment rule is entirely suspended whenever any juror violates the trial court's instructions regarding proper deliberation procedures and evidence evaluation.
(c) No, because the federal rules of evidence strictly prohibit the use of juror testimony to inquire into the validity of a verdict under any circumstances.
(d) No, because the juror's statement did not involve an outside influence or extraneous prejudicial information being improperly brought into the jury deliberation room.
(e) No, because the constitutional exception only applies when a juror explicitly uses a racial slur during deliberations, which did not occur in this specific situation.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** In *Peña-Rodriguez v. Colorado*, the Supreme Court recognized a Sixth Amendment exception to the no-impeachment rule (FRE 606(b)) where a juror makes a clear statement indicating that they relied on racial stereotypes or animus to convict a criminal defendant. Juror #4's statement explicitly linking race to criminality and overriding the need for evidence meets this standard (Fact 6). (a) is correct. (b) is wrong because the no-impeachment rule is not entirely suspended for general violations of jury instructions. (c) is wrong because *Peña-Rodriguez* established a binding constitutional exception to the strict prohibition in FRE 606(b). (d) is wrong because while extraneous information is a standard statutory exception, *Peña-Rodriguez* created a separate constitutional exception specifically for internal racial bias. (e) is wrong because the exception requires a clear statement of racial animus, but does not strictly require the use of a specific racial slur.

**Grounding:** Peña-Rodriguez v. Colorado, 580 U.S. 206 (2017)
