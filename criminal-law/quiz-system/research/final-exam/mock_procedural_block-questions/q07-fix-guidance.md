## Issue 1 — grounding

**Q7.** Sal missed his intended target and killed Victor, who was walking his dog directly behind the officer. Assume for this question only that Sal's belief that he was firing at a hitman was objectively reasonable. What is the most likely legal effect of Victor's death on Sal's liability?

(a) Sal is fully acquitted of all homicide charges because an objectively reasonable self-defense justification perfectly transfers to any unintended civilian victims.
(b) Sal is guilty of intentional murder because the doctrine of transferred intent nullifies any affirmative defenses when an innocent bystander is killed.
(c) Sal is guilty of reckless homicide because firing a weapon into a crowded sidewalk constitutes criminal recklessness, defeating the transferred justification defense. <!-- correct -->
(d) Sal is fully acquitted because the death of a bystander during a shootout is classified as an unforeseeable intervening cause under the law.
(e) Sal is guilty of felony murder because invoking self-defense automatically triggers strict liability for any deaths occurring during the commission of the act.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** Transferred justification generally allows an objectively reasonable self-defense claim to cover the accidental killing of a bystander. However, if the defendant's conduct toward the bystander is reckless—such as firing into a crowded sidewalk directly behind the target—the justification does not transfer, resulting in liability for reckless homicide. (a) is wrong because the justification does not perfectly transfer if the defendant's actions recklessly endangered innocent bystanders. (b) fails because transferred intent transfers both the intent and the valid defenses, nullifying the defense only if recklessness toward the bystander is present. (d) is incorrect because a bystander's death from wild shooting is a highly foreseeable consequence, not an unforeseeable intervening cause. (e) is wrong because invoking self-defense does not trigger strict liability under the felony-murder rule.

**Grounding:** Chapter 22 (Self-Defense) and Chapter 13 (Unintentional Homicide)
