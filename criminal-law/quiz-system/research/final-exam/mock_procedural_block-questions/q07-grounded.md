**Q7.** Assume that Sal's use of force against the perceived hitman was justified as self-defense. How does this impact his liability for Victor's death?

(a) Guilty of intentional murder because the transferred intent doctrine strictly applies to any death caused during a violent shooting.
(b) Guilty of reckless homicide because firing into a crowded sidewalk constitutes a gross deviation from the standard of care. <!-- correct -->
(c) Not guilty of any homicide because the doctrine of transferred justification provides absolute immunity for all accidental collateral damage.
(d) Not guilty of any homicide because Victor's presence on the sidewalk was an independent intervening cause that severed causation.
(e) Guilty of intentional murder because the doctrine of transferred justification only applies when defending a third party, not oneself.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** The doctrine of transferred justification transfers the self-defense privilege from the intended target to an unintended bystander, defeating a charge of *intentional* murder. However, it does not provide absolute immunity. If the act of defending oneself creates a substantial and unjustifiable risk to innocent bystanders—such as firing blindly into a "crowded sidewalk"—the defendant can still be liable for reckless homicide (manslaughter or depraved-heart murder). (a) is wrong because transferred justification defeats the specific intent required for intentional murder. (c) is wrong because transferred justification only protects against intentional crimes; it does not excuse recklessness toward bystanders. (d) is wrong because a bystander walking on a sidewalk is entirely foreseeable, not an independent intervening cause. (e) is wrong because transferred justification applies equally to self-defense and defense of others.
