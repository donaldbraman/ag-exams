**Q7.** Assume Thorne files a selective prosecution discovery motion, relying exclusively on his leaked statistical data showing the DA's policies have a 95% disparate impact on minority defendants. Will the court grant discovery?

(a) No, because under the *Armstrong* standard, statistical disparate impact alone is insufficient; Thorne must produce credible evidence of discriminatory intent. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because statistical evidence of an overwhelmingly disparate impact creates a mandatory constitutional presumption of prosecutorial discriminatory intent.
(c) Yes, because discovery motions in criminal cases only require a basic showing of relevance to the defendant's broader constitutional rights.
(d) No, because selective prosecution claims must be exclusively raised during jury selection rather than through pretrial evidentiary discovery motions.
(e) No, because the Equal Protection Clause does not restrict the discretionary charging decisions of state or federal criminal prosecutors.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under *United States v. Armstrong*, a defendant seeking discovery for a selective prosecution claim must produce "credible evidence" showing that similarly situated individuals of a different race were not prosecuted. Statistical evidence of disparate impact is insufficient without evidence of discriminatory intent or similarly situated comparators. (b) is incorrect because *McCleskey* and *Armstrong* explicitly reject the idea that systemic statistics mandate a presumption of individual intent. (c) is incorrect because discovery for selective prosecution has a heightened "credible evidence" threshold to protect prosecutorial discretion. (d) is incorrect because selective prosecution is properly raised via pretrial motion, not during jury selection. (e) is incorrect because the Equal Protection Clause does apply to prosecutors, though the standard to prove a violation is exceptionally high.
