## Issue 1 — grounding

**Q13.** Federal investigators uncover the $25,000 check that Frank sent to Senator Vance's consulting firm one month after the fentanyl analog bill was passed. The investigation confirms there was absolutely no prior agreement or promise of payment when Frank initially met with the Senator. Are Frank and Senator Vance guilty of bribery under 18 U.S.C. § 666?

(a) Yes, because the payment of $25,000 to a state official's consulting firm clearly establishes a corrupt intent to influence ongoing legislative decision-making.
(b) Yes, because *Snyder* clarified that any significant financial transaction between a lobbyist and a state legislator automatically triggers federal criminal bribery liability.
(c) No, because the payment was a post-act gratuity lacking a prior agreement, which the Supreme Court ruled falls outside the scope of § 666. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because the legislative office only receives $10,000 in federal funding, which fails to meet the strict jurisdictional threshold required by the statute.
(e) No, because state legislators possess absolute legislative immunity from federal bribery charges for any actions taken during the drafting of a criminal bill.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** Under *Snyder v. United States*, 18 U.S.C. § 666 criminalizes pre-act bribes but not post-act gratuities. Because there was no prior agreement, understanding, or promise of payment when Vance sponsored the bill, Frank's $25,000 check constitutes a gratuity outside the statute's scope. (a) is wrong because the payment occurred after the official act without a prior agreement, making it a gratuity rather than a pre-act bribe. (b) fails because *Snyder* specifically narrowed the statute to exclude post-act gratuities, rejecting automatic liability for financial transactions. (d) is incorrect because receiving over $10,000 in federal funding expressly satisfies the strict jurisdictional threshold of § 666. (e) is wrong because state legislative immunity does not shield legislators from federal criminal bribery and corruption statutes.

**Tags:** chapters: [5], topics: [corruption-bribe-vs-gratuity], difficulty: medium, cognitive: application

**Grounding:** Chapter 5 (Legislatures and Courts) - Snyder v. United States
