**Q14.** Was the trial judge's instruction regarding jury nullification legally permissible?

(a) Permissible because courts have an absolute duty to forestall jury nullification using any definitive instructions necessary to ensure compliance.
(b) Permissible because juries only have the factual power to nullify, not a legal right, and therefore cannot be instructed on nullification.
(c) Impermissible because courts are constitutionally required to provide an affirmative jury nullification instruction upon the defense's request.
(d) Impermissible because instructing jurors that nullification is impossible or that it violates the law crosses the line into unconstitutional coercion. <!-- correct -->
(e) Impermissible because the instruction improperly infringed upon the jury's exclusive role as the ultimate finder of fact in criminal trials.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** While courts have no obligation to inform juries of their power to nullify and may instruct them to follow the law, *United States v. Kleinman* established clear limits on anti-nullification instructions. Instructing a jury that "there is no such thing as valid jury nullification" (saying it is impossible) and that they will "violate [their] oath and the law" (implying punitive consequences) crosses the line into coercion and is legally erroneous. (a) is wrong because the duty to forestall nullification is not absolute; courts cannot use coercive threats of punishment or falsehoods. (b) is wrong because while juries lack a legal right to a pro-nullification instruction, this does not give judges permission to issue coercively false anti-nullification instructions. (c) is wrong because courts are never required to give an affirmative nullification instruction. (e) is wrong because general instructions to follow the law do not infringe on the fact-finding role; it is the specific coercive phrasing that is impermissible.
