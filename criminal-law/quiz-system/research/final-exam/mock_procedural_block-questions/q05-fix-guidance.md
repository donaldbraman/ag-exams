## Issue 1 — grounding

**Q5.** Under the *Reves v. Ernst & Young* "operation or management" test, is Thorne liable for conducting the affairs of the RICO enterprise?

(a) Yes, because Thorne's daily review and approval of altered shipping manifests demonstrates that he had some part in directing the enterprise's affairs. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because any professional who provides specialized services to a criminal enterprise is automatically deemed to be participating in its high-level management.
(c) No, because Thorne is an outside legal counsel and the operation or management test restricts RICO liability exclusively to upper-management insiders of the enterprise.
(d) No, because Thorne's activities were limited to traditional legal representation and did not involve the physical distribution or oversight of the fentanyl crates.
(e) No, because the operation or management test requires proof that the defendant possessed ultimate and exclusive decision-making authority over the enterprise's illicit goals.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** The *Reves* "operation or management" test requires the defendant to have "some part in directing the enterprise's affairs." By personally taking over the daily review and approval of altered manifests to evade detection, Thorne stepped beyond traditional legal advice and took a role in directing the operation's illicit logistics (Fact 3). (a) is correct. (b) is wrong because merely providing outside professional services does not satisfy *Reves*; the professional must actively direct affairs. (c) is wrong because outsiders and lower-rung participants can be liable if they exert control or direction over the enterprise's activities. (d) is wrong because taking over manifest approval to hide drug shipments is not traditional legal representation, and physical drug handling is not required. (e) is wrong because "some part" in directing is legally sufficient; ultimate or exclusive authority is not required.

**Grounding:** Reves v. Ernst & Young, 507 U.S. 170 (1993)
