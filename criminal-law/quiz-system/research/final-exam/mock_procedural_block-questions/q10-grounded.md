**Q10.** Assume DA Paula is federally indicted for honest-services fraud based on accepting the law firm partnership. Will she be convicted?

(a) Guilty because she accepted a clear quid pro quo exchange of value for official acts, satisfying the bribery and kickback core of the statute. <!-- correct -->
(b) Guilty because any undisclosed conflict of interest held by a public official automatically constitutes a deprivation of honest services.
(c) Not guilty because a law firm partnership is an intangible benefit rather than the direct receipt of money or physical property.
(d) Not guilty because her actions occurred during the judicial phase of a trial, which falls outside the scope of honest services fraud.
(e) Not guilty because derailing a trial represents an informal exercise of prosecutorial discretion rather than a formal official act.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under *Skilling v. United States*, 18 U.S.C. § 1346 (honest-services fraud) was narrowed to criminalize only bribery and kickback schemes. DA Paula's acceptance of a lucrative law firm partnership in explicit exchange for intentionally derailing a criminal trial is a classic quid pro quo bribe, placing her conduct squarely within the statute's surviving core. (b) is wrong because *Skilling* specifically excluded mere undisclosed self-dealing and conflicts of interest from the statute. (c) is wrong because bribery includes the promise of lucrative employment/partnership; it is not limited to physical cash. (d) is wrong because the "judicial phase" standard applies to civil immunity, not criminal liability for bribery. (e) is wrong because intentionally sabotaging an active prosecution in exchange for a bribe is the corrupt manipulation of a formal official act, not an informal exercise of discretion.
