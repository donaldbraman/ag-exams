**Q6.** Assume that, independent of the stop's legality, Sal genuinely and reasonably believed the plainclothes officer was a rival cartel hitman preparing to kill him. Can Sal assert a justification defense for shooting at the officer?

(a) He can claim self-defense because his reasonable mistake of fact regarding the plainclothes officer's identity justifies his use of force. <!-- correct -->
(b) He can claim self-defense because the plainclothes officer failed to announce his official law enforcement status prior to the stop.
(c) He cannot claim self-defense because he was engaged in the commission of a felony drug transport at the time of the shooting.
(d) He cannot claim self-defense because the right to use deadly force never extends to resisting an on-duty law enforcement officer.
(e) He cannot claim self-defense because his actions constituted an unreasonable escalation of a routine traffic stop for a signaling violation.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Self-defense is a justification based on the defendant's reasonable belief that they face an imminent threat of unlawful deadly force. A reasonable mistake of fact regarding the attacker's identity (believing a plainclothes officer is a cartel hitman) allows the defendant to claim self-defense, as the law assesses the justification based on the facts as the defendant reasonably believed them to be. (b) is wrong because failure to announce status goes to the reasonableness of the mistake, but the justification itself flows from the reasonable belief of a deadly threat, not merely the lack of an announcement. (c) is wrong because engaging in a felony does not automatically forfeit the right of self-defense against a perceived unlawful murder attempt. (d) is wrong because citizens can defend themselves against officers if they reasonably believe the officer is an unlawful attacker. (e) is wrong because, on Sal's perceived facts, he was reacting to a hitman, not escalating a traffic stop.
