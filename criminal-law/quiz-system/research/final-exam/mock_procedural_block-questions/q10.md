**Q10.** Assume that the judge instructs the jury: "If you acquit contrary to the evidence, you will be held in contempt of court and sent to jail." Is this instruction permissible?

(a) No, because while judges may instruct juries to follow the law, they cannot coercively threaten jurors with punishment for exercising their unreviewable power to acquit. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because judges have a strict constitutional duty to prevent jury nullification by any means necessary to preserve the integrity of the judicial process.
(c) Yes, because jury nullification constitutes a severe criminal offense under both federal and state law, justifying the threat of contempt and incarceration.
(d) No, because defendants have an absolute Sixth Amendment right to a formal jury instruction explicitly explaining their power to engage in jury nullification.
(e) No, because the Double Jeopardy Clause strictly requires judges to remain entirely silent on the topic of jury deliberations and potential acquittals.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under *United States v. Kleinman*, a court crosses the line into impermissible coercion when it instructs jurors that they could be punished for nullification or that nullification is impossible. Juries have an unreviewable power to acquit. (b) is incorrect because a judge's authority to discourage nullification is limited and cannot include coercive threats. (c) is incorrect because nullification is a legally unreviewable power, not a criminal offense. (d) is incorrect because the Supreme Court has consistently held that defendants have no right to a nullification instruction. (e) is incorrect because judges are permitted to give general instructions reminding jurors of their oath to follow the law; they are not required to remain silent.
