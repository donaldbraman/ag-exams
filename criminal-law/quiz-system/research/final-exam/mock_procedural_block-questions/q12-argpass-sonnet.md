**Q12.** Did DA Paula's use of peremptory challenges to strike the Hispanic prospective jurors violate the *Batson* framework?

(a) It survives the Batson challenge because skepticism of police testimony is recognized as a valid, race-neutral reason for a peremptory strike.
(b) It survives the Batson challenge because the defense must prove a systemic pattern of discrimination across multiple trials, not just one.
(c) It violates Batson because striking all Hispanic jurors automatically satisfies the ultimate burden of proving purposeful discrimination.
(d) It violates Batson because the prosecutor's stated reason was objectively contradicted by the record, proving the explanation was pretextual. <!-- correct -->
(e) It violates Batson because peremptory challenges may never be used to remove minority jurors in cases involving allegations of police misconduct.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** Under the three-step *Batson* framework, once a prima facie case is made (Step 1) and the prosecutor offers a race-neutral explanation (Step 2), the trial court must determine whether purposeful discrimination occurred (Step 3). Here, Paula's race-neutral reason ("they expressed skepticism") was objectively contradicted by the record (three of the jurors never spoke). This establishes that the stated reason was pretextual, satisfying the burden of proving discrimination at Step 3. (a) is wrong because while skepticism is a facially neutral reason at Step 2, it fails Step 3 if it is demonstrably false. (b) is wrong because *Batson* permits proving discrimination within a single trial's jury selection. (c) is wrong because striking all Hispanic jurors establishes a prima facie case (Step 1), but does not "automatically" end the inquiry without giving the prosecutor a chance to explain. (e) is wrong because minorities can be struck for genuine race-neutral reasons, even in police misconduct cases.
