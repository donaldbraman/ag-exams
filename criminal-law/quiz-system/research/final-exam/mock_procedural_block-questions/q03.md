**Q3.** Assume the court must determine whether Silas possessed the drugs locked in the bedroom. Officers found the only key to that room in Silas's pocket. Is Silas guilty of possessing the drugs in the bedroom?

(a) Guilty, because possessing the only key to the locked room establishes his power and intent to exercise dominion and control over the drugs. <!-- correct -->
(b) Guilty, because physical presence in a structure where drugs are found automatically establishes legal possession of all the building's contents.
(c) Not guilty, because he did not have physical, actual possession of the drugs inside the bedroom at the specific time of his arrest.
(d) Not guilty, because constructive possession requires the state to prove that he actually purchased or manufactured the controlled substances.
(e) Not guilty, because the drugs located in the living room cannot transfer legal possession to the separate drugs stored in the bedroom.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Constructive possession exists when a defendant lacks physical custody but has the power and intent to exercise dominion and control over the item, which is established here by his exclusive possession of the only key. (b) is incorrect because mere physical presence near contraband is insufficient to establish possession without evidence of control. (c) is incorrect because it describes actual possession and ignores the doctrine of constructive possession. (d) is incorrect because possession does not require proof of ownership, purchase, or manufacture. (e) is incorrect because possession of one stash is independent of the other; the key, not the living room drugs, establishes control.
