**Q8.** Assume Thorne challenges DA Miller's peremptory strikes of all five Black jurors under *Batson v. Kentucky*. Miller defends her strikes by claiming the jurors are inherently biased against police because they live in high drug-crime neighborhoods. How should the judge rule on the strikes?

(a) The strikes are likely unconstitutional, because Miller's stated reason relies on a proxy for race, and *Batson* prohibits striking jurors based on systemic assumptions tied to racial demographics. <!-- correct -->
(b) The strikes are automatically valid, because peremptory challenges cannot be restricted by the Equal Protection Clause under any circumstances during trial.
(c) The strikes are automatically valid, because living in a high-crime neighborhood constitutes a per se challenge for cause under federal trial rules.
(d) The strikes are unconstitutional, because *Batson* completely prohibits prosecutors from using peremptory strikes for any reason other than a juror's criminal record.
(e) The strikes are unconstitutional, because the first step of *Batson* requires the prosecutor to definitively prove she lacked discriminatory intent.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** At the third step of the *Batson* framework, the judge must determine if the prosecutor's race-neutral reason is actually a pretext for discrimination. Striking all Black jurors based on the demographic proxy of "high-crime neighborhoods" is frequently recognized as pretextual stereotyping that violates the Equal Protection Clause. (b) is incorrect because *Batson* explicitly restricts peremptory challenges using the Equal Protection Clause. (c) is incorrect because neighborhood residence is not a valid basis for a "for cause" challenge. (d) is incorrect because prosecutors may use peremptory strikes for any genuine race-neutral and gender-neutral reason, not just criminal records. (e) is incorrect because the first step of *Batson* places the burden on the defendant to make a prima facie case, not on the prosecutor to prove a negative.
