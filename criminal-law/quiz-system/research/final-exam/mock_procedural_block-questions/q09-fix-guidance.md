**Q9.** Thorne explicitly bases his closing argument for jury nullification on Paul Butler's academic framework. Does Thorne's argument properly align with Butler's specific proposal?

(a) No, because Butler's framework restricts race-conscious nullification exclusively to nonviolent offenses, whereas Silas is charged with a violent felony resulting in death. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because Butler argues that Black jurors should nullify any prosecution of a Black defendant whenever the criminal justice system demonstrates systemic racial bias.
(c) No, because Butler expressly limits the exercise of race-conscious nullification to victimless regulatory offenses and strictly excludes all drug-related property crimes.
(d) Yes, because Butler's theory actively encourages nullification in all cases involving severe police misconduct or pretextual Fourth Amendment violations.
(e) No, because Butler concludes that jury nullification is always a violation of democratic principles and should never be utilized by marginalized communities.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Professor Paul Butler's proposal for race-conscious jury nullification is specifically limited to nonviolent crimes, particularly malum prohibitum drug offenses. He expressly argues that violent offenders should not be nullified because they pose a danger to the community. Since Silas is charged with killing a pedestrian (felony murder), his crime is violent. (b) is wrong because Butler does not endorse blanket nullification for all crimes. (c) is wrong because Butler specifically includes nonviolent drug offenses in his proposal, not just regulatory crimes. (d) is wrong because the presence of police pretext does not override Butler's explicit exclusion of violent offenses. (e) is wrong because Butler views nullification as the most direct form of democracy available to politically excluded minorities, not a violation of it.
