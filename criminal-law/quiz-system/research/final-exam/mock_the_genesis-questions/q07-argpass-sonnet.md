**Q7.** Assume Albert is charged with criminal homicide for Chloe's death. He argues he is not the proximate cause of her death because the stray bullet was the immediate cause of her death. How should the court rule?

(a) Proximate cause is severed because the stray bullet is an independent, superseding intervening cause that was unforeseeable. <!-- correct -->
(b) Proximate cause is severed because the stray bullet establishes a new "but-for" factual cause that negates Albert's initial actions entirely.
(c) Proximate cause is established because the chemical explosion necessitated her presence at the ambulance, making the bullet a dependent intervening cause.
(d) Proximate cause is established because the time-framing doctrine automatically traces liability back to Albert's initial decision to risk a muscle spasm.
(e) Proximate cause is established because the stray bullet acted as a concurrent sufficient cause alongside the explosion.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** In assessing proximate causation, independent intervening causes (like an unrelated third-party shooting) that are highly unforeseeable generally break the causal chain. A stray bullet from an unrelated shootout is a classic independent, superseding cause that severs liability for the resulting death (exactly as tested in the class slides). (a) is correct. (b) fails because a superseding cause severs proximate (legal) causation, not factual (but-for) causation; Albert's explosion remains a but-for cause of her being at that exact spot. (c) fails because mere 'but-for' spatial presence does not transform an unrelated shooting into a *dependent* intervening cause. (d) fails because the time-framing doctrine deals with the voluntary act requirement (*Decina*), not superseding causes. (e) fails because concurrent sufficient causes apply when two forces act simultaneously to independently cause death (e.g., two shooters hitting the victim at once), not a sequential, superseding event.
