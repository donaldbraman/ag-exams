**Q7.** Benny is charged with possession of the pseudoephedrine precursors. Benny argues he never physically touched the precursors after placing them in the cabinet and leaving the laboratory. Under the doctrine of constructive possession, which of the following is the most accurate assessment of his liability?

(a) He is guilty because his retention of a key to the shared cabinet demonstrates his continuing ability and intent to exercise dominion and control over the precursors. <!-- correct -->
(b) He is guilty because the delivery of any chemical precursor automatically establishes absolute liability for its subsequent use under the traditional rules of accomplice liability.
(c) He is not guilty because constructive possession requires the defendant to maintain exclusive physical access to the location where the contraband is securely stored.
(d) He is not guilty because the moment he closed the cabinet, his actual physical possession ended, immediately terminating his criminal liability for the stored chemicals.
(e) He is not guilty because Albert was the primary user of the laboratory and held superior legal title to the precursors, negating Benny's possessory interest.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Constructive possession requires the defendant to have awareness of the contraband and the ability and intent to exercise dominion and control over it. Benny's retention of a key to the shared cabinet where he stored the precursors demonstrates this continuing control. (b) is incorrect because accomplice liability requires purposeful facilitation, and constructive possession is based on control, not absolute liability. (c) is incorrect because constructive possession can be joint; exclusive access is not required. (d) is incorrect because physical handling is not necessary to maintain constructive possession. (e) is incorrect because superior title or another person's access does not defeat a defendant's own possessory interest and control.
