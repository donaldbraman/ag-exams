**Q6.** Assume Albert claims that he cannot be held criminally liable for any crimes stemming from the explosion because the violent muscle spasm that caused the spill was an involuntary act. Will this defense succeed?

(a) The voluntary act requirement is satisfied because Albert made a prior voluntary choice to proceed with the synthesis despite knowing his condition caused spasms. <!-- correct -->
(b) The voluntary act requirement is satisfied because a severe muscle spasm is legally classified as a conscious and deliberate action under the penal code.
(c) The voluntary act requirement fails because Albert was completely unconscious and lacking all bodily control at the exact moment the flask tipped over.
(d) The voluntary act requirement fails because the fatal explosion was directly caused by the involuntary reflex rather than Albert's earlier preparatory conduct.
(e) The voluntary act requirement is satisfied because strict liability regulatory offenses completely dispense with the fundamental need to prove a voluntary act.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Under the "time-framing" doctrine (as seen in *Decina*), courts trace liability back to a prior voluntary choice. Albert knew his medication caused severe, uncontrollable spasms, yet voluntarily chose to begin the volatile synthesis. (b) is wrong because a muscle spasm is an involuntary reflex, not a conscious determination. (c) is wrong because Albert was not unconscious, and time-framing bridges the involuntary moment by looking to his earlier voluntary conduct regardless. (d) is wrong because the foreseeable involuntary act does not break the causal chain originating from his earlier conscious choice. (e) is wrong because even strict liability offenses universally require a baseline voluntary act.
