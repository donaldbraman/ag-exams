# Scenario: The Desperate Professor

Professor Albert Vance, a tenured chemistry professor, received a terminal cancer diagnosis and faced mounting medical debts. Desperate for funds, he decided to secretly manufacture "Blue-X," a Schedule I controlled substance. 

Albert approached Benny, a former student with street connections. Albert asked him to "fund the logistics for a lucrative new chemistry project." Benny nodded, took the keys to a shared storage cabinet, and began dropping off cash advances, though neither explicitly mentioned drug manufacturing. 

To procure precursors, Albert contacted Dan, a commercial chemical supplier. Dan knew Albert intended to manufacture illicit drugs. Citing his own legal risk, Dan charged Albert a 500% markup on 10 gallons of highly volatile ether, explicitly stating, "I don't want any share of whatever you're selling; just pay my invoice."

Albert needed a high-speed centrifuge for the synthesis. He walked into an adjacent university lab and removed a commercial centrifuge to his own workspace. He genuinely believed it was the machine assigned to his own grant, though it actually belonged to Dr. Smith.

The State Public Welfare Chemical Act states: "It shall be unlawful to store more than 5 gallons of ether in a single facility without a fire safety permit." The statute is silent on mental state and carries a maximum penalty of 30 days in jail. Albert read the Act online but did not apply for a permit. He personally concluded, based on his reading of the statute's preamble, that university laboratories were implicitly exempt from the requirement.

Benny delivered the final pseudoephedrine precursors to the lab, locking them in the shared storage cabinet to which both Benny and Albert retained keys. 

Albert had recently started an experimental cancer medication. The warning label explicitly cautioned that it causes "sudden, severe, and uncontrollable muscle spasms." Despite experiencing three such spasms that week, Albert unlocked the cabinet, transferred the sealed ether and catalyst containers to his workstation, and sat down to review his synthesis notes.

Before opening any containers or beginning the synthesis, Albert experienced a severe, medication-induced muscle spasm. His arm flailed involuntarily, knocking the sealed catalyst flask heavily into the glass ether jug. The impact breached both containers, initiating an uncontrolled chemical reaction. The resulting overpressurization fractured the laboratory's windows.

A glass fragment struck Chloe Davis, an adult lab assistant walking in the adjacent hallway who had no knowledge of the illicit manufacturing, causing a severe laceration to her neck. Paramedics transported Chloe to the hospital. In the emergency department, a nurse made a routine charting error, noting Chloe's weight in kilograms instead of pounds. Relying on this chart, the attending physician administered an incorrectly dosed blood thinner, which prevented her laceration from clotting and contributed to her fatal hemorrhage.

### Stem 1: The Preparatory Phases

The prosecution is building its case against Albert, Benny, and Dan for the preparatory phases of the operation, including the supply logistics, the acquisition of the centrifuge, and the illegal storage of volatile chemicals.
