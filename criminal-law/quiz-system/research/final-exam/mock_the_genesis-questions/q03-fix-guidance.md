**Q3.** Assume Albert is charged with the theft of Dr. Smith's centrifuge. How does Albert's belief about the centrifuge affect his liability for theft?

(a) Not guilty of theft because his genuine belief that the machine belonged to him negates the required mental state of taking the property of another. <!-- correct -->
(b) Not guilty of theft because the centrifuge was located in an adjacent, broadly accessible university laboratory rather than a locked private storage area.
(c) Guilty of theft because his mistaken belief regarding the ownership of the equipment constitutes an invalid mistake of law rather than a mistake of fact.
(d) Guilty of theft because the underlying offense is a strict liability crime that completely dispenses with any requirement to prove a culpable mental state.
(e) Guilty of theft because his physical removal of the equipment successfully satisfies both the objective conduct and subjective mental state elements.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Theft requires the specific intent to take the "property of another." Albert's genuine, honest belief that the centrifuge was his own machine assigned to his grant is a mistake of fact that negates this specific mental state, even if the mistake was careless. (b) is wrong because the property's physical location does not negate the elements of theft. (c) is wrong because mistaking who holds factual ownership of an item is a mistake of fact, not an invalid mistake of penal law. (d) is wrong because theft is a classic specific intent offense, not a strict liability public welfare offense. (e) is wrong because physical conduct alone is insufficient; the prosecution must prove both the actus reus and the specific intent to deprive another of their property.
