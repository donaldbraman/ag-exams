## Issue 1 — audit

**Q4.** The State Public Welfare Chemical Act carries a maximum penalty of 30 days in jail and is silent on mens rea. Albert argues he cannot be convicted of violating the Act because the prosecution cannot prove he knew about the permit requirement. Which of the following is the prosecution's best response under the traditional public welfare offense doctrine?

(a) Ignorance of the law is an affirmative defense that shifts the burden of proof to the defendant to establish his lack of knowledge by a preponderance.
(b) The Act is a strict liability public welfare offense because ether is a highly dangerous item that puts the possessor on notice of likely regulation. <!-- correct -->
(c) The Act requires proof of civil negligence, meaning the prosecution need only show Albert failed to read the statute carefully enough to understand its terms.
(d) The Act imposes strict liability because all environmental and chemical statutes are categorically exempt from mens rea requirements under federal and state common law.
(e) The prosecution need only prove that Albert had the specific intent to manufacture illicit drugs to satisfy the transferred intent for the storage violation.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** The Act operates as a public welfare offense. Under the *Morissette* factors, offenses carrying minor penalties (like 30 days in jail) and regulating inherently dangerous items (like highly volatile ether) that put the possessor on notice of likely regulation impose strict liability without proof of mens rea. (a) is incorrect because ignorance of the law is generally not a defense at all, not a burden-shifting affirmative defense. (c) is incorrect because public welfare offenses impose strict liability, meaning no proof of negligence is required. (d) is incorrect because there is no categorical exemption for environmental laws; strict liability is assessed case-by-case using the *Morissette* factors. (e) is incorrect because specific intent for a different crime (manufacturing) does not transfer to satisfy the mens rea of a distinct regulatory violation.
