## Issue 1 — grounding

**Q7.** Assuming Locke claims self-defense for firing his weapon during the hijacking, how does his blind firing toward an occupied convenience store affect his liability for the clerk's death?

(a) Locke is likely guilty of a reckless homicide offense because firing blindly into an occupied store constitutes a reckless use of defensive force. <!-- correct -->
(b) Locke has a valid claim of perfect self-defense because the hijackers pointed shotguns at him first, allowing him to use lethal force in any direction.
(c) Locke is guilty of intentional murder under imperfect self-defense, which applies whenever a defendant creates the danger by transporting narcotics.
(d) Locke's status as a drug trafficker categorically strips him of any right to self-defense, automatically classifying his actions as first-degree premeditated murder.
(e) Locke is shielded from liability by the transferred intent doctrine, which transfers his justified defensive intent from the hijackers to the innocent clerk.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Even if a defendant has a right to self-defense against an attacker, using force in a manner that consciously disregards a substantial and unjustifiable risk to innocent bystanders constitutes recklessness. This strips the justification defense as to the bystander, making Locke liable for a reckless homicide offense (manslaughter or extreme indifference murder). (b) is wrong because the right to self-defense does not grant a license to recklessly endanger innocent third parties. (c) is wrong because imperfect self-defense mitigates murder to voluntary manslaughter based on an honest but unreasonable belief; it does not mandate an intentional murder conviction. (d) is wrong because participating in a crime does not automatically strip a person of self-defense rights (unless they were the initial aggressor in the confrontation). (e) is wrong because transferred intent does not shield a defendant from liability for recklessly injuring a bystander.
