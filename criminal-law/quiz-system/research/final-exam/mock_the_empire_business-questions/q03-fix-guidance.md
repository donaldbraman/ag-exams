**Q3.** Is Thorne liable as an accomplice to Vance's narcotics distribution operation?

(a) Yes, because Thorne provided routing services with the purpose of facilitating the distribution in order to secure his twenty percent salary premium. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because accomplice liability strictly requires the physical handling of the contraband, which Thorne intentionally avoided doing throughout his employment.
(c) No, because Thorne only possessed knowledge of the illicit distribution, rather than the required specific purpose to bring about the criminal result.
(d) Yes, because under the natural and probable consequences doctrine, any employee is strictly liable for their employer's criminal activities.
(e) Yes, because Thorne's act of routing the crates satisfies the actus reus, and the law presumes purpose from any continued employment.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Accomplice liability requires the *actus reus* of aiding the principal and the *mens rea* of purpose to facilitate the underlying offense. Thorne aided Vance by routing the crates, and his 20% financial premium establishes his purpose to make the distribution succeed. (b) is wrong because the actus reus for accomplice liability encompasses any form of assistance (like routing logistics); physical handling is not required. (c) is wrong because his financial premium elevates his mental state from mere knowledge to purpose. (d) is wrong because the natural and probable consequences doctrine extends liability for secondary foreseeable crimes, but it does not make general employees strictly liable for an employer's primary crimes. (e) is wrong because the law never presumes purpose merely from continued employment; it must be proven.
