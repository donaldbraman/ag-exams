## Issue 1 — audit

**Q11.** How would Locke's liability for attempted robbery of the Apex safe differ under the MPC's "substantial step" test compared to the common law "proximity" test?

(a) Locke is guilty under the MPC because his actions strongly corroborate his criminal purpose, but not guilty under the common law because he remained distant from the safe. <!-- correct -->
(b) Locke is guilty under the common law because buying ski masks crosses the line of dangerous proximity, but not guilty under the MPC because he lacked a blowtorch.
(c) Locke is guilty under both tests because the act of communicating his plan to an outside crew automatically satisfies the actus reus requirements for attempt.
(d) Locke is not guilty under either test because discarding the ski mask legally erases his prior conduct, completely nullifying any actus reus that had already occurred.
(e) Locke is guilty under the MPC because it imposes strict liability for criminal thoughts, but not guilty under the common law which requires actual completion.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** The MPC's "substantial step" test pushes attempt liability much earlier in the timeline, requiring only conduct strongly corroborative of the actor's criminal purpose (like reconnoitering or acquiring materials). The common law "proximity" tests require the defendant to be dangerously close to completing the crime. Drawing maps and buying masks satisfies the MPC but fails proximity because the safe breach was still a day away. (b) is wrong because it inverts the two tests; buying a mask is mere preparation under common law. (c) is wrong because communicating a plan is conspiracy, but usually amounts to mere preparation for attempt under the common law. (d) is wrong because subsequent acts of discarding evidence do not retroactively erase a completed actus reus for attempt. (e) is wrong because the MPC still requires a substantial step, not merely strict liability for a thought; and the common law requires dangerous proximity, not actual completion.
