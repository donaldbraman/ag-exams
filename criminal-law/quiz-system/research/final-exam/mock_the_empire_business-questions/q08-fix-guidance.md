**Q8.** Setting aside any self-defense claims, which theory of murder best supports convicting Locke for the death of the convenience store clerk?

(a) Extreme indifference murder, because firing an AR-15 blindly into an occupied store demonstrates a conscious disregard for a grave risk to human life. <!-- correct -->
(b) First-degree premeditated murder, because Locke expressly stated earlier that he needed "heavier firepower," proving he planned to kill someone on his route.
(c) Misdemeanor manslaughter, because Locke's underlying crime of transporting mislabeled crates is a strict liability regulatory offense rather than a dangerous felony.
(d) Felony murder, because the convenience store clerk was killed by a rival hijacking crew, and Locke is strictly liable for deaths caused by competitors.
(e) Intentional murder, because the law presumes that anyone who blindly discharges a firearm possesses the specific conscious object to cause death.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Extreme indifference (depraved heart) murder requires a reckless mental state that manifests an extreme indifference to the value of human life. Firing an assault rifle blindly into an occupied convenience store perfectly illustrates this standard. (b) is wrong because stating a need for firepower does not prove a specific, premeditated intent to kill the clerk. (c) is wrong because transporting 400 grams of fentanyl is an inherently dangerous felony, not a strict liability misdemeanor, which would trigger felony murder, not misdemeanor manslaughter. (d) is wrong because the facts state Locke's bullet struck the clerk, not the hijackers' bullet, so agency/proximate cause limits regarding competitors' bullets are irrelevant here. (e) is wrong because blind firing proves extreme recklessness, not a specific conscious object (intent) to cause death.
