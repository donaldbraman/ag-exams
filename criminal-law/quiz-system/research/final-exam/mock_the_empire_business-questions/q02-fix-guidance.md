**Q2.** Based on Thorne's arrangement with Vance, is Thorne guilty of conspiring to distribute narcotics?

(a) Yes, because Thorne's receipt of a twenty percent salary premium demonstrates a financial stake in the venture, allowing an inference of his purpose to further the illegal distribution. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because Thorne personally despised the drug trade and refused to physically handle the narcotics, precluding the necessary specific intent to distribute.
(c) No, because Thorne only provided routine routing services that any warehouse manager would provide, completely preventing any legal inference of criminal purpose.
(d) Yes, because Thorne's mere knowledge of the crates' contents combined with his continued employment automatically satisfies the mens rea for conspiracy.
(e) Yes, because conspiracy is a strict liability offense when the target crime involves Schedule II narcotics transported across state lines.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Conspiracy requires the purpose to commit the target offense. Under *Lauria*, a provider of legitimate goods or services can be inferred to possess the requisite purpose if they have a financial stake in the venture, such as charging a premium rate. Thorne's 20% bump satisfies this. (b) is wrong because personal distaste for the crime does not legally negate purposeful intent when a financial stake proves the defendant wanted the venture to succeed. (c) is wrong because Thorne did not just provide routine services; he received a premium specifically tied to the illegal routing. (d) is wrong because mere knowledge without a stake is generally insufficient to prove purpose under *Lauria*. (e) is wrong because conspiracy is a specific intent crime, never a strict liability offense.
