**Q12.** Assume that, rightly or wrongly, the court finds the existence of an ongoing narcotics conspiracy between Artie, Cole, and Damon was legally established. Did Cole's actions following the fatal shooting validly withdraw him from the enterprise, thereby terminating his liability for any future crimes?

(a) Withdrawal is invalid because simply fleeing the scene and passively avoiding communication does not satisfy the affirmative, communicative renunciation required to exit a conspiracy. <!-- correct -->
(b) Withdrawal is valid because by successfully evading Artie and ignoring his directed phone calls, Cole effectively and unambiguously communicated his abandonment of the enterprise.
(c) Withdrawal is invalid because once an individual commits an overt act in furtherance of a conspiracy, the common law absolutely forbids any subsequent termination of vicarious liability.
(d) Withdrawal is valid because a conspiracy automatically terminates by operation of law the moment an unplanned murder occurs, legally severing all prior conspirators.
(e) Withdrawal is invalid because under modern conspiracy doctrine, a conspirator must physically surrender to law enforcement to formally dissolve their ongoing liability.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** The correct answer is (a). To formally withdraw from a conspiracy and sever liability for future offenses, the law requires an affirmative act to disavow the conspiracy, such as communicating the withdrawal to all coconspirators or notifying law enforcement. Simply fleeing to a girlfriend's apartment and passively "laying low" or ignoring phone calls completely fails this required affirmative step. (b) is wrong because passive evasion does not constitute unambiguous legal communication of withdrawal. (c) is wrong because withdrawal is always legally possible to terminate *future* liability, even after an overt act has been committed. (d) is wrong because an unplanned murder commits further crimes but does not automatically dissolve the underlying distribution conspiracy by operation of law. (e) is wrong because neither the common law nor the MPC strictly mandates physical surrender to law enforcement; communicating withdrawal to coconspirators is often sufficient.
