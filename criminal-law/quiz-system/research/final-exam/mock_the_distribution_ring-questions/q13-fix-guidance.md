**Q13.** Under the traditional *Pinkerton* doctrine, is Artie liable for Damon's murder of Vic?

(a) Yes, because the murder was a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the armed stash house takeover committed in furtherance of the ongoing conspiracy Artie organized. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because Artie's subsequent text message praising the kill and wiring funds retroactively establishes his specific intent to commit the homicide.
(c) No, because Damon's personal rage during the encounter means the murder was legally independent of the conspiracy's goal to secure drugs and control the corners.
(d) No, because Artie was completely absent from the scene and had no prior knowledge that Damon had brought a loaded firearm to the stash house.
(e) Yes, because *Pinkerton* imposes strict liability on all ringleaders for any violent act committed by their subordinates, regardless of foreseeability or the conspiracy's scope.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because under the traditional *Pinkerton* doctrine, each conspirator is liable for the foreseeable substantive offenses committed by coconspirators in furtherance of the ongoing conspiracy; a lethal shooting during a drug raid is highly foreseeable. (b) is wrong because *Pinkerton* relies on vicarious liability through the conspiracy agreement, not the retroactive ratification of specific intent. (c) is wrong because even if Damon acted in rage, the killing occurred during the execution of the conspiracy's objective, making it generally foreseeable in furtherance of the plot. (d) is wrong because absence from the scene and lack of specific knowledge about the weapon do not defeat *Pinkerton* liability, which is specifically designed to catch absent planners. (e) is wrong because *Pinkerton* requires the crime to be reasonably foreseeable and in furtherance of the conspiracy; it is not absolute strict liability.
