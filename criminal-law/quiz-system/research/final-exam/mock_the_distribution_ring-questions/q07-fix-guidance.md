## Issue 1 — audit

**Q7.** Assume the jurisdiction applies the traditional natural and probable consequences (NPC) doctrine. Is Cole liable for Vic's murder as an accomplice?

(a) Yes, because a fatal shooting is a highly foreseeable outcome of an armed stash house robbery, making the murder a natural and probable consequence of the agreed-upon crime. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because Cole acted as a lookout with purpose to facilitate the robbery, which automatically transfers his specific intent to any subsequent acts committed by the principal.
(c) No, because Damon killed Vic in a sudden personal rage rather than to secure the drugs, breaking the causal chain required for the natural and probable consequences doctrine.
(d) No, because Cole was stationed outside at the back door and could not have reasonably foreseen what specific words Vic would use to provoke Damon into an immediate homicidal rage.
(e) Yes, because the traditional NPC doctrine applies strict liability to all co-felons for any injury occurring on the premises, regardless of the target crime or the participants' foreseeability.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because under the traditional NPC doctrine, an accomplice is liable for any crime that is a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the intended crime; a lethal shooting during an armed drug raid is highly foreseeable. (b) is wrong because NPC liability is based on objective foreseeability, not on "transferring" the specific intent from the underlying crime to the newly committed crime. (c) is wrong because the principal's impulsive or personal motive at the moment of the trigger pull does not defeat NPC liability so long as a shooting during the robbery was generally foreseeable. (d) is wrong because foreseeability applies to the *type* of crime (a shooting during an armed robbery), not the granular conversational trigger that led to it. (e) is wrong because NPC requires a negligence standard (reasonable foreseeability), not absolute strict liability.
