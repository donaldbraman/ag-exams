**Q4.** Assume Ben is charged with attempted distribution of a controlled substance under a modern state statute that follows the Model Penal Code. Ben argues that because "Vulture" was a fictional persona and no actual buyer existed, it was factually impossible for him to commit the crime. Will Ben's impossibility defense succeed?

(a) Yes, because the government manufactured the crime and created a factual impossibility, which constitutes automatic entrapment.
(b) Yes, because under the MPC, distributing drugs to a non-existent person is a pure legal impossibility that bars conviction.
(c) No, because the MPC abolished the impossibility defense so long as the conduct would constitute a crime if the attendant circumstances were as the defendant believed them to be. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because traditional common law courts always rejected factual impossibility, and the MPC adopted the common law rule without modification.
(e) No, because the buyer-seller exception dictates that the existence of a buyer is irrelevant to the prima facie elements of drug distribution.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** The MPC abolishes both factual and legal impossibility (except pure legal impossibility where the act isn't criminalized at all). If the circumstances were as Ben believed them to be (that "Vulture" was a real buyer), his conduct would constitute a crime, making his impossibility defense fail. (a) is wrong because government-manufactured impossibility does not automatically trigger entrapment; entrapment requires lack of predisposition. (b) is wrong because this is not pure legal impossibility, as distributing drugs is generally illegal. (d) is wrong because while the common law rejected factual impossibility, the MPC modified the framework to focus on subjective belief. (e) is wrong because the buyer-seller exception limits conspiracy formation, not attempt or impossibility.
