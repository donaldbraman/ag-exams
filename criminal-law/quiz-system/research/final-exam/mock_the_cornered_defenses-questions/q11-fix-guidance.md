## Issue 1 — audit

**Q11.** Assume the jurisdiction generally imposes a duty to retreat before using deadly force, but explicitly recognizes the traditional Castle Doctrine. Was Dante required to use the unobstructed fire-escape window before shooting Hector?

(a) Yes, because the window provided a completely safe and immediate avenue of retreat that a reasonable person would have definitively utilized to avoid criminal homicide.
(b) Yes, because Hector had not yet pointed his weapon at Dante, triggering the strict duty to exhaust all peaceful alternatives before escalating to deadly force.
(c) No, because the recognized Castle Doctrine categorically eliminates the duty to retreat when a person is directly attacked by an intruder within their own home. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because the duty to retreat is strictly waived whenever the defending occupant is confronted by an armed aggressor affiliated with a known criminal enterprise.
(e) No, because Dante's intense subjective state of panic legally excused him from accurately assessing whether the fire escape provided a genuinely safe and viable exit.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** The Castle Doctrine is a universally recognized exception to the duty to retreat. Even in jurisdictions that strictly require retreat before using deadly force (like New York), a person attacked in their own home is not required to flee. Since Hector kicked open Dante's apartment door, Dante was inside his "castle." (a) is incorrect because the presence of a safe, immediate escape route is exactly what the Castle Doctrine renders legally irrelevant. (b) is incorrect because the duty to retreat focuses on location and escape routes, not whether the aggressor's weapon is fully drawn. (d) is incorrect because the waiver of the duty to retreat is based on the location (the home), not the gang affiliation or criminal status of the attacker. (e) is incorrect because the Castle Doctrine provides an objective legal waiver of the duty to retreat, rather than relying on the defendant's subjective panic or failure to perceive an exit.
