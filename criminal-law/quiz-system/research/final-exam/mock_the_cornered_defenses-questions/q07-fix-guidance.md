## Issue 1 — grounding

**Q7.** Assume the jurisdiction explicitly enumerates illicit drug manufacturing as a predicate offense for felony murder. Under the traditional common law, assume the jurisdiction explicitly enumerates illicit drug manufacturing as a predicate offense in its first-degree murder statute. Is Dante guilty of felony murder for the death of Vargas?

(a) Guilty, because Vargas's death occurred during the ongoing commission of the manufacturing felony and Silas fired the shots strictly to protect the illicit operation. <!-- correct -->
(b) Guilty, because Silas's recognized status as a formal co-conspirator automatically satisfies the malice aforethought element required for all degrees of criminal homicide charges.
(c) Not guilty, because Silas's decision to shoot the rival was motivated by a schizophrenic hallucination rather than a conscious intent to advance the manufacturing felony.
(d) Not guilty, because Dante's physical absence from the warehouse means the homicide was not committed within the immediate res gestae of his individual actions.
(e) Not guilty, because Vargas was an armed rival dealer actively engaged in criminal conduct, exempting him from the class of innocent victims protected by law.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Felony murder applies when a death occurs within the "res gestae" (time, place, and causal scope) of a qualifying predicate felony. The lab was actively synthesizing drugs when Silas shot Vargas to protect the perimeter, bringing the homicide squarely within the commission of the enumerated felony. As a co-felon, Dante is strictly liable for deaths caused by his accomplice during the crime. (b) is incorrect because felony murder substitutes the intent to commit the felony for malice aforethought; co-conspirator status alone does not magically supply traditional malice. (c) is incorrect because felony murder focuses on objective causation and temporal proximity; Silas's subjective delusion does not sever the res gestae when his objective act protected the active lab. (d) is incorrect because all participants in the underlying felony are liable for deaths caused during its commission, regardless of their physical presence at the scene. (e) is incorrect because the felony murder rule generally does not exempt criminal rivals from being qualifying victims when killed by a co-felon in furtherance of the predicate crime.
