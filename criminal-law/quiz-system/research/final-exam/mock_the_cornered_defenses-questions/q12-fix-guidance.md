**Q12.** Assume the jurisdiction recognizes the doctrine of imperfect self-defense. If the jury finds that Dante's belief in the need to use deadly force was genuine but objectively unreasonable, what is the legal result?

(a) Dante is fully acquitted because his genuinely held belief completely legally justifies his use of deadly force under the accepted imperfect self-defense legal doctrine.
(b) Dante's charge is mitigated from murder to voluntary manslaughter because his honest belief effectively negates the core malice element required for an intentional murder conviction. <!-- correct -->
(c) Dante's charge is mitigated to involuntary manslaughter because his highly unreasonable assessment of the threat objectively constituted extreme criminal negligence under the governing penal statute.
(d) Dante remains fully liable for murder because an objectively unreasonable belief cannot fundamentally alter the baseline culpability required for an intentional and premeditated criminal homicide.
(e) Dante is granted a mistrial because the jury's explicit finding of a genuine but unreasonable belief creates a fatal legal contradiction on the final verdict form.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** In jurisdictions that recognize imperfect self-defense (such as California), an honest but unreasonable belief in the need to use deadly force acts as a partial defense. It does not completely exonerate the defendant, but it legally negates the element of malice required for murder, mitigating the charge to voluntary manslaughter. (a) is incorrect because an unreasonable belief prevents the application of perfect self-defense, meaning the defendant is not fully acquitted. (c) is incorrect because imperfect self-defense negates malice to drop the charge to voluntary manslaughter, not involuntary manslaughter (which is typically based on recklessness or criminal negligence rather than intentional but mitigated killings). (d) is incorrect because this describes the outcome in a traditional all-or-nothing jurisdiction; the prompt specifies that this jurisdiction recognizes imperfect self-defense. (e) is incorrect because finding a belief to be genuine (subjective) but unreasonable (objective) is exactly how the doctrine is designed to function; there is no legal contradiction.
