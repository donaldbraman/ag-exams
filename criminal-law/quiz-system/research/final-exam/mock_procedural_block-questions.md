**Q1.** Is Mike legally liable as an accomplice to the transportation of fentanyl?

(a) Guilty because his custom compartment construction provided substantial material assistance to the cartel's drug smuggling operation.
(b) Guilty because he knew he was facilitating illegal smuggling activity and charged a fee for his mechanical services.
(c) Not guilty because a flat-rate mechanic cannot be held liable as an accomplice without an ongoing partnership agreement.
(d) Not guilty because he lacked the specific purpose to facilitate fentanyl distribution and had no stake in its success. <!-- correct -->
(e) Not guilty because he did not physically possess the drugs or participate directly in the transportation to the safehouse.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** Under the *Lauria* standard for accomplice liability, a provider of lawful goods or services must have the specific purpose to facilitate the target offense, not mere knowledge of criminal activity. Purpose can be inferred if the provider has a stake in the venture's success, but Mike charged a standard flat rate. Furthermore, Mike's mistake of fact (believing he was aiding cigarette smuggling) negates the *mens rea* for the specific offense of fentanyl transport. (a) is wrong because material assistance alone without purpose is insufficient. (b) is wrong because mere knowledge plus normal service fees does not equal purpose. (c) is wrong because an explicit partnership is not required; a stake in the venture would suffice. (e) is wrong because physical possession is an *actus reus* requirement for the principal, not the accomplice.

(a) It constitutes an enterprise because it is an ongoing organization functioning as a continuing unit with a formal hierarchy. <!-- correct -->

(c) is wrong because RICO expressly covers illegitimate associations-in-fact, not just chartered corporations. (d) is wrong because drug trafficking is a predicate racketeering activity under RICO. (e) is wrong because individual deviations by members do not dissolve the overarching enterprise structure.

**Q4.** Assume Sal claims duress as a defense to the drug transport charges. Will his defense succeed?

(a) Valid because Leo's position in a dangerous cartel makes his threats inherently and continuously imminent to his subordinates.
(b) Valid because Sal subjectively believed his family was in grave danger if he failed to deliver the drugs to the safehouse.

(a) is wrong because the law evaluates the specific threat's timing, not the general dangerousness of the threatener. (b) is wrong because duress requires an objectively imminent threat, not just a subjective fear of grave danger. (d) is wrong because duress is generally available for drug felonies (unlike intentional murder). (e) is wrong because while creating one's own peril limits the defense, joining an organization does not absolutely waive the defense absent specific foreseeability of coercion, and the "tomorrow" fact is the direct, dispositive doctrinal bar here.

**Q5.** Assume Sal challenges the validity of the traffic stop. Under established constitutional doctrines, what is the likely outcome?

(a) Unconstitutional under the Fourth Amendment because internal police communications prove the officer's primary motivation was racial bias.
(b) Constitutional under the Fourth Amendment because the objective traffic violation provides probable cause, regardless of subjective intent. <!-- correct -->
(c) Unconstitutional under the Equal Protection Clause because statistical evidence of racial bias automatically invalidates individual stops.
(d) Constitutional under the Equal Protection Clause because police officers are absolutely immune from selective prosecution claims for traffic stops.
(e) Unconstitutional under both amendments because pretextual stops are strictly prohibited when an unmarked police vehicle is utilized.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** Under *Whren v. United States*, a traffic stop is valid under the Fourth Amendment as long as the officer has objective probable cause to believe a traffic violation occurred (here, failure to signal). The officer's subjective motivations, even if demonstrably racist, are irrelevant to the Fourth Amendment analysis. While racially motivated stops violate the Equal Protection Clause, proving this in an individual case is extremely difficult under *Armstrong*, requiring evidence of similarly situated white drivers who were not stopped. (a) is wrong because *Whren* explicitly forecloses Fourth Amendment challenges based on subjective intent. (c) is wrong because statistical evidence alone does not automatically invalidate an individual stop under Equal Protection (*McCleskey*, *Armstrong*). (d) is wrong because officers are not absolutely immune from Equal Protection claims, even if they are hard to prove. (e) is wrong because unmarked vehicles do not render probable-cause stops unconstitutional.

**Q7.** Assume that Sal's use of force against the perceived hitman was justified as self-defense. How does this impact his liability for Victor's death?

(a) Guilty of intentional murder because the transferred intent doctrine strictly applies to any death caused during a violent shooting.
(b) Guilty of reckless homicide because firing into a crowded sidewalk constitutes a gross deviation from the standard of care. <!-- correct -->

**Q8.** Assume Sal is convicted of felony-murder for Victor's death during the drug trafficking operation. Is Leo liable for this death under the *Pinkerton* doctrine?

(a) Guilty because violence is an objectively reasonably foreseeable consequence of a major drug trafficking conspiracy, despite Leo's instructions. <!-- correct -->
(b) Guilty because a cartel leader is held to a standard of strict vicarious liability for any acts committed by subordinate members.
(c) Not guilty because Leo's express instruction to use "no guns, no violence" effectively negated the foreseeability required for Pinkerton liability.
(d) Not guilty because Sal's belief that he was shooting at a hitman constituted an unforeseeable independent intervening act.
(e) Not guilty because the actual victim was an innocent bystander rather than a law enforcement officer or rival cartel member.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under the *Pinkerton* doctrine, a conspirator is liable for the substantive offenses committed by co-conspirators if those acts are in furtherance of the conspiracy and are reasonably foreseeable as a necessary or natural consequence of the unlawful agreement. Courts consistently hold that armed violence and homicides are reasonably foreseeable consequences of large-scale drug trafficking. A boss's internal instruction ("no guns") does not defeat the objective legal foreseeability of violence in the narcotics trade. (b) is wrong because *Pinkerton* is not pure strict vicarious liability; it requires the act to be reasonably foreseeable and in furtherance of the conspiracy. (c) is wrong because objective foreseeability cannot be negated merely by internal cartel rules prohibiting what is otherwise a natural consequence of the crime. (d) is wrong because mistaking someone for a rival hitman is highly foreseeable in cartel operations. (e) is wrong because the identity of the victim does not alter the foreseeability of the shooting itself.

**Q9.** Did DA Paula's threat to charge Sal with a specialized aggravated trafficking offense carrying a mandatory 25-year sentence violate his constitutional rights?

(a) It violates due process because threatening a mandatory twenty-five-year sentence to secure a five-year plea is inherently coercive.
(b) It violates the separation of powers because choosing between overlapping statutes improperly usurps the legislature's sentencing authority.
(c) It is constitutional because prosecutors have unreviewable discretion to threaten harsher charges during negotiations if probable cause exists. <!-- correct -->
(d) It is constitutional because a mandatory minimum sentence automatically triggers a presumption of validity for any plea bargain offered.
(e) It violates equal protection because a standard five-year plea deal cannot be leveraged against a specialized aggravated trafficking offense.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** Under *Bordenkircher v. Hayes*, a prosecutor does not violate due process by threatening to seek a harsher charge if the defendant refuses a plea offer, so long as probable cause supports the threatened charge. Furthermore, under *Batchelder*, when overlapping statutes cover the same conduct, the prosecutor has unreviewable discretion to charge under whichever statute she chooses, including the one with the harsher penalty. (a) is wrong because *Bordenkircher* expressly allows this exact type of plea-or-trial pressure, ruling it is not unconstitutionally coercive. (b) is wrong because *Batchelder* held that choosing between overlapping statutes passed by the legislature does not violate separation of powers. (d) is wrong because the validity comes from the existence of probable cause, not from the presence of a mandatory minimum. (e) is wrong because there is no equal protection bar to leveraging a severe statute against a lenient plea offer.

**Answer:** (c)

(a) is wrong because temporal proximity does not convert a post-act gratuity into a pre-act bribe. (d) is wrong because state officials whose agencies receive over $10,000 in federal funds are explicitly covered by § 666. (e) is wrong because the federal funds do not need to trace to the specific consulting firm; they only need to meet the $10,000 threshold for the agency/office.

**Q14.** Was the trial judge's instruction regarding jury nullification legally permissible?

(a) Permissible because courts have an absolute duty to forestall jury nullification using any definitive instructions necessary to ensure compliance.
(b) Permissible because juries only have the factual power to nullify, not a legal right, and therefore cannot be instructed on nullification.

**Explanation:** While courts have no obligation to inform juries of their power to nullify and may instruct them to follow the law, *United States v. (a) is wrong because the duty to forestall nullification is not absolute; courts cannot use coercive threats of punishment or falsehoods. (c) is wrong because courts are never required to give an affirmative nullification instruction. (e) is wrong because general instructions to follow the law do not infringe on the fact-finding role; it is the specific coercive phrasing that is impermissible.
