**Q11.** Arthur and Silas are charged with conspiracy to distribute a Schedule I controlled substance. Arthur argues there was no valid conspiracy because his mistaken belief that the substance was a legal "nootropic" negates the required intent to agree to commit a crime. How will a court likely resolve this argument?

(a) The charge fails because Arthur's genuine mistake of law negates the specific purpose required to form a valid criminal conspiracy agreement.
(b) The charge fails because a valid conspiracy requires both co-conspirators to share an identical subjective understanding of the conduct's legal status.
(c) The charge succeeds because conspiracy requires only the intent to agree to the conduct, and ignorance of the penal law punishing that conduct is no excuse. <!-- correct -->
(d) The charge succeeds because the *Pinkerton* doctrine automatically imputes Silas's accurate knowledge of the law onto Arthur regardless of his mistake.
(e) The charge succeeds because conspiracy is a strict liability offense that completely dispenses with any mens rea requirement for the criminal agreement.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** The mens rea for conspiracy is the intent to agree and the intent to commit the target offense. Modern conspiracy law generally mirrors the mens rea of the substantive offense. Because drug distribution typically does not require knowledge of the drug's exact legal status (under public welfare principles), neither does the conspiracy to distribute it. Ignorance of the penal law is no excuse. (a) is incorrect because his mistake of law does not negate the purpose to distribute the chemical he synthesized. (b) is a made-up rule; identical subjective legal understanding is not required. (d) is incorrect because *Pinkerton* imputes liability for substantive acts committed in furtherance of the conspiracy, not knowledge of the law to form the conspiracy. (e) is entirely incorrect; conspiracy is a specific intent crime, not strict liability.
