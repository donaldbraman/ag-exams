**Q11.** Assume a conspiracy to distribute narcotics is established. Is Vance liable under the *Pinkerton* doctrine for Thorne's specific act of distributing the inaugural batch to Gable?

(a) Yes, because the distribution of the drugs was a reasonably foreseeable act committed by Thorne in furtherance of their ongoing conspiracy to sell the synthetic narcotics. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because under *Pinkerton*, any crime committed by a co-conspirator automatically creates strict liability for all other members of the conspiracy, regardless of reasonable foreseeability.
(c) No, because Vance did not personally hand the drugs to Gable, and the *Pinkerton* doctrine requires the defendant to commit the physical actus reus of the offense.
(d) No, because Vance's explicit insistence on being present in the RV during the transaction demonstrates that he had legally abandoned the conspiracy prior to the sale.
(e) No, because Thorne's sale to Gable was an independent intervening act that fundamentally exceeded the originally agreed-upon scope of their specific target distribution offense.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because under the Pinkerton doctrine, a co-conspirator is liable for the substantive offenses committed by another co-conspirator if those acts were reasonably foreseeable and committed in furtherance of the conspiracy. (b) fails because Pinkerton liability is not absolute strict liability; it requires the acts to be reasonably foreseeable. (c) fails because the entire purpose of Pinkerton is to impose liability on conspirators who do not commit the physical actus reus. (d) fails because being present does not constitute legal abandonment, which requires an affirmative act communicating withdrawal. (e) fails because selling the drugs to a buyer was the exact target offense of the conspiracy, not an independent intervening act.
