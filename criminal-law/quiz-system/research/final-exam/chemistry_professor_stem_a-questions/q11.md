**Q11.** Arthur and Silas are charged with conspiracy to distribute a Schedule I controlled substance. Arthur argues that because he misread the database and genuinely believed the chemical was an unscheduled, legal supplement, they did not form the requisite conspiratorial intent. Will this defense succeed?

(a) Yes, because conspiracy requires the specific intent to violate the law, meaning Arthur's genuine belief that the substance was legal negates his conspiratorial intent.
(b) Yes, because the bilateral agreement requirement fails if one of the parties operates under a mistake of fact regarding the criminality of the enterprise.
(c) No, because Arthur had the purpose to distribute the exact molecular structure, and his ignorance of its prohibited status is a mistake of law that provides no excuse. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because conspiracy is a strict liability offense that requires only a mutual agreement to perform an act, regardless of the target offense's statutory elements.
(e) No, because the Corrupt Motive doctrine dictates that an agreement to sell any unregulated health supplement is inherently immoral and satisfies the mens rea for conspiracy.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** (c) is correct because Arthur knew the exact molecular structure he was synthesizing and agreeing to distribute. His ignorance that this structure was on the Schedule I list is a mistake of penal law ("ignorance of the law is no excuse"), which does not negate the specific intent to distribute that substance. (a) fails because conspiracy requires the purpose to commit the target acts, not the specific knowledge that those acts violate the penal code. (b) fails because Arthur's mistake was one of law, not fact, so the bilateral agreement to distribute the substance remained legally intact. (d) fails because conspiracy is a specific intent crime, not a strict liability offense. (e) fails because the Corrupt Motive doctrine is largely obsolete and is not required when the defendant has the purpose to commit the actual elements of the offense.
