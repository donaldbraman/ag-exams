**Q11.** Assume a conspiracy is established. Under the *Pinkerton* doctrine, is Vance liable for Thorne's specific act of distributing the drugs to Gable?

(a) Yes, because Thorne's specific act of distributing the drugs to Gable was a reasonably foreseeable offense committed directly in furtherance of their ongoing synthetic narcotics conspiracy. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because Vance merely manufactured the drugs and remained inside the RV, meaning he did not physically hand the narcotics to Gable during the actual distribution transaction.
(c) Yes, because the Pinkerton doctrine imposes absolute liability on all conspirators for any crimes committed by co-conspirators, regardless of whether the crimes furthered the original criminal objective.
(d) No, because Thorne specifically arranged the sale to a single recreational buyer, which constitutes an independent frolic that legally severs Vance's liability for the targeted distribution offense.
(e) Yes, because Vance held a position of authority as Thorne's former professor, which automatically creates principal-agent liability for all actions Thorne takes while on university or public property.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because the *Pinkerton* doctrine holds co-conspirators liable for foreseeable substantive offenses committed by their partners in furtherance of the conspiracy. (b) is wrong because *Pinkerton* expressly allows conviction without physical participation in the target offense. (c) is wrong because liability is not absolute; it is limited to foreseeable crimes committed in furtherance of the conspiracy. (d) is wrong because selling to a buyer is exactly what the conspiracy was formed to do, not an independent frolic outside the scope of the agreement. (e) is wrong because criminal liability relies on conspiratorial agency, not general university employment relationships.
