**Q11.** Assume Silas is charged with felony murder for Julian's death. Under the constitutional floor established by *Tison v. Arizona*, what is Silas's strongest defense against this charge?

(a) Silas cannot be convicted because his attempt to call 911 and verbal protests show he lacked the required reckless indifference to human life. <!-- correct -->
(b) Silas can be convicted because his role as a getaway driver automatically makes him a major participant, satisfying the entire constitutional standard.
(c) Silas cannot be convicted because the standard categorically prohibits applying felony murder to an accomplice who did not personally kill the victim.
(d) Silas can be convicted because his mere presence during the drug sale creates a conclusive presumption of implied malice and extreme recklessness.
(e) Silas cannot be convicted because the standard requires proof that the accomplice acted with the specific purpose to cause the victim's death.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. To receive the death penalty or a felony murder conviction under the *Tison* standard (adopted broadly in reformed states like California), an accomplice who does not personally kill must be a "major participant" who acts with "reckless indifference to human life." Silas's immediate attempt to call 911 and his verbal protests strongly suggest he lacked the required reckless indifference.
(b) is incorrect because being a getaway driver does not automatically satisfy the "major participant" standard; it requires a fact-intensive inquiry.
(c) is incorrect because *Tison* specifically allows felony murder convictions for non-killers if the major participant/reckless indifference test is met.
(d) is incorrect because mere presence is insufficient to establish implied malice or reckless indifference under any constitutional standard.
(e) is incorrect because *Tison* explicitly rejected the requirement that the accomplice must have the specific intent to kill.
