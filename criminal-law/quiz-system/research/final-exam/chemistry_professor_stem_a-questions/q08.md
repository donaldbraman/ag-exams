**Q8.** Assume the jurisdiction applies the facts-as-committed approach and finds the manufacturing felony is inherently dangerous. Vance argues the manufacturing charge cannot serve as a felony-murder predicate because it merges with the homicide. Is this argument likely to succeed?

(a) Yes, because all felonies that result in death inherently merge with the homicide to prevent the felony-murder rule from applying to every unintentional death.
(b) Yes, because manufacturing drugs is treated as an assaultive crime that lacks any independent felonious purpose beyond the physical harm it directly causes the victim.
(c) No, because the felony of manufacturing a controlled substance has an independent felonious purpose—producing drugs to fund his debts—separate from the violence that caused the death. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because the merger doctrine historically only applies to predicate felonies involving theft or property damage, not to standalone synthetic drug manufacturing offenses.
(e) No, because the merger limitation has been universally abolished in all modern jurisdictions that recognize the facts-as-committed test for inherent dangerousness in felony-murder cases.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** (c) is correct because the merger doctrine applies to assaultive felonies that have no independent purpose beyond the violence; drug manufacturing has an independent felonious purpose (creating drugs/money), so it does not merge. (a) fails because not all felonies merge; only those lacking an independent purpose merge. (b) fails because manufacturing drugs is not an inherently assaultive crime like battery or assault with a deadly weapon. (d) fails because the merger doctrine applies broadly to assaultive crimes, not just theft or property damage. (e) fails because jurisdictions that use the facts-as-committed test often still apply the merger doctrine to prevent all assaults from becoming felony murder.
