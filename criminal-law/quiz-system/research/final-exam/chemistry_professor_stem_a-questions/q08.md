**Q8.** Assume the court finds the predicate felony inherently dangerous. Does the "unlicensed manufacturing" predicate satisfy the merger doctrine's independent-purpose limitation?

(a) Yes, because the primary goal of manufacturing the synthetic drugs was financial gain, which represents a felonious purpose entirely independent from causing the victim's physical death. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because the manufacturing and the subsequent fatal overdose occurred within the same general sequence of events, meaning the predicate offense completely merges into the resulting homicide.
(c) Yes, because the merger doctrine only applies to predicate felonies that involve direct physical assaults, and manufacturing drugs never involves any form of physical interaction with buyers.
(d) No, because Vance specifically manufactured the drugs to sell them to recreational buyers like Gable, which means the manufacturing process shared the exact same purpose as the homicide.
(e) Yes, because the independent-purpose limitation is automatically satisfied whenever a defendant engages an accomplice to assist with the distribution and sale of the manufactured controlled substances.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because the merger doctrine (*Wilson*) only bars predicates that are purely assaultive; manufacturing drugs for profit has a distinct, independent felonious purpose separate from the physical injury that caused the death. (b) is wrong because temporal sequence does not dictate merger; the legal purpose of the felony is what matters. (c) is wrong because while merger typically applies to assaultive felonies, the rule is based on independent purpose, not a strict categorical ban on all non-physical crimes. (d) is wrong because selling to buyers for profit is a distinct purpose from causing their death. (e) is wrong because accomplice involvement has no bearing on whether the underlying felony has an independent purpose.
