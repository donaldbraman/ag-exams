**Q8.** Silas argues that he effectively withdrew from the conspiracy when he protested, pulled out his phone, and stated, "He's dying, I'm calling 911." Under the traditional common law requirements for withdrawal, is Silas's argument likely to succeed?

(a) Yes, because his attempt to call 911 constituted an affirmative act that fully communicated his intent to withdraw before the victim's death.
(b) Yes, because his verbal protest to Arthur legally nullified his prior agreement and immediately severed him from the ongoing criminal enterprise.
(c) No, because withdrawal from a completed conspiracy is legally impossible under any circumstances once the initial agreement has been formed.
(d) No, because although he briefly protested, he ultimately acquiesced and fled with Arthur, failing to effectuate a complete and unequivocal abandonment. <!-- correct -->
(e) No, because a legally valid withdrawal universally requires a defendant to surrender to law enforcement and fully confess to the underlying agreement.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** To successfully withdraw from a conspiracy under the common law, a defendant must take an affirmative act that completely and unequivocally communicates their abandonment to all co-conspirators in time for them to abandon the plan. While Silas protested and tried to call 911, he capitulated when Arthur broke the phone, and he followed Arthur out of the room. This demonstrates he did not fully and unequivocally abandon the enterprise. (a) and (b) are incorrect because his verbal protest was negated by his subsequent acquiescence and flight. (c) is incorrect because withdrawal is possible (to cut off liability for future substantive crimes), even if it doesn't wipe out the initial conspiracy charge. (e) is incorrect because communicating to co-conspirators is sufficient; surrendering to police is one method, but not universally required.
