**Q8.** Assume Silas argues that by stating "He's dying, I'm calling 911," and protesting Arthur's decision to leave, he successfully withdrew from the conspiracy and avoided liability for the subsequent homicide. Will this defense succeed?

(a) Yes, because his attempt to call 911 constituted an overt act contrary to the conspiracy's purpose, automatically terminating his liability for any subsequent crimes.
(b) Yes, because under the MPC, verbal protest combined with a genuine desire to abandon the criminal enterprise is sufficient to establish a complete withdrawal.
(c) No, because an effective withdrawal requires an affirmative, unequivocal repudiation of the agreement, and Silas ultimately acquiesced by following Arthur out of the room. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because withdrawal from a conspiracy requires a defendant to physically prevent the co-conspirator from committing any further overt acts in furtherance of the agreement.
(e) No, because once an overt act has been committed by any member, the conspiracy is legally complete and the withdrawal defense is permanently unavailable.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** (c) is correct because a valid withdrawal from a conspiracy requires an affirmative and unequivocal repudiation communicated to all co-conspirators. Although Silas protested, his ultimate compliance in leaving the room negated any unequivocal withdrawal. (a) fails because an uncompleted attempt to call 911 does not constitute a legally sufficient withdrawal if the defendant subsequently acquiesces to the co-conspirator's cover-up. (b) fails because the MPC requires that the defendant actually thwart the success of the conspiracy to establish a complete withdrawal defense. (d) fails because physical prevention is not required for withdrawal; unequivocal communication of abandonment is the standard. (e) fails because retaining a financial stake is evidence of ongoing participation, but no rule permanently bars withdrawal based solely on a prior financial arrangement.
