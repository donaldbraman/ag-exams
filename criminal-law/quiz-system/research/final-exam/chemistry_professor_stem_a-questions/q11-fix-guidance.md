**Q11.** Arthur and Silas are charged with conspiracy to distribute a Schedule I controlled substance. Arthur argues that because he genuinely believed the substance was a legal "nootropic," he and Silas lacked the specific intent required for conspiracy. How should the court rule on the conspiracy charge?

(a) Not guilty, because conspiracy requires the specific intent to commit an illegal act, which is negated by Arthur's genuine mistake of law.
(b) Not guilty, because the unilateral theory of conspiracy prevents conviction when one primary party lacks the requisite specific intent.
(c) Guilty, because conspiracy requires only the purpose to distribute the specific substance; ignorance of its regulatory status is no defense. <!-- correct -->
(d) Guilty, because conspiracy is a strict liability offense when the underlying target crime is an inherently dangerous public welfare offense.
(e) Guilty, because Silas's knowledge of the glassware and 20% cut imputes the required mental state to Arthur under the Pinkerton doctrine.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** (c) is correct. Conspiracy requires the purpose to commit the target offense (distributing this specific substance). Because the target crime is a public welfare offense where ignorance of the legal scheduling is no defense, conspiracy to commit it does not require knowledge that the substance is illegal either. Arthur had the purpose to distribute the substance, so his mistake of penal law is no defense. (a) fails because mistake of penal law generally provides no excuse, and conspiracy does not require knowledge of illegality. (b) fails because even under a unilateral theory, Arthur possessed the intent to distribute the substance. (d) fails because conspiracy is never a strict liability offense; it always requires the specific intent to agree and to achieve the object. (e) fails because *Pinkerton* attributes liability for substantive crimes, not the mens rea for the conspiracy itself.
