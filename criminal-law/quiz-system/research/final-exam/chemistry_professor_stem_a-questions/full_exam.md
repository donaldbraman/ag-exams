# Scenario: Adulterated Synthetics and the RV Tragedy

### Facts

1. Dr. Julian Vance, a tenured chemistry professor at a midwestern university, received a terminal pancreatic cancer diagnosis and began manufacturing synthetic narcotics in his mobile RV laboratory to fund his medical debts. He reconnected with his former student, Marcus Thorne, who had a history of street-level drug sales.
2. Vance would text Thorne when a 'special project' was ready. Thorne would find buyers, bring them to the RV, and split the cash with Vance, though they meticulously avoided explicitly discussing an ongoing business arrangement or partnership.
3. Due to a degraded precursor chemical, the inaugural batch's purity was unusually high and contained a toxic byproduct; Vance honestly believed the batch was standard-grade and safe, having omitted a final purity test.
4. Thorne arranged to sell the first batch to a single recreational buyer, Leo Gable, in a secluded commuter parking lot. Vance insisted on being present in the RV during the transaction.
5. The state is considering charging Vance under a health and safety code provision prohibiting the 'distribution of adulterated synthetic narcotics.' The statute is silent on mens rea and carries a maximum penalty of ten years in prison.
6. Gable purchased the drug, immediately ingested a triple-normal dose while standing just outside the RV door, and within three minutes collapsed onto the pavement experiencing severe convulsions and respiratory distress.
7. Thorne yelled, 'He's dying, call 911!' Vance dialed 9-1, but stopped, stating, 'If the police come, I lose my pension and my daughter gets nothing.' Vance pocketed the phone and ordered Thorne to drive away, leaving the convulsing Gable in the parking lot.
8. Gable died 30 minutes later; the medical examiner established that Gable would have had a 90% chance of survival if paramedics had been called immediately, though Gable's decision to take a massive dose contributed to the rapid onset of the reaction.
9. The state is evaluating felony-murder charges against Vance predicated on the felony of 'unlicensed manufacturing of a controlled substance.'
10. The state is also evaluating whether Thorne can be charged with felony murder or as an accomplice to homicide for Gable's death, despite Vance's independent decision to abandon the victim.

### Stem 2: The Lockbox Discovery

11. Two days later, police arrested Vance and Thorne and impounded the RV. During a search, officers found a locked lockbox under the passenger seat containing 500 grams of the synthetic narcotic.
12. Vance owned the RV and held the only key to the lockbox, but Thorne had a key to the passenger door, frequently slept in that seat, and knew the lockbox contained their inventory.

**Q1.** The state charges Vance with distribution of adulterated synthetic narcotics. Assuming the court applies the dangerous-items heuristic, does this rule support imposing strict liability on Vance for the adulterated batch?

(a) The dangerous-items heuristic supports strict liability because synthetic narcotics are inherently dangerous materials that place a dealer on notice of strict regulation, substituting for conscious knowledge. <!-- correct -->
(b) The dangerous-items heuristic rejects strict liability because the ten-year maximum penalty automatically overrides any inferences drawn from the dangerous character of the regulated substances.
(c) The dangerous-items heuristic rejects strict liability because synthetic narcotics have widespread lawful uses in medical settings, meaning ordinary possession does not reliably signal criminal regulation.
(d) The dangerous-items heuristic supports strict liability because the statute is entirely silent on mens rea, meaning courts must presume the legislature intended to eliminate all traditional intent elements.
(e) The dangerous-items heuristic supports strict liability because Vance operated an unlicensed commercial enterprise for personal financial gain rather than engaging in an isolated transaction.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because under *Balint* and *Morissette*, dealing in inherently dangerous items like narcotics puts the possessor on notice of likely regulation, which justifies substituting for conscious knowledge of the specific violation. (b) is wrong because while *Staples* says severe penalties push against strict liability, the dangerous-items heuristic itself is about notice, not penalty limits. (c) is wrong because unlike the firearms in *Staples*, illicit synthetic narcotics do not have widespread lawful, unregulated civilian uses. (d) is wrong because the *Morissette* presumption pushes against strict liability for silent statutes unless the factors are met. (e) is wrong because commercial enterprise alone does not trigger strict liability without regulatory bounds.

**Q2.** Assume that Vance is charged with homicide. Does Gable's decision to immediately ingest a triple-normal dose constitute an independent superseding cause that severs Vance's causal liability for the death?

(a) Yes, because Gable's voluntary and reckless decision to ingest three times the normal dose was an independent intervening act that fully severs the legal causal chain.
(b) No, because Gable's ingestion of the drug he just purchased was a reasonably foreseeable dependent consequence of the illicit sale, maintaining the direct causal chain. <!-- correct -->
(c) Yes, because the medical examiner established that Gable would have survived if he had taken a standard dose, making his own conduct the sole but-for cause.
(d) No, because any intervening act by a victim automatically qualifies as a dependent cause whenever the underlying criminal statute involves the distribution of controlled substances.
(e) Yes, because Gable's decision occurred after the transaction was entirely completed, rendering his subsequent overdose temporally and legally severed from the defendants' distribution.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** (b) is correct because under proximate cause principles, a victim's predictable reaction to the defendant's crime (such as ingesting purchased drugs) is a dependent intervening cause that does not sever legal liability. (a) is wrong because the overdose was a highly foreseeable consequence, not an unforeseeable independent superseding act. (c) is wrong because multiple actors can be but-for causes simultaneously; the victim's contribution does not legally erase the defendant's conduct as a cause. (d) is wrong because there is no categorical rule making all acts dependent in drug cases; it requires a foreseeability analysis. (e) is wrong because proximate cause extends beyond the strict temporal completion of the immediate transaction.
