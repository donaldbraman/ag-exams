**Q10.** Arthur is charged with possessing the remaining 498 grams of the substance in the backpack with intent to distribute. Because Silas freely reached into the backpack to retrieve the two grams, Arthur argues he lacked exclusive control over the bag, defeating the possession charge. How should the court rule?

(a) Arthur is not guilty of possession, because Silas's unrestricted access to the backpack transferred exclusive possession to Silas.
(b) Arthur is not guilty of possession, because constructive possession cannot be established in a jointly occupied physical space without a confession.
(c) Arthur is guilty of possession, because he maintained exclusive control of the bag despite Silas's momentary intrusion.
(d) Arthur is guilty of possession, because even in non-exclusive control situations, his ownership of the bag and physical carrying of it provide the required affirmative link to the drugs. <!-- correct -->
(e) Arthur is guilty of possession, because the law presumes that all individuals present during a drug transaction have joint constructive possession of the entire quantity.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** (d) is correct. When a defendant lacks exclusive control over the location of drugs, the prosecution must show an "affirmative link" connecting the defendant to the contraband. Arthur's physical carrying of the backpack and ownership of it establish this affirmative link, proving actual or constructive possession despite Silas's shared access. (a) fails because access by others does not defeat possession; multiple people can jointly possess contraband. (b) fails because an affirmative link can be proven by circumstantial evidence. (c) fails because allowing another to freely retrieve items demonstrates the control was not strictly exclusive, triggering the affirmative link requirement. (e) fails because mere presence at a drug transaction is insufficient to presume constructive possession without an affirmative link.
