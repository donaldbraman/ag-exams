**Q15.** The state considers charging Thorne as an accomplice to Vance's homicide by omission (fleeing without completing the 911 call). Will Thorne's act of driving the RV away make him liable as an accomplice to the homicide?

(a) Yes, because driving the RV away provided physical assistance to Vance, and Thorne knew that Vance was purposefully abandoning Gable to protect his pension and daughter.
(b) Yes, because Thorne's initial shout to call 911 proves he subjectively understood the medical danger, completely fulfilling the required mental state for accomplice liability to homicide.
(c) No, because accomplice liability requires the purpose to promote the crime, and Thorne drove away only because Vance ordered him to, not because he intended Gable to die. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because Thorne cannot be an accomplice to a crime of omission, as accomplice liability strictly requires an affirmative physical act by the primary principal to the crime.
(e) No, because Thorne and Vance had a formal conspiracy, meaning Thorne can only be charged under the *Pinkerton* doctrine and not as an accomplice to the omission.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** (c) is correct because accomplice liability generally requires the purpose to promote or facilitate the underlying offense; Thorne drove away because Vance ordered him to, not because Thorne had the conscious object of causing Gable's death. (a) fails because knowing that Vance was abandoning Gable establishes knowledge, not the required purpose to facilitate the homicide. (b) fails because recognizing the danger does not equal a purpose to cause the resulting death. (d) fails because a person can theoretically be an accomplice to an omission if they purposefully facilitate the principal's breach of duty. (e) fails because the existence of a conspiracy does not legally preclude alternative charges under accomplice liability theories.
