**Q15.** The state considers charging Thorne as an accomplice to Vance's homicide by omission. Under the standard requiring "purpose" for accomplice liability, does Thorne's conduct satisfy the mens rea element?

(a) No, because Thorne explicitly shouted for Vance to call emergency services, demonstrating that his true purpose was to rescue the victim rather than to facilitate Vance's fatal criminal omission. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because Thorne previously arranged the illicit transaction, meaning his original intent to sell the drugs legally transfers to provide the requisite purpose for the subsequent homicide by omission.
(c) No, because accomplice liability strictly requires the defendant to physically participate in the actus reus, and Thorne was merely a passive observer when Vance pocketed the phone and fled.
(d) Yes, because Thorne knew that leaving the scene would result in Gable's death, and mere knowledge of the principal's criminal outcome automatically satisfies the purpose standard for accomplice liability.
(e) No, because Thorne was a subordinate in the criminal enterprise, and the purpose requirement dictates that only the ringleader can possess the specific intent necessary for aiding and abetting.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because accomplice liability generally requires the "purpose" to promote or facilitate the crime; Thorne's desperate plea to save the victim negates the purpose to commit homicide by omission. (b) is wrong because intent does not transfer from a drug sale to a homicide by omission. (c) is wrong because accomplices can aid by psychological support or failing to intervene if they have a duty, though here Thorne's words negate the purpose element anyway. (d) is wrong because knowledge of the principal's crime is generally insufficient for accomplice liability; the standard is purpose or conscious object. (e) is wrong because subordinates can absolutely possess the required purpose to aid and abet.
