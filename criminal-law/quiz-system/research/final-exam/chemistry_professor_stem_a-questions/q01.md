**Q1.** Assume Arthur is charged with manslaughter under the Model Penal Code based solely on synthesizing and distributing the drug. How does Arthur's mistaken belief about the drug's purity affect this charge?

(a) Not guilty of manslaughter because his genuine mistake of fact negates the subjective awareness of risk required to establish recklessness under the MPC. <!-- correct -->
(b) Guilty of manslaughter because a mistake of fact must be objectively reasonable to serve as a legal defense to any criminal homicide charge.
(c) Guilty of manslaughter because the Morissette presumption reads in a strict liability standard that applies automatically to all unregulated synthetic drug offenses.
(d) Not guilty of manslaughter because his mistake regarding the chemical byproduct is classified as a valid mistake of law concerning the chemical composition.
(e) Guilty of manslaughter because an honest mistake only negates specific intent, and involuntary manslaughter is consistently classified as a general intent crime.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Under the MPC, a mistake of fact is a defense if it negates the mental state required for the offense. Manslaughter requires recklessness, which means conscious disregard of a substantial and unjustifiable risk. Arthur's genuine belief that the drug was pure means he lacked subjective awareness of the toxicity risk, negating recklessness.
(b) is incorrect because the MPC focuses on whether the mistake negates the specific mental state, meaning an honest but unreasonable mistake can negate subjective recklessness.
(c) is incorrect because the *Morissette* presumption limits strict liability and presumes a mens rea requirement, rather than reading one out.
(d) is incorrect because mistaking the physical chemical makeup of the drug is a mistake of fact, not a mistake of law.
(e) is incorrect because the specific/general intent distinction is a common law concept that the MPC abandoned in favor of defined mental states.
