**Q1.** Arthur is charged with manufacturing a Schedule I controlled substance. The statute is silent on mens rea. He argues he genuinely believed he was making a legal supplement. Is he guilty?

(a) Guilty, because under the public welfare doctrine, individuals dealing in inherently dangerous items like narcotics act at their peril and cannot claim ignorance of the law. <!-- correct -->
(b) Guilty, because the Morissette presumption automatically reads absolute strict liability into any federal statute that protects broad public health and consumer safety interests.
(c) Not guilty, because when a criminal statute is silent on mental state, courts must presume the legislature intended to require actual knowledge of the item's illegality.
(d) Not guilty, because his genuine belief that the substance was a legal nootropic constitutes an honest mistake of fact that negates the voluntary act requirement.
(e) Not guilty, because ordinary possession or manufacture of a chemical compound does not provide reasonable notice that the activity is subject to stringent public regulation.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because under the public welfare doctrine (e.g., *Balint*), individuals dealing in inherently dangerous items like narcotics are put on notice of regulation and act at their peril, eliminating the ignorance of the law defense. (b) fails because the Morissette presumption reads mens rea *into* statutes, rather than imposing strict liability. (c) fails because the dangerous items heuristic creates an exception to the general presumption of mens rea. (d) fails because a mistake of law is generally no excuse, and the public welfare doctrine precludes a mistake defense for dangerous instrumentalities. (e) fails because courts have consistently held that narcotics and chemical manufacturing are exactly the types of dangerous activities that put a reasonable person on notice of regulation.
