**Q1.** Arthur is charged with manufacturing a Schedule I controlled substance. He argues that he cannot be convicted because he honestly and genuinely believed the chemical he was synthesizing was a legal, unscheduled supplement. Assume the jurisdiction follows federal public welfare offense principles for narcotics. Is Arthur's defense likely to succeed?

(a) Yes, because his genuine mistake of fact entirely negates the general intent required for criminal liability under traditional common law doctrine.
(b) Yes, because a defendant cannot be strictly liable for a felony offense carrying substantial prison time without proof he knew the substance was illegal.
(c) No, because under *Balint*, those who deal in narcotics must ascertain the legal status of the substance at their peril, and ignorance is no defense. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because the *Morissette* presumption automatically categorizes all drug manufacturing offenses as strict liability crimes regardless of the penalty.
(e) No, because his mistake was an unreasonable mistake of law rather than a mistake of fact, and only reasonable mistakes of law provide a valid defense.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** Under *United States v. Balint*, the dangerous-items heuristic applies to narcotics. Those who deal in inherently dangerous drugs are put on notice of likely regulation and must ascertain the legal status of their products at their peril. The statute's silence on mens rea allows for strict liability regarding the substance's legal classification. (a) is incorrect because mistake does not excuse public welfare offenses involving dangerous drugs. (b) applies the *Staples* limit for firearms, which does not override *Balint* for narcotics. (d) is incorrect because *Morissette* actually presumes *mens rea*, not strict liability, unless specific factors (like dangerous items) apply. (e) is incorrect because even a reasonable mistake of law would fail here; the doctrine simply does not require knowledge of the legal status.
