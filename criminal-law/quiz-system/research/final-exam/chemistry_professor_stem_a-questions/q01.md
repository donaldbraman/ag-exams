**Q1.** The state charges Vance with distribution of adulterated synthetic narcotics. Assuming the court applies the dangerous-items heuristic, does this rule support imposing strict liability on Vance for the adulterated batch?

(a) The dangerous-items heuristic supports strict liability because synthetic narcotics are inherently dangerous materials that place a dealer on notice of strict regulation, substituting for conscious knowledge. <!-- correct -->
(b) The dangerous-items heuristic rejects strict liability because the ten-year maximum penalty automatically overrides any inferences drawn from the dangerous character of the regulated substances.
(c) The dangerous-items heuristic rejects strict liability because synthetic narcotics have widespread lawful uses in medical settings, meaning ordinary possession does not reliably signal criminal regulation.
(d) The dangerous-items heuristic supports strict liability because the statute is entirely silent on mens rea, meaning courts must presume the legislature intended to eliminate all traditional intent elements.
(e) The dangerous-items heuristic supports strict liability because Vance operated an unlicensed commercial enterprise for personal financial gain rather than engaging in an isolated transaction.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because under *Balint* and *Morissette*, dealing in inherently dangerous items like narcotics puts the possessor on notice of likely regulation, which justifies substituting for conscious knowledge of the specific violation. (b) is wrong because while *Staples* says severe penalties push against strict liability, the dangerous-items heuristic itself is about notice, not penalty limits. (c) is wrong because unlike the firearms in *Staples*, illicit synthetic narcotics do not have widespread lawful, unregulated civilian uses. (d) is wrong because the *Morissette* presumption pushes against strict liability for silent statutes unless the factors are met. (e) is wrong because commercial enterprise alone does not trigger strict liability without regulatory bounds.
