**Q1.** The state charges Vance with violating a health and safety code provision prohibiting the "distribution of adulterated synthetic narcotics." Vance argues the statute is silent on mens rea and he did not know the drugs were adulterated. The state counters that under the dangerous-items heuristic, no mens rea needs to be proven. Is the state's argument regarding the heuristic doctrinally sound?

(a) Yes, because a dealer in narcotics deals in inherently dangerous items and must ascertain at his peril whether his products comply with the law, satisfying the heuristic's notice requirement. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because the statute carries a maximum penalty of ten years in prison, which is the exact minor penalty threshold required to apply the dangerous-items heuristic.
(c) No, because the dangerous-items heuristic only applies to statutory offenses where the legislature has explicitly specified a strict liability standard in the text.
(d) No, because synthetic narcotics have widespread innocent uses and their possession does not place an individual on notice of regulation.
(e) No, because Vance did not have subjective knowledge of the toxic byproduct, and the heuristic requires conscious awareness of the specific danger.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because under the dangerous-items heuristic (Balint), dealing in inherently dangerous items like narcotics places the seller on notice of regulation, substituting for proof of specific mens rea regarding the regulatory status. (b) fails because a ten-year penalty is severe and actually cuts against applying strict liability (Staples). (c) fails because the heuristic applies to statutes that are silent on mens rea, not explicit ones. (d) fails because narcotics are not considered items in widespread innocent use. (e) fails because the heuristic replaces the need for subjective knowledge about the item's specific danger or regulatory status.
