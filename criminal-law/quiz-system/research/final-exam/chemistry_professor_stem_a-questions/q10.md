**Q10.** The state charges Vance and Thorne with conspiracy to distribute narcotics. Does their failure to explicitly discuss an ongoing business arrangement defeat the conspiracy charge?

(a) No, because a valid criminal agreement can be reasonably inferred from their highly coordinated pattern of texting, arranging meetings with buyers, and consistently splitting the illicit cash. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because conspiracy requires the prosecution to produce direct evidence of an express verbal or written contract between the parties outlining their specific criminal roles and duties.
(c) No, because the strict liability nature of synthetic narcotics distribution automatically negates the requirement to prove any mutual agreement between the primary manufacturer and the street-level dealer.
(d) Yes, because Vance and Thorne meticulously avoided discussing their partnership, successfully triggering the recognized safe-harbor defense for conspirators who intentionally maintain plausible deniability of their arrangement.
(e) No, because Thorne's history of street-level drug sales legally relieves the prosecution of its burden to establish that an actual agreement existed between Thorne and the chemistry professor.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because the agreement inference doctrine allows juries to infer a conspiracy from coordinated actions, mutual benefit, and synchronized conduct, even without explicit words. (b) is wrong because explicit verbal or written contracts are never required for a conspiracy conviction. (c) is wrong because conspiracy is a separate inchoate crime that always requires proof of an agreement, regardless of the target crime's strict liability status. (d) is wrong because plausible deniability is not a legal safe harbor if circumstantial actions prove the agreement. (e) is wrong because past character evidence does not relieve the state of proving the specific agreement for the charged conspiracy.
