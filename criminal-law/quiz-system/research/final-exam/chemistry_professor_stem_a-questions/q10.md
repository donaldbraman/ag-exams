**Q10.** The state charges Vance and Thorne with conspiracy to distribute narcotics. They argue they never explicitly discussed an ongoing business arrangement or partnership. Does their conduct establish the agreement element of conspiracy?

(a) Yes, because an agreement can be inferred from a coordinated course of conduct, such as Vance texting Thorne and Thorne consistently finding buyers and splitting cash. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because under the bilateral requirement, as long as Vance intended to distribute the drugs, Thorne's mere presence at the transactions is sufficient to form an agreement.
(c) No, because conspiracy strictly requires proof of an explicit, verbal or written agreement between the parties to commit the target offense before any liability can attach.
(d) No, because Thorne only acted as a buyer-seller conduit for Gable, which invokes the buyer-seller exception and legally prevents the formation of a conspiracy with Vance.
(e) No, because the parties meticulously avoided discussing their arrangement, which completely negates the specific intent to agree to a criminal enterprise required for a conspiracy charge.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because an agreement in a conspiracy does not need to be explicit; it can be inferred from a coordinated course of conduct, such as Vance's texts and Thorne consistently bringing buyers and splitting cash. (b) fails because conspiracy requires the intent to agree and intent to commit the target offense, not merely presence under a bilateral approach. (c) fails because explicit verbal or written agreements are not required to prove conspiracy. (d) fails because Thorne did more than a single spot sale; he had an ongoing relationship splitting profits, taking him outside the narrow buyer-seller exception. (e) fails because intentionally avoiding discussion while still coordinating actions does not negate the underlying intent to agree to the scheme.
