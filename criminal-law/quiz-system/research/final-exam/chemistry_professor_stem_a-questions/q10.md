**Q10.** Assume the prosecution seeks to hold Silas liable as an accomplice to Arthur's homicide offenses. Under the majority rule for the mens rea of accomplice liability, how does Silas's act of driving the getaway car affect his culpability?

(a) Silas is not liable as an accomplice unless he drove the car with the specific purpose of promoting or facilitating Arthur's actions causing death. <!-- correct -->
(b) Silas is liable as an accomplice because his knowledge that driving the car would assist Arthur's escape is entirely sufficient under the majority rule.
(c) Silas is not liable as an accomplice because his verbal protest before driving away operates as a complete legal defense to accessorial mens rea.
(d) Silas is liable as an accomplice because the act of driving a getaway car is a strict liability element of criminal accomplice participation.
(e) Silas is not liable as an accomplice because his subordinate academic status to Arthur prevents him from forming the requisite independent criminal intent.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Under the majority rule, accomplice liability requires that the defendant aid or encourage the principal with the specific purpose of promoting or facilitating the crime. Silas is only liable as an accomplice if he drove the getaway car with the purpose of helping Arthur succeed in his homicide offenses, not merely out of panic.
(b) is incorrect because mere knowledge that one's actions will aid a crime is generally insufficient for accomplice liability under the majority rule.
(c) is incorrect because a verbal protest does not automatically negate mens rea if the defendant subsequently acts with the purpose to aid the crime.
(d) is incorrect because there are no strict liability elements of accomplice participation; the purpose mens rea must always be proven.
(e) is incorrect because subordinate status does not legally prevent a person from forming the purpose to aid a crime.
