**Q10.** Assume Silas is charged with possession of the entire 500 grams of the Schedule I substance. The 500 grams were entirely inside Arthur's backpack. However, Silas was able to freely reach into the backpack to take two grams. Under the doctrine of constructive possession, does Silas possess the entire 500 grams?

(a) Yes, because his ability to freely reach into the backpack and remove two grams demonstrates he had the power and intent to exercise dominion and control over it. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because Arthur and Silas were standing together in the same small motel room, establishing that Silas had exclusive physical control over the entire bag.
(c) No, because the doctrine of constructive possession strictly requires the defendant to be the legal owner of the container holding the illicit contraband.
(d) No, because Silas's access to the backpack was shared with Arthur, and non-exclusive access automatically defeats any claim of constructive possession as a matter of law.
(e) No, because taking only two grams out of the bag proves Silas affirmatively and intentionally rejected dominion and control over the remaining 498 grams.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Constructive possession requires that a defendant have knowledge of the contraband and the power and intent to exercise dominion and control over it. Silas's ability to freely access the backpack and remove drugs from it establishes that he had sufficient power and control over the stash, even though Arthur physically held the bag. (b) is incorrect because mere proximity in a room does not establish exclusive physical control (and constructive possession is used precisely when physical control is lacking). (c) is incorrect because legal ownership of the container is not required. (d) is incorrect because constructive possession can be joint; non-exclusive access does not automatically defeat the claim if control is otherwise proven. (e) is incorrect because taking a portion demonstrates access and control over the whole source.
