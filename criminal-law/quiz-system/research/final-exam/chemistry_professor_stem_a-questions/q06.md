**Q6.** Assume the jurisdiction charges Arthur with felony murder for the death, using drug distribution as the predicate felony. The jurisdiction follows the "abstract elements" test (*Phillips*) for determining if a felony is inherently dangerous. How will a court applying this test likely rule on the validity of the predicate felony?

(a) The felony qualifies because Arthur's specific conduct of providing an untested chemical was objectively dangerous to human life on these specific facts.
(b) The felony qualifies because drug distribution is categorically recognized as an assaultive crime that directly threatens the victim's physical safety.
(c) The felony does not qualify because the statutory elements of drug distribution can theoretically be committed safely without creating a high probability of death. <!-- correct -->
(d) The felony does not qualify because the independent felonious purpose of making a profit means the crime automatically merges with the resulting homicide.
(e) The felony does not qualify because felony murder categorically requires the predicate offense to be a violent crime directed at the victim's physical person.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** The "abstract elements" test (*Phillips*) assesses whether a felony is inherently dangerous by looking solely at the statutory definition, ignoring the defendant's actual conduct. If the felony can theoretically be committed in a way that does not endanger human life (e.g., distributing a safe but unprescribed drug), it is not inherently dangerous in the abstract. (a) incorrectly applies the "facts-as-committed" test instead of the abstract elements test. (b) is incorrect because drug distribution is not an assaultive crime. (d) misunderstands the merger doctrine; crimes with an independent felonious purpose (like making a profit) *do not* merge. Only assaultive crimes lacking an independent purpose merge. (e) is incorrect because predicate felonies do not have to be violent crimes directed at a person (e.g., arson, burglary).
