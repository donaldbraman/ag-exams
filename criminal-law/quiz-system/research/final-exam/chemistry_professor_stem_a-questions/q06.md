**Q6.** If a jury determines that Vance's abandonment of Gable does not rise to the level of depraved-heart murder, could he still be convicted of involuntary manslaughter based on gross criminal negligence?

(a) Yes, because a reasonable person would have perceived the grave medical risk, making Vance's failure to complete the emergency call a gross deviation from ordinary care standards. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because if a jury acquits a defendant of depraved-heart murder, the double jeopardy clause strictly prohibits a subsequent conviction for involuntary manslaughter on the same facts.
(c) Yes, because gross criminal negligence merely requires proof of ordinary civil carelessness, which is inherently satisfied whenever a victim suffers a fatal injury during a commercial transaction.
(d) No, because involuntary manslaughter requires proof that the defendant was subjectively unaware of the risk, whereas Vance clearly recognized the danger when he initially dialed the phone.
(e) Yes, because the abandonment of a dying victim is classified as an intentional specific-intent offense, which perfectly mirrors the mens rea required for gross criminal negligence manslaughter.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because gross criminal negligence requires a gross deviation from the standard of care a reasonable person would observe, which Vance breached by abandoning a dying man. (b) is wrong because involuntary manslaughter is a lesser included offense of murder, and juries routinely convict on the lesser charge if the greater fails. (c) is wrong because criminal negligence requires a gross deviation, not just ordinary civil carelessness. (d) is wrong because gross negligence is an objective standard that can apply whether or not the defendant subjectively perceived the risk, and many jurisdictions use recklessness (subjective awareness) for manslaughter anyway. (e) is wrong because manslaughter is an unintentional homicide, not a specific-intent offense.
