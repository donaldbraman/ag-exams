**Q6.** Assume the prosecution charges Arthur with felony murder based on the predicate felony of drug distribution. In a jurisdiction that applies the California "elements in the abstract" approach, how will the court assess whether this felony is inherently dangerous?

(a) The predicate felony fails if the court determines that the statutory elements of drug distribution can theoretically be committed without endangering human life. <!-- correct -->
(b) The predicate felony qualifies because the court will evaluate the specific factual circumstances of Arthur knowingly distributing a highly toxic and lethal batch.
(c) The predicate felony fails because drug distribution lacks an independent felonious purpose and must automatically merge with the resulting unintentional homicide.
(d) The predicate felony qualifies because any offense involving Schedule I narcotics is automatically deemed a malum in se offense that implies inherent danger.
(e) The predicate felony fails because the doctrine requires that all predicate felonies be specifically and explicitly enumerated in the jurisdiction's penal code.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Under the California "elements in the abstract" approach (*Phillips*), a court ignores the defendant's specific dangerous conduct. Instead, it looks solely at the statutory definition of the felony to determine if the crime can theoretically be committed without endangering human life. If it can, it is not inherently dangerous.
(b) is incorrect because examining the specific facts of Arthur's toxic batch describes the majority "facts-as-committed" approach, which California rejects.
(c) is incorrect because it confuses the inherently dangerous limitation with the merger doctrine's independent purpose test.
(d) is incorrect because malum in se classifications do not automatically satisfy the abstract elements test for inherent danger.
(e) is incorrect because unenumerated felonies can still serve as predicates for second-degree felony murder if they pass the inherently dangerous test.
