**Q3.** Assume it is established that Gable's ingestion was not an independent intervening cause. Does Vance's failure to complete the 911 call satisfy the factual (but-for) cause element for Gable's death?

(a) Yes, because but for Vance pocketing the phone and ordering Thorne to drive away, Gable would have survived since he had a 90% chance of survival with immediate medical care. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because Vance's actions were the sole and exclusive cause of Gable's death, which is required since factual causation demands more than mere necessity.
(c) No, because Gable's ingestion of the massive drug dose was already fatal, meaning he would have died at the exact same moment regardless of the phone call.
(d) No, because factual causation requires an affirmative act, and a failure to complete a phone call is a mere omission that cannot satisfy the but-for test.
(e) No, because multiple concurrent sufficient causes existed in this case, meaning that Vance's failure to complete the call cannot be isolated as the required but-for cause of death.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because the but-for test asks if the result would have occurred without the defendant's conduct; since Gable had a 90% chance of survival if Vance had not stopped the 911 call, Vance's omission is a but-for cause of the death. (b) fails because factual causation only requires necessity (that the harm would not have occurred but for the act), not that the act be the sole and exclusive cause. (c) fails because the medical evidence establishes Gable would have likely survived with immediate care. (d) fails because an omission can satisfy the but-for test if the failure to act factually resulted in the harm. (e) fails because the drug and the lack of medical care are sequential contributing factors, not simultaneous concurrent sufficient causes.
