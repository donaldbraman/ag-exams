**Q3.** Evaluate whether Vance's failure to complete the 911 call satisfies the basic factual causation (but-for) requirement for Gable's death.

(a) Yes, because the medical evidence establishing a high survival chance proves that Gable's death would not have occurred but for Vance's failure to complete the 911 call. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because Gable's voluntary ingestion of the massive dose was the primary precipitating factor, meaning Vance's omission cannot be considered the singular cause of the death.
(c) Yes, because Vance possessed a special relationship with Thorne, which legally transferred the factual responsibility for any resulting injuries directly to Vance as the primary seller.
(d) No, because a ninety percent survival probability is insufficient to establish factual causation, which strictly requires absolute medical certainty that the victim would have survived.
(e) Yes, because Vance intentionally terminated the call to protect his pension, demonstrating a malicious state of mind that automatically satisfies the factual causation element for homicide.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because factual (but-for) causation only requires that the harm would not have occurred without the defendant's conduct or omission, which is satisfied by the medical examiner's finding of a 90% survival chance if paramedics had been called. (b) is wrong because the law permits multiple concurrent but-for causes; Vance's omission need not be the *singular* cause. (c) is wrong because factual causation relies on physical reality, not legal transfers of duty. (d) is wrong because the standard is proof beyond a reasonable doubt, not absolute 100% medical certainty. (e) is wrong because malicious intent is a mens rea concept, which cannot substitute for the physical actus reus of factual causation.
