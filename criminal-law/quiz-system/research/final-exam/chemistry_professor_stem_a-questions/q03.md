**Q3.** Assume that, whether or not Arthur caused the death by selling the drugs, prosecutors argue Arthur is liable for homicide based on his failure to summon medical aid. Does Arthur owe Julian an affirmative legal duty to act?

(a) Yes, because Arthur's conduct created the initial peril and his subsequent actions secluded Julian, both of which establish an affirmative legal duty to act. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because any individual who witnesses a life-threatening medical emergency has a general common law duty to contact emergency services and render reasonable aid.
(c) Yes, because the transaction created a formal contractual relationship between buyer and seller, which inherently carries a mutual duty of care and rescue.
(d) No, because Arthur and Julian lacked a recognized special status relationship, meaning Arthur's failure to call for help was a legally permissible pure omission.
(e) No, because Arthur genuinely believed that Julian would simply sleep off the reaction, and this honest mistake of fact negates any affirmative duty to intervene.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because Arthur triggered affirmative legal duties under two distinct common law categories: he created the initial peril by providing the dangerous drug, and he secluded the victim by locking the door and destroying the phone. (b) fails because there is no general common law duty to rescue strangers, absent a special relationship or assumption of care. (c) fails because a standard commercial transaction does not create a special status relationship requiring affirmative rescue. (d) fails because the lack of a status relationship is overcome by the creation of peril and seclusion exceptions. (e) fails because Arthur's subjective belief that Julian would recover does not erase the objective legal duty triggered by his affirmative acts of creating peril and secluding the victim.
