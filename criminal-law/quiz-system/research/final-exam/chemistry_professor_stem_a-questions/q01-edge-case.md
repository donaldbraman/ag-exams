**Q1.** If Arthur is charged with manufacturing and distributing Compound Y, how will the court likely interpret the statute's silence regarding mens rea?

(a) Read in a mens rea requirement because the severe 15-year penalty creates a presumption against strict liability. <!-- correct -->
(b) Treat it as a strict liability public welfare offense because dealing in drugs involves inherently dangerous items.
(c) Read in a mens rea requirement because all criminal statutes silent on mens rea require purpose.
(d) Treat it as strict liability because the statute does not explicitly contain the word "knowingly" or "purposely".
(e) Treat it as strict liability because Arthur possessed the capacity to prevent the harm to the public.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Under *Staples*, when a regulatory or criminal statute carries a severe penalty (like 15 years in prison), courts presume Congress intended a mens rea requirement even if the statute is silent. (b) fails because the dangerous items heuristic is generally limited by the penalty ceiling; major felonies rarely permit strict liability without explicit legislative intent. (c) fails because the default mens rea under the MPC is recklessness, not purpose. (d) fails because the *Morissette* presumption reads in mens rea despite statutory silence. (e) fails because the capacity to prevent harm justifies strict liability only for minor regulatory infractions, not serious felonies carrying decade-plus sentences.
