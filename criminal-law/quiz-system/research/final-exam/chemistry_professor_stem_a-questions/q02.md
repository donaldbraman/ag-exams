**Q2.** Vance argues that Gable's decision to ingest a triple-normal massive dose of the drug constitutes an independent intervening cause that severs Vance's causal liability for Gable's overdose. Is this argument likely to succeed under common law causation principles?

(a) Yes, because Gable's ingestion of a triple dose was a voluntary and independent act by a third party that breaks the chain of proximate causation.
(b) Yes, because Gable's decision was a dependent intervening cause that completely overtook Vance's original conduct in producing the death.
(c) No, because a victim's use of a distributed drug is a dependent intervening cause that is highly extraordinary and therefore maintains the causal chain.
(d) No, because the victim's ingestion of the drug he just purchased is a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the sale, making it a dependent rather than independent intervening cause. <!-- correct -->
(e) No, because under the common law, any intervening action by a victim is automatically disregarded if the defendant's act was the but-for cause of the harm.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** (d) is correct because Gable's ingestion of the drug he just purchased is a reasonably foreseeable continuation of the drug sale, making it a dependent intervening cause that does not break the causal chain. (a) fails because a buyer's expected use of the product is not completely independent of the seller's distribution. (b) fails because a dependent cause does not sever proximate causation; it must be independent to do so. (c) fails because ingesting a drug one just bought is not highly extraordinary, which would otherwise sever causation under some common law tests. (e) fails because the common law does not automatically disregard victim actions; unforeseeable independent acts can sever the chain.
