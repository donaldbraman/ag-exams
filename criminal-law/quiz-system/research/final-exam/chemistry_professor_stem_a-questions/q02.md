**Q2.** Assume Arthur is charged with homicide for Julian's death. He argues that Julian's voluntary act of ingesting the drug was an independent intervening cause that severed the causal chain from Arthur's distribution. Will this defense succeed under the common law?

(a) Yes, because Julian's ingestion of the drug was a deliberate, voluntary act by a third party, which categorically acts as an independent, superseding chain-breaker.
(b) Yes, because it was not reasonably foreseeable that Julian would suffer a fatal cardiac arrhythmia from a substance Arthur genuinely thought was a safe supplement.
(c) No, because Julian's voluntary ingestion of the distributed substance was a dependent, foreseeable consequence of the drug sale, rather than an independent chain-breaker. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because under the common law, only a completely unforeseeable act of nature can sever the chain of proximate causation and relieve a defendant of liability.
(e) No, because any factual but-for contribution to a victim's death automatically satisfies the requirement for legal causation regardless of subsequent intervening human acts.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** (c) is correct because a victim's voluntary ingestion of a distributed drug is a highly foreseeable, dependent consequence of the sale, not an independent intervening cause that severs liability. (a) fails because voluntary acts by third parties or victims do not automatically sever the causal chain if they are foreseeable and dependent on the defendant's conduct. (b) fails because the specific mechanism of death need not be foreseen, only that the distribution of a dangerous substance carries a foreseeable risk of serious harm. (d) fails because human actions, including reckless medical care or third-party violence, can also serve as superseding causes if they are truly independent. (e) fails because legal (proximate) causation requires more than mere factual but-for contribution; the result must not be too remote or accidental.
