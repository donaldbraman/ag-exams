**Q2.** Assume Arthur is charged with homicide for Julian's death. Arthur argues that Julian's voluntary, deliberate decision to ingest the substance was an independent intervening cause that severed Arthur's causal chain. Will this argument succeed?

(a) Yes, because a third party's voluntary ingestion of drugs constitutes an independent intervening act that universally breaks the chain of proximate causation.
(b) Yes, because Julian's decision to ingest the two grams of the drug was a completely unforeseeable superseding event under these specific factual circumstances.
(c) No, because Julian's ingestion was a dependent intervening cause that was a highly foreseeable consequence of Arthur supplying the drugs for that purpose. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because any subsequent action by a victim, no matter how unforeseeable or extraordinary, preserves causation if basic factual but-for causation is met.
(e) No, because a victim's voluntary actions are legally incapable of acting as superseding causes in any homicide cases involving the distribution of narcotics.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** In causation analysis, a dependent intervening cause (one set in motion by the defendant or highly foreseeable based on the defendant's acts) does not sever proximate causation. When Arthur and Silas provided the drug to Julian for the purpose of consumption, Julian's voluntary consumption was the exact foreseeable mechanism of harm. (a) is incorrect because voluntary ingestion of a supplied drug is highly foreseeable, not an independent chain-breaker. (b) is factually incorrect; the ingestion was entirely foreseeable. (d) conflates factual and legal causation; unforeseeable extraordinary events *do* break proximate causation. (e) is too broad; if a victim engaged in a completely unforeseeable, bizarre voluntary act (e.g., mixing the drug with a rare poison on a whim), it could theoretically sever causation.
