**Q2.** Assume Arthur argues that Julian's voluntary choice to snort the drug was an intervening cause that relieves Arthur of causal responsibility for the death. How will a court likely rule?

(a) The ingestion does not sever the causal chain because Julian's voluntary ingestion in response to Arthur's encouragement was a foreseeable dependent intervening cause. <!-- correct -->
(b) The ingestion severs the causal chain because all voluntary human actions are categorically treated as unforeseeable independent intervening causes under the law.
(c) The ingestion does not sever the causal chain because Arthur's act of synthesizing the drug was legally concurrent with Julian's act of ingestion.
(d) The ingestion severs the causal chain because Arthur's conduct was a factual cause but failed to satisfy the requirements for proximate legal causation.
(e) The ingestion does not sever the causal chain because the MPC applies a strict liability standard to all subsequent intervening causes of harm.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. An intervening cause only severs liability if it is independent and unforeseeable. Julian's ingestion was highly foreseeable because Arthur supplied the drug, encouraged him to take it, and watched him do so. This makes it a dependent intervening cause that does not break the causal chain.
(b) is incorrect because voluntary acts can be dependent intervening causes if they are normal, foreseeable responses to the defendant's conduct.
(c) is incorrect because synthesizing the drug occurred weeks prior and was not concurrent with the ingestion.
(d) is incorrect because Arthur's conduct satisfies both factual (but-for) and proximate cause due to foreseeability.
(e) is incorrect because the MPC does not use strict liability for intervening causes; it asks whether the result was "too remote or accidental."
