**Q12.** The state's distribution statute is silent on mens rea. Does the *Morissette* presumption against strict liability apply to require a mens rea element for Vance's charge?

(a) Yes, because the severe maximum penalty of ten years in prison strongly rebuts the inference that the legislature intended to create a strict-liability public welfare regulatory offense. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because the statute explicitly regulates public health and safety, which permanently overrides any judicial presumption requiring proof of a culpable mental state for criminal liability.
(c) Yes, because the Morissette presumption guarantees that every statutory offense must require specific intent to harm, even when dealing with inherently dangerous items like synthetic narcotics.
(d) No, because the statute involves adulterated substances, and historical precedents universally mandate absolute strict liability for all crimes involving the production or sale of degraded precursor chemicals.
(e) Yes, because the statute omits explicit strict-liability phrasing, meaning the court must automatically downgrade the ten-year felony charge to a minor civil infraction carrying no prison time.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because the *Morissette* and *Staples* line of cases establish that severe felony penalties (like 10 years) strongly rebut the presumption that a silent statute is a strict-liability public welfare offense. (b) is wrong because public health framing does not permanently override the mens rea presumption when penalties are high. (c) is wrong because *Morissette* does not guarantee specific intent; it often results in reading in knowledge or recklessness. (d) is wrong because there is no universal strict liability mandate for precursor chemicals that overrides the penalty analysis. (e) is wrong because courts read in a mens rea requirement; they do not unilaterally alter the statutory penalty limits.
