**Q12.** The health and safety code provision prohibiting the "distribution of adulterated synthetic narcotics" is silent on mens rea and carries a maximum penalty of ten years. Under the *Morissette* presumption, how will a court likely interpret the mens rea requirement?

(a) The court will interpret the statute as a strict liability offense because it is a public welfare regulation intended to protect community health from adulterated substances.
(b) The court will presume that a mens rea requirement applies, because a ten-year maximum penalty is too severe for strict liability and intent is inherent in serious offenses. <!-- correct -->
(c) The court will interpret the statute as a strict liability offense because the legislature's silence always establishes an absolute intent to dispense with all mental state requirements.
(d) The court will require the state to prove that Vance had the specific intent to kill the victim, because the statute carries a potential ten-year felony sentence.
(e) The court will strike down the statute as unconstitutionally vague because it fails to explicitly specify whether purpose, knowledge, or recklessness is required for a criminal conviction.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** (b) is correct because under the Morissette presumption, courts presume a mens rea requirement when statutes are silent, and Staples confirms that severe penalties like ten years in prison cut strongly against applying strict liability public welfare exceptions. (a) fails because the ten-year penalty is too severe for the traditional minor-penalty structure of public welfare offenses. (c) fails because the Morissette presumption explicitly rejects the idea that legislative silence implies strict liability. (d) fails because the presumed mens rea would apply to the distribution of the adulterated substance (e.g., knowledge), not a specific intent to kill. (e) fails because courts routinely read in mental states rather than immediately striking statutes down for vagueness based on silence.
