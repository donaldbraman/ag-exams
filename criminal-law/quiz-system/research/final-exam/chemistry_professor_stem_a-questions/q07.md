**Q7.** Assume Silas is charged with homicide for Julian's death based on Arthur's actions of knocking the phone away and locking the door. Silas argues he did not assist in or agree to these specific acts. Under the Pinkerton doctrine, is Silas liable for Arthur's actions?

(a) Yes, because Arthur's actions to prevent rescue were committed in furtherance of the drug distribution conspiracy and were a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the enterprise. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because under the Pinkerton doctrine, any act committed by a co-conspirator automatically imposes strict liability on all other members, regardless of foreseeability.
(c) No, because Silas was merely a logistics handler and Pinkerton liability only applies to the primary actors who physically complete the lethal overt acts.
(d) No, because Arthur's actions constituted an independent, unforeseeable superseding crime that exceeded the original scope of the agreement to sell the chemical substance.
(e) No, because the Pinkerton doctrine requires the prosecution to prove that Silas explicitly agreed to the specific acts of obstruction used to conceal the conspiracy.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because under the Pinkerton doctrine, a conspirator is liable for any crime committed by a co-conspirator that is in furtherance of the conspiracy and is a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the agreement, including predictable efforts to cover up the crime. (b) fails because Pinkerton liability is strictly limited to acts that are both in furtherance of the conspiracy and reasonably foreseeable. (c) fails because Pinkerton liability applies equally to all members of the conspiracy, regardless of their specific role or physical presence during the overt acts. (d) fails because covering up a crime or preventing rescue during a drug transaction is generally considered a foreseeable consequence of a narcotics conspiracy. (e) fails because Pinkerton imputes liability for foreseeable substantive offenses without requiring an explicit agreement to commit those specific secondary acts.
