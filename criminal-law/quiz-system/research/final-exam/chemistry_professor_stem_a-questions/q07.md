**Q7.** Assume the state charges Vance with felony murder predicated on the felony of "unlicensed manufacturing of a controlled substance." How would a jurisdiction applying the abstract "elements-vs-facts approach" determine if this predicate is inherently dangerous?

(a) It would examine only the statutory elements of unlicensed manufacturing to determine if it can be committed safely, entirely ignoring the highly toxic nature of Vance's actual batch. <!-- correct -->
(b) It would evaluate Vance's specific conduct in failing to test the precursor chemicals to determine whether his particular manufacturing process posed a substantial risk to human life.
(c) It would submit the question directly to the jury to assess whether the victim's decision to ingest a triple dose rendered the underlying manufacturing process inherently dangerous.
(d) It would assess whether the unlicensed manufacturing statute carries a maximum penalty exceeding one year, which automatically classifies the offense as inherently dangerous in the abstract.
(e) It would strictly focus on Thorne's involvement in the distribution phase, because the elements test requires analyzing the physical proximity between the manufacturer and the ultimate buyer.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because California's abstract elements test (*Phillips*) examines whether the felony can theoretically be committed safely in the abstract, completely ignoring the defendant's actual dangerous conduct in the specific case. (b) is wrong because evaluating the specific conduct describes the facts-as-committed approach, not the abstract test. (c) is wrong because dangerousness in the abstract is a question of law for the judge, not a factual question for the jury evaluating victim decisions. (d) is wrong because penalty duration does not dictate inherent dangerousness. (e) is wrong because the test focuses solely on the statutory elements, not the physical proximity of the actors.
