**Q13.** Regarding Stem 2: The state charges Thorne with possession of the 500 grams of narcotics found in the lockbox. Thorne argues he did not have exclusive control over the lockbox since Vance held the only key. Can Thorne be found to constructively possess the narcotics?

(a) Yes, because Thorne's immediate physical proximity to the lockbox in the passenger seat creates an irrebuttable presumption of actual possession of the synthetic narcotics inside it.
(b) Yes, because constructive possession can be joint, and Thorne's knowledge of the contents, frequent presence, and access establish his ability and intent to exercise control over it. <!-- correct -->
(c) No, because constructive possession strictly requires the defendant to have exclusive control over the specific premises or container where the illegal contraband is ultimately discovered.
(d) No, because Thorne did not have the physical key to the lockbox, meaning he completely lacked the immediate physical ability to control the narcotics secured inside.
(e) No, because Vance was the legal owner of the RV, which precludes any passenger from exercising constructive possession over items stored inside the vehicle's locked compartments.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** (b) is correct because constructive possession can be joint and does not require exclusive control; Thorne's knowledge of the lockbox, his access to the passenger side, and his role in the inventory establish his ability and intent to control it. (a) fails because proximity alone is insufficient to create an irrebuttable presumption of possession. (c) fails because constructive possession can be exercised jointly by multiple people; exclusive control of the premises is not strictly required. (d) fails because having the key is not strictly required if he had joint access and ability to direct the control of the inventory. (e) fails because vehicle ownership does not legally preclude passengers from exercising joint constructive possession.
