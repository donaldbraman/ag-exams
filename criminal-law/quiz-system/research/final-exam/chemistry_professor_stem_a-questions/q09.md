**Q9.** The state charges Thorne with felony murder as an accomplice. Under the *Tison* standard, can Thorne be subjected to the harshest felony-murder penalties for Gable's death?

(a) No, because Thorne immediately urged Vance to call emergency services, demonstrating that he did not possess the extreme reckless indifference to human life required by the standard. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because Thorne was physically present during the transaction and split the cash, which legally establishes both major participation and an intentional desire to cause the overdose.
(c) No, because Thorne only handled the distribution aspect rather than the manufacturing, meaning he cannot be classified as a major participant in any resulting felony-murder criminal enterprise.
(d) Yes, because the Tison standard applies strict liability to all co-felons present at the scene of a death, regardless of their subjective mental state or attempts to intervene.
(e) No, because Thorne was merely a former student of Vance, and the major participant doctrine strictly requires a formally documented partnership or business agreement between the co-felons.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because *Tison* requires both major participation AND reckless indifference to human life; Thorne's desperate plea to call 911 directly negates the reckless indifference element. (b) is wrong because presence and splitting cash establish participation, but not the required mens rea of reckless indifference or intent to kill. (c) is wrong because distributors can easily be major participants in the overall felony. (d) is wrong because *Tison* specifically rejects strict liability for accomplices in capital contexts, requiring individualized culpability. (e) is wrong because major participation looks at the factual role in the crime, not formal documentation of a partnership.
