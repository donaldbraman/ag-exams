**Q9.** Assume the jurisdiction applies the *Tison* standard for accomplice liability in felony murder cases. The prosecution charges Silas with felony murder for Julian's death. Does Silas's conduct meet the constitutional threshold required by *Tison* for the death penalty or severe felony murder liability?

(a) Yes, because providing the specialized glassware and being physically present when the victim died automatically establishes major participation and intent to kill.
(b) Yes, because any individual who participates in an inherently dangerous felony is categorically deemed to have acted with reckless indifference to human life.
(c) No, because Silas's affirmative attempt to call 911 and his verbal protest against abandoning the victim negate the "reckless indifference to human life" requirement. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because Silas was merely the logistics handler for the operation and was not the actual principal who supplied the fatal dose directly to the victim.
(e) No, because Silas's low-level drug record and subordinate role prove he lacked the criminal sophistication necessary to be considered a major participant.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** Under *Tison v. Arizona*, a non-killer accomplice can only be held liable for severe felony murder penalties if they were a "major participant" in the felony AND acted with "reckless indifference to human life." Silas actively attempted to call 911 to save Julian and protested Arthur's actions. This behavior demonstrates regard for human life, directly negating the "reckless indifference" requirement. (a) is incorrect because physical presence does not automatically establish intent to kill or reckless indifference. (b) is incorrect because *Tison* requires individualized culpability findings, rejecting automatic categorical assumptions. (d) is incorrect because a non-killer accomplice *can* be liable under *Tison* if both prongs are met; being the logistics handler doesn't automatically shield him if he had been recklessly indifferent. (e) is irrelevant; prior record doesn't govern major participation in the current offense.
