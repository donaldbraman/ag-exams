**Q9.** Assume the jurisdiction recognizes the Pinkerton doctrine. Can Silas be held vicariously liable for Arthur's substantive homicide offenses?

(a) Silas may be liable if Arthur's acts of blocking the 911 call and locking the door were in furtherance of the conspiracy and reasonably foreseeable. <!-- correct -->
(b) Silas cannot be liable because the doctrine requires the prosecution to prove that Silas shared Arthur's specific intent to cause Julian's death.
(c) Silas is strictly liable for the murder because the doctrine holds conspirators responsible for all acts of co-conspirators regardless of any foreseeability.
(d) Silas cannot be liable because his verbal protest constituted a legally sufficient withdrawal that shielded him from any subsequent substantive offenses.
(e) Silas may be liable because his act of driving the getaway car automatically establishes his vicarious liability for all of Arthur's preceding crimes.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Under the *Pinkerton* doctrine, a conspirator is vicariously liable for the substantive offenses committed by co-conspirators if those offenses are committed in furtherance of the conspiracy and are a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the unlawful agreement. A jury would have to determine if letting an overdosing buyer die to protect the enterprise was reasonably foreseeable.
(b) is incorrect because *Pinkerton* liability is vicarious; the prosecution does not need to prove Silas personally shared the mens rea for Arthur's substantive offense.
(c) is incorrect because *Pinkerton* is not boundless strict liability; the acts must be reasonably foreseeable and in furtherance of the conspiracy.
(d) is incorrect because verbal protest alone does not constitute legally sufficient withdrawal; withdrawal requires an affirmative act communicating abandonment to co-conspirators or police.
(e) is incorrect because driving the getaway car relates to accomplice liability, whereas *Pinkerton* liability stems solely from participation in the conspiracy itself.
