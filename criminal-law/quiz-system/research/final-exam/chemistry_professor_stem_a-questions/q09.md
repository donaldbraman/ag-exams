**Q9.** Assume Silas is convicted of felony murder for Julian's death. The state seeks the death penalty against Silas. Under the constitutional limits established in Tison v. Arizona, may Silas be sentenced to death?

(a) Yes, because any participant in an inherently dangerous felony that results in death is eligible for capital punishment under the strict liability felony murder framework.
(b) Yes, because Silas provided the specialized glassware and was present at the scene, which automatically demonstrates the required specific intent to kill the victim.
(c) No, because the death penalty cannot constitutionally be applied to an accomplice who did not actually pull the trigger or physically inflict the fatal injury.
(d) No, unless the prosecution proves that Silas was a major participant in the underlying felony and acted with reckless indifference to human life. <!-- correct -->
(e) No, because Silas's attempt to call 911 proves he acted under duress, which serves as an absolute constitutional bar to capital punishment in homicide cases.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** (d) is correct because *Tison v. Arizona* established the constitutional floor for the death penalty in felony murder cases involving accomplices who do not kill: the defendant must be a major participant in the felony and act with reckless indifference to human life. (a) fails because the Eighth Amendment prohibits applying the death penalty based on strict liability felony murder for an accomplice who did not kill. (b) fails because providing supplies and being present does not automatically establish intent to kill or reckless indifference. (c) fails because *Tison* explicitly allows the death penalty for non-killers if the major participant and reckless indifference requirements are met. (e) fails because attempting to call 911 may serve as mitigating evidence, but it does not constitute the affirmative defense of duress or an absolute constitutional bar.
