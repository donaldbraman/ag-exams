## Issue 1 — audit

**Q7.** Assume Silas is charged with homicide for Julian's death. The prosecution argues Silas is liable for Arthur's actions (stepping on the phone, locking the door) under the *Pinkerton* doctrine. Will Silas be held liable for Arthur's acts under this theory?

(a) Yes, because Silas was physically present in the room when Arthur committed the acts, making him an accomplice to the murder.
(b) Yes, because Arthur's actions to silence the victim and flee were reasonably foreseeable acts committed in furtherance of the distribution conspiracy. <!-- correct -->
(c) No, because the Pinkerton doctrine only holds co-conspirators liable for the specific target offense of the conspiracy, not collateral crimes.
(d) No, because Silas was merely a subordinate in the conspiracy and did not personally authorize or assist Arthur's actions.
(e) No, because Arthur's actions occurred after the two grams of drugs had already been fully distributed to the victim.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** (b) is correct. Under the *Pinkerton* doctrine, a conspirator is liable for any reasonably foreseeable substantive offenses committed by a co-conspirator in furtherance of the conspiracy. Arthur's acts to prevent rescue and avoid police detection were committed to protect the drug distribution conspiracy and were reasonably foreseeable. (a) relies on accomplice liability, not the requested *Pinkerton* (conspiracy) theory. (c) fails because *Pinkerton* explicitly extends liability to collateral crimes committed in furtherance of the conspiracy. (d) fails because *Pinkerton* applies to all conspirators regardless of rank or specific authorization. (e) fails because the conspiracy to distribute encompasses covering up the crime and escaping the scene.
