**Q4.** Assume it is established that Arthur breached a legal duty to act by failing to summon help. Arthur argues he is not the cause of Julian's death because the drug ingestion was the actual cause. Is Arthur's omission a but-for cause of Julian's death?

(a) Yes, because the medical examiner established that Julian would likely have survived if paramedics had been called, meaning the delay was necessary to the death. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because Arthur's act of locking the door demonstrated a malicious intent, which relaxes the strict necessity requirement for factual causation in homicide cases.
(c) No, because the acute cardiac arrhythmia was the sole medical mechanism of death, meaning Arthur's omission was merely a concurrent sufficient cause, not a but-for cause.
(d) No, because under the common law, a failure to act can be a proximate cause of death but can never be the factual but-for cause of injury.
(e) No, because Julian's ingestion of the drug had already set a lethal sequence in motion before Arthur's omission occurred, permanently precluding but-for causation.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because factual (but-for) causation for an omission asks whether the harm would have occurred if the defendant had performed the required act. Since the ME established a 95% survival chance if paramedics were called, the omission was a necessary but-for cause of death. (b) fails because malicious intent does not alter the empirical but-for test for factual causation. (c) fails because concurrent sufficient causation only applies when two independent forces are each fully sufficient to cause the harm simultaneously, which is not the case here. (d) fails because an omission can serve as the factual but-for cause of physical injury if the performance of the duty would have prevented the harm. (e) fails because a pre-existing lethal condition does not preclude but-for causation if the defendant's omission deprives the victim of a high probability of survival.
