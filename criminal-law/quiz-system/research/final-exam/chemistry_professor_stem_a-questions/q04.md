**Q4.** Assume it is established that Arthur owed a duty to rescue Julian. Arthur argues that he cannot be held liable for Julian's death because the drug itself, not Arthur's omission, killed Julian. Is Arthur's failure to summon help a factual (but-for) cause of Julian's death?

(a) Yes, because the medical examiner established that Julian had a 95% chance of survival if paramedics had been called, meaning the delay was a necessary condition for death. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because the doctrine of concurrent sufficient causes applies, making both the ingestion of the drug and the delay in treatment independent factual causes.
(c) No, because the acute cardiac arrhythmia was an internal medical condition that operated completely independently of Arthur's failure to summon medical assistance.
(d) No, because factual causation strictly requires the defendant's affirmative act or omission to be the sole and exclusive physical cause of the fatal result.
(e) No, because omissions and failures to act are legally incapable of serving as the factual but-for cause of a tangible physical result like a cardiac arrhythmia.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Factual (but-for) causation for an omission asks whether the victim would have died when they did "but for" the defendant's failure to act. Because the ME established a 95% chance of survival if 911 had been called, Julian would not have died but for Arthur's delay and prevention of rescue. (b) is incorrect because these are not concurrent sufficient causes; the drug alone would not have been fatal if treatment had been summoned, and the delay alone wouldn't have killed without the drug. (c) is incorrect because the medical condition would have been survivable without the delay. (d) is incorrect because criminal law does not require the defendant's conduct to be the *sole* cause, only a necessary one. (e) is incorrect because omissions routinely satisfy but-for causation when a duty exists.
