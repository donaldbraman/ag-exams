**Q4.** Assume Vance's omission is a factual cause of Gable's death. Does Vance's failure to call 911 satisfy the actus reus requirement for homicide via omission?

(a) Yes, because Vance's role in distributing the highly toxic synthetic narcotics created the victim's peril, which imposed a recognized affirmative legal duty to summon medical assistance. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because the criminal law uniformly rejects homicide liability based solely on a failure to act, requiring an affirmative physical action that directly terminates the victim's life.
(c) Yes, because Vance's status as a university professor imposes a universal mandatory reporting duty toward all individuals injured in his immediate presence, regardless of how it occurred.
(d) No, because Vance and Gable did not have a recognized special relationship, such as parent-child or spouses, which is the only valid basis for omission liability.
(e) Yes, because Vance partially dialed 9-1 before stopping, and any attempt to rescue immediately creates a binding contractual obligation to successfully complete the emergency medical intervention.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because a person who creates the victim's peril (here, by distributing highly toxic adulterated drugs) assumes an affirmative legal duty to rescue under the traditional *Jones* categories. (b) is wrong because omissions can perfectly support homicide liability when a legal duty to act exists. (c) is wrong because there is no universal duty for professors to report injuries absent a specific statutory or relational trigger. (d) is wrong because special relationships are only one of several duty categories, alongside the creation-of-peril category. (e) is wrong because partially dialing does not create a formal contract, even if it relates to voluntary assumption of care.
