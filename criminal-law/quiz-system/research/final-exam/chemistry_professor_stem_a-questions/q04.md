**Q4.** Assume that Vance's failure to call 911 was a but-for cause of Gable's death. Can this omission satisfy the actus reus element for a criminal homicide charge against Vance?

(a) Yes, because Vance's distribution of the toxic drug created the peril, imposing an affirmative legal duty on him to rescue the victim from the resulting danger. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because any person who witnesses another experiencing a medical emergency is subject to a universal legal duty to intervene under the good samaritan baseline.
(c) No, because Vance did not have a special status relationship with Gable, which is the only recognized basis for an affirmative duty to act under criminal law.
(d) No, because Vance's decision to stop dialing the phone was a continuation of his failure to act rather than a voluntary physical movement required for a duty.
(e) No, because criminal liability for omissions only applies to contractual obligations and statutory duties, neither of which are present in this specific parking lot scenario.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because Vance's act of distributing the highly toxic drug created the peril that Gable faced, which imposes an affirmative legal duty on Vance to rescue or seek aid under the creation of peril category of omissions. (b) fails because there is no universal duty to rescue under the general criminal law baseline; bystanders generally have no duty. (c) fails because creation of peril is a distinct, recognized exception that creates a duty independent of special status relationships like parent-child. (d) fails because stopping the call and fleeing are affirmative acts and omissions that breach his duty to rescue. (e) fails because the creation of peril category is a well-established common law basis for an affirmative duty alongside contracts and statutes.
