**Q14.** Assume the court rules that the distribution of adulterated narcotics statute requires the state to prove Vance knew the batch was adulterated. Vance asserts a mistake of fact defense, arguing he honestly believed the batch was safe and standard-grade. Is his mistake of fact defense likely to succeed?

(a) Yes, because his honest belief that the drug was not adulterated directly negates the required mental state of knowledge regarding that specific material element of the offense. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because a mistake of fact completely excuses all criminal liability as long as the defendant's underlying conduct in manufacturing the drug was not morally wrong in itself.
(c) No, because his failure to conduct a final purity test was negligent, and a mistake of fact must be perfectly reasonable to negate a strict knowledge requirement.
(d) No, because the one-for-all presumption dictates that if he knew he was distributing a synthetic narcotic, he is strictly liable for any resulting attendant circumstances or byproducts.
(e) No, because his mistake was ultimately a mistake of law regarding the chemical safety standards, and ignorance of the law provides no excuse under established criminal doctrine.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because if the statute requires knowledge that the drug was adulterated, Vance's honest belief that it was safe and unadulterated negates that specific mental state, providing a valid mistake of fact defense. (b) fails because a mistake of fact only excuses liability if it negates the required mens rea, not broadly based on moral wrongness alone. (c) fails because if the standard is knowledge, an honest but unreasonable (negligent) mistake still negates knowledge. (d) fails because the "one for all" presumption does not strictly impose liability if the defendant genuinely lacked knowledge of the specific attendant circumstance. (e) fails because his mistake was about the factual purity of the batch, not the legal definition of the statute.
