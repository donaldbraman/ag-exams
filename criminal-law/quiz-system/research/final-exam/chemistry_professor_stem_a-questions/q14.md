**Q14.** Assume the court applies the *Morissette* presumption and reads a "knowledge" requirement into the distribution statute. Can Vance successfully raise a mistake of fact defense based on his honest belief that the batch was safe?

(a) Yes, because Vance's genuine belief that the batch was standard-grade and safe directly negates the required knowledge element regarding the adulterated nature of the distributed synthetic narcotics. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because a mistake of fact defense is only available for specific intent crimes, and the distribution of narcotics is universally classified as a strict liability general intent offense.
(c) Yes, because any subjective misunderstanding regarding the chemical composition of a controlled substance automatically provides a complete affirmative defense against all federal and state drug manufacturing charges.
(d) No, because Vance willfully omitted the final purity test, and a defendant's negligence in failing to verify the facts strictly precludes them from raising any valid mistake defense.
(e) Yes, because Vance was suffering from terminal pancreatic cancer, which legally reduces the requisite mens rea standard from knowledge to mere negligence due to diminished mental capacity.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because under mistake of fact doctrine, an honest mistake that negates the required mens rea (here, knowledge of the adulteration) is a valid defense. (b) is wrong because mistake of fact can defend against general intent/knowledge crimes if it directly negates the required element. (c) is wrong because subjective misunderstandings are not automatic defenses to all drug charges; they must negate a specific required element. (d) is wrong because if the statute requires "knowledge," mere negligence in failing to test does not substitute for actual knowledge, making the mistake defense valid. (e) is wrong because physical illness does not legally alter statutory mens rea requirements.
