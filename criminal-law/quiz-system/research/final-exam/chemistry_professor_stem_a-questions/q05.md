**Q5.** Assume Vance had a legal duty to act. Does his decision to pocket the phone and order Thorne to drive away satisfy the mens rea for depraved-heart murder?

(a) Yes, because knowingly leaving a severely convulsing victim to die without calling for medical aid to protect his pension manifests extreme indifference to the value of human life. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because depraved-heart murder strictly requires proof that the defendant subjectively intended to cause the victim's death, and Vance only intended to protect his financial assets.
(c) Yes, because any failure to render aid to an injured buyer automatically elevates a standard drug distribution charge to depraved-heart murder under the Model Penal Code.
(d) No, because fleeing a crime scene is considered a universally predictable human reaction, meaning it cannot legally constitute the extreme recklessness required for a murder conviction.
(e) Yes, because the victim's underlying decision to ingest a triple dose legally transfers all conscious disregard and extreme recklessness onto the victim rather than the fleeing defendant.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because under both the MPC and common law, abandoning a dying victim in a peril you created, solely to protect financial assets, easily demonstrates a conscious and extreme indifference to human life. (b) is wrong because depraved-heart murder requires extreme recklessness, not specific intent to kill. (c) is wrong because there is no automatic elevation; it always requires a factual finding of extreme indifference by the jury based on the specific circumstances. (d) is wrong because fleeing a victim whose peril you created can absolutely constitute extreme recklessness. (e) is wrong because the victim's own reckless choices do not transfer or negate the defendant's independent culpable mens rea regarding his omission.
