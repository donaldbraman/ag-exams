**Q5.** The prosecution charges Arthur with depraved heart murder (second-degree) for abandoning Julian. Arthur argues he lacked the required mental state because he genuinely believed Julian was "just having a bad reaction" and would recover. Under the common law objective/constructive recklessness standard (*Welansky*), is Arthur's genuine belief a valid defense?

(a) Yes, because depraved heart murder requires that the defendant subjectively and consciously desire that the victim suffer severe bodily harm or fatal injuries.
(b) Yes, because Arthur's subjective and genuine belief that Julian would fully recover negates the conscious disregard of risk required for criminal recklessness.
(c) No, because Arthur's failure to recognize the extreme risk to Julian's life was caused by his own panic, which is legally equivalent to voluntary intoxication.
(d) No, because under the objective standard, a defendant is treated as constructively reckless if an ordinary reasonable person would have perceived the grave danger. <!-- correct -->
(e) No, because Arthur's intentional and affirmative act of shattering the cell phone automatically satisfies the specific intent requirement for first-degree murder.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** Under the *Welansky* constructive recklessness standard, a defendant who lacks actual subjective awareness of a grave risk can still be held to a "wanton or reckless" standard if an ordinary reasonable person in their position would have perceived the danger. Arthur's genuine but objectively unreasonable belief that Julian would "sleep it off" does not shield him. (a) incorrectly describes depraved heart murder, which requires extreme indifference, not specific intent to harm. (b) incorrectly applies a purely subjective MPC-style recklessness standard, ignoring the prompt's instruction to apply *Welansky*. (c) is doctrinally baseless; panic is not legally equivalent to voluntary intoxication. (e) is incorrect because shattering a phone does not prove specific intent to kill.
