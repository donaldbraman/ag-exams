**Q5.** Assume Arthur is the legal cause of Julian's death. Prosecutors charge Arthur with depraved heart murder based on his abandonment of the convulsing victim. Arthur argues he genuinely believed Julian would "sleep it off" and therefore did not consciously perceive a risk of death. Will Arthur's defense succeed under the common law?

(a) Yes, because unintentional homicide grading strictly requires subjective awareness of the risk, meaning Arthur's genuine belief limits his liability to merely negligent homicide.
(b) Yes, because depraved heart murder requires a showing that the defendant desired the victim's death, which is negated by Arthur's belief that Julian would recover.
(c) No, because under constructive recklessness, Arthur's awareness of facts that would cause a reasonable person to perceive grave danger is legally equivalent to actual awareness. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because the malum in se nature of drug distribution automatically elevates any resulting death to murder, regardless of the defendant's perception of the risk.
(e) No, because a breach of an affirmative duty to act completely eliminates the prosecution's burden to prove any culpable mental state regarding the resulting death.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** (c) is correct because under the doctrine of constructive recklessness (*Welansky*), a defendant who is subjectively unaware of a risk can still be held liable if they knew facts that would cause a reasonable person to perceive a grave danger. (a) fails because the common law permits constructive (objective) recklessness, meaning subjective awareness is not strictly required. (b) fails because depraved heart murder requires extreme indifference to human life, not a specific desire to cause death. (d) fails because the malum in se nature relates to misdemeanor manslaughter, whereas murder requires an assessment of the totality of the circumstances to establish extreme indifference. (e) fails because a breach of an affirmative duty satisfies the actus reus, but the prosecution must still prove the requisite mental state for the charged grade of homicide.
