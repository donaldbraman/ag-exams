# chemistry_professor_stem_a Scenario

## Fact Pattern
1. Dr. Arthur Pendelton, a former chemistry professor facing terminal cancer and financial ruin, agreed to synthesize a controlled substance to generate cash for his family.
2. Arthur partnered with his former student, Chloe Vance. They agreed to split the profits. Chloe acquired a decommissioned mobile veterinary clinic (a large van) to serve as a mobile laboratory, kept the only set of keys to the van, and arranged all buyer meetings.
3. Arthur manufactured the drugs and kept the bulk supply in a locked safe inside the van. Arthur was the only person who knew the combination to the safe.
4. The jurisdiction's Controlled Substances Act makes it a felony to manufacture or distribute Compound Y. The statute is silent regarding a mens rea requirement, but it carries a maximum penalty of 15 years in prison.
5. Arthur believed he had successfully synthesized a legal, unregulated analogue of Compound Y because he misread a complex chemical diagram. Chemical testing later confirmed the substance he synthesized was identically Compound Y.
6. On October 12, Chloe brought their first buyer, Marcus Thorne, to the van. While Chloe stood watch by the door, Arthur personally handed Marcus a small packet of the drug in exchange for $500.
7. Marcus immediately ingested the drug in front of them. Within two minutes, Marcus collapsed on the floor of the van, seizing and foaming at the mouth.
8. Arthur recognized that Marcus was suffering a fatal overdose and pulled out his cell phone to dial 911.
9. Chloe grabbed Arthur's arm and said, 'No! If the cops come, we go to prison. Grab his legs.' Together, they dragged the unconscious Marcus out of the van, left him concealed in dense brush, and drove off in the van.
10. Marcus died in the brush. The medical examiner concluded that Compound Y was the biological cause of death, and established that if 911 had been called when Marcus initially collapsed, emergency responders would have arrived with Naloxone in time to save his life.

## Characters
- **Dr. Arthur Pendelton** (Primary Defendant): mistake of fact and omission liability
- **Chloe Vance** (Co-Conspirator): constructive possession and felony murder
- **Marcus Thorne** (Victim): causation

**Q1.** If Arthur is charged with manufacturing and distributing Compound Y, how will the court likely interpret the statute's silence regarding mens rea?

(a) Read in a mens rea requirement because the severe 15-year penalty creates a presumption against strict liability. <!-- correct -->
(b) Treat it as a strict liability public welfare offense because dealing in drugs involves inherently dangerous items.
(c) Read in a mens rea requirement because all criminal statutes silent on mens rea require purpose.
(d) Treat it as strict liability because the statute does not explicitly contain the word "knowingly" or "purposely".
(e) Treat it as strict liability because Arthur possessed the capacity to prevent the harm to the public.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Under *Staples*, when a regulatory or criminal statute carries a severe penalty (like 15 years in prison), courts presume Congress intended a mens rea requirement even if the statute is silent. (b) fails because the dangerous items heuristic is generally limited by the penalty ceiling; major felonies rarely permit strict liability without explicit legislative intent. (c) fails because the default mens rea under the MPC is recklessness, not purpose. (d) fails because the *Morissette* presumption reads in mens rea despite statutory silence. (e) fails because the capacity to prevent harm justifies strict liability only for minor regulatory infractions, not serious felonies carrying decade-plus sentences.

**Q2.** Assume the court requires proof that Arthur "knowingly" manufactured and distributed Compound Y. Does Arthur's mistaken belief regarding the chemical composition provide a valid defense?

(a) Yes, because his factual error negates the required knowledge that he was manufacturing the prohibited substance. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because a mistake of law provides a complete defense to complex regulatory distribution charges.
(c) No, because his mistake was unreasonable given his extensive expertise as a former chemistry professor.
(d) No, because the Prince doctrine imposes strict liability for morally suspect conduct like synthesizing drugs.
(e) No, because under the dangerous items heuristic, dealing in chemicals puts him on notice at his peril.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. A mistake of fact is a valid defense if it negates the mental state required for a material element of the offense. If the statute requires knowledge that the substance is Compound Y, Arthur's genuine belief that he synthesized a legal analogue negates that specific subjective knowledge. (b) fails because this is a mistake of fact regarding the chemical identity of the substance, not a mistake of law. (c) fails because for crimes requiring knowledge, even an unreasonable but honest mistake negates the required subjective awareness. (d) fails because the *Prince* doctrine applies to strict liability elements (like a victim's age), not elements explicitly requiring knowledge. (e) fails because the dangerous items heuristic establishes notice of regulation for strict liability, but does not defeat a mistake negating an explicitly required mens rea element.

**Q3.** Assume Arthur is charged with homicide for his omission. The prosecution argues he had a legal duty to call 911 when Marcus collapsed. Does Arthur have a recognized legal duty to act?

(a) Yes, because by personally handing Marcus the drugs that caused the fatal overdose, Arthur created the peril. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because Arthur voluntarily assumed a legal duty to act by pulling out his phone to dial 911.
(c) No, because Marcus voluntarily ingested the drugs, severing Arthur's legal connection to the resulting medical peril.
(d) No, because Arthur and Marcus were engaged in an illegal transaction, which voids any protective status relationship.
(e) No, because calling 911 would have required Arthur to unconstitutionally incriminate himself in the drug distribution.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. A legal duty to act arises when a person's conduct creates the peril that the victim faces. By handing Marcus the lethal dose of Compound Y, Arthur actively created the peril, triggering a recognized legal duty to summon aid when Marcus collapsed. (b) fails because merely pulling out a phone does not constitute voluntarily assuming care and secluding the victim. (c) fails because the victim's voluntary ingestion does not negate the peril created by the defendant who supplied the lethal hazard. (d) fails because the creation of peril duty relies entirely on the act of endangerment, not on a lawful or contractual relationship between the parties. (e) fails because the Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination does not excuse a legal duty to summon life-saving medical aid for a peril the defendant created.

**Q4.** Assume Arthur had a duty to act. He argues that Marcus's voluntary decision to ingest the drug constitutes an independent intervening cause, severing Arthur's liability for the death. Will this argument succeed?

(a) No, because Marcus's immediate ingestion of the drug was a foreseeable, dependent consequence of Arthur handing it to him. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because the but-for causation test ignores all intervening acts regardless of their objective or subjective foreseeability.
(c) Yes, because a victim's voluntary act is categorically an independent intervening cause that breaks the causal chain.
(d) Yes, because Marcus's rapid overdose was highly extraordinary and not the direct intended result of the drug sale.
(e) Yes, because the MPC proximate cause standard precludes criminal liability when the victim brings about their own harm.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. A dependent intervening cause is one set in motion by the defendant's initial act. In a drug sale, the buyer's immediate and voluntary ingestion of the substance is a highly foreseeable response to the distribution, making it a dependent intervening cause that does not sever proximate causation. (b) fails because proximate cause (legal cause) explicitly filters liability based on foreseeability; it does not ignore intervening acts. (c) fails because a victim's voluntary act only severs the causal chain if it is highly extraordinary or truly independent of the defendant's conduct. (d) fails because an overdose is a perfectly foreseeable risk of illicit drug consumption, not a highly extraordinary event. (e) fails because the MPC standard ("too remote or accidental") does not categorically exclude harms predictably resulting from a victim's use of a provided hazard.

**Q5.** Assume Arthur is prosecuted for depraved heart murder. Which of the following provides the strongest doctrinal basis for sustaining this specific charge?

(a) Arthur's active participation in dragging the unconscious Marcus into dense brush and abandoning him manifested an extreme indifference to human life. <!-- correct -->
(b) Arthur's status as a former chemistry professor meant his failure to perform a professional rescue constituted an intent to cause death.
(c) The sale of any illicit controlled substance constitutes an inherently dangerous felony sufficient to establish implied malice under the traditional rule.
(d) Arthur's initial attempt to dial 911 before being stopped by Chloe proves he had a conscious object to cause Marcus's demise.
(e) Marcus's death was a highly extraordinary result that satisfies the objective foreseeability standard required for second-degree depraved heart murder convictions.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Depraved heart murder requires recklessness manifesting extreme indifference to the value of human life. Dragging an unconscious, overdosing person into dense brush and abandoning them to die without medical intervention easily meets this elevated standard of gross recklessness. (b) fails because professional status does not automatically convert an omission into an intentional murder. (c) fails because this describes the structure of felony murder (implied malice via a predicate felony), not the gross recklessness required for a depraved heart murder conviction. (d) fails because attempting to dial 911 demonstrates a desire to save the victim, which directly contradicts having a conscious object (intent) to cause death. (e) fails because depraved heart murder requires subjectively reckless conduct highly likely to cause death, not merely an extraordinary result.

**Q6.** Assume the state charges Arthur with felony murder, using drug distribution as the predicate felony. In a jurisdiction applying the "abstract elements" approach, will this charge qualify as a valid predicate?

(a) Yes, if the statutory definition of distributing Compound Y cannot be violated without creating a substantial risk to human life. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because Arthur's actual conduct in selling a lethal dose to Marcus proved highly dangerous in this specific instance.
(c) Yes, because all drug distribution offenses are categorically enumerated as first-degree felony murder predicates under the modern common law.
(d) No, because the abstract elements approach only permits directly assaultive felonies to serve as predicates for any felony murder charge.
(e) No, because Arthur did not subjectively intend for the distribution of the drugs to proximately cause Marcus's fatal medical overdose.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. The "abstract elements" approach (used in California) examines the statutory definition of the felony, ignoring the specific facts of the case. A felony qualifies as inherently dangerous only if the crime, by its very definition, cannot possibly be committed without creating a substantial risk to human life. (b) fails because looking at Arthur's actual fatal conduct is applying the competing "facts-as-committed" test, not the abstract elements test. (c) fails because drug distribution is typically a non-enumerated felony (enumerated ones are usually the BARRK felonies). (d) fails because the abstract elements approach tests for inherent danger, while the assaultive nature of a felony is analyzed under the separate merger doctrine. (e) fails because felony murder imposes strict liability for the death; subjective intent to cause death is never required.

**Q7.** Assume the court rules that the distribution of Compound Y is an inherently dangerous felony. Arthur argues the felony murder charge must be dismissed under the merger doctrine. Will this argument succeed?

(a) No, because the drug distribution had an independent felonious purpose—generating cash—beyond causing physical harm or death. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because the merger doctrine only applies to strict liability public welfare offenses, not inherently dangerous predicate felonies.
(c) Yes, because the distribution of the lethal drug was the very act that caused Marcus's death, making it inherently assaultive.
(d) Yes, because Arthur's act of distribution merged with Chloe's subsequent act of concealing the body, eliminating independent criminal liability.
(e) Yes, because under the majority common law approach, all non-enumerated felonies automatically merge with the resulting homicide offense.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. The merger doctrine prohibits using an assaultive felony (one whose sole purpose is violence or injury) as a predicate for felony murder. Drug distribution has an independent felonious purpose—financial gain—so it does not merge with the homicide and can validly serve as a predicate. (b) fails because the merger doctrine acts specifically as a limitation on inherently dangerous felonies to prevent felony murder from swallowing all homicides. (c) fails because causing death does not retroactively make the distribution's purpose assaultive; the primary purpose remained financial. (d) fails because the merger doctrine applies to the relationship between the predicate felony and the homicide itself, not the merging of different defendants' actions. (e) fails because the MPC rejects the traditional felony murder rule entirely; it does not automatically merge all non-enumerated felonies.

**Q8.** Assume the DA charges Chloe with possession of the bulk supply of Compound Y found in the van's safe. Under the doctrine of constructive possession, how will the court likely rule?

(a) Not guilty, because her lack of access to the locked safe defeats the requirement that she had the ability to control the drugs. <!-- correct -->
(b) Not guilty, because Arthur had actual physical possession of the drugs, which precludes a finding of constructive possession by anyone else.
(c) Guilty, because her ownership of the only set of keys to the van establishes exclusive control over all contents within it.
(d) Guilty, because her underlying agreement to split the profits from the drug sales automatically imputes physical possession of the chemical inventory.
(e) Guilty, because the presence of illicit drugs in a vehicle creates an irrebuttable presumption of possession by the registered vehicle owner.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Constructive possession requires both the intent and the physical power to exercise dominion and control over an item. Because Chloe did not know the combination to the locked safe, she lacked the physical ability to access and control the bulk drugs inside it. (b) fails because joint constructive possession is legally possible; actual possession by one person does not strictly preclude constructive possession by another. (c) fails because ownership of the keys to the outer vehicle does not establish control over a locked inner container that requires a separate, unknown combination. (d) fails because an agreement to share profits establishes conspiracy, not the physical power to control required for possessory offenses. (e) fails because no such irrebuttable presumption exists in criminal law; the state must prove control beyond a reasonable doubt.

(a) Chloe and Arthur's agreement to split the profits, coupled with Chloe's acquisition of the van as an overt act in furtherance. <!-- correct -->
(b) The bilateral agreement between Chloe and Arthur, because conspiracy requires no further acts once a criminal agreement is successfully formed.

(d) Arthur's act of handing the drugs to Marcus, because the overt act must involve the actual commission of the target offense.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Conspiracy requires an agreement to commit a crime and, in most jurisdictions, an overt act in furtherance of the agreement. The agreement to split profits and the acquisition of the van satisfy these elements perfectly. (b) fails because modern conspiracy statutes typically require an overt act in addition to the bilateral agreement. (c) fails because mere presence is insufficient to prove the existence of an agreement without further evidence of a meeting of the minds. (d) fails because an overt act need only be a step toward the crime (like buying the van); it does not need to be the completed target offense itself. (e) fails because providing legal goods only establishes conspiracy if accompanied by the explicit intent to promote the criminal enterprise.

**Q11.** Assume Chloe is convicted of conspiracy to distribute Compound Y. The DA seeks to hold her liable for Marcus's death under the *Pinkerton* doctrine. What is required to sustain this charge?

(a) The death must have been a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the drug distribution conspiracy and committed in furtherance of it. <!-- correct -->
(b) Chloe must have personally performed an overt act that was the direct but-for cause of Marcus's subsequent fatal overdose and death.
(c) The prosecution must prove that Chloe specifically intended for Marcus to die as a direct result of the conspiratorial agreement.
(d) Chloe must have possessed a depraved heart and extreme indifference to human life at the time the initial conspiracy was formed.
(e) The death must have occurred before Chloe committed any overt acts, ensuring it fell entirely within the scope of the agreement.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Under the Pinkerton doctrine, a co-conspirator is liable for substantive offenses committed by other members if those offenses are reasonably foreseeable and committed in furtherance of the conspiracy. (b) fails because Pinkerton liability is vicarious; the defendant does not need to personally commit any act causing the target harm. (c) fails because Pinkerton extends liability for unintended but foreseeable consequences; specific intent for the homicide is not required. (d) fails because Pinkerton uses objective foreseeability, substituting the conspiracy agreement for the mental state of the substantive offense (like depraved heart). (e) fails because Pinkerton liability applies to acts committed during the ongoing conspiracy, regardless of when the defendant's specific overt acts occurred.

**Q12.** Assume the prosecution charges Chloe with capital felony murder. Chloe argues she cannot receive the death penalty because she did not personally hand the drugs to Marcus. Under *Tison v. Arizona*, how will a court rule?

(a) She is eligible for the death penalty because providing the van and directing the concealment demonstrates major participation and reckless indifference. <!-- correct -->
(b) She is eligible for the death penalty because any participant in an inherently dangerous felony is strictly liable for resulting deaths.
(c) She is ineligible for the death penalty because she did not possess the specific intent to kill Marcus during the sale.
(d) She is ineligible for the death penalty because the actual killer, Arthur, must be the only person subject to capital punishment.
(e) She is ineligible for the death penalty because drug distribution is not an enumerated felony in the traditional felony murder framework.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. *Tison v. Arizona* holds that an accomplice who does not kill may be sentenced to death if they were a "major participant" in the felony and acted with "reckless indifference to human life." Providing the van and actively directing the concealment of the dying victim meets this standard. (b) fails because *Tison* explicitly rejected strict liability for capital punishment, requiring individualized culpability. (c) fails because *Tison* established that reckless indifference is a sufficient mental state for capital eligibility; specific intent to kill is not required. (d) fails because *Tison* allows non-killers to be executed if the major participant/reckless indifference test is met. (e) fails because while drug distribution may not be a traditional enumerated BARRK felony, the *Tison* standard applies to any valid felony murder predicate carrying capital exposure.

**Q13.** Assume the prosecution charges Chloe with criminal homicide for failing to summon medical aid for Marcus, independent of the drug distribution. Does Chloe have a legal duty to act supporting omission liability?

(a) Yes, because by dragging Marcus into the brush and driving away, she secluded a helpless person, preventing others from rendering aid. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because her status as Arthur's former student created a special relationship requiring her to intervene when his product caused harm.
(c) Yes, because all citizens have a generalized legal duty to report medical emergencies to law enforcement under traditional Good Samaritan laws.
(d) No, because Marcus's voluntary intoxication severed any duty of care Chloe might have owed him as a business invitee to the van.
(e) No, because Chloe's actions constituted an affirmative act of concealment, meaning she cannot legally be prosecuted under a criminal omission theory.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. A legal duty to act arises when a person voluntarily assumes care of another and secludes the helpless person so as to prevent others from rendering aid. By hiding Marcus in the dense brush, Chloe prevented rescue. (b) fails because the special relationship duty applies to immediate family, caretakers, or specific contractual relationships, not a former student/teacher dynamic. (c) fails because traditional common law explicitly rejects a generalized legal duty to rescue or report emergencies. (d) fails because a victim's voluntary intoxication does not sever a duty that arises from the defendant's affirmative act of secluding a helpless person. (e) fails because affirmative acts of concealment that isolate a victim are exactly what create the omission duty to render aid under the *Jones* categories.
