**Q4.** Assume Marcus successfully argues duress for the arson, establishing that his actions were coerced by Vance's threat. Will Leo be able to claim a derivative defense?

(a) Yes, because Marcus was compelled by a threat of imminent death, which transforms the underlying arson into a legally justified act for all participants.
(b) Yes, because an accomplice's liability is strictly derivative, meaning the principal's successful affirmative defense mandates the accomplice's full acquittal as a matter of law.
(c) No, because Marcus's duress claim acts as an excuse, which is personal to Marcus and does not make the arson a legally justified act. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because Leo was subjectively unaware of Vance's coercive threat when he voluntarily agreed to assist Marcus in burning down the Syndicate's evidence warehouse.
(e) Yes, because necessity and duress are both universal defenses that categorically extend to any third parties who unknowingly assist the primary coerced principal actor.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** (c) is correct. Duress is an excuse defense, meaning the act remains legally wrongful but the actor is not blamed because their free will was overborne. Because the act remains wrongful, a knowing accomplice (Leo) cannot share in the defense. (a) is wrong because duress does not render the act legally justified (right), only excused. (b) is wrong because an accomplice's liability requires evaluating their own culpability; excuses do not transfer. (d) is wrong because Leo's unawareness of the threat is irrelevant; the core issue is that duress is an excuse that cannot be derivative. (e) is wrong because necessity is a justification (which can extend to accomplices), while duress is an excuse (which cannot).
