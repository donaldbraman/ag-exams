**Q14.** Assume that the confrontation between Silas and Trey occurred in a state that applies the traditional duty to retreat. How does Silas's unobstructed path to his car affect his self-defense claim?

(a) In a retreat jurisdiction, Silas's defense fails because he knew he had a completely safe avenue of escape to his vehicle but deliberately chose to use deadly force instead. <!-- correct -->
(b) In a retreat jurisdiction, Silas's defense succeeds because the castle doctrine universally extends to public streets whenever the defendant is confronted by a known criminal syndicate hitman.
(c) In a stand-your-ground jurisdiction, Silas's defense fails because those laws strictly apply only when the defendant is physically inside their own vehicle during the violent confrontation.
(d) In a stand-your-ground jurisdiction, Silas's defense succeeds automatically because the prosecution is constitutionally barred from ever questioning the necessity of lethal force in public spaces.
(e) In either jurisdiction, Silas's defense fails because the duty to retreat is a strict federal constitutional requirement that supersedes any state-level stand-your-ground statutory protections.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. In jurisdictions that impose a duty to retreat (such as New York), a defendant is legally barred from using deadly physical force if they know they can retreat with complete personal safety. Because Silas had a clear, unobstructed path to get into his car and drive away safely, his failure to utilize that safe escape route defeats his self-defense claim entirely. (b) is wrong because the castle doctrine is a specific exception to the duty to retreat that applies to a person's home, not to public streets. (c) is wrong because stand-your-ground laws generally eliminate the duty to retreat in any place the defendant has a legal right to be, not solely inside vehicles. (d) is wrong because stand-your-ground laws eliminate the duty to retreat but still require the use of force itself to be objectively reasonable. (e) is wrong because there is no federal constitutional requirement mandating a duty to retreat.
