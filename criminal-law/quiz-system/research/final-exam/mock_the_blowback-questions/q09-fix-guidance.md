**Q9.** Assume the jurisdiction recognizes both traditional accomplice liability and the *Pinkerton* doctrine. Which of the following best describes the difference in how the prosecution could establish Sloane's liability for Chloe's death (assuming the death constitutes a substantive offense)?

(a) Accomplice liability requires proving she intentionally aided the specific distribution to Chloe, while *Pinkerton* only requires proving the fatal distribution was a foreseeable consequence of the drug conspiracy. <!-- correct -->
(b) Accomplice liability requires proving she was physically present during the actual street-level sale, while *Pinkerton* allows liability based merely on her passive status as a named co-conspirator.
(c) Accomplice liability allows for a conviction even if the principal distributor is acquitted, while *Pinkerton* strictly requires the principal to be convicted of the target homicide offense.
(d) Accomplice liability requires that she possessed the lethal narcotics constructively at the time of sale, while *Pinkerton* requires that she maintained actual physical control of the drug supply.
(e) Accomplice liability automatically limits her punishment to a lesser grade than the principal actor, while *Pinkerton* mandates identical and equal punishment for all participating members of the conspiracy.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Accomplice liability requires that the defendant possess the specific intent to aid or encourage the commission of the target offense. The *Pinkerton* doctrine is much broader, extending liability to any substantive crime committed by a co-conspirator in furtherance of the conspiracy that was a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the agreement. (b) is wrong because accomplice liability does not require physical presence (e.g., providing weapons or drugs in advance is sufficient). (c) is wrong because both doctrines generally permit conviction even if the principal is acquitted. (d) is wrong because neither doctrine requires physical or constructive possession as a strict element of accessorial liability. (e) is wrong because accomplices can be punished equally to the principal, and neither doctrine categorically limits grading in the described manner.
