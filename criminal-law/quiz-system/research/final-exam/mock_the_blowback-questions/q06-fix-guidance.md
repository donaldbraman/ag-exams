## Issue 1 — audit

Assume Deacon is charged with felony murder for Omar's death as an accomplice to Trey's evasion, and the prosecution seeks the death penalty or life without parole under the *Tison* standard. Which of the following is the most likely outcome?

(a) Not eligible for the maximum penalty, because by staying home and only providing a crowbar, Deacon's conduct likely fails the constitutional requirements of major participation and reckless indifference. <!-- correct -->
(b) Eligible for the maximum penalty, because Deacon acted as the mastermind of the criminal operation and therefore is strictly liable for any deaths proximately caused by his co-felons.
(c) Not eligible for the maximum penalty, because the traditional agency rule entirely precludes holding an absent accomplice liable when the actual killer is an active participant in the felony.
(d) Eligible for the maximum penalty, because Deacon's intent to commit the underlying commercial burglary constructively transfers to the mental state required for a first-degree capital murder conviction.
(e) Not eligible for the maximum penalty, because under the *Tison* standard, only the individual who physically inflicted the fatal injury can ever be convicted of capital felony murder.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. *Tison v. Arizona* sets the constitutional floor for imposing the death penalty (or LWOP) on felony murder accomplices who do not personally kill: they must be "major participants" in the felony and act with "reckless indifference to human life." Providing a tool and staying home places Deacon on the remote fringes of the actual execution of the crime, likely failing both requirements. (b) and (d) are wrong because they describe traditional felony murder liability but ignore the specific constitutional limits *Tison* places on maximum penalties. (c) is wrong because the agency rule applies to killings by *non-felons* (like police), whereas Trey was a co-felon. (e) is wrong because *Tison* expressly allows accomplices to receive the maximum penalty if they meet the major-participant and reckless-indifference thresholds.
