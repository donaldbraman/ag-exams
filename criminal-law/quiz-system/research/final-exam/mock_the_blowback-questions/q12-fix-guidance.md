**Q12.** Assume Trey is charged with attempted burglary of the storage unit. Which of the following best compares his liability under the traditional common law proximity test versus the Model Penal Code's substantial step test?

(a) He is more likely to be convicted under the MPC, because walking toward the fence masked strongly corroborates criminal purpose, whereas he might fail the proximity test's strictness. <!-- correct -->
(b) He is more likely to be convicted under the proximity test, because his physical presence near the scene satisfies the last proximate act requirement, whereas the MPC requires touching.
(c) He is equally likely to be convicted under both tests, because both frameworks require the prosecution to definitively prove that it was factually impossible for him to succeed.
(d) He is less likely to be convicted under the MPC, because the MPC universally recognizes a complete defense of voluntary abandonment since he never actually breached the physical perimeter.
(e) He is more likely to be convicted under the proximity test, because the common law strictly prohibits punishing mere preparatory actions, whereas the MPC solely punishes internal mental intent.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. The MPC's substantial step test focuses on what the actor has already done to corroborate their criminal purpose (e.g., possessing tools and a mask near the target), pushing the line of liability earlier. The common law proximity tests (like physical proximity or last proximate act) look at what remains to be done; since Trey hadn't reached or attempted to scale the fence, a common law court might deem his actions mere preparation. (b) is wrong because the proximity test is generally harder to satisfy, and the MPC does not require a physical touching. (c) is wrong because factual impossibility is not an element of attempt. (d) is wrong because while the MPC recognizes abandonment, it must be complete and voluntary; Trey was spotted by a guard, so his failure was involuntary. (e) is wrong because the MPC requires an overt act (a substantial step), not merely internal intent.
