**Q2.** Assume Deacon argues that his murder conviction for Vance's death should be mitigated to voluntary manslaughter under the traditional common law categorical approach. Which of the following is the strongest argument that the defense fails?

(a) The stealthy and calculated act of retrieving and pouring poison while Vance's back was turned negates the requirement that Deacon was in an actual, uncontrolled heat of passion. <!-- correct -->
(b) The words spoken by Vance constitute mere verbal insults, which are categorically excluded from serving as adequate provocation even when accompanied by an offensive physical battery like spitting.
(c) The brief moment it took for Vance to turn his back and walk toward the counter provided sufficient time for a reasonable person's passions to completely cool and subside.
(d) The law categorically excludes premeditated killings from ever receiving voluntary manslaughter mitigation, regardless of the objective adequacy of the provocation or the subjective emotional state of the defendant.
(e) The act of spitting, while offensive, does not fall into any of the rigidly recognized historical categories of provocation that were originally rooted in protecting early modern honor culture.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Common law voluntary manslaughter requires adequate provocation, actual heat of passion, insufficient cooling time, and causal connection. Surreptitiously pouring poison behind a victim's back demonstrates calculated, stealthy behavior that contradicts the requirement of acting in an actual, uncontrolled heat of passion. (b) is wrong because while words alone are inadequate, a physical battery (spitting) can qualify as adequate provocation. (c) is wrong because cooling time is measured objectively, and a brief moment may not suffice for a reasonable person to cool off. (d) is wrong because there is no categorical bar; premeditation addresses the murder baseline before mitigation is considered. (e) is wrong because an offensive battery historically fits within the recognized categories of adequate provocation.
