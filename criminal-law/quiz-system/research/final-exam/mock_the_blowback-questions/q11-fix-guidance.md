**Q11.** Assume Trey is charged with possession of the fentanyl found in the van's center console. How will a court likely evaluate his liability under the doctrine of constructive possession?

(a) He is not guilty if the jury believes he lacked knowledge of the drugs, because constructive possession requires awareness of the contraband's presence. <!-- correct -->
(b) He is not guilty, because the drugs were located in a closed console rather than on his physical person, negating the actus reus of possession.
(c) He is guilty, because the legal doctrine of actual possession applies whenever a defendant operates a vehicle containing illegal narcotics.
(d) He is guilty, because strict liability offenses like drug possession do not require proof of a culpable mental state.
(e) He is guilty, because the nexus requirement of constructive possession is automatically satisfied by his status as the primary driver.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Constructive possession requires that the defendant have both the power and the intention to exercise control over the item, which absolutely necessitates awareness of its presence. Mere proximity to hidden drugs in another person's vehicle is insufficient if the defendant genuinely lacked knowledge, making (a) correct. (b) is wrong because constructive possession specifically exists to criminalize possession when the item is *not* on the defendant's physical person. (c) misidentifies the doctrine as "actual possession," which requires physical contact. (d) is legally false; drug possession is not a strict liability offense and requires knowledge. (e) is wrong because driver status is merely a factor in the totality of circumstances, not an automatic legal nexus overriding the knowledge requirement.
