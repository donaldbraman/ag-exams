## Issue 1 — audit

**Q11.** Under California's SB 1437, can Leo be convicted of felony murder for Wendy's death?

(a) Yes, because as the driver of the getaway vehicle, he is strictly liable for any death resulting from the high-speed pursuit.
(b) Yes, because a pedestrian's death is a foreseeable consequence of fleeing from the police in a motor vehicle.
(c) No, because he was not the actual killer and the facts do not establish that he acted with reckless indifference to human life, as he was unaware of the weapon. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because the underlying predicate offense of hijacking merges with the homicide, precluding a felony murder charge.
(e) No, because he successfully completed his role by remaining in the vehicle while Dom committed the primary actus reus.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** California's SB 1437 dramatically restricts accomplice felony murder. A defendant who is not the actual killer can only be convicted if they aided with intent to kill or were a "major participant" who acted with "reckless indifference to human life" (*Tison* standard). Under *People v. Emanuel*, an accomplice who does not know their co-felon is armed generally lacks the requisite reckless indifference to human life. Because weapons were not explicitly discussed and Leo claimed he had no idea there would be a gun, the reckless indifference element is likely defeated. (a) is wrong because SB 1437 abolished strict liability for non-killer accomplices. (b) is wrong because foreseeability is the standard for proximate cause, not the subjective "reckless indifference" required by SB 1437. (d) is wrong because robbery/hijacking has an independent felonious purpose and does not merge with homicide. (e) is wrong because remaining in the vehicle makes him a getaway driver, which would ordinarily sustain accomplice liability absent SB 1437's specific limits.
