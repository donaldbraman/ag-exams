**Q6.** The ADA wishes to charge Benny with conspiracy to hijack the electronics truck, pointing to Benny's silent nod during Carmine's pitch. Under general conspiracy principles, does Benny's non-verbal response satisfy the agreement element?

(a) Yes, because an agreement need not be explicit and can be validly inferred from a nod or other non-verbal assent to a proposed criminal plan. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because his mere presence at the meeting of the organized crime syndicate imposes strict liability for any conspiracy discussed by the group's leadership.
(c) No, because a valid conspiracy agreement requires an overt act in furtherance of the plan by all participants, and Benny did nothing but silently nod.
(d) No, because Benny took no active part in the subsequent planning of the hijacking, which retroactively invalidates his initial momentary expression of apparent agreement.
(e) No, because a silent nod is legally insufficient to prove the specific intent required to commit the underlying target offense of armed commercial vehicle hijacking.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Conspiracy requires an agreement to commit a crime, but the agreement does not have to be formal, written, or explicit; it can be fully inferred from conduct, gestures, or non-verbal assent. (a) is correct because a silent nod in response to a proposed plan is legally sufficient to constitute an agreement. (b) fails because mere presence at a meeting is insufficient to prove conspiracy without evidence of agreement. (c) fails because only one co-conspirator needs to commit an overt act to satisfy that element for the whole group, and an overt act is distinct from the agreement itself. (d) fails because lack of subsequent participation does not invalidate an agreement once it has been formed. (e) fails because a nod can indeed serve as circumstantial evidence of both the intent to agree and the intent to commit the target offense.
