**Q6.** Based on the crew's fentanyl operation and the planned hijacking, can prosecutors establish the elements of a RICO violation against Carmine?

(a) Yes, because the group possesses a shared purpose, relationships, and longevity sufficient to constitute an enterprise engaging in a pattern of racketeering. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because any conspiracy to commit a felony automatically constitutes a criminal enterprise under the RICO statute.
(c) No, because the planned hijacking and the separate fentanyl manufacturing operation lack the relatedness required to form a pattern.
(d) No, because RICO requires proof that the enterprise infiltrated a legitimate, legal business to launder its illicit profits.
(e) No, because Carmine did not personally commit the underlying predicate acts of dumping the chemicals or attempting the hijacking.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under RICO (*Boyle v. United States*), an "enterprise" requires a purpose, relationships among those associated with it, and longevity sufficient to pursue its purpose. The syndicate here satisfies this, and the multiple, ongoing illicit operations (narcotics manufacturing, hijackings) constitute a pattern of racketeering activity. (b) is wrong because a simple conspiracy is not automatically a RICO enterprise; RICO requires an organizational structure and a pattern of predicate acts. (c) is wrong because the predicate acts (fentanyl manufacturing, hijacking rival shipments) are related through the enterprise's overarching purpose of generating illicit profit. (d) is wrong because RICO explicitly applies to wholly illegitimate criminal organizations, not just infiltrated legitimate businesses. (e) is wrong because under *Salinas*, a defendant can be convicted of RICO conspiracy if they adopt the goal of furthering the enterprise, even without personally committing the predicate acts.
