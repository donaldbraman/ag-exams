**Q3.** Assume Benny is charged with unintentional homicide in an MPC jurisdiction. Based on his actions and recorded statement while dumping the chemical solvent, what is the most appropriate charge?

(a) Guilty of manslaughter, because his recorded statement proves he consciously recognized and disregarded a substantial and unjustifiable risk that the fumes could be lethal to humans. <!-- correct -->
(b) Guilty of negligent homicide only, because his genuine belief that the rain would wash the chemicals away proves he failed to actually perceive the risk to human life.
(c) Guilty of manslaughter, because discarding highly toxic chemicals near a public trail involves a gross deviation from the standard of care, regardless of his subjective awareness.
(d) Guilty of negligent homicide only, because his decision to save time by dumping the barrels locally merely failed to exercise the ordinary caution of a reasonably prudent person.
(e) Guilty of manslaughter, because he voluntarily intoxicated himself with the chemical fumes, which under the MPC prevents him from claiming he was unaware of the risk.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because MPC manslaughter requires recklessness, defined as conscious disregard of a substantial and unjustifiable risk. Benny's statement ("before any human comes near it") proves he actively perceived the danger to humans but recklessly chose to rely on the rain rather than safely disposing of the barrels. (b) is wrong because his statement demonstrates he *did* perceive the risk to humans; relying on a mitigating factor like rain is a conscious gamble, not ignorance of the risk. (c) is wrong because it describes the objective gross negligence standard, while MPC manslaughter specifically requires subjective awareness (recklessness). (d) is wrong because ordinary negligence is insufficient for criminal liability, and his conduct went far beyond ordinary carelessness. (e) is wrong because while the MPC prevents voluntary intoxication from negating recklessness, there is no evidence Benny was intoxicated; he simply inhaled the fumes while working.
