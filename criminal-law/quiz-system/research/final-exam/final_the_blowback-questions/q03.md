**Q3.** With respect to the MPC definitions of recklessness and negligence, how should a court classify Benny's mental state regarding the risk of a human death?

(a) Reckless, because any reasonable person would have recognized that dumping toxic chemicals near a popular jogging trail posed a substantial risk of death.
(b) Reckless, because dumping chemicals in a manner that "could kill a stray dog" demonstrates a generalized extreme indifference to all forms of biological life.
(c) Negligent, because he consciously disregarded the risk of human death by ignoring the trail's popularity to save time.
(d) Negligent, if the jury finds he genuinely believed no human would come near the fumes but a reasonable person would have perceived the substantial risk. <!-- correct -->
(e) Strictly liable, because the disposal of hazardous chemical solvents is an inherently dangerous activity that eliminates the mens rea requirement.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** The dividing line between MPC manslaughter (recklessness) and negligent homicide is subjective awareness. Recklessness requires that the actor *consciously disregards* a substantial and unjustifiable risk, while negligence means the actor *should have been aware* of the risk but wasn't. Because Benny muttered that he was sure the rain would wash it away before any human came near, he arguably failed to perceive the risk to human life, making his conduct negligent if a reasonable person would have realized the danger of the nearby trail. (a) is wrong because "any reasonable person would have recognized" describes negligence, not recklessness. (b) is wrong because recklessness requires conscious disregard of the specific risk of *human* death. (c) is wrong because conscious disregard defines recklessness, not negligence. (e) is wrong because the MPC does not impose strict liability for unintentional homicides.
