**Q10.** Assume Nora is charged with murder for Rachel's death and seeks to mitigate the charge to voluntary manslaughter under the traditional common law rule. Will Nora's defense succeed?

(a) Yes, because discovering that a trusted associate is a federal informant constitutes legally adequate provocation that would inflame a reasonable person.
(b) Yes, because Rachel's mocking insults were uttered in close physical proximity aboard the boat, satisfying the strict requirement for mutual combat.
(c) No, because under the common law categorical approach, words alone—no matter how opprobrious—do not constitute legally adequate provocation to mitigate. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because Nora's immediate reaction of shooting Rachel demonstrates that an insufficient amount of cooling time had elapsed before the killing.
(e) No, because Nora's trembling hands and flushed face indicate that she was not actually acting in a subjective heat of passion.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** The common law *Girouard* rule strictly categorizes adequate provocation, holding that words alone, regardless of how insulting or upsetting, can never constitute adequate provocation to mitigate murder to manslaughter. Therefore, Rachel's mocking words are categorically insufficient to mitigate Nora's charge. (a) is wrong because the sudden discovery of an informant is not one of the recognized common law provocation categories (like the sudden discovery of spousal adultery). (b) is wrong because mutual combat requires physical altercation, not just yelling insults in close quarters. (d) is wrong because an immediate reaction actually favors the defendant by showing insufficient cooling time; the defense fails on the adequate provocation prong, not the cooling time prong. (e) is wrong because trembling hands and a flushed face actually constitute strong evidence that she *was* in a subjective heat of passion.

**Tags:** chapters: [12], topics: [voluntary manslaughter, adequate provocation, words alone], difficulty: easy, cognitive: application

**Grounding:** Chapter 12, words-alone-girouard; Girouard v. State establishes that words alone are never adequate provocation at common law.
