**Q5.** Assume the prosecution charges Carmine and his crew with conspiracy based on the initial sit-down meeting. Have the elements of conspiracy been satisfied?

(a) Guilty of conspiracy, because the explicit agreement to hijack the shipment satisfies the intent requirement and sketching the route constitutes a sufficient overt act. <!-- correct -->
(b) Not guilty of conspiracy, because sketching a route on a napkin is mere preparation and fails to satisfy the substantial step requirement for the overt act element.
(c) Guilty of conspiracy, because the crew's presence at the sit-down establishes a corrupt motive, which eliminates the need to prove an independent overt act in furtherance.
(d) Not guilty of conspiracy, because the failure to explicitly discuss weapons means the crew lacked the specific intent as to every material element of the target offense.
(e) Guilty of conspiracy, because the interstate commerce aspect of the pharmaceutical shipment automatically imputes the required specific intent to all participating crew members.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because conspiracy requires an agreement with specific intent to commit the target offense, plus an overt act. The explicit agreement covers the intent, and sketching the route on the napkin easily satisfies the minimal overt act requirement. (b) is wrong because the "substantial step" test applies to attempt, not conspiracy; the overt act for conspiracy can be purely preparatory and trivial. (c) is wrong because the overt act element cannot be eliminated merely by showing a corrupt motive; a physical act in furtherance is still required in most jurisdictions. (d) is wrong because the specific intent element requires intent to commit the core target offense (hijacking/robbery); the failure to discuss the specific operational details or weapons does not negate the intent to hijack. (e) is wrong because interstate commerce is merely a jurisdictional hook for federal crimes, not a substitute for the substantive mens rea of the conspiracy itself.
