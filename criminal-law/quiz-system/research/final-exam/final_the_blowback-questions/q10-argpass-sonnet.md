**Q10.** Assume Dom is charged with felony murder for the death of Wendy. In a jurisdiction that applies the proximate-cause theory of felony murder, what is the most likely outcome?

(a) Guilty of felony murder, because under the proximate-cause theory, it is reasonably foreseeable that an armed hijacking attempt will trigger a lethal police response. <!-- correct -->
(b) Not guilty of felony murder, because under the proximate-cause theory, liability is strictly limited to deaths directly caused by the physical actions of a co-felon.
(c) Guilty of felony murder, because under the proximate-cause theory, the merger doctrine automatically applies when the predicate felony involves an independent felonious purpose.
(d) Not guilty of felony murder, because the officer's missed shot was an independent intervening cause that entirely severed the causal chain between the felony and the death.
(e) Guilty of felony murder, because under the proximate-cause theory, any death that occurs during the flight from a felony is presumed to be the actual killer's specific intent.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because the proximate-cause theory of felony murder (*Smith v. State*) holds felons liable for any death that is a foreseeable consequence of the felony, including deaths caused by third parties like responding police officers. (b) is wrong because it accurately describes the competing "agency theory," which is the direct opposite of the proximate-cause theory being tested. (c) is wrong because the merger doctrine acts as a limitation to *prevent* felony murder charges when the felony is purely assaultive; it does not expand liability here. (d) is wrong because responsive police gunfire is considered a highly foreseeable, dependent intervening cause that preserves the causal chain, not an independent unforeseeable one. (e) is wrong because the proximate-cause theory relies on foreseeability and strict-liability substitution, not a legal presumption about the actual killer's specific mental state.
