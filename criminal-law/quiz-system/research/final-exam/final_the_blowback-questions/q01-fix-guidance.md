## Issue 1 — audit

**Q1.** Assume the District Attorney charges Carmine with first-degree murder in a jurisdiction that follows the traditional Pennsylvania rule for premeditation and deliberation. Does Carmine's split-second decision to draw his weapon and shoot Paulie satisfy the premeditation element?

(a) Yes, because under the Pennsylvania rule, the instant of conscious decision is sufficient to satisfy the premeditation requirement without any minimum period of prior reflection. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because the act of luring Paulie to the private boat demonstrates the prolonged planning and calculation required for first-degree murder.
(c) No, because first-degree premeditated murder requires an actual period of reflection and weighing of options prior to forming the intent to kill.
(d) No, because he acted in immediate response to Paulie reaching into his jacket, which automatically negates the specific intent required for murder.
(e) No, because his subjective but unreasonable belief that Paulie was drawing a weapon prevents the prosecution from proving express malice.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under the Pennsylvania rule (*Commonwealth v. Carroll*), "no time is too short" for a wicked man to frame the intent to kill. The instant of conscious decision completely satisfies the legal element of premeditation. Therefore, (a) is correct because Carmine made a conscious decision to shoot before firing. (b) fails because the facts stipulate Carmine had no initial intent to kill when he lured Paulie to the boat. (c) fails because requiring a prolonged period of reflection is contrary to the Pennsylvania rule's central holding. (d) fails because an immediate response to an action provides a motive but does not legally negate the intent to kill. (e) fails because an unreasonable belief pertains to the affirmative defense of imperfect self-defense, not whether the defendant formed the intent to kill.
