**Q1.** Based on the initial diner meeting, is Nora guilty of conspiracy to manufacture and distribute methamphetamine?

(a) Yes, because her provision of funding in exchange for a 40% stake in the profits establishes her agreement to the illicit venture. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because her knowledge of their intent to manufacture methamphetamine is legally sufficient to make her a conspiratorial participant.
(c) No, because she expressly disclaimed any desire to know how they sourced the chemicals, negating the bilateral agreement requirement.
(d) No, because she did not participate in any direct overt acts toward the physical manufacturing or distribution of the product.
(e) No, because an agreement to merely fund a criminal enterprise is treated exclusively as accomplice liability rather than conspiracy.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under general conspiracy doctrine, an agreement can be inferred from a defendant's provision of essential resources in exchange for a stake in the venture. Nora's $100,000 funding and demand for a 40% cut of the profits clearly establish her agreement to the illicit meth enterprise, even if she willfully blinded herself to the operational details. (b) is wrong because mere knowledge of a crime without an agreement or a stake is insufficient for conspiracy. (c) is wrong because disclaiming knowledge of the logistics does not negate her explicit agreement to profit from the overarching distribution. (d) is wrong because an overt act can be committed by any co-conspirator (like Oscar renting the warehouse). (e) is wrong because funding a venture for profit can concurrently constitute both conspiracy and accomplice liability.

**Tags:** chapters: [15], topics: [conspiracy, agreement], difficulty: easy, cognitive: application

**Grounding:** General conspiracy doctrine; providing capital for a share of profits constitutes an agreement (e.g., Lauria stake-in-the-venture analysis).
