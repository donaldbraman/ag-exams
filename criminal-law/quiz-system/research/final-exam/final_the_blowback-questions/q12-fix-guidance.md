## Issue 1 — audit

**Q12.** Is Carmine liable under the *Pinkerton* doctrine for Dom's armed assault with the shotgun?

(a) Yes, because an armed escalation is a reasonably foreseeable consequence of a conspiracy to hijack a cartel's shipment, even if weapons were not explicitly planned. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because the *Pinkerton* doctrine makes conspirators strictly liable for all acts committed by co-conspirators, regardless of foreseeability.
(c) No, because Dom's decision to bring a shotgun was an independent, unforeseeable deviation from the agreed-upon plan.
(d) No, because Carmine was not present at the scene and did not personally participate in the attempted robbery.
(e) No, because the *Pinkerton* doctrine was abolished by the Model Penal Code and is no longer valid in any jurisdiction.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under the *Pinkerton* doctrine, a conspirator is liable for all substantive offenses committed by co-conspirators that are in furtherance of the conspiracy and reasonably foreseeable as a necessary or natural consequence of the unlawful agreement. Hijacking a rival drug cartel's shipment is an inherently dangerous operation, making the use of firearms reasonably foreseeable even if the crew did not explicitly discuss weapons at the sit-down. (b) is wrong because *Pinkerton* requires the substantive offense to be reasonably foreseeable; it is not absolute strict liability for wild deviations. (c) is wrong because armed escalation in a cartel hijacking is highly foreseeable, not an independent deviation. (d) is wrong because presence at the scene is not required for *Pinkerton* liability. (e) is wrong because while the MPC rejects *Pinkerton*, the doctrine remains valid and heavily used in federal law and many states.
