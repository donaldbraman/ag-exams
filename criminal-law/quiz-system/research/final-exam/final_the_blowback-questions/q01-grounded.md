**Q1.** Assume that Carmine is charged with first-degree premeditated murder in a jurisdiction that follows the Pennsylvania rule. Based on the shooting on the boat, what is the most likely outcome?

(a) Guilty of first-degree murder, because his conscious decision to shoot Paulie, however briefly held, satisfies the premeditation requirement under the Pennsylvania rule. <!-- correct -->
(b) Not guilty of first-degree murder, because the ten-second interval between the insult and shooting provided insufficient time for the reflection necessary for premeditation.
(c) Guilty of first-degree murder, because his routine habit of keeping a loaded weapon on the boat's console establishes the necessary advance planning for the homicide.
(d) Not guilty of first-degree murder, because the uncontested evidence proves that he did not plan to kill Paulie when they initially boarded the vessel together.
(e) Guilty of first-degree murder, because the law automatically presumes premeditation whenever a deadly weapon is intentionally directed at a vital area of the victim's body.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because under the Pennsylvania rule (*Commonwealth v. Carroll*), premeditation requires only a conscious decision to kill, and "no time is too short for a wicked man to frame in his mind his scheme of murder." (b) is wrong because it applies the majority common law requirement of actual reflection over time, which the Pennsylvania rule explicitly rejects. (c) is wrong because prior planning is not required under the Pennsylvania rule, and his general habit was unrelated to Paulie. (d) is wrong because the lack of an initial plan upon boarding is irrelevant if the intent crystallized in the ten seconds before the shot. (e) is wrong because while the use of a deadly weapon may infer intent to kill, it does not automatically legally presume premeditation.
