## Issue 1 — audit

**Q2.** Assume Carmine claims the common-law mitigation of voluntary manslaughter for shooting Paulie. How will the court rule?

(a) He is guilty of voluntary manslaughter because Paulie's admission of talking to the FBI constitutes adequate provocation by threatening his liberty.
(b) He is guilty of voluntary manslaughter because he acted under extreme emotional disturbance without a sufficient cooling-off period.
(c) He is guilty of murder because words alone, no matter how opprobrious, cannot constitute legally adequate provocation. <!-- correct -->
(d) He is guilty of murder because a reasonable person in Carmine's circumstances would not have been enraged by an insult about their spouse.
(e) He is guilty of murder because he failed to demonstrate actual, subjective heat of passion when shooting Paulie.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** Under common-law voluntary manslaughter (*Girouard v. State*), verbal insults, regardless of how graphic or vicious, do not constitute legally adequate provocation. The provocation must fall into a recognized physical category (e.g., assault, discovery of adultery). Therefore, Carmine's defense fails on the adequate provocation element. (a) is wrong because threats to liberty or admissions of informing are not recognized categorical provocations at common law. (b) is wrong because "extreme emotional disturbance" is the MPC standard, not the strict common-law standard. (d) is wrong because the failure is categorical (words alone), not a factual judgment about the objective reasonableness of the rage. (e) is wrong because even if he subjectively experienced passion, the objective inadequacy of the provocation bars the defense.
