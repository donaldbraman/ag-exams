## Issue 1 — argpass-sonnet

**Q2.** Assume that, whether or not Nora is a conspirator, the jurisdiction strictly requires the "purpose" standard for accomplice liability. Is Nora guilty as an accomplice to Penelope's theft of the depot equipment?

(a) Guilty, because she provided the $100,000 that enabled the overall methamphetamine operation to succeed and generate profits.
(b) Guilty, because by demanding a 40% cut of the profits, she acquired a stake in the venture that imputes purpose to all crimes.
(c) Guilty, because she knew that sourcing chemicals and equipment would likely involve some form of illegal acquisition or theft.
(d) Not guilty, because her statement disclaiming knowledge of their sourcing methods demonstrates she lacked the specific purpose to facilitate a theft. <!-- correct -->
(e) Not guilty, because accomplice liability strictly requires physical presence at the scene of the crime or active encouragement during its commission.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** When a jurisdiction strictly requires the "purpose" standard for accomplice liability, the defendant must have the specific intent to facilitate the target offense. While Nora had the purpose to facilitate the overarching meth operation, she explicitly distanced herself from the sourcing methods, demonstrating she lacked the specific purpose that Penelope commit a theft. (a) is wrong because enabling the general operation does not automatically establish purpose for an unapproved, specific collateral crime. (b) is wrong because the "stake in the venture" doctrine establishes purpose for the known, intended crime (meth distribution), not all foreseeable acts. (c) is wrong because mere knowledge that illegal acquisition might occur does not equate to the purpose that it happen. (e) is wrong because accomplice liability can be established without physical presence.

**Grounding:** General accomplice liability doctrine; a general stake in a venture does not automatically satisfy the purpose requirement for distinct, unapproved substantive offenses.
