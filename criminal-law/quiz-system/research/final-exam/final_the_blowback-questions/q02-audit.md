**Q2.** Assume that, whether or not he is guilty of murder, Carmine seeks a jury instruction for common law voluntary manslaughter based on Paulie's graphic insult. What is the most likely result?

(a) Entitled to mitigation, because the graphically detailed insult regarding his wife's fidelity constitutes legally adequate provocation under the historical honor-culture categories.
(b) Not entitled to mitigation, because mere insulting words or gestures, no matter how opprobrious, can never constitute adequate provocation under the traditional common law rule. <!-- correct -->
(c) Entitled to mitigation, because the brief ten-second interval between the insult and the shooting proves that insufficient time had elapsed for a reasonable person to cool off.
(d) Not entitled to mitigation, because the traditional common law approach strictly requires that the provocation must arise from a sudden discovery of actual spousal adultery.
(e) Entitled to mitigation, because the extreme emotional disturbance caused by the victim's sudden admission of betrayal provides a reasonable explanation for his conduct.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** (b) is correct because the traditional common law categorical approach (*Girouard v. State*) strictly dictates that words alone, regardless of how graphically insulting they are, can never serve as legally adequate provocation. (a) is wrong because while adultery could be a trigger, the historical categories required physical discovery or actual assault, not mere verbal insults. (c) is wrong because while the cooling-time element is satisfied by the short interval, the underlying provocation itself remains legally inadequate. (d) is wrong because while adultery is the most famous category, other categories like mutual combat or extreme assault existed; it was not the *only* category. (e) is wrong because it applies the modern MPC extreme emotional disturbance standard, whereas the question asks for the traditional common law rule.
