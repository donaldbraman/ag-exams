**Q8.** Did Dom commit the actus reus of attempted hijacking before the police arrived?

(a) Yes, under the MPC substantial step test, but not under the common-law dangerous proximity test, because he had not yet demanded the keys.
(b) Yes, under both the MPC substantial step test and the common-law dangerous proximity test, because lying in wait with an aimed weapon satisfies both standards. <!-- correct -->
(c) No, under both tests, because his actions constituted mere preparation until the driver actually noticed the weapon.
(d) No, because his failure to communicate a threat meant the target offense of hijacking could not technically be completed.
(e) No, under the MPC substantial step test, because dropping the shotgun negated his prior corroborating conduct.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** Dom's conduct satisfies both major attempt tests. Under the MPC, lying in wait for the victim is explicitly listed as conduct that strongly corroborates the actor's criminal purpose, satisfying the "substantial step" test. Under the stricter common-law "dangerous proximity" test, being immediately next to the truck with a shotgun aimed directly at the approaching driver is dangerously close to completing the crime. (a) is wrong because his conduct was close enough to satisfy the common-law test as well. (c) is wrong because lying in wait with a drawn weapon goes far beyond mere preparation under any modern standard. (d) is wrong because attempt does not require the victim to perceive the threat, only that the defendant takes sufficient steps toward completion. (e) is wrong because later abandonment (even if valid) does not erase the fact that the actus reus of attempt was previously achieved.
