**Q13.** Assume Leo is charged with possession of the fentanyl found in the getaway car. Based on the doctrine of constructive possession, what is the most likely outcome?

(a) Guilty of possession, because the presence of the visible drugs on the passenger seat directly next to him is sufficient to establish his awareness and ability to exercise control. <!-- correct -->
(b) Not guilty of possession, because Dom was the exclusive owner of the backpack, which categorically prevents any other person from simultaneously exercising constructive possession.
(c) Guilty of possession, because strict liability applies to the transportation of controlled substances whenever the quantity exceeds the statutory threshold for personal use.
(d) Not guilty of possession, because his explicit denial of knowledge effectively negates the required mental state of specific intent to distribute the pharmaceutical shipment.
(e) Guilty of possession, because operating a rented vehicle automatically legally transfers the actual possession of all its contents to the current driver.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because constructive possession requires the knowledge of the item and the power/intent to control it. The baggies of fentanyl spilling out in plain view directly on the seat next to Leo provides strong circumstantial evidence of both awareness and the physical capability to exercise dominion over the drugs. (b) is wrong because constructive possession can be joint; multiple people can constructively possess the same item simultaneously regardless of technical ownership. (c) is wrong because possession of narcotics requires a culpable mental state (awareness of the nature and presence of the substance), not strict liability. (d) is wrong because a defendant's self-serving denial of knowledge is merely evidence for the jury to weigh; it does not legally negate the strong circumstantial evidence of plain view proximity. (e) is wrong because driving a vehicle does not automatically confer possession of all items inside; the prosecution must still prove the driver knew the contraband was present and had the ability to control it.
