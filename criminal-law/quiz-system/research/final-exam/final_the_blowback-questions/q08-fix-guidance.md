**Q8.** Assume the jurisdiction applies the common law "constructive recklessness" standard from *Commonwealth v. Welansky* rather than the MPC. How does this change Oscar's exposure for the explosion?

(a) He is strictly liable for the death because he was engaged in an unlawful drug manufacturing enterprise at the time of dumping.
(b) He can be convicted of manslaughter because a reasonable person knowing the solvent was highly volatile would have perceived the grave danger. <!-- correct -->
(c) He cannot be convicted of manslaughter because the Welansky rule requires the defendant to owe a preexisting affirmative duty to the victim.
(d) He can be convicted of first-degree murder because his deliberate act of dumping the chemicals constitutes an inherently dangerous environmental felony.
(e) He cannot be convicted of any degree of homicide because his subjective belief in the rain's diluting effect negates malice aforethought.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** Under the common law "constructive recklessness" standard established in *Commonwealth v. Welansky*, a defendant is treated as reckless if they knew facts that would cause an ordinary, reasonable person to perceive a grave danger, even if the defendant subjectively believed conditions were safe. Because a reasonable person would know dumping 500 gallons of highly volatile solvent poses a grave risk, Oscar's subjective belief does not protect him. (a) is wrong because constructive recklessness is an objective negligence-like standard, not strict liability for being engaged in an unlawful enterprise. (c) is wrong because criminal recklessness can arise from the creation of a deadly hazard, without requiring a preexisting special relationship or duty of care. (d) is wrong because dumping chemicals, while dangerous, typically supports manslaughter or second-degree murder, not first-degree murder. (e) is wrong because *Welansky* explicitly allows a conviction despite the defendant's subjective belief in safety.
