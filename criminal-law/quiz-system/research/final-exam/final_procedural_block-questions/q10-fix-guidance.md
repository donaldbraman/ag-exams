**Q10.** Assume for this question that the jurisdiction firmly rejects the application of the felony-murder rule. Can Luis be convicted of depraved-heart murder for the bystander's death?

(a) Yes, because discharging a firearm in a public space automatically establishes the extreme subjective recklessness strictly required for a depraved-heart murder conviction in all modern jurisdictions.
(b) Yes, because his initial choice to transport illicit narcotics created the perilous situation, legally attributing all subsequent violent outcomes directly to his inherently depraved and malignant heart.
(c) No, because returning fire to survive a lethal gang ambush demonstrates a motive of self-preservation rather than a callous and extreme indifference to the value of human life. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because depraved-heart murder explicitly requires the defendant to specifically intend to cause serious bodily harm to the actual civilian victim who is ultimately struck and killed.
(e) No, because a depraved-heart murder conviction is legally impossible in any factual scenario where the defendant is actively attempting to strike a completely different individual target.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** Depraved-heart murder requires conduct that demonstrates an extreme and callous disregard for the value of human life. Returning fire to survive a sudden lethal gang ambush indicates a primary motive of self-preservation, which generally negates the "malignant heart" element, making (c) correct. (a) is incorrect because discharging a firearm defensively in public does not automatically establish the extreme subjective recklessness required for this specific charge. (b) is incorrect because merely creating a perilous situation through non-violent drug transport does not automatically equate to a depraved indifference regarding subsequent shootouts. (d) is incorrect because depraved-heart murder specifically does not require an explicit intent to cause serious bodily harm to any specific victim. (e) is incorrect because a defendant can still be convicted of depraved-heart murder for reckless actions affecting bystanders, even if aiming at a different target.
