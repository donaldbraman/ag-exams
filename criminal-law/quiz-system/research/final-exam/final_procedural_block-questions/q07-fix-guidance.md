**Q7.** Moving to Stem 2, evaluate Kevin's liability for Luis's actions. Under the *Pinkerton* doctrine, is Kevin vicariously liable for Luis's substantive possession of the cocaine shipment?

(a) Kevin is guilty of Luis's possession because the active possession of the cocaine was a reasonably foreseeable act committed in direct furtherance of their joint distribution conspiracy. <!-- correct -->
(b) Kevin is guilty of Luis's possession because he serves as the corporate executive of WasteCorp, establishing automatic strict liability for the actions of all his subordinate truck drivers.
(c) Kevin is not guilty of Luis's possession because he was not physically present in the vehicle and did not maintain immediate constructive control over the specific waste drums.
(d) Kevin is not guilty of Luis's possession because Luis was operating under extreme duress when the rival gang members ambushed him, permanently severing the causal chain of liability.
(e) Kevin is not guilty of Luis's possession because the Pinkerton doctrine solely applies to violent physical felonies and explicitly does not extend to possessory statutory narcotics offenses.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** The *Pinkerton* doctrine holds co-conspirators vicariously liable for the substantive offenses committed by their partners if those acts are in furtherance of the conspiracy and reasonably foreseeable. Luis's transportation and possession of the cocaine directly furthered their distribution agreement, making (a) correct. (b) is incorrect because criminal liability is not based on strict corporate executive liability, but rather on the parameters of the conspiracy. (c) is incorrect because *Pinkerton* explicitly provides for vicarious liability even when a defendant lacks physical presence or constructive control. (d) is incorrect because the subsequent ambush does not retroactively negate Luis's voluntary possession of the narcotics beforehand. (e) is incorrect because *Pinkerton* applies broadly to all foreseeable substantive offenses, including possessory drug crimes.

**Tags:** chapters: [14], topics: [Pinkerton liability, substantive offenses, vicarious liability], difficulty: medium, cognitive: application

**Grounding:** Pinkerton v. United States holds that a conspirator is vicariously liable for the substantive offenses committed by co-conspirators in furtherance of the conspiracy that are reasonably foreseeable.
