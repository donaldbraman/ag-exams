**Q9.** Assume the jurisdiction has adopted the Model Penal Code's homicide framework, and Alex seeks mitigation based on Blake's insult and his own enraged reaction. How does the MPC approach apply?

(a) He may be entitled to manslaughter mitigation, because the MPC abandons rigid categorical bars and allows the jury to assess the reasonableness of his emotional disturbance from his perspective. <!-- correct -->
(b) He is fully barred from manslaughter mitigation, because the MPC strictly preserves the traditional common-law rule that verbal insults can never constitute adequate and legally recognizable provocation.
(c) He may be entitled to manslaughter mitigation, because the MPC automatically reduces any murder charge to manslaughter whenever a defendant experiences subjective feelings of extreme anger or rage.
(d) He is fully barred from manslaughter mitigation, because his fear of the victim drawing a weapon supersedes his anger, and the MPC strictly prohibits mixing self-defense claims with emotional disturbance.
(e) He may be entitled to manslaughter mitigation, because the MPC intentionally eliminates the requirement that the defendant's emotional disturbance possess any reasonable explanation or objectively measurable excuse.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because the Model Penal Code replaces traditional heat of passion with the Extreme Emotional Disturbance (EED) defense. The MPC explicitly abandons rigid common-law categories (including the "words alone" rule) and delegates to the jury the task of determining whether the defendant was suffering from an EED for which there is a reasonable explanation or excuse, viewed from the defendant's situation under the circumstances as he believed them to be. (b) is wrong because the MPC expressly rejects the common-law categorical rules, meaning words can be sufficient. (c) is wrong because the MPC does not provide an automatic reduction; the jury must still find that the subjective rage had a reasonable explanation or excuse. (d) is wrong because the MPC frequently allows defendants to run EED defenses parallel to, or intermixed with, fear-based imperfect self-defense claims. (e) is wrong because the MPC explicitly requires that the extreme emotional disturbance have a "reasonable explanation or excuse," retaining an objective boundary on the mitigation.
