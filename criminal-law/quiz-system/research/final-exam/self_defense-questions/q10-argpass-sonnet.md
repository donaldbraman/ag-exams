**Q10.** Assume that Alex had a legal duty to call 911 and failed to do so. Under standard principles of factual causation, did Alex's omission cause Blake's death?

(a) No, because a complete failure to act cannot conceptually serve as the factual but-for cause of a physiological death resulting from a gunshot wound.
(b) No, because the subsequent medical error at the hospital completely broke the chain of factual causation flowing from the initial failure to summon aid.
(c) Yes, because the medical examiner concluded that Blake would have survived if 911 had been called immediately, making the delay a factual but-for cause. <!-- correct -->
(d) Yes, because Alex's conscious failure to act demonstrated an extreme indifference to the value of human life that substitutes for factual causation.
(e) Yes, because the 45-minute delay on the lawn was the sole and exclusive physiological factor that resulted in the neighbor's fatal decline.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** (c) is correct because factual causation ("but-for" cause) asks whether the result would have occurred without the defendant's conduct; since Blake would have survived if 911 had been called (Fact 12), the omission is a but-for cause. (a) is incorrect because omissions can absolutely serve as but-for causes in criminal law when a duty exists. (b) is incorrect because subsequent errors affect proximate (legal) causation, not factual (but-for) causation. (d) is incorrect because extreme indifference is a mens rea standard for depraved heart murder, not a substitute for the actus reus of causation. (e) is incorrect because the omission was not the "sole" factor; the gunshot and the hospital error were also causal factors.
