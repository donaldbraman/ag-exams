**Q12.** Assume Blake is prosecuted in a Model Penal Code (MPC) jurisdiction. Blake argues that under the MPC, dropping the gas can and leaving the room means his actions amounted only to mere preparation for arson. How should the court rule?

(a) Guilty of attempt, because pouring the gasoline across the floor was a substantial step that strongly corroborated his criminal purpose. <!-- correct -->
(b) Not guilty of attempt, because the MPC requires the defendant to possess all necessary tools to complete the crime before an attempt is recognized.
(c) Guilty of attempt, because dropping the empty can constituted a legally sufficient overt act in furtherance of a criminal conspiracy.
(d) Not guilty of attempt, because the police intervention in the hallway prevented him from taking a substantial step toward igniting the fuel.
(e) Guilty of attempt, because the MPC categorizes any verbal expression of criminal intent as a substantial step when paired with physical movement.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** The MPC's substantial step test shifts the focus from what remains to be done to what the actor has already done. An act is an attempt if it is a "substantial step in a course of conduct planned to culminate in his commission of the crime" that strongly corroborates the actor's criminal purpose. Pouring gasoline across a living room floor easily meets this standard. (b) is wrong because the MPC does not require the defendant to possess every tool (like the match) to cross the attempt threshold. (c) is wrong because it conflates the actus reus of attempt with the lower "overt act" standard of conspiracy. (d) is wrong because the substantial step (pouring the gas) had already occurred before the police intervened. (e) is wrong because verbal expression paired with general movement is not the MPC standard for a substantial step.
