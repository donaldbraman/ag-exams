**Q10.** Assume that a jury determines Blake's fear of Chris reaching into his jacket was genuine but objectively unreasonable under the circumstances. What is the effect of the imperfect self-defense doctrine in jurisdictions that recognize it?

(a) Guilty of voluntary manslaughter, because his honest but unreasonable belief in the need for deadly force legally negates malice aforethought. <!-- correct -->
(b) Guilty of murder, because the doctrine of imperfect self-defense acts as a complete justification that results in a full acquittal when successfully applied.
(c) Guilty of voluntary manslaughter, because any use of excessive force during a physical altercation automatically mitigates a homicide charge.
(d) Guilty of murder, because an unreasonable belief only mitigates the offense when the defendant was legally provoked by adequate physical violence.
(e) Guilty of voluntary manslaughter, because the jury's finding of a genuine belief legally converts the shooting into an unintentional negligent homicide.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** In jurisdictions that recognize it, imperfect self-defense applies when a defendant honestly (genuinely) but unreasonably believes that deadly force is necessary. This honest belief negates the malice required for murder, reducing the conviction to voluntary manslaughter. (b) is wrong because imperfect self-defense is a partial defense that mitigates the charge; it does not yield a full acquittal. (c) is wrong because excessive force does not automatically mitigate a charge; the defendant must possess the requisite honest belief in the necessity of the force. (d) is wrong because imperfect self-defense is a distinct mitigatory doctrine from heat-of-passion provocation. (e) is wrong because voluntary manslaughter under imperfect self-defense is still an intentional killing, not a negligent one.
