**Q3.** Assume Alex asserts a self-defense claim against the homicide charge. How does the initial aggressor doctrine apply to Alex?

(a) Alex is barred from claiming self-defense because he initiated the confrontation and did not clearly communicate withdrawal. <!-- correct -->
(b) Alex is barred from claiming self-defense because his original intent when forming the conspiracy was to commit an assault.
(c) Alex is not barred from claiming self-defense because he immediately dropped the baseball bat before drawing his handgun.
(d) Alex is not barred from claiming self-defense because Blake escalated the confrontation by reaching for a weapon.
(e) Alex is not barred from claiming self-defense because he was standing in an open area when the shooting occurred.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** The initial aggressor bar prohibits a person who starts a physical confrontation from claiming self-defense unless they withdraw and clearly communicate that withdrawal. Alex initiated the confrontation by trespassing onto Blake's lawn wielding a baseball bat (Fact 4). Although he dropped the bat, he did not communicate withdrawal before shooting (Fact 7), meaning the bar remains intact. Therefore, (a) is correct. Option (b) is incorrect because the initial aggressor analysis focuses on the conduct that initiated the immediate physical confrontation, not the prior mental formation of the conspiracy. Option (c) is incorrect because dropping the bat silently does not clearly communicate withdrawal to the opponent. Option (d) is incorrect because Blake's reaction to an armed trespasser does not erase Alex's status as the initial aggressor, barring a disproportionate response to a non-deadly provocation. Option (e) is incorrect because the initial aggressor bar applies regardless of whether the confrontation occurs in an open or enclosed space.
