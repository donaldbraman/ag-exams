## Stem 2: The Fire

The fire marshal confirmed the living room was soaked in gasoline. The DA is adding attempted arson charges for both defendants. Evaluate the attempt doctrines for Blake and derivative liability for Alex based on the text message.

**Q11.** Following the shooting, Blake poured gasoline across the living room floor but had no ignition source and was apprehended in the hallway. Under the traditional common-law proximity test, is Blake guilty of attempted arson?

(a) Not guilty of attempt, because lacking an immediate ignition source means he was not dangerously close to the final consummation of the arson.
(b) Guilty of attempt, because the physical act of purchasing and storing the gasoline constituted a sufficient preparatory step under the proximity analysis.
(c) Not guilty of attempt, because he successfully abandoned his criminal purpose when he voluntarily dropped the gas can and walked toward his bedroom.
(d) Guilty of attempt, because he formed the specific intent to burn the dwelling and successfully communicated that intent to a co-conspirator via text.
(e) Not guilty of attempt, because the crime of arson requires the actual charring of the physical structure before any inchoate liability can attach.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** The traditional proximity test requires the defendant to be dangerously close to completing the crime, leaving almost no locus poenitentiae (opportunity to repent). Because Blake had no matches or lighter in the room and had to walk down the hall to find one, he lacked the immediate physical means to ignite the fire and was not on the very brink of consummation. (b) is wrong because purchasing/storing are mere acts of preparation under the proximity test, though they might suffice under broader modern tests. (c) is wrong because walking to find a match is a continuation of the crime, not an abandonment of criminal purpose. (d) is wrong because specific intent and communication do not satisfy the strict actus reus requirements of proximity. (e) is wrong because charring is required for a completed arson, not for an attempt.
