## Issue 1 — audit

**Q12.** Evaluate the prosecutor's strategy of charging Alex with a mandatory 25-year-to-life firearm enhancement to secure a 5-year plea deal for manslaughter. How is this evaluated under constitutional law?

(a) The strategy is unconstitutional, because leveraging a mandatory minimum solely to secure a plea deal violates the core doctrine of equitable prosecutorial discretion.
(b) The strategy is unconstitutional, because the extreme disparity between five years and life imprisonment inherently constitutes cruel and unusual punishment under the Eighth Amendment.
(c) The strategy is constitutional, because courts universally afford prosecutors broad executive discretion to use mandatory minimums to lawfully induce subsequent plea agreements. <!-- correct -->
(d) The strategy is constitutional, because the grand jury retains exclusive procedural authority over plea negotiations and formally approved the substantial downward departure.
(e) The strategy is constitutional, because mandatory minimum statutes explicitly contain provisions that legally require prosecutors to offer leniency during the formal plea process.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** Under *Bordenkircher v. Hayes*, the Supreme Court established that it is constitutional for prosecutors to use the threat of severe, legally authorized charges (including mandatory minimums) to leverage a defendant into accepting a plea deal. This is considered a valid exercise of prosecutorial discretion and the recognized "trial penalty" inherent in the plea-bargaining system. Option (a) asserts the exact opposite of established constitutional doctrine. Option (b) is incorrect; while heavily criticized by reformers, courts do not view plea-deal disparities as Eighth Amendment violations so long as the threatened sentence is statutorily authorized. Option (d) incorrectly assigns authority over plea bargaining to the grand jury, which only issues indictments, not plea deals. Option (e) is factually false; mandatory minimums compel harsh sentencing upon conviction but do not statutorily require prosecutors to offer leniency during the plea process.
