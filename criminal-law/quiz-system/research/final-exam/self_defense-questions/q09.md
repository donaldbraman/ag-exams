**Q9.** Assume the jurisdiction has adopted the Model Penal Code's extreme emotional disturbance (EED) framework. How does the EED inquiry differ from the common law provocation doctrine?

(a) EED allows mitigation based on any genuine emotional disturbance, regardless of whether a reasonable explanation or excuse exists for the actor's intense emotional state.
(b) EED expands mitigation by allowing the jury to assess the reasonableness of Alex's disturbance from his situational viewpoint without requiring a specific categorical trigger. <!-- correct -->
(c) EED requires strict proof that the emotional disturbance was caused directly by a physical assault or battery committed by the victim against the defendant.
(d) EED eliminates the requirement that the defendant was actually suffering from an emotional disturbance, focusing entirely on the objective reasonableness of the ultimate action.
(e) EED strictly limits mitigation to situations where the defendant discovers spousal adultery, explicitly rejecting the common law's broader mutual combat category for homicide mitigation.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** The MPC's Extreme Emotional Disturbance (EED) formulation significantly broadens mitigation by discarding the common law's rigid provocation categories and the strict requirement of suddenness. Instead, it asks whether the defendant suffered from an extreme emotional disturbance for which there is a reasonable explanation or excuse, assessed from the viewpoint of a person in the defendant's situation under the circumstances as they believed them to be. Option (a) is incorrect because EED still requires a "reasonable explanation or excuse" for the disturbance; it is not boundlessly subjective. Option (c) is incorrect because EED intentionally abandons the common law's requirement for physical assault or specific triggers. Option (d) is incorrect because EED requires both subjective actual disturbance and an objective (situational) reasonable explanation. Option (e) is incorrect because EED abolishes the specific categories entirely, allowing juries to consider a much wider array of stressors.
