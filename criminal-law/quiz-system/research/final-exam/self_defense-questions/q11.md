**Q11.** Assume Alex is charged with Blake's homicide. Alex argues that Dr. Davis's charting error broke the chain of causation. How will a court likely resolve the causation issue?

(a) The charting error breaks the chain of causation because it was an independent human action that directly and significantly accelerated Blake's resulting death.
(b) The charting error breaks the chain of causation because medical professionals are held strictly liable for any resulting patient mortality during emergency trauma care.
(c) The charting error does not break the causal chain because ordinary medical negligence in treating a wound is considered a reasonably foreseeable intervening cause. <!-- correct -->
(d) The charting error does not break the causal chain because only intentional acts of malice by third parties can ever legally serve as superseding causes.
(e) The charting error does not break the causal chain because any medical treatment, regardless of how grossly negligent, is entirely imputed to the original assailant.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** Under the doctrine of proximate cause, an intervening act breaks the causal chain only if it is completely unforeseeable or highly extraordinary. Courts uniformly hold that ordinary medical negligence in the treatment of a wound caused by the defendant is a reasonably foreseeable consequence of inflicting the injury. Because the medical board classified the charting error as ordinary medical negligence (Fact 10), it does not serve as a superseding cause. Option (a) is incorrect because human action alone does not sever causation unless it is unforeseeable. Option (b) is incorrect because medical professionals are not held strictly liable for mortality, and such a standard does not govern the criminal causation analysis for the initial attacker. Option (d) is incorrect because grossly negligent acts or intentional recklessness by third parties can sometimes serve as superseding causes; it is not limited purely to intentional malice. Option (e) is incorrect because extreme or gross medical negligence (e.g., operating while intoxicated) can occasionally sever the causal chain.
