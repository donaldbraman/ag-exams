**Q6.** Assume the trial occurs in a traditional duty-to-retreat jurisdiction and Alex is not the initial aggressor. Does the duty to retreat defeat Alex's self-defense claim?

(a) Yes, because Alex knew he had an unobstructed path to safely retreat to the public sidewalk but deliberately chose to use deadly force instead. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because the duty to retreat mandates that a defendant exhaust all possible non-deadly alternatives, such as warning shots, before escalating a conflict.
(c) No, because Alex was standing outdoors where the widely recognized castle doctrine exception automatically eliminates the strict duty to retreat from a confrontation.
(d) No, because the duty to retreat is categorically waived when a defendant faces an objectively imminent threat of death or serious bodily injury.
(e) No, because Alex possessed a valid concealed carry permit, which statutorily exempts him from the standard duty to retreat in all public spaces.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** In traditional duty-to-retreat jurisdictions (e.g., New York), a defendant must retreat before resorting to deadly force if a completely safe avenue of retreat is available and known to the defendant. Because Alex was standing on the lawn with an unobstructed path to safely run back to the public sidewalk (Fact 7), he had a duty to retreat before shooting. Option (b) is incorrect because the duty to retreat focuses specifically on leaving the scene if safe to do so, not on exhausting a checklist of non-deadly force alternatives like warning shots. Option (c) is incorrect because the castle doctrine exception applies only when the defendant is inside their own home, not while trespassing on someone else's property. Option (d) is incorrect because the existence of an imminent deadly threat is what triggers the retreat analysis; it does not waive it. Option (e) is incorrect because a concealed carry permit does not eliminate the doctrinal duty to retreat in a retreat jurisdiction.
