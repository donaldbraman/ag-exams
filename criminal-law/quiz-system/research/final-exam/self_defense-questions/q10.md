**Q10.** Following the shooting, Alex realizes Blake is bleeding heavily but flees without calling emergency services. Can Alex's failure to call 911 satisfy the actus reus for criminal liability?

(a) Yes, because by shooting Blake, Alex created the peril and therefore assumed a legal duty to render or summon reasonable medical assistance. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because all citizens have a generalized legal duty to contact emergency services when they witness a life-threatening injury occurring in their immediate presence.
(c) No, because a fatal omission can only satisfy the actus reus requirement if there is a formal, recognized contractual relationship between the two parties.
(d) No, because requiring Alex to call 911 would fundamentally violate his constitutional privilege against self-incrimination by forcing him to implicitly admit criminal guilt.
(e) No, because Alex's initial affirmative act of shooting Blake supersedes any subsequent liability for failing to seek appropriate medical attention for the dying victim.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Criminal liability for an omission requires a legal duty to act. One of the well-established common law categories that creates such a duty is the creation of peril: a person who places another in danger (even accidentally or in self-defense) has a legal duty to take reasonable steps to safeguard them. By shooting Blake (Fact 7), Alex created the peril and breached his legal duty by fleeing without calling 911 (Fact 8). Option (b) is incorrect because the law generally imposes no baseline duty to rescue strangers (the "no-duty baseline"). Option (c) is incorrect because contractual relationships are just one of several categories (like creation of peril or status relationships) that generate a duty to act. Option (d) is incorrect because courts have consistently held that duties to report or render aid do not violate the Fifth Amendment. Option (e) is incorrect because creation of peril specifically establishes an independent basis for liability stemming from the subsequent omission.
