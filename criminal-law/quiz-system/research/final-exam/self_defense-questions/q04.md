**Q4.** Assume that, whether or not Alex was the initial aggressor, the court is evaluating the reasonableness of Alex's belief that deadly force was necessary. Under the standard established in *People v. Goetz*, how will the court assess his belief?

(a) The jury must determine whether a reasonable person possessing Alex's specific knowledge of Blake's prior threats would have believed deadly force was necessary. <!-- correct -->
(b) The jury must determine solely whether Alex personally and genuinely believed that Blake was about to draw a weapon and kill him.
(c) The jury must determine whether an idealized, objective reasonable person with no prior history between the parties would have used deadly force.
(d) The jury must exclude evidence of Blake's prior threats because reasonableness must be evaluated based exclusively on the immediate physical circumstances.
(e) The jury must find the belief unreasonable as a matter of law because Blake's pocket did not actually contain a deadly weapon.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** In *People v. Goetz*, the court held that the objective standard of reasonableness must incorporate the defendant's specific situation, including their prior experiences, physical condition, and knowledge of the attacker. Therefore, Alex's knowledge that Blake had a permit and had threatened him with a gun the previous month (Fact 6) must be considered when determining what a reasonable person would believe in that moment. Option (b) describes a purely subjective standard, which *Goetz* explicitly rejected. Option (c) is incorrect because the reasonable person standard is not completely stripped of the defendant's situational context. Option (d) is incorrect because prior experiences and specific knowledge of the attacker are legitimately incorporated into the reasonableness inquiry. Option (e) is incorrect because self-defense requires only a reasonable belief in imminent harm, even if that belief turns out to be factually mistaken.
