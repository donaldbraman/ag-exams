**Q7.** Assume the trial occurs in a Stand Your Ground jurisdiction mirroring Florida law. Can Alex successfully claim Stand Your Ground pretrial immunity?

(a) Yes, because Stand Your Ground immunity eliminates the duty to retreat regardless of the specific physical location where the violent confrontation occurs.
(b) Yes, because the prosecution cannot definitively prove by clear and convincing evidence that Alex lacked a subjective fear for his own life.
(c) No, because Stand Your Ground immunity does not apply when the defendant is actively trespassing in a place he has no legal right to be. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because Stand Your Ground immunity is strictly limited to defendants defending against an unlawful intrusion into their own residence or vehicle.
(e) No, because pretrial immunity requires the defendant to affirmatively prove beyond a reasonable doubt that the victim acted as the initial aggressor.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** Under Florida's Stand Your Ground statute, the elimination of the duty to retreat applies only to a person who is "not engaged in an unlawful activity and who is attacked in any other place where he or she has a right to be." Because Alex was actively trespassing past a visible sign on Blake's private property (Fact 4), he had no legal right to be there, rendering him ineligible for SYG statutory immunity. Option (a) is incorrect because SYG explicitly conditions the no-retreat rule on the defendant being in a place they have a right to be. Option (b) is incorrect because before reaching the burden of disproving fear, the threshold locational and lawful activity requirements must be met. Option (d) is incorrect because Florida's SYG law extends broadly beyond the home to any place a person has a right to be. Option (e) is incorrect because the burden of proof at an SYG hearing post-2017 rests on the prosecution, not the defendant.
