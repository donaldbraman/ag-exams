**Q14.** The District Attorney declines to charge Chris, citing limited resources and stating he "isn't worth the paperwork." If a citizen challenges this decision, how will a court likely respond?

(a) The court will mandate charges because prosecutors have a mandatory constitutional duty to bring formal charges whenever sufficient probable cause exists for a felony.
(b) The court will mandate charges because the DA's stated rationale of limited resources violates the constitutional guarantee of equal protection under the law.
(c) The court will uphold the declination because prosecutorial charging decisions are generally shielded from judicial review absent a suspect classification or vindictive motive. <!-- correct -->
(d) The court will uphold the declination because Chris's early withdrawal from the conspiracy granted him absolute statutory immunity from all subsequent criminal prosecution.
(e) The court will uphold the declination because the separation of powers doctrine explicitly forbids the legislative branch from broadly defining criminal offenses and penalties.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** Prosecutors enjoy extraordinarily broad discretion in deciding whether to bring charges. Under the selective prosecution doctrine (e.g., *Wayte v. United States*), a court will not review or mandate a prosecutor's declination decision unless a challenger can show it was based on an unjustifiable, unconstitutional standard such as race, religion, or vindictiveness. Resource allocation ("not worth the paperwork") is a standard, judicially protected rationale for declination. Option (a) is incorrect because there is no constitutional duty to charge every provable crime. Option (b) is incorrect because allocating limited resources based on the perceived severity of involvement does not violate equal protection. Option (d) is incorrect because while withdrawal shields Chris from subsequent *Pinkerton* liability, he could still be charged with the initial conspiracy; his lack of prosecution is due to prosecutorial discretion, not absolute statutory immunity. Option (e) is incorrect because the legislature does hold the power to define offenses, but it is the executive branch's prerogative to enforce them.
