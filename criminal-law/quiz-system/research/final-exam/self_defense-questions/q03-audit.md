**Q3.** Assume that Blake possessed the drugs. The prosecution charges Alex and Blake with conspiracy to distribute fentanyl. They argue there is no evidence they ever explicitly agreed to distribute drugs together. Is the evidence sufficient to establish a conspiracy agreement?

(a) Yes, because their ongoing weekly operations over two years allow a jury to infer a mutual agreement to distribute the drugs. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because a valid conspiracy charge requires proof of an express verbal or written agreement between the parties to commit a crime.
(c) Yes, because the mere fact that Alex supplied Blake with fentanyl creates an irrebuttable presumption of an agreement to distribute.
(d) No, because the unilateral actions of Alex in dropping off the safe cannot legally bind Blake to a bilateral conspiracy agreement.
(e) Yes, because strict liability applies to any ongoing narcotics enterprise involving more than one participant under the federal criminal code.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** The agreement element of a conspiracy rarely relies on an explicit or written contract. A jury may infer a mutual agreement from a continuous, coordinated course of conduct between the parties. Operating a drug ring weekly for two years provides overwhelming circumstantial evidence of an agreement. (b) is wrong because express agreements are not required; tacit understandings inferred from actions suffice. (c) is wrong because criminal law does not permit irrebuttable presumptions of mens rea elements against a defendant. (d) is wrong factually and doctrinally: Blake actively housed the safe and operated the ring, satisfying the bilateral agreement requirement. (e) is wrong because conspiracy is a specific intent offense, not a strict liability crime.
