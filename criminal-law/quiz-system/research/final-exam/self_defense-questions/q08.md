**Q8.** Assume Alex is charged with intentional murder and seeks to mitigate the charge to voluntary manslaughter under the common law doctrine of provocation. Will this defense likely succeed?

(a) Yes, because Blake's threat to ruin his life combined with reaching into his jacket constituted an adequate provocation under the traditional common law.
(b) Yes, because Alex was genuinely enraged by the sudden insult, fully satisfying the subjective requirement for acting in the sudden heat of passion.
(c) No, because under strict common law categories, mere words and threatening gestures without an actual physical assault do not constitute legally adequate provocation. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because Alex's response of shooting Blake was grossly disproportionate to the spoken insult, fundamentally negating the necessary adequate provocation element at trial.
(e) No, because Alex had sufficient time to rationally cool off between hearing the spoken insult and making the deliberate decision to pull the trigger.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** Under strict common law provocation, a defendant's passion must be triggered by legally adequate provocation, which was limited to rigid categories (extreme assault/battery, mutual combat, illegal arrest, injury to a relative, or sudden discovery of spousal adultery). The common law categorically held that words alone, no matter how insulting or threatening, could never constitute adequate provocation. Therefore, Blake's verbal threat and gesture (Facts 5 and 6) fail the categorical test. Option (a) is incorrect because it ignores the strict categorical limits of the common law. Option (b) is incorrect because genuine rage alone is insufficient without legally adequate objective provocation. Option (d) is incorrect because while disproportionate response is a factor, the defense fails at the threshold because mere words are categorically inadequate. Option (e) is incorrect factually, as Alex fired "immediately" after the insult, meaning there was no cooling-off period.
