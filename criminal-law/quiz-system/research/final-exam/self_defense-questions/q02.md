**Q2.** Assume that, whether or not Chris is guilty of conspiracy, he is charged with Blake's shooting under the *Pinkerton* doctrine. Is Chris liable for the shooting?

(a) Liable, because the shooting was a reasonably foreseeable consequence of bringing a weapon to a confrontation.
(b) Liable, because he failed to notify law enforcement or actively prevent Alex from continuing the planned assault.
(c) Not liable, because he effectively withdrew from the conspiracy by communicating his departure to his co-conspirator. <!-- correct -->
(d) Not liable, because the conspiracy was strictly limited to an assault with a bat rather than a shooting.
(e) Not liable, because he lacked the specific intent to kill Blake required for liability as an accomplice.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** Under the *Pinkerton* doctrine, an individual can be held vicariously liable for the foreseeable substantive crimes committed by co-conspirators in furtherance of the conspiracy. However, a defendant can escape liability for subsequent crimes if he effectively withdraws from the conspiracy by taking affirmative action communicated to his co-conspirators. Chris expressly told Alex "I'm out" and left (Fact 3), successfully withdrawing before the shooting occurred. Thus, (c) is correct. Option (a) is incorrect because withdrawal cuts off *Pinkerton* liability regardless of foreseeability. Option (b) is incorrect because common law withdrawal generally requires clear communication to co-conspirators, not necessarily notifying law enforcement or preventing the crime. Option (d) is incorrect because *Pinkerton* covers foreseeable deviations, but withdrawal serves as a complete bar. Option (e) is incorrect because *Pinkerton* imposes vicarious liability without requiring the specific intent needed for accomplice liability.
