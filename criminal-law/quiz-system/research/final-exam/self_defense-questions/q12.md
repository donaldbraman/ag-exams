**Q12.** The District Attorney charges Alex with felony murder predicated on the initial aggravated assault with the baseball bat. How does the merger doctrine affect this charge?

(a) The charge is valid because an assault with a deadly weapon is an enumerated felony that automatically elevates the crime to first-degree felony murder.
(b) The charge is valid because the assault was completed before the shooting occurred, fully satisfying the requirement of an independent felonious purpose.
(c) The charge is barred because the assault was inextricably linked to the resulting death and therefore merges into the underlying intentional homicide charge. <!-- correct -->
(d) The charge is barred because the underlying felony of assault is considered a malum prohibitum offense rather than a malum in se offense.
(e) The charge is barred because the prosecution failed to prove that Alex acted with extreme indifference to the value of human life during the assault.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** The merger doctrine (established in cases like *Ireland*) dictates that an underlying felony cannot serve as the predicate for felony murder if it is an assaultive act that is an integral part of the homicide itself. Without this limit, nearly every intentional killing (which involves an assault) would be bootstrapped into felony murder, stripping the jury of the need to find malice aforethought. The assault with the bat was part of the single continuous confrontation leading to death (Facts 4-7) and thus merges. Option (a) is incorrect because assault is exactly the type of crime the merger doctrine is designed to block as a predicate. Option (b) is incorrect because stepping on the lawn with the bat and the subsequent shooting were part of the same continuous violent transaction, lacking an independent collateral purpose (like robbery). Option (d) is incorrect because assault is malum in se, not malum prohibitum. Option (e) is incorrect because extreme indifference relates to depraved heart murder, not the structural limits of felony murder predicates.
