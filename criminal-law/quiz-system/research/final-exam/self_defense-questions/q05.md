**Q5.** Assume the trial occurs in a jurisdiction that recognizes imperfect self-defense. If the jury concludes that Alex genuinely but unreasonably believed Blake was drawing a gun, what is the effect on Alex's liability?

(a) He will be fully acquitted of all homicide charges because a genuine belief in the need for self-defense negates the core actus reus.
(b) He will be convicted of voluntary manslaughter because his genuine but unreasonable belief negates the malice element required for a murder conviction. <!-- correct -->
(c) He will be convicted of involuntary manslaughter because an unreasonable belief in self-defense constitutes gross criminal negligence rather than a purposeful killing.
(d) He will be convicted of intentional murder because an unreasonable belief provides no mitigating defense to a purposeful and intentional use of deadly force.
(e) He will be convicted of second-degree murder because his initial trespass completely invalidates any mitigating benefit derived from his genuine belief in danger.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** In jurisdictions that recognize imperfect self-defense (such as California), a defendant who honestly but unreasonably believes deadly force is necessary is entitled to a partial defense. This genuine belief negates the malice aforethought required for murder, reducing the charge from murder to voluntary manslaughter. Therefore, (b) is correct. Option (a) is incorrect because imperfect self-defense is a partial defense that mitigates the charge, not a complete justification that results in acquittal. Option (c) is incorrect because the resulting mitigated charge is voluntary manslaughter, recognizing the intentional nature of the act minus malice, not involuntary manslaughter. Option (d) is incorrect because it describes the rule in all-or-nothing jurisdictions that do not recognize imperfect self-defense. Option (e) is incorrect because an initial trespass does not automatically negate the subjective genuineness inquiry that underpins imperfect self-defense mitigation.
