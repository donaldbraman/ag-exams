## Issue 1 — audit

**Q9.** Regarding the 45-minute delay while Blake was bleeding on the lawn, did Alex have a legal duty to call 911 under these circumstances?

(a) Alex had a duty to call 911, because his affirmative act of shooting Blake created the physical peril that necessitated emergency medical intervention. <!-- correct -->
(b) Alex had a duty to call 911, because all individuals physically present at the scene of a life-threatening emergency are statutorily required to assist.
(c) Alex had no legal duty to call 911, because he was on Blake's private property and therefore owed no special relationship to his neighbor.
(d) Alex had no legal duty to call 911, because successfully claiming self-defense legally extinguishes any subsequent obligations toward the original physical aggressor.
(e) Alex had no legal duty to call 911, because the criminal law never imposes liability for a failure to act unless specified by contract.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** In criminal law, a legal duty to act (omission liability) arises in five specific situations, one of which is the "creation of peril." When a person's affirmative act creates a risk of harm or actual peril to another person, the actor has a legal duty to take reasonable steps to render or summon assistance. Because Alex shot Blake (Fact 7), he created the peril and therefore owed a legal duty to call for medical help. Option (b) describes a general "Good Samaritan" duty, which does not exist in the vast majority of U.S. criminal jurisdictions. Option (c) fails because "special relationships" (like parent-child) are a separate category; the duty here arises from creating the peril, not the relationship. Option (d) is legally false; even if a use of force is legally justified in self-defense, the actor still has a duty to summon aid once the threat is neutralized. Option (e) ignores the other four categories of legal duties (statute, status, assumption, creation of peril).
