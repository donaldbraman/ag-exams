**Q14.** Assume the jurisdiction applies the traditional Pinkerton doctrine. Is Felix liable for Ian's stash house robbery?

(a) Yes, Felix is liable under Pinkerton. The jury could reasonably find that raiding the rival's stash house was a foreseeable act in furtherance of the overarching turf-defense conspiracy. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, Felix is liable under Pinkerton. Crew chiefs are strictly vicariously liable for all crimes committed by their members, regardless of whether the acts were foreseeable or authorized.
(c) No, Felix is not liable under Pinkerton. He was asleep in his apartment during the robbery, which completely negates the personal actus reus required for vicarious liability.
(d) No, Felix is not liable under Pinkerton. Ian proposed the robbery directly to Helen, which automatically creates a completely separate wheel conspiracy that structurally excludes Felix.
(e) Yes, Felix is liable under Pinkerton. He paid Ian $500 the previous day, which serves as an open-ended down payment for any future robberies Ian might independently commit.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** The traditional *Pinkerton* doctrine holds conspirators vicariously liable for the foreseeable substantive crimes of their co-conspirators committed in furtherance of the agreement. A jury could reasonably conclude that a stash house raid furthers the "defend turf by any means necessary" conspiracy. (b) is wrong because *Pinkerton* is not strict liability; it requires the acts to be foreseeable and in furtherance of the conspiracy. (c) is wrong because vicarious liability specifically bypasses the need for the defendant to be awake or physically participate. (d) is wrong because the raid was proposed by a member of the existing conspiracy, making it a continuation rather than a separate wheel. (e) is wrong because paying a member to defend turf does not serve as a blank check for unrelated future crimes unless they meet the furtherance test.
