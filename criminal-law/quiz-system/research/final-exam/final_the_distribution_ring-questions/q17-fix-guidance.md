## Issue 1 — grounding

**Q17.** Compare Helen's liability for the guard's death under the traditional natural and probable consequences (NPC) doctrine versus modern NPC reform.

(a) Under both approaches, Helen is fully liable for murder. Any resulting death that occurs during a dangerous felony automatically transfers malice to all participating lookouts.
(b) Under both approaches, Helen is not liable for murder. She did not personally pull the trigger or provide the murder weapon, which completely shields her from homicide liability.
(c) Under traditional NPC, she is likely liable based on the foreseeability of violence. Under modern reform, she likely escapes liability because she lacked reckless indifference to human life. <!-- correct -->
(d) Under traditional NPC, she escapes liability because she lacked specific intent to kill. Under modern reform, she is held strictly liable for acting as a lookout during a felony.
(e) Under traditional NPC, she is liable for misdemeanor manslaughter only. Modern reform elevates her legal liability to first-degree murder based on her use of a cell phone.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** Traditional NPC doctrine holds an accomplice liable for any reasonably foreseeable crime committed by the principal, making Helen likely liable for the foreseeable shooting. Modern reforms like SB 1437 require reckless indifference to human life, which Helen, an unarmed lookout who expected no guns, likely lacks. (a) is wrong because modern reforms explicitly abolished the automatic transfer of malice. (b) is wrong because traditional NPC imposes liability precisely on those who do not pull the trigger. (d) is wrong because traditional NPC does not require specific intent for the escalated crime, and modern reform abolished strict liability. (e) is wrong because traditional NPC can elevate liability to murder, while modern reform would likely reduce or eliminate her homicide liability entirely.
