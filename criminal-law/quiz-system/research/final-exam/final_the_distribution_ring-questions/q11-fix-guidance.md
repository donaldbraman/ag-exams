**Q11.** Assume the jurisdiction has adopted modern NPC reform (like California's SB 1437). Is Helen liable for the guard's murder?

(a) Yes, she is liable for the murder. Modern reform statutes maintain strict liability for all lookouts who participate in drug-related offenses that ultimately result in a death.
(b) Yes, she is liable for the murder. Her deliberate act of texting Ian demonstrates the personal malice aforethought uniquely required by modern statutory homicide reforms.
(c) No, she is not liable for the murder. As an unarmed lookout who thought no guns were involved, she likely lacked the required reckless indifference to human life. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, she is not liable for the murder. Modern reforms completely abolish all forms of accomplice liability for any underlying crime that results in an unintended casualty.
(e) Yes, she is liable for the murder. She failed to physically stop Ian from entering the building, which fully satisfies the major participant requirement of the reform.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** Modern NPC reforms like California's SB 1437 require that an accomplice act with personal malice or be a "major participant" acting with "reckless indifference to human life." As an unarmed lookout who believed the raid involved only a bat, Helen lacks the requisite reckless indifference. (a) is wrong because SB 1437 specifically abolished strict liability for felony murder and NPC murder. (b) is wrong because texting does not inherently demonstrate malice aforethought. (d) is wrong because modern reforms do not abolish all accomplice liability; they merely require major participation and reckless indifference. (e) is wrong because acting as a peripheral lookout does not automatically satisfy the demanding "major participant" standard.
