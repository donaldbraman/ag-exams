**Q8.** Assume Ian's self-defense claim fails. The prosecution charges Ian with first-degree felony murder.

(a) Guilty, because Ian caused the guard's death during the commission of a predicate felony, regardless of whether he intended to kill. <!-- correct -->
(b) Guilty, because Ian possessed the specific intent to kill the guard when he intentionally drew the concealed handgun and fired it.
(c) Not guilty, because the guard drew his weapon first, breaking the direct causal chain required for standard felony murder liability.
(d) Not guilty, because Ian's original plan was only to smash equipment and grab cash, not to commit a homicide during the raid.
(e) Not guilty, because the merger doctrine prevents a burglary that results in a death from serving as a valid felony murder predicate.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under the felony murder rule, a defendant is strictly liable for a death caused during the commission or attempted commission of a predicate felony (such as robbery or burglary). Ian caused the guard's death during the stash house raid. (b) is wrong because felony murder requires no proof of specific intent to kill. (c) is wrong because a victim's armed resistance is a highly foreseeable response that does not break the causal chain. (d) is wrong because the original plan not to kill is irrelevant; felony murder holds defendants liable for unintended deaths. (e) is wrong because the merger doctrine applies to assaultive felonies, not independent felonies like robbery or burglary.
