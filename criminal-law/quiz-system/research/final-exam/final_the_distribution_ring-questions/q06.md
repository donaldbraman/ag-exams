**Q6.** Assume Trey is also charged with the murder of Dexter. How would Trey's liability for the homicide be assessed under the traditional natural and probable consequences (NPC) doctrine compared to California's SB 1437 reform?

(a) Under both approaches, Trey is strictly liable for murder because the killing occurred during the commission of a felony in which he intentionally participated, which automatically satisfies the malice aforethought requirement for homicide.
(b) Under traditional NPC, Trey is liable if the murder was a foreseeable result of the robbery, but under SB 1437, he must have acted with personal malice or been a major participant acting with reckless indifference. <!-- correct -->
(c) Under traditional NPC, Trey must have shared Silas's specific intent to kill the guard, whereas under SB 1437, his mere participation in the underlying stash house robbery is sufficient to establish imputed malice.
(d) Under traditional NPC, Trey is guilty because his act of driving the getaway car was the but-for cause of the murder, but under SB 1437, he must have explicitly agreed to the killing beforehand.
(e) Under both approaches, Trey cannot be held liable for murder because he was sitting outside in the getaway car during the shooting and did not personally pull the trigger or direct the enforcer's actions.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** The correct answer is (b). Under traditional NPC, Trey would be liable for murder if the killing was a foreseeable result of the robbery. California's SB 1437 reform abolished NPC murder liability, requiring either personal malice or that the defendant was a major participant acting with reckless indifference to human life. (a) is wrong because SB 1437 eliminated strict imputed liability for felony murder/NPC. (c) is wrong because traditional NPC relies on foreseeability, not shared specific intent, while SB 1437 does not impute malice. (d) is wrong because but-for causation is not the NPC test, and SB 1437 does not require explicit prior agreement. (e) is wrong because getaway drivers can still face liability under either regime depending on foreseeability or their level of participation.
