**Q7.** Ian is charged with homicide for killing the guard. Can he successfully claim self-defense?

(a) Yes, he can successfully claim self-defense. The guard drew a weapon first, which gave Ian the immediate right to use deadly force to protect his own life.
(b) Yes, he can successfully claim self-defense. Dropping the baseball bat before drawing his gun served as a legally sufficient withdrawal from the initial violent conflict.
(c) No, he cannot successfully claim self-defense. Ian was the initial aggressor in the robbery and he did not successfully withdraw and communicate that withdrawal to the guard. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, he cannot successfully claim self-defense. Self-defense is categorically unavailable to any person who is unlawfully present in a building, regardless of the immediate threat faced.
(e) Yes, he can successfully claim self-defense. Because he only brought a bat to smash equipment, he lacked the initial malice required to completely forfeit his self-defense claim.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** An initial aggressor who provokes a violent conflict forfeits the right to claim self-defense unless they successfully withdraw and communicate that withdrawal to the other party. Ian dropping the bat was not communicated as a withdrawal before he drew his gun. (a) is wrong because the initial aggressor cannot claim self-defense when the victim responds with lawful force. (b) is wrong because dropping a weapon without communicating withdrawal does not restore self-defense rights. (d) is wrong because an unlawful trespasser can theoretically regain self-defense if the owner escalates to deadly force without justification, though Ian's armed robbery precludes this. (e) is wrong because armed robbery is an inherently violent felony that establishes initial aggressor status.
