**Q8.** Assume that, independent of the prosecution's theory of malice, Silas asserts self-defense to the homicide charge. He argues he reasonably feared for his life when Dexter lunged at him with a machete. Will Silas's self-defense claim succeed?

(a) Yes, because a machete constitutes an imminent deadly threat, and Silas was entitled to stand his ground and meet deadly force with deadly force to protect his own life.
(b) Yes, but only under the doctrine of imperfect self-defense, which will reduce the murder charge to manslaughter because his belief in the absolute need for deadly force was objectively unreasonable.
(c) No, because Silas was the initial aggressor by violently breaking into the stash house, which categorically bars him from claiming perfect self-defense unless he successfully withdrew from the encounter. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because Dexter, as a stash house guard, was acting under a recognized public authority justification to protect the rival crew's property and inventory from an armed intrusion and theft.
(e) Yes, because the castle doctrine applies to Silas once he crossed the physical threshold of the stash house, relieving him of any common law duty to retreat from the machete attack.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** The correct answer is (c). Silas was the initial aggressor who violently broke into the stash house to commit a robbery. The initial aggressor rule categorically bars him from claiming self-defense against the victim's lawful defensive force unless Silas clearly communicated his withdrawal from the encounter, which he did not do. (a) is wrong because the initial aggressor bar overrides his right to stand his ground. (b) is wrong because imperfect self-defense is unavailable to a defendant whose initial aggression created the need for force. (d) is wrong because Dexter is using self-defense or defense of property against a home invasion, not public authority. (e) is wrong because the castle doctrine applies to lawful occupants, not armed intruders.
