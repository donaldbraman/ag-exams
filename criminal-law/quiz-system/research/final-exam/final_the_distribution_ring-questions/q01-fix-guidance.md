## Issue 1 — grounding

**Q1.** Evaluate Felix's liability for possession of fentanyl.

(a) Felix is guilty of constructive possession. He maintained exclusive control over the key to the vault containing the drugs, which establishes his power and intent to control them. <!-- correct -->
(b) Felix is not guilty of constructive possession. The fentanyl was located in a communal courtyard rather than his personal residence, precluding any finding of exclusive dominion.
(c) Felix is guilty of actual possession. He was the acknowledged leader of the crew that operated the turf where the drugs were physically stored in the vault.
(d) Felix is not guilty of any possession. The facts do not show he ever physically handled the glassine envelopes or the scale during the period of the investigation.
(e) Felix is guilty of constructive possession. Mere knowledge that illicit drugs are present in a geographic area automatically establishes legal possession under modern statutes.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Felix is guilty of constructive possession because he had exclusive control of the vault key, satisfying the requirement of power and intent to exercise dominion over the drugs. (b) is wrong because constructive possession does not require the drugs to be in a personal residence. (c) is wrong because actual possession requires physical handling, which is absent here. (d) is wrong because constructive possession explicitly covers situations where the defendant does not physically handle the items. (e) is wrong because mere knowledge of presence is insufficient; the prosecution must prove power and intent to control.

**Grounding:** Chapter 15 - Constructive Possession Doctrine
