## Issue 1 — grounding

**Q12.** After hearing the gunshot, Helen ran away yelling, "I'm completely out of this!" Does this constitute a valid withdrawal defense to her ongoing liability?

(a) Yes, this constitutes a valid withdrawal. Her physical flight from the dangerous scene effectively severed her legal connection to the ongoing criminal enterprise before the police arrived.
(b) Yes, this constitutes a valid withdrawal. Her verbal declaration clearly established her subjective intent to abandon the conspiracy and avoid participating in any further violent crimes.
(c) No, this does not constitute a valid withdrawal. A valid withdrawal must be communicated to her accomplices and occur in time to prevent the actual commission of the crime. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, this does not constitute a valid withdrawal. The withdrawal defense is categorically unavailable under all modern statutes for any crime that involves the distribution of narcotics.
(e) Yes, this constitutes a valid withdrawal. Running away in a panic demonstrates a complete and voluntary renunciation of her prior criminal purpose under the Model Penal Code.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** To successfully withdraw from a conspiracy or accomplice liability, a defendant must take affirmative steps to disavow the crime and communicate that withdrawal to co-conspirators in time to prevent the offense, or notify law enforcement. Yelling to herself fails the communication requirement. (a) is wrong because physical flight alone is insufficient without communicating the withdrawal to the principal. (b) is wrong because a subjective intent to abandon is legally meaningless if it is not communicated. (d) is wrong because the withdrawal defense is generally available for all inchoate crimes if the strict requirements are met. (e) is wrong because the MPC also requires that the withdrawal actually prevent the commission of the crime or be communicated to police.
