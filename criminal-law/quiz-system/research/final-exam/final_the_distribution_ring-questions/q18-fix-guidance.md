**Q18.** Evaluate Felix's liability for Ian's stash house robbery under both the traditional Pinkerton doctrine and the MPC's complicity framework.

(a) Both frameworks hold Felix liable for the robbery. His structural role as a crew chief makes him vicariously responsible for all independent actions taken by his subordinates.
(b) Both frameworks acquit Felix of the robbery. He was asleep during the stash house raid and did not physically participate in the break-in or share in the proceeds.
(c) Pinkerton holds Felix liable if the robbery was a foreseeable consequence of the conspiracy. The MPC acquits him because he did not purposefully aid or encourage this specific crime. <!-- correct -->
(d) Pinkerton acquits Felix because he lacked specific intent for the stash house raid. The MPC holds him strictly liable based entirely on his initial agreement to defend the turf.
(e) Pinkerton holds Felix liable for attempted robbery only. The MPC elevates his vicarious liability to the completed substantive offense because he provided the initial funding.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** The *Pinkerton* doctrine relies on vicarious liability, meaning Felix is liable if the robbery was foreseeable and in furtherance of the conspiracy. The MPC rejects vicarious liability and requires the prosecution to prove Felix actually aided or encouraged the specific robbery with purposeful intent, which he did not. (a) is wrong because the MPC strictly rejects vicarious liability for co-conspirators. (b) is wrong because *Pinkerton* imposes liability even if Felix was asleep and not physically present. (d) is wrong because the MPC does not hold him strictly liable based on his initial agreement; it requires accomplice mens rea for the specific crime. (e) is wrong because *Pinkerton* holds him liable for the completed substantive offense, not merely the attempt.
