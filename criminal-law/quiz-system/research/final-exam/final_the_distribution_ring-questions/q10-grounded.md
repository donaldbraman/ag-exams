**Q10.** During the search of Marcus's apartment, police found a key on his nightstand that unlocked a safe in the building's communal basement. Marcus argues he cannot be convicted of possessing the drugs inside because the safe was in a shared, accessible area. Will his argument succeed?

(a) Yes, because constructive possession requires the defendant to have exclusive physical control over the physical premises where the illicit contraband is ultimately found by law enforcement officers during a search.
(b) Yes, because the physical distance between Marcus's upstairs apartment and the communal basement negates the requirement that the drugs be within his immediate physical wingspan or grasp at the time of arrest.
(c) No, because the key found on his nightstand establishes his awareness of the drugs and his power to exercise control over them, satisfying constructive possession even in a non-exclusive shared space. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because under the traditional common law, any items found within the same residential building as a defendant's apartment are automatically imputed to their actual physical possession regardless of locked containers.
(e) Yes, because the drugs turned out to be baking soda rather than cocaine, which legally precludes any finding of dominion or control over an actual controlled substance under constructive possession doctrine.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** The correct answer is (c). Constructive possession requires awareness of the item and the power and intent to exercise control over it. Having the sole key to the safe establishes this control, even though the safe is in a communal area. (a) is wrong because control over the contraband must be exclusive (or shared by joint possessors), but the premises need not be exclusive if the defendant retains control over the specific container. (b) is wrong because constructive possession does not require immediate physical reach or wingspan. (d) is wrong because common law requires specific proof of dominion and control, not automatic geographic imputation. (e) is wrong because control is a physical reality regarding the object, independent of its chemical composition.
