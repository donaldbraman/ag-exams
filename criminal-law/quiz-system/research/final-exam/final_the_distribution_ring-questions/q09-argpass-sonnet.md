**Q9.** The prosecution seeks to hold Marcus directly liable for Silas's murder of Dexter. Which of the following accurately describes Marcus's potential liability under accomplice liability and Pinkerton conspiracy theories?

(a) Marcus is liable under Pinkerton because the homicide was a foreseeable act furthering the conspiracy, but not under accomplice liability because handing over the weapon was not the direct but-for cause.
(b) Marcus is liable under both theories: under Pinkerton because the shooting furthered the conspiracy, and as an accomplice because handing Silas the gun with instructions easily satisfies the actus reus threshold. <!-- correct -->
(c) Marcus is not liable under either theory because he was not physically present at the stash house when the shooting occurred and did not personally pull the trigger to kill Dexter.
(d) Marcus is liable as an accomplice because he explicitly commanded the killing, but he cannot be liable under Pinkerton because Silas acted independently to defend himself against a sudden machete attack.
(e) Marcus is liable under Pinkerton only if the jury concludes that Silas's specific use of the gun in self-defense was explicitly discussed and authorized at the group's weekly conspiracy meetings.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** The correct answer is (b). Marcus is liable under Pinkerton because the homicide was foreseeable and in furtherance of the ongoing conspiracy. He is also liable as an accomplice because handing Silas the gun with instructions easily satisfies the low actus reus threshold for aiding and abetting. (a) is wrong because accomplice actus reus does not require but-for causation, only facilitation. (c) is wrong because physical presence is not required for either accomplice or Pinkerton liability. (d) is wrong because Silas's claim of self-defense fails (he was the initial aggressor), so the crime remains intact for Pinkerton purposes. (e) is wrong because Pinkerton requires only that the act be foreseeable, not explicitly authorized in advance.
