**Q15.** Assume the jurisdiction follows the Model Penal Code. Is Felix liable for Ian's stash house robbery?

(a) Yes, Felix is liable under the MPC. The Model Penal Code expressly retains vicarious liability for all foreseeable crimes committed by co-conspirators engaged in major drug enterprises.
(b) Yes, Felix is liable under the MPC. His previous instruction to George demonstrates a generalized intent to promote any violence against the rival crew members at any time.
(c) No, Felix is not liable under the MPC. The MPC rejects Pinkerton, and Felix did not actually aid, abet, or encourage this specific robbery with the purpose to promote it. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, Felix is not liable under the MPC. The Model Penal Code requires the prosecution to definitively prove that Felix physically participated in the actus reus of the robbery itself.
(e) Yes, Felix is liable under the MPC. Under the MPC's unilateral approach, Felix's initial agreement automatically binds him to all subsequent illegal acts of his co-conspirators.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** The Model Penal Code explicitly rejects the *Pinkerton* doctrine. Under the MPC, a conspirator is only liable for a co-conspirator's substantive offense if they satisfy the traditional elements of accomplice liability—meaning they must actually aid or encourage the specific crime with the purpose to promote it. (a) is wrong because the MPC completely abolishes vicarious liability based solely on conspiracy membership. (b) is wrong because generalized intent is insufficient; the MPC requires purpose as to the specific target offense. (d) is wrong because physical participation in the actus reus is not required; providing aid or encouragement from afar would suffice if the intent were present. (e) is wrong because the unilateral approach applies to forming the conspiracy, not to imputing liability for subsequent substantive crimes.
