**Q11.** Lab tests revealed the 500 grams of powder in the safe was baking soda, though Marcus's text messages confirmed he purchased it believing it was pure cocaine. Assume Marcus is charged with attempted possession of a controlled substance with intent to distribute. How does impossibility doctrine apply to his defense?

(a) Under the traditional common law, his defense fails because factual impossibility is no defense, and under the MPC, the defense also fails because liability turns on his subjective beliefs about the circumstances. <!-- correct -->
(b) Under the traditional common law, his defense succeeds because this is pure legal impossibility, as baking soda is not a regulated substance under any state or federal criminal narcotics statute.
(c) Under the MPC, his defense succeeds because the code requires that the defendant's acts come dangerously near to a completed crime, which cannot physically happen when distributing inert baking soda.
(d) Under both approaches, his defense succeeds because the government cannot prove the core actus reus element of actually possessing a controlled substance, rendering the completion of the crime factually impossible.
(e) Under the traditional common law, his defense fails, but under the MPC, he has an affirmative defense of impossibility because no harm to the public could ever result from distributing baking soda.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** The correct answer is (a). At common law, factual impossibility (the powder wasn't drugs) is not a defense to attempt. Under MPC § 5.01(1)(a), impossibility is also irrelevant because liability turns on whether the conduct would constitute a crime if the circumstances were as the defendant believed them to be. (b) is wrong because pure legal impossibility involves conduct that wouldn't be a crime even if the facts were as believed; possessing cocaine is a crime. (c) is wrong because "dangerously near" is the common law proximity test, not the MPC test. (d) is wrong because attempt actus reus does not require completing the core element. (e) is wrong because the MPC abolished the impossibility defense and does not grant one based on lack of public harm.
