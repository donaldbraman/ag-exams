## Issue 1 — grounding

**Q16.** Evaluate Felix's liability for possession of fentanyl with intent to distribute.

(a) Felix is guilty of the distribution offense. His constructive possession of bulk fentanyl, a digital scale, and hundreds of empty envelopes strongly implies an intent to distribute. <!-- correct -->
(b) Felix is not guilty of the distribution offense. Proving intent to distribute requires direct physical evidence of a completed sale to an undercover officer or confidential informant.
(c) Felix is guilty of the distribution offense. Any possession of a controlled substance inside a locked vault automatically triggers an irrebuttable presumption of intent to distribute.
(d) Felix is not guilty of the distribution offense. The drugs were kept in a central courtyard, which demonstrates a benign intent to hoard the substance for personal medical use.
(e) Felix is guilty of the distribution offense. He paid his crew members in cash, which is the only legally sufficient method to prove a sophisticated drug distribution conspiracy.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Possession with intent to distribute can be proven circumstantially. Felix's constructive possession of bulk fentanyl, combined with distribution paraphernalia like a digital scale and hundreds of empty envelopes, overwhelmingly supports the inference of intent to distribute. (b) is wrong because circumstantial evidence of paraphernalia and bulk quantity is legally sufficient; direct evidence of a sale is not required. (c) is wrong because there is no automatic irrebuttable presumption; the jury must infer intent from the totality of the circumstances. (d) is wrong because hoarding for personal use is contradicted by the presence of a scale and hundreds of empty packaging envelopes. (e) is wrong because while paying in cash is suspicious, it is not the only legally sufficient method to prove a distribution scheme.

**Grounding:** Chapter 15 - Proving Intent to Distribute via Circumstantial Evidence
