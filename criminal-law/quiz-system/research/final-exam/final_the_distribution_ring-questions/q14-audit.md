**Q14.** The prosecution charges Felix with the stash house robbery under the Pinkerton doctrine.

(a) Guilty, because raiding the rival stash house was a foreseeable crime committed by Ian in furtherance of the ongoing turf defense conspiracy. <!-- correct -->
(b) Guilty, because Felix's status as a crew chief makes him strictly liable for all criminal acts committed by his organizational subordinates.
(c) Not guilty, because Felix was asleep during the robbery and did not personally commit any overt act in furtherance of it.
(d) Not guilty, because the stash house raid was proposed by Ian independently, severing the direct causal link to Felix's original agreement.
(e) Not guilty, because Felix had not been informed of the specific plan, meaning he lacked the specific intent required for robbery.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** The Pinkerton doctrine holds conspirators vicariously liable for the foreseeable substantive crimes committed by their coconspirators in furtherance of the ongoing conspiracy. Raiding a rival's stash house is a highly foreseeable act in a conspiracy dedicated to defending drug turf "by any means necessary." (b) is wrong because Pinkerton liability is based on conspiracy membership and foreseeability, not a strict liability theory of corporate or organizational command. (c) is wrong because Pinkerton does not require the defendant to be awake or personally participate in the substantive offense. (d) is wrong because independent planning by coconspirators does not sever Pinkerton liability if the act remains in furtherance of the overarching agreement. (e) is wrong because Pinkerton imputes liability even without specific intent for the target offense.
