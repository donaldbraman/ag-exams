**Q10.** Assume the jurisdiction applies the traditional natural and probable consequences (NPC) doctrine. Is Helen liable for the guard's death?

(a) Yes, she is liable for the guard's death. A fatal shooting is a highly foreseeable consequence of an armed stash house raid, even if she only anticipated a robbery. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, she is liable for the guard's death. The traditional NPC doctrine holds accomplices strictly liable for any subsequent crime committed by the principal within the same calendar year.
(c) No, she is not liable for the guard's death. She only explicitly agreed to participate in a property crime, which legally precludes liability for any crimes against persons.
(d) No, she is not liable for the guard's death. She did not personally possess the specific intent to kill the guard, which is strictly required under traditional NPC doctrine.
(e) Yes, she is liable for the guard's death. Traditional NPC automatically imposes first-degree murder liability on anyone who serves as a lookout for any misdemeanor or felony.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under the traditional natural and probable consequences (NPC) doctrine, an accomplice to a target crime is liable for any subsequent crime committed by the principal that is reasonably foreseeable. A fatal shooting is a highly foreseeable outcome of an armed stash house robbery. (b) is wrong because NPC is constrained by foreseeability and the scope of the conspiracy, not a blanket one-year window. (c) is wrong because NPC explicitly bridges the gap between property crimes and violent crimes if the escalation was foreseeable. (d) is wrong because NPC was designed precisely to bypass the requirement of specific intent for the resulting crime. (e) is wrong because NPC relies on a case-by-case foreseeability analysis rather than automatic imposition of murder for any misdemeanor.
