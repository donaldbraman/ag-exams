**Q5.** Did Kevin "use" a firearm during and in relation to a drug trafficking crime under 18 U.S.C. § 924(c) when he received the handgun on October 1?

(a) Yes, because trading drugs to acquire a firearm constitutes the active employment of the weapon as currency in a prohibited drug trafficking transaction.
(b) Yes, because he possessed the firearm immediately following a narcotics transaction, satisfying the federal statutory definition of both carrying and using a deadly weapon.
(c) No, because under Supreme Court precedent, a person who receives a firearm in exchange for drugs does not "use" the firearm within the meaning of the statute. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because Justice Ginsburg's "use as a weapon" standard is the controlling law and strictly prohibits criminal liability for merely using a gun as transactional currency.
(e) No, because the 9mm handgun was neither physically brandished nor actively fired by either party during the narcotics exchange with the rival syndicate member.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** (c) is correct because under *Watson v. United States*, a defendant who trades drugs to acquire a gun does not "use" a firearm under § 924(c). Only the party tendering the gun uses it as consideration. (a) is wrong because *Watson* expressly held that receiving a gun as payment is not "use." (b) is wrong because while possessing the gun afterward might support a separate "possession in furtherance" or "carrying" charge, the specific act of receiving it does not constitute "using" under the statute. (d) is wrong because Justice Ginsburg's "use as a weapon" theory was merely a concurrence; *Smith v. United States* remains good law that trading a gun *for* drugs is use, but *Watson* governs the reverse transaction here. (e) is wrong because *Smith* allows "use" without brandishing or firing (e.g., as currency).
