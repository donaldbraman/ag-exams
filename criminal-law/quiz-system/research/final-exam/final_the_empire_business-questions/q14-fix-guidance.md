**Q14.** The FBI found a 9mm handgun in Dominic's wife's locked drawer. Is Dominic guilty of constructively possessing the 9mm?

(a) Yes, because his statement proves he was aware the firearm was in the house, which automatically satisfies the control requirement for joint occupants of a shared household.
(b) No, because the handgun was in a locked drawer containing only his wife's belongings to which he lacked access, defeating any legal inference of his dominion and control. <!-- correct -->
(c) Yes, because as the recognized head of the household, he maintains constructive possession over any contraband located within the master bedroom regardless of who owns the specific furniture.
(d) No, because the Second Amendment categorically prohibits prosecuting felons for constructive possession of firearms that are lawfully owned by their spouses for legitimate residential self-defense purposes.
(e) Yes, because his prior criminal convictions permit a jury to reasonably infer that he constructively possessed any dangerous weapons found during a lawful search of his primary residence.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** Under Louisiana v. White and the rules of constructive possession, a defendant's awareness of a firearm's presence is insufficient to establish dominion and control. Because the gun was in a locked drawer containing only his wife's belongings to which he had no access, the space was under her exclusive control, defeating constructive possession. (a) is incorrect because awareness does not equal dominion. (c) is incorrect because courts reject the "head of household" shared-possession presumption when specific spaces are exclusively controlled by a co-occupant. (d) is incorrect because while Chief Justice Weimer's concurrence in White raised Second Amendment concerns about shared households, there is no categorical constitutional ban on prosecuting these cases. (e) is incorrect because prior convictions are not substantive evidence of present possession.
