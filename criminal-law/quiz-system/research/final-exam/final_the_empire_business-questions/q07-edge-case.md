**Q7.** Assume the government indicts Jack under § 1962(d) for RICO conspiracy based solely on the November 1 written agreement to launch "Tri-State Waste." Is Jack guilty of RICO conspiracy?

(a) Yes, because an agreement to associate with and participate in a yet-to-be-formed racketeering enterprise constitutes a completed RICO conspiracy. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because no overt acts were committed in furtherance of the Tri-State Waste syndicate prior to the federal indictment being unsealed.
(c) Yes, because he already operated Empire Waste and the new agreement immediately merged into the existing corporate enterprise structure.
(d) No, because an association-in-fact enterprise must have an established structure and longevity before a conspiracy can be formed to participate in it.
(e) Yes, because agreeing to any two predicate offenses automatically creates a RICO enterprise conspiracy without requiring proof of an overarching structure.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under *United States v. Rich*, an agreement to associate with and participate in a yet-to-be-formed racketeering enterprise can constitute a completed RICO conspiracy offense. The enterprise need not actually exist at the time of the agreement. (b) is wrong because, unlike general federal conspiracy, RICO conspiracy does not require an overt act; the agreement itself completes the crime. (c) is wrong because the facts explicitly state they are forming a "new syndicate," and guilt is based on agreeing to that new venture, not merging it. (d) is wrong because *Salinas* and *Rich* establish that conspiring to form a future enterprise is sufficient; it does not need to pre-exist the agreement. (e) is wrong because agreeing to commit crimes is not enough; the defendants must specifically agree to conduct or participate in an enterprise's affairs through a pattern of racketeering.
