**Q8.** Assume Jack's liability depends on whether Kevin's conduct at the restaurant constituted an attempted extortion. How would Kevin's actions be evaluated under the dominant attempt tests?

(a) Kevin is guilty under the "substantial step" test because he strongly corroborated his criminal purpose, but he might be acquitted under the "proximity" test because he had not commenced breaking. <!-- correct -->
(b) Kevin is guilty under the "proximity" test because he was within 50 feet of the target, but he would be acquitted under the "substantial step" test because he made no demand.
(c) Kevin is guilty under both tests because driving to the target location with a weapon automatically satisfies the actus reus for any attempt jurisdiction in the country.
(d) Kevin is not guilty under either test because he had not yet entered the premises or made physical contact with the victim's property with the crowbar.
(e) Kevin is not guilty under the "substantial step" test because carrying a crowbar is lawful conduct that cannot strongly corroborate a criminal purpose in an extortion scheme.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** The MPC's "substantial step" test requires an act strongly corroborative of the actor's criminal purpose, which Kevin easily satisfied by arriving masked and armed with a crowbar. The traditional common law "physical proximity" test is much stricter, requiring the defendant to be dangerously close to completing the crime, making Kevin's status 50 feet away a closer call that could result in acquittal. (b) is wrong because it flips the stringency of the tests; the proximity test is harder to satisfy than the substantial step test. (c) is wrong because mere preparation (like driving) often fails the strict proximity test. (d) is wrong because the MPC substantial step test explicitly allows attempt liability well before entering the premises. (e) is wrong because possessing lawful items (crowbar, ski mask) can strongly corroborate criminal purpose when combined and brought to the target scene.
