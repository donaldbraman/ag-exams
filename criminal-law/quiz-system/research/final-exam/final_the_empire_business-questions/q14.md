**Q14.** Carmine is charged with Victor's murder. The prosecution relies on the Pinkerton doctrine. Is Carmine guilty of the murder?

(a) Guilty, because the murder was committed in furtherance of the extortion conspiracy and was a reasonably foreseeable consequence of sending an armed crew to collect payments. <!-- correct -->
(b) Guilty, because as the boss of the enterprise, Carmine is strictly liable for any and all crimes committed by members of his criminal organization.
(c) Not guilty, because Carmine did not purposefully facilitate the specific act of shooting Victor, which is required to establish derivative criminal liability.
(d) Not guilty, because the Pinkerton doctrine only applies to predicate acts listed in the federal RICO statute, not to state-level homicide charges.
(e) Not guilty, because Victor's violent resistance was an unforeseeable intervening event that shielded Carmine from any derivative conspiratorial liability.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under the *Pinkerton* doctrine, a conspirator is liable for any substantive offenses committed by co-conspirators that are in furtherance of the conspiracy and are reasonably foreseeable consequences of the agreement. Armed extortion foreseeably leads to violence. (b) fails because enterprise leadership does not create strict liability; liability must flow through conspiracy or accomplice doctrines. (c) fails because it states the test for accomplice liability, which *Pinkerton* bypasses. (d) fails because *Pinkerton* applies generally to conspiracy liability in jurisdictions that adopt it, independent of RICO. (e) fails because victim resistance is highly foreseeable during armed extortion.
