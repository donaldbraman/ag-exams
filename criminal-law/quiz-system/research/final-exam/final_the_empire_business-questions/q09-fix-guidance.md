**Q9.** Assume Kevin committed an attempted crime. Can Jack be held liable for Kevin's carrying of the concealed 9mm handgun during the restaurant incident under the *Pinkerton* doctrine?

(a) Yes, because the handgun had previously traveled in interstate commerce, providing the necessary jurisdictional hook to impute Kevin's possession directly to Jack.
(b) No, because Jack and Kevin only explicitly agreed to break the windows, meaning the handgun fell completely outside the defined scope of their illicit agreement.
(c) Yes, because under strict agency principles, a conspiracy ringleader is automatically strictly liable for any penal offenses committed by subordinates while on the clock.
(d) No, because Kevin abandoned his attempt to break the windows before firing the weapon, effectively terminating Jack's derivative liability for the underlying firearms offense.
(e) Yes, because carrying a firearm was a reasonably foreseeable consequence of sending Kevin to forcefully extort a business owner in furtherance of their conspiracy. <!-- correct -->

**Answer:** (e)

**Explanation:** The correct answer is (e). Under the *Pinkerton* doctrine, a conspirator is liable for the substantive offenses committed by a co-conspirator if those offenses are in furtherance of the conspiracy and are reasonably foreseeable as a necessary or natural consequence of the unlawful agreement. Carrying a weapon during a violent extortion attempt is reasonably foreseeable. (a) is wrong because interstate commerce is a jurisdictional element, not the substantive standard for imputing co-conspirator liability. (b) is wrong because *Pinkerton* liability covers foreseeable acts beyond the explicit terms of the agreement. (c) is wrong because *Pinkerton* requires foreseeability and furtherance of the conspiracy, not automatic strict liability for any on-the-clock offense. (d) is wrong because Kevin's later flight does not erase the fact that he was already carrying the firearm in furtherance of the conspiracy before the flight occurred.
