**Q12.** The jurisdiction has a standard common law felony-murder rule. Assume extortion is an enumerated predicate felony. Is Dominic guilty of felony murder for Victor's death?

(a) Guilty, because Victor's death occurred during the perpetration of the extortion, imposing strict liability for the homicide regardless of Dominic's mental state regarding the death. <!-- correct -->
(b) Guilty, because the use of a firearm during an extortion scheme automatically establishes the premeditation and deliberation necessary for first-degree murder.
(c) Not guilty, because Victor's act of throwing the ledger was an independent intervening cause that broke the chain of proximate causation for the homicide.
(d) Not guilty, because the extortion merger doctrine prohibits using an assaultive felony as the predicate for felony murder convictions.
(e) Not guilty, because Dominic shot Victor in specific response to the ledger strike, meaning the killing was legally independent of the underlying extortion objective.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** The felony-murder rule imposes strict liability for deaths that occur during the perpetration or attempted perpetration of an enumerated predicate felony, dispensing with the need to prove malice. (b) fails because felony murder substitutes for malice; it does not prove premeditation. (c) fails because victim resistance is highly foreseeable during extortion and does not break the causal chain. (d) fails because extortion has an independent felonious purpose (obtaining property) and does not merge into the homicide. (e) fails because deaths resulting from victim resistance are still considered to occur "in the perpetration" of the underlying felony.
