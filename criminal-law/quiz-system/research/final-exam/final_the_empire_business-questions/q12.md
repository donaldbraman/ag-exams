**Q12.** Assume the jurisdiction applies a traditional common law felony murder rule. Is Kevin guilty of felony murder for Matthew's death?

(a) Yes, because the killing occurred directly during the commission or attempted commission of extortion, which is legally classified as an inherently dangerous felony. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because any accidental or intentional death that occurs while a defendant is unlawfully armed automatically satisfies the traditional common law felony murder requirements.
(c) No, because Matthew shattered the car window first, meaning his tragic death was legally caused by his own unforeseeable intervening and superseding act of violence.
(d) No, because Kevin had not yet successfully completed the planned extortion, meaning the underlying target felony requirement was not legally satisfied at the time of death.
(e) No, because the killing was a deliberate and intentional act of shooting rather than a purely accidental death resulting inadvertently from the underlying target felony.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because under the traditional felony murder rule, a defendant is strictly liable for murder if a death occurs during the commission or attempted commission of an inherently dangerous felony (such as extortion). (b) is wrong because merely being unlawfully armed is generally a status or possession offense, not an inherently dangerous felony that triggers felony murder. (c) is wrong because a victim's predictable defensive response to an attempted violent felony is not an unforeseeable superseding cause that breaks the chain of causation. (d) is wrong because an *attempted* felony is completely sufficient to trigger the felony murder rule; successful completion is not required. (e) is wrong because felony murder applies to both accidental and intentional killings committed in furtherance of the predicate felony.
