## Issue 1 — grounding

**Q13.** Assume Matthew's self-defense claim fails. If charged with murder, could Matthew successfully reduce the charge to voluntary manslaughter under the common law heat-of-passion doctrine?

(a) Yes, because his trembling hands and flushed face demonstrate he was experiencing overwhelming emotional distress at the time he fired the gun.
(b) Yes, because the prolonged financial stress of repeated extortion payments constitutes adequate provocation for a lethal response against the extortionist.
(c) No, because common law adequate provocation is strictly limited to specific categories like observing spousal adultery or experiencing mutual combat or severe battery. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because he shot Kevin in the back from 40 feet away, which automatically establishes the depraved heart malice necessary for second-degree murder.
(e) No, because the cooling-off period between the previous extortion demands and the shooting permanently extinguished any legal claim to sudden passion.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** At common law, the heat-of-passion doctrine requires that the provocation fall into one of a few strict, categorical boxes—most notably mutual combat, severe battery, or witnessing spousal adultery. Fear of future extortion or property damage, even if genuine and enraging, does not constitute legally adequate provocation to mitigate murder to manslaughter under the traditional categorical approach. (a) is wrong because subjective extreme emotion is not enough; the provocation must be objectively adequate under the rigid common law categories. (b) is wrong because financial stress and property crimes are never recognized as adequate provocation at common law. (d) is wrong because while shooting in the back may show malice, the failure of the mitigation defense here turns on the lack of adequate provocation, not the method of killing. (e) is wrong because the relevant provocation would be Kevin arriving with a crowbar, not the past demands, so there was no cooling-off period—but the provocation itself was still legally inadequate.
