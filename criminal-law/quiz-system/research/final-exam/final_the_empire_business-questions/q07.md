**Q7.** Assume Dominic is charged with constructively possessing the 9mm handgun found in his wife's drawer. The prosecution offers only the location of the gun to prove possession. Is the evidence sufficient?

(a) No, because the gun was found in a space within his wife's exclusive control, defeating the inference that Dominic had the power to exercise dominion over it. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because the Second Amendment categorically protects the right of family members to lawfully own firearms in a shared household without triggering liability.
(c) Yes, because Dominic's status as a co-occupant of the marital bedroom establishes shared dominion and control over all personal items stored within it.
(d) Yes, because geographic proximity to the nightstand is legally sufficient to prove constructive possession in a residential search warrant execution.
(e) Yes, because a permissive legislative inference allows juries to presume all household occupants constructively possess any contraband found within the residence.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Where a space and its contents (a personal bedside drawer containing only her belongings) are within the exclusive control of a co-occupant, the government cannot establish that the defendant had dominion and control over contraband found there. (b) fails because while the Second Amendment protects the wife's ownership, the issue here is the factual element of constructive possession. (c) fails because shared occupancy of a room does not automatically grant dominion over another person's exclusive private spaces within that room. (d) fails because proximity alone is never sufficient for constructive possession (*Jenkins*). (e) fails because permissive inferences of this type apply to specific contexts like vehicles, not entire households generally.
