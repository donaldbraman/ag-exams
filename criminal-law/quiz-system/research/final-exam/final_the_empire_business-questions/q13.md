**Q13.** Under the *Pinkerton* doctrine, can Jack be convicted of the substantive crimes of attempted extortion and the murder of Matthew?

(a) Yes, because both the attempted extortion and the resulting homicide were reasonably foreseeable consequences of the violent conspiracy that Jack intentionally agreed to and directed. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because Jack's formal status as the sole owner and CEO of the corporate enterprise makes him strictly vicariously liable for all criminal acts of his employees.
(c) No, because Jack was not physically present at the restaurant and did not personally pull the trigger, legally shielding him from any substantive homicide liability.
(d) No, because the homicide was an independent and completely spontaneous act by Kevin that fell entirely outside the intended scope of their initial extortion agreement.
(e) No, because the *Pinkerton* doctrine of vicarious liability only strictly applies to federal racketeering offenses and legally cannot be used to charge state-level violent crimes.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because under the *Pinkerton* doctrine, a conspirator is vicariously liable for all substantive offenses committed by co-conspirators that are in furtherance of the conspiracy and are a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the agreement. A violent confrontation and resulting homicide are highly foreseeable when directing an armed subordinate to violently extort a victim. (b) is wrong because *Pinkerton* relies entirely on conspiratorial agreement, not corporate ownership status. (c) is wrong because physical presence is exactly what *Pinkerton* dispenses with; it expressly creates vicarious liability for absent co-conspirators. (d) is wrong because while the shooting was rapid, it was a direct and foreseeable result of the violent extortion Jack ordered, placing it squarely within the scope of the conspiracy. (e) is wrong because *Pinkerton* is a general principle of conspiracy liability applicable to all foreseeable crimes.
