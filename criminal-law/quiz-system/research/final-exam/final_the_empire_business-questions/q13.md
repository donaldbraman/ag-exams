**Q13.** Assume the felony-murder rule does not apply. Dominic requests a voluntary manslaughter instruction, arguing that Victor throwing the heavy ledger constituted adequate provocation (heat of passion). How should the court rule under the traditional common law?

(a) Deny the instruction, because a defendant who instigates a conflict by committing a felony cannot claim adequate provocation when the victim foreseeably resists. <!-- correct -->
(b) Deny the instruction, because a heavy ledger is an inanimate object, and only human physical contact can constitute adequate provocation at common law.
(c) Allow the instruction, because a sudden, severe physical battery is one of the historically recognized common law categories of adequate provocation.
(d) Allow the instruction, because the jury must determine whether a reasonable person in Dominic's situation would have been provoked into a heat of passion.
(e) Allow the instruction, because the immediate response to the ledger strike demonstrates an absence of premeditation and deliberation necessary for murder.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** The provocation defense is unavailable to a defendant who creates the situation that leads to the provocation. An armed extortionist cannot claim he was provoked into a heat of passion by his victim's lawful resistance. (b) fails because being struck by a heavy object would otherwise be a physical battery. (c) fails because while physical battery is a recognized category, the initial aggressor limitation bars its application here. (d) fails because at common law, the judge decides if the provocation falls into a legally adequate category before giving it to the jury, and here it is legally inadequate due to Dominic's fault. (e) fails because absence of premeditation negates first-degree murder, but does not affirmatively establish the elements of voluntary manslaughter.
