**Q11.** Dominic is charged with intentional homicide for Victor's death. He asserts a self-defense claim, arguing Victor threw a heavy object at his face. Will the self-defense claim succeed?

(a) No, because Dominic was the initial aggressor by entering the restaurant with an armed crew to commit extortion, stripping him of the right of self-defense. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because a heavy ledger does not constitute deadly force, meaning Dominic's use of a firearm was categorically disproportionate to the threat faced.
(c) Yes, because a person may use deadly force when facing an imminent threat of serious bodily injury from a heavy projectile thrown at their face.
(d) Yes, because Dominic had no legal duty to retreat when he was lawfully present in a place of public accommodation during business hours.
(e) Yes, because throwing the ledger constituted adequate provocation, which automatically perfects a self-defense claim against the provoking victim.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** A defendant cannot claim self-defense if he is the initial aggressor who provoked the conflict. Entering a business with an armed crew to commit extortion constitutes initial aggression. (b) fails because while proportionality is required, the initial aggressor bar preempts the defense entirely regardless of the ledger's danger level. (c) fails because an initial aggressor has no right to use defensive force unless they successfully withdraw and communicate that withdrawal. (d) fails because Dominic was not lawfully present when committing armed extortion. (e) fails because adequate provocation relates to the partial defense of voluntary manslaughter, not the complete justification of self-defense.
