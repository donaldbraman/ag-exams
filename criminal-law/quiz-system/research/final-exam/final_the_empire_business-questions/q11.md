**Q11.** Assuming Kevin is convicted of a homicide offense (and disregarding felony murder for this question), what is the most appropriate grading for his conduct under the Model Penal Code?

(a) Murder, because he intentionally drew his weapon and shot Matthew, fully satisfying the statutory requirement that the criminal killing be committed purposely or knowingly. <!-- correct -->
(b) Murder, because he was actively committing a violent felony at the time of the shooting, which automatically and strictly classifies the resulting homicide as a murder.
(c) Manslaughter, because his conscious disregard of the substantial risk of a deadly confrontation perfectly satisfies the Model Penal Code's baseline definition of a reckless homicide.
(d) Manslaughter, because Matthew's sudden and violent baseball bat attack provided adequate legal provocation to reduce the homicide grading under the traditional heat of passion doctrine.
(e) Negligent homicide, because he should have reasonably been aware that his highly visible and armed arrival would naturally trigger a violent defensive response from Matthew.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because under the MPC, a homicide committed purposely or knowingly is graded as murder. Drawing a gun and shooting someone directly is purposeful or knowing conduct. (b) is wrong because the question explicitly instructs to disregard felony murder, and the MPC itself rejects the strict felony murder rule in favor of a presumption of recklessness. (c) is wrong because while he was merely reckless in *provoking* the initial encounter, the actual act of *shooting* was purposeful/knowing, which legally elevates the crime to murder. (d) is wrong because an initial aggressor who provokes a confrontation cannot claim adequate provocation or extreme emotional disturbance when the victim predictably fights back. (e) is wrong because his mental state was far higher than negligence; he consciously disregarded the risk of a confrontation and then intentionally pulled the trigger.
