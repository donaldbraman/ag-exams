## Issue 1 — audit

**Q8.** During the extortion collection, Victor unexpectedly fired a shot at Dominic, and Dominic returned fire, killing Victor. If Dominic claims self-defense, how will the court rule?

(a) Allowed, because Victor escalated the encounter by firing the first shot, which legally restored Dominic's right to defend his own life with proportional deadly force.
(b) Barred, because Dominic's use of an unregistered firearm automatically strips him of the right to claim self-defense under the applicable federal and state criminal laws.
(c) Allowed, because Dominic only brandished his weapon to demand money, and Victor's sudden use of deadly force created an immediate and unavoidable threat to Dominic's life.
(d) Barred, because Dominic was the initial aggressor who threatened deadly force, and Victor did not respond disproportionately to that initial deadly threat when he fired back. <!-- correct -->
(e) Allowed, because the restaurant was a place of public accommodation, giving Dominic no legal duty to retreat before returning fire to protect himself from the cashier.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** Under the initial aggressor rule, a defendant who initiates a deadly confrontation loses the right to claim self-defense unless they clearly withdraw. By brandishing an AR-15 and demanding money, Dominic threatened deadly force, making him the initial aggressor and barring his self-defense claim when Victor lawfully responded. (a) is incorrect because Victor's lawful use of defensive force against an armed robber does not restore the robber's right to self-defense. (b) is incorrect because while using an unregistered firearm is a crime, status as a gun offender does not categorically strip the right of self-defense; being the initial aggressor does. (c) is incorrect because brandishing a firearm during a robbery constitutes a threat of deadly force. (e) is incorrect because the initial aggressor bar supersedes any stand-your-ground or no-retreat rules.

**Grounding:** Initial aggressor doctrine
