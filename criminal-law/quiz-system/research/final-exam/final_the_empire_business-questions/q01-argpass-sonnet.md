**Q1.** Assume the government seeks to charge Jack under RICO using Empire Waste Management LLC as the enterprise. Can Empire Waste, a legitimate state-licensed corporation, serve as a RICO enterprise?

(a) Yes, because a legitimate corporation can serve as a RICO enterprise when its ordinary business structure is repurposed to conduct racketeering activity. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because Empire Waste is a legitimate, state-licensed corporation and therefore cannot constitute a criminal enterprise under RICO.
(c) No, because the enterprise must be an association-in-fact with a formal hierarchy distinct from the underlying criminal acts.
(d) Yes, because any defendant who commits two or more predicate offenses within ten years automatically forms a RICO enterprise.
(e) No, because an enterprise requires horizontal relationships among multiple distinct criminal schemes connected by a single rim.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under RICO doctrine, a legitimate corporation can itself constitute the enterprise when its ordinary business structure is repurposed to conduct racketeering activity. The lawfulness of the corporation's formal charter does not shield it from being classified as an enterprise (as seen in the *Insys Therapeutics* prosecution). (b) is wrong because lawful entities routinely serve as the enterprise if their structure is used for racketeering. (c) is wrong because the association-in-fact structural requirements apply to informal groups, not incorporated entities. (d) is wrong because it conflates the "pattern of racketeering" requirement (predicate offenses) with the distinct structural "enterprise" requirement. (e) is wrong because it applies the hub-and-spoke "rim" requirement from *Pinson*, which governs informal networks, not single unified corporate entities like Empire Waste.
