**Q4.** Marcus is charged with participating in the illicit transport during the four months after Carmine's threat. He asserts a traditional duress defense. Will the defense succeed?

(a) No, because the threat was not of imminent harm, and Marcus had ample time over four months to escape the coercion by contacting authorities. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because the defense of duress is categorically unavailable as a matter of law when the defendant is charged with any drug trafficking offenses.
(c) Yes, because a person of reasonable firmness would have felt compelled by Carmine's violent threat to continue routing the drug-carrying trucks.
(d) Yes, because Carmine possessed the actual power and resources to carry out the threat of putting Marcus in a compactor at any future time.
(e) Yes, because Marcus's continued involvement was coerced by the nature of his employment, negating the voluntary act requirement of the criminal code.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** A successful duress defense requires a threat of present, imminent death or serious bodily harm, leaving the defendant no reasonable opportunity to escape. A vague threat followed by four months of compliance without contacting police defeats imminence. (b) fails because duress is generally available for drug offenses; it is only categorically barred for intentional homicide. (c) fails because even under broader standards, four months of compliance without seeking help defeats the defense. (d) fails because having the power to carry out a threat eventually is not the same as the threat being imminent. (e) fails because employment pressure does not negate the voluntary act requirement, which only excludes physical reflexes or unconscious acts.
