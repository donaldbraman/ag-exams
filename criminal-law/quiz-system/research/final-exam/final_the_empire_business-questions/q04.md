**Q4.** Assuming Kevin's constructive possession of the breakroom cocaine is established, can he be convicted of possession with intent to distribute?

(a) Yes, because the sheer bulk quantity of two kilograms of cocaine provides a sufficient circumstantial basis for a jury to logically infer an intent to distribute. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because any amount of a Schedule I or II controlled substance that is stored in a commercial workspace automatically triggers an intent to distribute charge.
(c) No, because the government must explicitly produce direct evidence of an actual sale or a mutual agreement to sell the specific drugs found in the bag.
(d) No, because the cocaine was locked inside a duffel bag, legally preventing Kevin from distributing it without first physically relocating it from the breakroom premises.
(e) No, because the federal mandatory minimum statutes strictly require proof of actual drug distribution before a defendant can be convicted of possession with intent to distribute.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because in most jurisdictions, possessing a bulk quantity of drugs (like two kilograms of cocaine) far exceeding personal use amounts provides a sufficient circumstantial basis for a jury to infer the defendant's intent to distribute. (b) is wrong because commercial storage alone does not legally transform simple possession into PWID; evidence of intent, such as quantity or packaging, is required. (c) is wrong because circumstantial evidence is sufficient to prove intent to distribute; direct evidence of an actual sale is not required. (d) is wrong because locking drugs in a bag does not negate the intent to distribute them at a later time. (e) is wrong because mandatory minimums under § 841(b) key to drug weight, but the underlying PWID charge requires only the *intent* to distribute, not proof of actual distribution.
