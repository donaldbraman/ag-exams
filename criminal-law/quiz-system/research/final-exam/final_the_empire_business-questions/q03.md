**Q3.** The jurisdiction follows the majority rule requiring purposeful conduct for accomplice liability. Is Marcus guilty as an accomplice to drug trafficking?

(a) Not guilty, because Marcus merely knew of the criminal purpose and lacked a true stake in the venture that would demonstrate a purpose to facilitate it. <!-- correct -->
(b) Not guilty, because Marcus's actions of routing trucks constituted mere preparation rather than a substantial step toward the commission of the target offense.
(c) Guilty, because routing the trucks provided concrete and necessary assistance to the operation while he had explicit knowledge of its criminal nature.
(d) Guilty, because an employee's regular salary constitutes a sufficient financial stake to infer purposeful facilitation under the modern rules of derivative liability.
(e) Guilty, because Marcus's explicit acknowledgment of the drug running satisfies the bilateral agreement requirement necessary to establish accomplice liability.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under the majority rule, accomplice liability requires the purpose to facilitate the underlying crime. Mere knowledge, coupled with providing routine services for normal compensation without a cut of the profits, does not establish purpose. (b) fails because "substantial step" is the actus reus test for attempt, not accomplice liability. (c) fails because concrete assistance with mere knowledge is insufficient under the purpose standard. (d) fails because a regular salary, without a specialized cut of the illicit profits, does not show a stake in the criminal venture. (e) fails because accomplice liability does not require a bilateral agreement, which is a conspiracy concept.
