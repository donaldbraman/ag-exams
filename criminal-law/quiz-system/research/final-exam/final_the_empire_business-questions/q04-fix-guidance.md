## Issue 1 — audit

**Q4.** Assume Marcus is charged with accomplice liability for his actions and claims duress. Is Marcus entitled to a duress defense?

(a) Entitled to a duress defense, because Carmine's threat to put him in a compactor involved a risk of death or serious bodily injury that overcame his will.
(b) Not entitled to a duress defense, because the threat was not sufficiently imminent since Marcus routed trucks for four months and had ample opportunity to seek help. <!-- correct -->
(c) Entitled to a duress defense, because he was dealing with an organized crime boss who possessed both the immediate means and the reputation to carry out the threat.
(d) Not entitled to a duress defense, because duress is never a legally recognized defense when the underlying crime involves the distribution of significant quantities of narcotics.
(e) Entitled to a duress defense, because the Model Penal Code applies a purely subjective standard to determine if the defendant genuinely felt coerced by the mafia boss.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** To successfully claim duress, a defendant must face an imminent threat of death or serious bodily injury. Because Marcus continued to route the trucks for four months without contacting the authorities, the threat was not imminent, and he had ample opportunity to escape the coercion. (a) is incorrect because while the threat of the compactor involves death or serious bodily harm, the temporal requirement of imminence is missing. (c) is incorrect because the reputation of the threatener does not excuse failing to seek help over a four-month period. (d) is incorrect because the only categorical bar to the duress defense is for intentional murder; it remains available for drug trafficking if all elements are met. (e) is incorrect because even the MPC's "reasonable firmness" standard is objective, not purely subjective, and requires that the individual not recklessly place themselves in the situation.

**Grounding:** Duress elements; Imminence requirement
