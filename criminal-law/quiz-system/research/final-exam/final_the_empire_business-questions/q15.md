**Q15.** Dominic is charged with attempted murder of Elias. How would his conduct be analyzed under the common law "proximity test" versus the MPC "substantial step" test?

(a) He would likely be guilty under the substantial step test because lying in wait strongly corroborates his criminal purpose, but might be not guilty under the proximity test because he had not yet aimed or fired. <!-- correct -->
(b) He would likely be guilty under the proximity test because he was geographically near the victim, but not guilty under the substantial step test because he had not yet physically injured Elias.
(c) He would be guilty under both tests because purchasing a specialized sniper rifle is categorically sufficient to establish attempt liability across all American jurisdictions.
(d) He would be not guilty under both tests because mere preparation is legally insufficient, and parking outside a target's house constitutes only preliminary preparation.
(e) He would likely be not guilty under the substantial step test because his actions were equivocal, but guilty under the proximity test because he possessed the specific intent to kill.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** The MPC "substantial step" test focuses on what the defendant has already done (lying in wait is a recognized substantial step strongly corroborating intent). The common law "proximity test" looks at what remains to be done, and courts often found attempts failed under this test if the defendant had not yet arrived at the exact scene or drawn the weapon. (b) fails because the substantial step test is easier to satisfy, not harder. (c) fails because purchasing a weapon is widely considered mere preparation under the proximity test. (d) fails because lying in wait with a loaded weapon easily satisfies the substantial step test. (e) fails because it reverses the stringency of the two tests.
