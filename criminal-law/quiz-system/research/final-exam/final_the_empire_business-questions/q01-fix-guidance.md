## Issue 1 — grounding

**Q1.** The government charges Jack with a substantive RICO violation under § 1962(c), alleging that Empire Waste Management LLC serves as the RICO "enterprise." Jack moves to dismiss, arguing that because Empire Waste is a legitimate, state-licensed corporation, it cannot satisfy the enterprise element. How should the court rule?

(a) Deny the motion, because a legitimate corporation's ordinary business structure can constitute a RICO enterprise when repurposed to conduct or facilitate a pattern of racketeering activity. <!-- correct -->
(b) Grant the motion, because the enterprise element under § 1962(c) requires proof of an association-in-fact whose primary purpose is inherently criminal in nature.
(c) Deny the motion, because any corporate entity with at least three employees automatically qualifies as an enterprise regardless of how the racketeering is conducted.
(d) Grant the motion, because Jack's racketeering activities (extortion and drug delivery) fall entirely outside the scope of Empire Waste's formal state-licensed charter.
(e) Deny the motion, but only if the prosecution can prove that Empire Waste derived the majority of its total revenue from the illegal extortion and drug distribution operations.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** The correct answer is (a) because courts have consistently held that a legitimate corporation can itself constitute a RICO enterprise when its formal structure—like Empire's delivery routes and dispatch system—is utilized to commit racketeering acts. (b) is wrong because an enterprise does not need an inherently criminal purpose; lawful entities suffice. (c) is wrong because the statute does not rely on an automatic employee count; the structure must actually be used for racketeering. (d) is wrong because repurposing an otherwise lawful business outside its charter is precisely how corporate RICO works. (e) is wrong because there is no requirement that the illegal proceeds constitute a majority of the corporate revenue.
