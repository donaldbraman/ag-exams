**Q10.** Assume Dominic knew his prior commercial fraud conviction was punishable by more than one year. He challenges the constitutionality of his § 922(g)(1) charge as applied to him under the Second Amendment. What is the current state of the law regarding this challenge?

(a) The challenge presents an open constitutional question, as courts are split on whether disarming purely nonviolent felons aligns with the nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation. <!-- correct -->
(b) The challenge will definitely fail, because the Supreme Court in Rahimi explicitly held that all felony convictions, regardless of violence, constitutionally justify total disarmament.
(c) The challenge will definitely fail, because drug and gun regulations both rest on plenary Commerce Clause authority and are immune from Second Amendment as-applied challenges.
(d) The challenge will definitely succeed, because the government must identify an identical founding-era statute prohibiting fraudsters from possessing weapons to satisfy the Bruen test.
(e) The challenge will definitely succeed, because Bruen established that only those actively serving a criminal sentence may be constitutionally deprived of their Second Amendment rights.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Post-*Bruen*, the constitutionality of 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1) as applied to purely nonviolent felons is actively contested, with the Third Circuit (*Range*) initially striking it down and circuits remaining split. (b) fails because *Rahimi* addressed domestic violence restraining orders and dangerousness, not all nonviolent felons. (c) fails because while drug regulations rely solely on the Commerce Clause, gun regulations must also satisfy the Second Amendment. (d) fails because *Rahimi* clarified that the government needs a "relevantly similar" historical analogue, not a historical twin. (e) fails because *Bruen* did not limit disarmament solely to actively serving prisoners.
