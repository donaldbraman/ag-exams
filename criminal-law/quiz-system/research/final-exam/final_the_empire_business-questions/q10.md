**Q10.** Assume the jurisdiction follows the common law rules of self-defense. Is Kevin entitled to a self-defense justification for shooting Matthew?

(a) Yes, because Matthew aggressively escalated the encounter to deadly force by shattering the car window, an action that instantly restored Kevin's fundamental right to armed self-defense.
(b) Yes, because Kevin was trapped inside his vehicle and possessed no reasonable opportunity to safely retreat before Matthew launched his sudden and completely unprovoked physical attack.
(c) No, because Kevin intentionally brought a loaded firearm to the scene, a provocative action that automatically and legally precludes any defendant from asserting a self-defense justification.
(d) No, because Kevin's highly visible and armed arrival with unlawful intent actively provoked the conflict, classifying him as the initial aggressor who had not communicated withdrawal. <!-- correct -->
(e) No, because a defendant who is actively engaged in the commission of any felony is strictly barred from raising a justifiable self-defense claim under all legal circumstances.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** (d) is correct because under common law, a defendant who provokes a physical conflict by arriving with obvious unlawful intent and tools is the initial aggressor, and loses the right to self-defense unless he clearly withdraws. Kevin provoked the encounter and did not withdraw before shooting. (a) is wrong because even if the victim escalates, an initial aggressor must still attempt to retreat or communicate withdrawal before responding with deadly force. (b) is wrong because Matthew's attack was not "unprovoked"—Kevin provoked it by arriving as the enforcer. (c) is wrong because merely possessing a firearm does not categorically or automatically eliminate the right to self-defense. (e) is wrong because the felony limitation on self-defense applies primarily through the initial aggressor doctrine or felony murder, but there is no universal strict bar for "any felony" in pure self-defense doctrine independent of provocation.
