**Q6.** Dominic is charged federally with possession with intent to distribute fentanyl. He argues that he cannot be convicted of the fentanyl offense because he genuinely believed the substance was heroin. Will this defense succeed?

(a) No, because the government must only prove that Dominic knew the substance was a controlled substance, not its precise chemical identity or type. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because strict liability applies to all attendant circumstances in federal drug trafficking offenses, rendering his subjective belief completely irrelevant.
(c) Yes, because a defendant must possess the specific intent to distribute the exact controlled substance that is charged in the federal indictment.
(d) Yes, because his mistake of fact regarding the substance's identity was both subjectively honest and objectively reasonable under the circumstances.
(e) Yes, because under the rule of lenity, ambiguities in the defendant's mental state must be resolved in favor of the lesser included offense of heroin distribution.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under federal drug law (*McFadden*), the mens rea for drug possession/distribution only requires knowledge that the substance is a controlled substance (its general nature), not knowledge of its specific identity. (b) fails because federal drug offenses are not strict liability as to the presence of a controlled substance. (c) fails because the specific identity of the drug is an attendant circumstance for which general knowledge suffices. (d) fails because a mistake about the specific type of drug does not negate the required mental state (knowledge of possessing *a* drug). (e) fails because there is no ambiguity; the general-nature rule is well established across circuits.
