**Q6.** Can Kevin successfully defend against a federal felon-in-possession charge (§ 922(g)) by asserting that he genuinely believed his prior conviction was a misdemeanor?

(a) Yes, because under the *Rehaif* decision, the government must prove the defendant knew he belonged to the relevant category of persons legally prohibited from possessing a firearm. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because reasonable and genuine reliance on the formal advice of private legal counsel constitutes a complete affirmative defense to any general intent federal firearms offense.
(c) No, because ignorance of the law is never a valid excuse, and his subjective misunderstanding of the statutory classification of his prior offense is legally irrelevant.
(d) No, because absolute strict liability applies to the status element of the felon-in-possession statute, meaning the government requires only objective proof of the underlying prior conviction.
(e) No, because his prior conviction carried a maximum statutory penalty of 18 months in prison, an objective fact that legally supersedes any subjective belief he genuinely held.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because under *Rehaif v. United States*, the government must prove not only that the defendant knowingly possessed the firearm, but also that he knew he had the relevant status (e.g., a felony conviction). Since Kevin genuinely believed his conviction was a misdemeanor based on his lawyer's email, a jury could find he lacked the required mens rea for the status element. (b) is wrong because advice of private counsel is not a standalone defense; rather, it merely serves as evidence that he lacked the requisite factual knowledge of his status. (c) is wrong because *Rehaif* established that knowing one's status is a factual predicate for liability under § 922(g), overcoming the traditional "ignorance of the law" bar in this context. (d) is wrong because *Rehaif* explicitly rejected strict liability for the status element. (e) is wrong because the objective maximum penalty does not substitute for his subjective knowledge of that reality.
