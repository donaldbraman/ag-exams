**Q8.** The prosecution introduces Dominic's statement ("I told my wife to get rid of that 9mm last week. I knew it was still in the house.") as evidence of constructive possession. Does this statement alone establish the elements of constructive possession?

(a) No, because while the statement proves his awareness of the gun's presence, it does not prove he had the intent or power to exercise dominion and control over it. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because the statement constitutes an out-of-court admission that must be strictly corroborated by physical evidence of his handling the firearm.
(c) Yes, because acknowledging the presence of contraband in a shared space constitutes an assumption of legal ownership and satisfies the possession element.
(d) Yes, because his explicit instruction to his wife demonstrates that he actually possessed the firearm before she placed it in her private drawer.
(e) Yes, because under the snapshot limitation, his knowledge of the gun's exact location at the time of the search completes the constructive possession offense.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** A defendant's awareness that contraband is present is necessary but not sufficient for constructive possession; the government must separately prove the intent to exercise dominion and control. Knowing his wife's gun was there is not controlling it. (b) fails because the corpus delicti rule does not strictly require physical handling evidence to corroborate statements. (c) fails because awareness is fundamentally distinct from dominion and control. (d) fails because telling someone else to get rid of their own property does not inherently prove the speaker previously possessed it. (e) fails because the snapshot limitation in *Jenkins* protects defendants from mere proximity inferences; it does not turn knowledge into possession.
