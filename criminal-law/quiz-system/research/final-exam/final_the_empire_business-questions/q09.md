**Q9.** Assume the 9mm handgun was in Dominic's possession. He is charged under 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1) (felon-in-possession). He argues he cannot be convicted because he genuinely believed his prior offense carried a maximum sentence of only six months. If the jury believes him, is he guilty?

(a) Not guilty, because the government must prove he knew he belonged to the prohibited class, meaning he knew his prior offense was punishable by more than one year. <!-- correct -->
(b) Not guilty, because ignorance of the penal law governing the prior offense provides a valid mistake of law defense under the strict Cheek exception.
(c) Guilty, because the Morissette presumption does not extend the knowledge requirement to the attendant circumstance of a defendant's legal status.
(d) Guilty, because the maximum statutory penalty of a prior conviction is a collateral legal fact for which strict liability universally applies.
(e) Guilty, because his prior conviction was a matter of public record, satisfying the "reasonable opportunity to observe" standard for mens rea.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under *Rehaif v. United States*, the government must prove the defendant knew both that he possessed a firearm and that he belonged to the prohibited class (e.g., that he had a prior conviction punishable by more than one year). (b) fails because this is analyzed as a mistake regarding a status element under *Rehaif*, not the *Cheek* tax willfulness exception. (c) fails because *Rehaif* explicitly extended the *Morissette* presumption to status elements. (d) fails because the Supreme Court expressly rejected strict liability for this element. (e) fails because the "reasonable opportunity to observe" standard applies to the victim's age in sex trafficking, not to a defendant's own criminal record.
