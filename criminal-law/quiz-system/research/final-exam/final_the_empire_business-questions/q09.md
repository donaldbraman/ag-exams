**Q9.** Assume that the jurisdiction applies the Model Penal Code's "substantial step" test. Is Kevin guilty of attempted extortion?

(a) Yes, because parking at the specific target location while possessing the necessary tools constitutes a substantial step that is strongly corroborative of his unlawful criminal purpose. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because merely driving on a public road while possessing a ski mask and crowbar fully satisfies the traditional common law "dangerous proximity" requirement for criminal attempt.
(c) No, because Matthew's unexpected and sudden emergence interrupted Kevin before he could exit the vehicle, thereby legally negating the required voluntary act for an attempt charge.
(d) No, because he had not yet actually put on the ski mask or physically wielded the crowbar, meaning his initial actions legally remained in the realm of mere preparation.
(e) No, because breaking commercial windows is inherently a misdemeanor crime of property vandalism, which legally cannot serve as the foundational basis for an attempted felony extortion charge.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because under the MPC, attempt requires an act that is a "substantial step" strongly corroborative of the actor's criminal purpose. Arriving at the exact scene with the materials needed to complete the crime (crowbar, mask) easily satisfies this standard. (b) is wrong because the prompt explicitly asks for the MPC standard, not the common law "dangerous proximity" test. (c) is wrong because an external interruption (Matthew emerging) does not erase the attempt liability that had already attached when Kevin took the substantial step. (d) is wrong because under the MPC, a defendant need not reach the "last act" or physically begin the final assault; assembling materials at the target site crosses the line from preparation to attempt. (e) is wrong because the vandalism was the intended coercive mechanism to illegally obtain money, which squarely fits the definition of extortion.
