**Q2.** Assume that, whether or not Jack is liable, Laura is charged with aiding and abetting Kevin's drug distribution under federal law. Based on her conduct in dispatching Kevin's routes, is Laura guilty as an accomplice?

(a) Guilty, because she possessed the knowledge that her routing dispatch facilitated Kevin's ongoing delivery of wholesale quantities of cocaine to local dealers.
(b) Not guilty, because dispatching trucks constitutes an omission rather than an affirmative act of assistance toward the commission of the drug distribution.
(c) Guilty, because by retaining her lucrative $80,000-per-year salary, she acquired a direct financial stake in the success of the illicit drug distribution venture.
(d) Not guilty, because her sole motivation was to keep her lawful salary, meaning she lacked the purpose to facilitate the underlying drug distribution offense. <!-- correct -->
(e) Guilty, because her continued dispatching after acquiring knowledge of the drug operation satisfied the strict liability standard for corporate supervisors.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** The correct answer is (d). Under the general federal standard (*Peoni*), accomplice liability requires that the defendant act with the purpose of facilitating the underlying offense. Laura acted solely to retain her lawful salary, not to promote the drug trade, meaning she lacked the required purpose. (a) is wrong because mere knowledge that one's ordinary lawful services will facilitate a crime is generally insufficient for accomplice liability. (b) is wrong because dispatching routes is an affirmative act, not an omission. (c) is wrong because earning a standard, pre-existing lawful salary does not constitute a "stake in the venture" that elevates knowledge to purpose. (e) is wrong because accomplice liability requires a specific mental state and does not employ a strict liability standard for supervisors.

**Grounding:** General Doctrine - Accomplice Liability (Mens Rea: Purpose vs. Knowledge; Peoni / Lauria)
