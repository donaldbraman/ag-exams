**Q14.** Assume the prosecution must prove factual causation for Blake's homicide charge. If Chris died instantly from Dana's injection before the 10-minute overdose window expired, is Blake's omission the factual (but-for) cause of Chris's death?

(a) No, because Chris died from the injection before Blake's failure to provide aid could actually produce the intended fatal overdose result. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because omissions can never serve as the factual cause of a death when a third party takes an affirmative lethal act.
(c) Yes, because Blake's failure to provide aid accelerated Chris's death by forcing Dana to take extreme and highly dangerous medical measures.
(d) Yes, because the combination of Blake's omission and Dana's injection operated as concurrent sufficient causes of the ultimate fatal cardiac rupture.
(e) Yes, because Blake had a contractual duty to provide medical care, which automatically satisfies the factual causation requirement for homicide offenses.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Under the but-for test, the defendant's conduct must be a necessary condition for the result that actually occurred. Because Dana's injection killed Chris instantly before Blake's omission could, Blake's omission is not the factual cause of death at that moment. (b) is wrong because omissions can be factual causes if the omitted aid would have prevented the specific death that occurred. (c) is wrong because accelerating a death makes one a cause, but Blake's omission delayed care rather than directly causing the immediate rupture. (d) is wrong because they were not concurrent sufficient causes; Dana's act preempted the overdose timeline entirely. (e) is wrong because a legal duty establishes the actus reus, but factual causation must still be independently proven.
