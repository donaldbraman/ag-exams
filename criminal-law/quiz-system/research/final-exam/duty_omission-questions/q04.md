**Q4.** Assume Evan is charged as an accomplice to Blake's crime for helping push the deadbolt shut. Evan argues that because Blake acted under duress, Evan should be shielded from derivative liability. Will Evan's derivative defense succeed?

(a) No, because duress is merely an excuse, and excuses do not extend derivative protection to accomplices who assist the excused actor. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because Evan's status as a delivery driver means he lacked the specific intent required to claim any derivative criminal defense.
(c) Yes, because duress is a justification, meaning the primary act was legally correct and thereby protects any third party who assists.
(d) Yes, because the law allows any bystander to affirmatively assist another person who is acting under the threat of imminent death.
(e) Yes, because Evan abandoned the enterprise by breaking the window and fleeing immediately after helping Blake shut the heavy deadbolt.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Duress is an excuse, not a justification. It excuses the specific actor whose free will was overborne but does not render the act legally correct, meaning third parties cannot derive protection from it. (b) is wrong because employment status is irrelevant to derivative defenses. (c) is wrong because duress is definitively categorized as an excuse, unlike self-defense. (d) is wrong because the law does not permit bystanders to assist in crimes merely because another person is coerced. (e) is wrong because abandonment requires neutralizing the assistance given, not merely fleeing after helping lock the deadbolt.
