## Issue 1 — audit

**Q4.** Assume Dane is charged with destruction of property for breaking Bex's door. Does Dane have a valid necessity defense?

(a) Yes, because his subjective belief that a medical emergency was occurring automatically immunizes him from any property crimes.
(b) Yes, because the harm of Cody dying from an overdose clearly outweighed the harm of the $800 in property damage caused by the entry. <!-- correct -->
(c) No, because he failed to exhaust reasonable legal alternatives by calling 911 and waiting for on-duty emergency personnel to arrive.
(d) No, because he was not the cause of the overdose and therefore lacked the required standing to intervene in a private residence.
(e) No, because the defense of necessity is categorically unavailable whenever the defendant commits an intentional property crime.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** (b) is correct because necessity requires a choice of evils where the harm avoided (Cody's death) clearly outweighs the harm caused (the $800 property damage), which it definitively does here. (a) is wrong because the necessity defense is based on objective reasonableness and a balancing of harms, not absolute subjective immunity. (c) is wrong because kicking the door was the only immediate alternative given Cody's agonal breathing; waiting for EMS was not a reasonable alternative in this imminent crisis. (d) is wrong because necessity can be invoked to protect third parties; the defendant does not need to be the cause of the crisis. (e) is wrong because necessity is routinely applied to justify intentional property crimes when preventing greater harms.
