**Q13.** Assume Blake asserts a duress defense to a murder charge for his role in causing Chris's death by guarding the door. How will a court applying the traditional common law rule?

(a) It will reject the defense, because duress is categorically unavailable as a justification or excuse for murder under the traditional common law. <!-- correct -->
(b) It will allow the defense, because any reasonable person facing an immediate threat of death would have guarded the cabin door.
(c) It will allow the defense, because the immediate threat to Blake's life outweighed the potential medical harm caused to Chris's life.
(d) It will reject the defense, because Blake accepted payment to act as security, thereby voluntarily placing himself in a coercive situation.
(e) It will reject the defense, because Alex's conditional statement about shooting did not satisfy the strict physical imminence requirement for duress.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Under the traditional common law, duress is categorically barred as a defense to intentional murder, based on the rationale that one cannot take an innocent life to save their own. (b) is wrong because the categorical bar applies regardless of the reasonableness of the defendant's actions. (c) is wrong because the common law does not permit comparing or balancing lives in duress homicide cases. (d) is wrong because while placing oneself in a coercive situation defeats duress, the categorical murder bar is the primary doctrinal hurdle. (e) is wrong because a gun pointed at the chest with a threat to shoot "right now" satisfies the imminence requirement.
