**Q6.** Assume Alex is charged with Dana's burglary of the veterinary clinic under a conspiracy theory of liability. Will Alex be convicted of the burglary?

(a) Yes, because the burglary was a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the drug conspiracy to protect the illicit manufacturing operation. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because Alex implicitly ordered the burglary by pointing the gun at Blake and demanding that the police not be called.
(c) No, because Alex did not explicitly agree to burglarize the veterinary clinic when forming the initial drug distribution conspiracy.
(d) No, because Dana was merely a freelance supplier of precursor chemicals and therefore could not act in furtherance of the conspiracy.
(e) No, because property crimes cannot serve as substantive offenses under Pinkerton when the underlying conspiracy involves narcotics distribution.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Under the *Pinkerton* doctrine, a conspirator is liable for the substantive offenses of co-conspirators if they are committed in furtherance of the conspiracy and are reasonably foreseeable. (b) is wrong because *Pinkerton* does not require an explicit or implicit order to commit the specific substantive offense. (c) is wrong because *Pinkerton* liability attaches precisely when there is no specific agreement to commit the collateral crime. (d) is wrong because charging a premium based on illicit purpose makes her a co-conspirator, not just a neutral vendor. (e) is wrong because there is no categorical rule exempting property crimes from *Pinkerton* drug conspiracies.
