**Q11.** Assume Alex is charged with possession with intent to distribute (PWID). The prosecution introduces evidence of the 5 kilograms of opioids, hundreds of baggies, and the digital scale. Is this sufficient to prove intent to distribute?

(a) Yes, because the massive quantity and presence of packaging paraphernalia permit a strong inference of intent to distribute rather than personal use. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because possession of any quantity of a synthetic opioid creates an irrebuttable presumption of distribution under modern federal statutes.
(c) No, because the prosecution must present direct witness testimony of an actual sale to sustain an intent to distribute conviction.
(d) No, because the drugs were securely locked in a safe, which legally negates any inference of an immediate intent to distribute.
(e) No, because the scale and baggies could have been used for legitimate purposes, rendering the evidence insufficient as a matter of law.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Intent to distribute can be strongly inferred from circumstantial evidence, including drug quantities too large for personal use (5kg) and the presence of packaging materials (baggies, scales). (b) is wrong because no modern statute creates an irrebuttable presumption of distribution based solely on quantity. (c) is wrong because circumstantial evidence is entirely sufficient to prove intent to distribute; direct observation is unnecessary. (d) is wrong because storing drugs securely does not negate the intent to distribute them later. (e) is wrong because while items may have dual uses, their presence alongside massive drug quantities legally permits the distribution inference.
