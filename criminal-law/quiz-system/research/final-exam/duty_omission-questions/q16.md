**Q16.** Assume Chris survived and Alex is charged with "attempted extreme-indifference murder" for preparing the untested opioid dose and handing it to Chris. Is this a legally valid charge?

(a) No, because attempt requires the specific intent to bring about the prohibited result, making attempted unintentional murder a logical doctrinal impossibility. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because providing an untested dose of drugs constitutes mere preparation rather than a substantial step toward the commission of homicide.
(c) Yes, because handing a highly dangerous substance to a vulnerable victim demonstrates a depraved heart sufficient to satisfy attempt liability requirements.
(d) Yes, because Alex's reckless disregard for human life automatically transfers to fulfill the specific intent element necessary for an attempt conviction.
(e) Yes, because the MPC allows attempt liability for any underlying offense so long as the defendant acts with ordinary criminal negligence.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Attempt requires the specific intent (purpose) to bring about the target offense's result. Extreme indifference murder is an unintentional homicide; one cannot purposefully attempt to commit an unintentional crime. (b) is wrong because preparing a dose and handing it to a victim easily satisfies the substantial step requirement. (c) is wrong because a depraved heart (recklessness) is insufficient to satisfy the specific intent requirement for criminal attempt. (d) is wrong because reckless disregard does not transfer to fulfill specific intent requirements. (e) is wrong because the MPC also requires purpose regarding the result for attempt liability.
