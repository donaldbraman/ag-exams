**Q5.** Assume Dane is charged with destruction of property for breaking Bex's door. Can Dane successfully raise a necessity defense?

(a) Yes, because Dane faced a choice of evils and breaking the door to administer a life-saving rescue clearly outweighed the harm of property damage. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because Dane was not personally in danger, and the necessity defense only applies to threats against the defendant.
(c) Yes, because the medical emergency automatically reclassifies property damage as a non-criminal civil tort.
(d) No, because Dane's action was not strictly imminent, as the medical examiner noted Cody survived for another twenty minutes.
(e) No, because Dane failed to exhaust legal alternatives by waiting for police to arrive and open the door.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because the necessity (choice of evils) defense applies when a defendant acts to prevent a greater harm (death) by causing a lesser harm (property damage), provided the threat is imminent and there are no reasonable legal alternatives. (b) is wrong because necessity, unlike common law duress, can be invoked to protect third parties. (c) is wrong because necessity is a criminal justification defense, not a jurisdictional reclassification. (d) is wrong because agonal breathing indicates an immediate threat to life, satisfying the imminence requirement. (e) is wrong because waiting for police during an active respiratory failure is not a *reasonable* legal alternative, as it would likely result in death.
