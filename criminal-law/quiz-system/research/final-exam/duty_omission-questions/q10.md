**Q10.** Assume Dana is charged with felony murder, with the burglary of the veterinary clinic serving as the predicate felony, after her injection kills Chris. Under the "merger" or "independent purpose" doctrine, will the burglary serve as a valid predicate?

(a) Yes, if the court finds that Dana's purpose in committing the burglary was to obtain the stimulant to save Chris, which is an independent purpose collateral to causing injury. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because burglary is universally enumerated as a strict liability felony that never merges with homicide under any circumstances.
(c) No, because her injection of Chris directly caused the death, meaning the burglary must merge into the homicide as a matter of law.
(d) No, because the independent purpose doctrine only allows felony murder charges when the underlying felony is a non-violent property crime.
(e) No, because Dana's status as a co-conspirator means the burglary merges into the drug conspiracy rather than the homicide.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under the merger doctrine, a felony does not merge into the homicide (and can thus serve as a predicate for felony murder) if it has an "independent felonious purpose" that is not purely assaultive. Dana's burglary to steal drugs has an independent collateral purpose. (b) is wrong because while burglary is often enumerated, courts still apply merger limits if the burglary's sole purpose was assaultive. (c) is wrong because direct causation does not automatically trigger merger; the inquiry focuses on the purpose of the underlying felony. (d) is wrong because the independent purpose doctrine is not strictly limited to non-violent property crimes. (e) is wrong because felonies merge with the homicide, not with collateral conspiracy charges.
