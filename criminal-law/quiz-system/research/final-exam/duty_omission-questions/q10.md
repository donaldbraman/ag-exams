**Q10.** Assume Dana is charged with felony murder for Chris's death, using the veterinary clinic burglary as the predicate felony. Under the merger doctrine (independent felonious purpose test), can the burglary serve as the predicate?

(a) Yes, because the burglary was committed with the independent felonious purpose of stealing drugs, not physically assaulting or injuring Chris. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because the merger doctrine only applies when the predicate felony is committed directly against an acting law enforcement officer.
(c) No, because the burglary was an integral part of the sequence of events that directly led to Chris's physical cardiac injury.
(d) No, because the burglary was completely disconnected in time and space from the location where the actual homicide eventually occurred.
(e) No, because property crimes are categorically excluded from serving as predicate offenses for felony murder liability in all modern jurisdictions.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Under the merger doctrine, a predicate felony must have an independent felonious purpose from the homicide. Stealing drugs is a property crime distinct from the physical assault on Chris. (b) is wrong because the merger doctrine applies generally, not just to crimes involving law enforcement victims. (c) is wrong because the burglary targeted the clinic's property, making it legally distinct from the assaultive act against Chris. (d) is wrong because physical distance does not automatically dictate the legal merger analysis. (e) is wrong because property crimes (like burglary or robbery) are classic, permissible predicate offenses for felony murder.
