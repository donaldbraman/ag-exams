(a) Argument-for: This relies on the "in the abstract" test for inherently dangerous felonies. Under this approach, courts examine the elements of the statute rather than the specific facts of the defendant's conduct. Because a general statute criminalizing the distribution of controlled substances could theoretically cover a physician or pharmacist committing a safe regulatory violation, the crime can be committed without endangering human life. Therefore, it is not an inherently dangerous felony and cannot serve as a predicate for felony murder.
(b) Argument-for: A defendant could argue that the felony murder rule is essentially a proxy for depraved heart murder, which requires extreme indifference to human life. Under the Model Penal Code approach, the commission of a felony does not automatically trigger strict liability for a resulting death, but merely creates a rebuttable presumption of recklessness and extreme indifference. Therefore, the defense would argue the prosecution still ultimately bears the burden to prove this mens rea.
(c) Argument-for: The defense could argue that proximate causation is a necessary element of felony murder, meaning the death must be a direct and foreseeable result of the defendant's actions. When the victim voluntarily chooses to inject a lethal dose of drugs, this volitional act by a third party breaks the causal chain. Thus, the voluntary consumption serves as an unforeseeable, superseding intervening act that legally shields the original distributor from homicide liability.
(d) Argument-for: The defense could argue that felony murder strictly requires the death to occur within the *res gestae* (the time, place, and causal sequence) of the underlying felony. The legal act of "distribution" is fully complete the moment the drugs are physically handed over to the buyer. Because the injection and subsequent overdose death occurred chronologically after the felony had successfully terminated, the death legally did not occur "during the commission" of the predicate crime.
(e) Argument-for: The defense could invoke the merger doctrine (often known as the *Ireland* rule), which limits felony murder by requiring the predicate felony to have an independent felonious purpose. Because the act of distributing a highly lethal drug is inherently dangerous and essentially assaultive in nature, its purpose cannot be separated from the risk of serious bodily injury or death it creates. Therefore, the distribution charge merges with the resulting homicide, precluding a felony murder conviction.
