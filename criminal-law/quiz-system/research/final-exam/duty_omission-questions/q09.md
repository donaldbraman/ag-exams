**Q9.** Assume Alba is charged with homicide. Did Dane's forced entry into the apartment sever the causal chain between Alba's initial drug distribution and the death?

(a) No, because a neighbor's intervention to render medical aid is a foreseeable response to an emergency and does not break the causal chain. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because Dane's property destruction was an independent, unlawful act that superseded the prior drug sale.
(c) No, because proximate cause is automatically established whenever factual causation is proven.
(d) Yes, because it is highly extraordinary for an off-duty paramedic to live next door to a stash house.
(e) No, because the Model Penal Code eliminates the concept of intervening causes entirely.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct because an intervening act only severs legal causation if it is highly extraordinary, independent, and unforeseeable. A neighbor attempting to render medical aid during a crisis is a dependent, highly foreseeable intervening cause. (b) is wrong because the law encourages rescue, and reasonable property damage to save a life does not constitute a superseding criminal act. (c) is wrong because legal cause is a separate normative inquiry from factual cause; both must be proven. (d) is wrong because the foreseeability standard applies to the general nature of the intervention (a neighbor helping), not the specific profession of the neighbor. (e) is wrong because while the MPC uses a "too remote or accidental" standard, it still evaluates whether intervening events disrupt the just bearing of liability.
