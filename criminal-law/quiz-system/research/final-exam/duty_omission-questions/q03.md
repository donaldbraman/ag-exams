**Q3.** Assume, whether or not Alex owed a duty, Alex is charged with murder and argues that Dana's forceful injection of the cardiac stimulant severed the causal chain between Alex's acts and Chris's death. How will a court applying common law proximate cause likely rule?

(a) Severed, because Dana injecting stolen animal-grade stimulants was an independent intervening cause that was highly extraordinary and unforeseeable. <!-- correct -->
(b) Severed, because any subsequent medical intervention by a third party automatically breaks the chain of proximate causation as a matter of law.
(c) Not severed, because Dana's intervention was a dependent cause set in motion by Alex providing the original overdose-inducing dose.
(d) Not severed, because the common law holds defendants strictly liable for any harm that ultimately befalls a victim after an initial poisoning.
(e) Not severed, because Dana was a co-conspirator and the acts of all co-conspirators are automatically attributed to the initial wrongdoer.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. An independent, superseding intervening cause severs proximate causation. Dana stealing animal-grade cardiac stimulants and breaking into the cabin to force an injection is highly extraordinary and unforeseeable. (b) is wrong because dependent medical interventions (e.g., normal ambulance care) do not automatically sever the chain. (c) is wrong because a reckless injection of veterinary drugs goes far beyond a dependent, foreseeable response to an overdose. (d) is wrong because the common law uses proximate cause limitations, not strict liability for subsequent harms. (e) is wrong because co-conspirator liability applies to substantive offenses, not the factual mechanics of causation.
