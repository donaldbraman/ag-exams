**Q7.** Assume, whether or not Alex is liable for the burglary, Dana claims necessity for breaking into the veterinary clinic to save Chris's life. The prosecution argues her fault in supplying the chemicals defeats the defense. Under the MPC, how does her fault affect the defense?

(a) It survives if her fault in creating the crisis involves a lower mental state than the mens rea required for the burglary. <!-- correct -->
(b) It completely bars the defense, because any fault in creating an emergency strictly precludes asserting necessity under all modern frameworks.
(c) It completely bars the defense, because supplying precursor chemicals for illicit opioid manufacturing constitutes an inherently dangerous strict liability felony.
(d) It survives only if she can prove by clear and convincing evidence that she did not act purposefully in creating the overdose.
(e) It survives without limitation, because the MPC does not consider a defendant's prior fault when evaluating the choice of evils defense.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** (a) is correct. Under the MPC's fault-calibration rule, a defendant who is at fault in creating the emergency is not absolutely barred from asserting necessity; the defense survives if their fault level is lower than the mens rea required for the charged offense. (b) is wrong because this describes the common law strict bar, not the MPC approach. (c) is wrong because strict liability does not categorically bar the MPC necessity defense framework. (d) is wrong because the standard compares mental states, not a clear and convincing evidence burden regarding purpose. (e) is wrong because the MPC expressly limits the defense based on the defendant's level of fault.
