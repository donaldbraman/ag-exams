**Q4.** Assume William is prosecuted under federal drug distribution statutes and raises a necessity defense, arguing his scheme prevented greater gang violence. Will his necessity defense succeed?

(a) Yes, because William's policy choice to contain gang violence clearly avoided a greater harm than the localized drug dealing caused.
(b) Yes, because under the common law standard, William subjectively believed he had no reasonable legal alternative to stop the bloodshed.
(c) No, because the federal legislature has already classified heroin as a prohibited substance, absolutely preempting judicial re-weighing of the harms. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because the necessity defense is categorically unavailable to law enforcement officers acting under color of official government authority.
(e) No, because William was at fault in creating the initial surge in gang violence that necessitated the creation of the Free Zone.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** Under the absolute legislative preemption limit to necessity, when a legislature (here, Congress) has already weighed the competing harms by classifying a substance as prohibited, courts cannot second-guess that judgment. The defense is categorically barred regardless of the facts. Option (a) fails because factual harm-weighing is preempted by the statute. Option (b) fails because necessity requires an objectively reasonable belief, and preemption overrides it anyway. Option (d) is factually incorrect; no categorical bar prevents police from claiming necessity if otherwise applicable. Option (e) is factually unsupported; William did not create the initial gang surge, he only reacted to it.
