**Q7.** Assume it is established that Xavier drew his weapon legally, but the jurisdiction strictly applies the imminence requirement. Can Xavier successfully claim perfect self-defense for shooting Yasmin?

(a) No, because Yasmin's clear surrender and retreat neutralized the immediate threat, failing the imminence requirement for self-defense. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because Yasmin was the initial aggressor and an armed robber, which permanently forfeited her right to be free from defensive force.
(c) No, because Xavier's hands were shaking, demonstrating that his use of force was motivated by improper vengeance rather than necessity.
(d) Yes, because Yasmin still held the gun in her hand, satisfying the requirement that the threat of death remain objectively continuous.
(e) No, because Xavier failed to verbally warn Yasmin before pulling the trigger, which is a prerequisite when using deadly force on an intruder.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Perfect self-defense requires that the threat of unlawful deadly force be imminent (immediate) at the moment defensive force is used. By lowering her weapon, backing away, and yelling her surrender (Fact 9), Yasmin neutralized the immediate threat. Shooting her after she retreated fails the imminence requirement. (b) is wrong because an initial aggressor does not permanently forfeit their rights; if they clearly communicate withdrawal, the victim's right to use deadly force ends. (c) relies on a misinterpretation of facts; shaking hands indicate fear or adrenaline, not necessarily vengeance. (d) fails because merely holding a gun at one's side while backing away and surrendering does not constitute an objectively imminent threat of death. (e) is incorrect because the law does not impose a strict requirement to give a verbal warning before using justified deadly force in self-defense.
