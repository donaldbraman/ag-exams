## Issue 1 — grounding

**Q11.** Yasmin seeks to mount an insanity defense for the gas station incident based on her drug-induced psychotic state (Fact 11). Does her condition satisfy the threshold "mental disease or defect" element?

(a) No, because a psychotic state caused by recent voluntary intoxication does not qualify as a legal mental disease or defect for the defense. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because her severe psychotic state genuinely destroyed her ability to perceive reality, regardless of its immediate chemical cause.
(c) No, because hallucinations alone constitute solely a personality disorder, which is explicitly excluded from standard insanity definitions.
(d) Yes, but only if she can prove that the hallucinogenic pills she ingested had an unexpected and disproportionate effect on her mind.
(e) No, because she swallowed the pills to cope with physical pain rather than experiencing a spontaneous, naturally occurring break from reality.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** The insanity defense requires that the defendant suffer from a qualifying "mental disease or defect." Conditions caused or substantially aggravated by recent voluntary intoxication are generally excluded by statute or case law from qualifying as a mental disease for the purposes of the affirmative defense. Yasmin voluntarily swallowed hallucinogenic pills (Fact 11), causing the psychosis. (b) is wrong because the legal definition of mental disease cares deeply about the cause; voluntary intoxication is carved out. (c) uses incorrect medical/legal framing; hallucinations are psychotic symptoms, not a "personality disorder." (d) fails because an unexpected effect from a massive dose of voluntarily ingested illegal drugs does not transform the resulting state into a legal mental disease. (e) misses the point; the reason she took the drugs (pain) doesn't change the fact that voluntary intoxication is legally disqualified from supporting an insanity defense.
