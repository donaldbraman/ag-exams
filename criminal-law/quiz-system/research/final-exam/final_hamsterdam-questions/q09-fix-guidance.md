## Issue 1 — audit

**Q09.** Under the *Pinkerton* doctrine, is Victor liable as a co-conspirator for Xavier's shooting of Yasmin?

(a) Yes, because armed violence is a reasonably foreseeable consequence of a drug distribution conspiracy and furthered the goal of protecting the stash. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because Victor only agreed to act as a lookout and never explicitly conspired to commit any form of physical assault or homicide.
(c) Yes, because Victor's physical presence at the scene satisfies the strict liability requirement for co-conspirator actions in all cases.
(d) No, because Xavier's decision to shoot a retreating robber was a personal act of unreasonable fear that fell outside the scope of the agreement.
(e) Yes, because Victor failed to affirmatively physically intervene to stop Xavier from pulling the trigger when Yasmin attempted to surrender.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** *Pinkerton* liability makes a conspirator liable for the substantive offenses committed by co-conspirators if those offenses are in furtherance of the conspiracy and reasonably foreseeable as a necessary or natural consequence of the unlawful agreement. Violence to protect a drug stash from an armed robber is a highly foreseeable consequence of a heroin distribution conspiracy. (b) fails because *Pinkerton* specifically holds conspirators liable for crimes they did not explicitly agree to, provided they are foreseeable. (c) is wrong because *Pinkerton* requires foreseeability and furtherance, not strict liability based solely on physical presence. (d) is incorrect because even if Xavier's fear was unreasonable, shooting a robber to protect the conspiracy's drugs still furthers the enterprise and falls within the foreseeable risks of the drug trade. (e) misstates the law; co-conspirator liability does not require an affirmative failure to intervene.
