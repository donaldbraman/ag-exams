## Issue 1 — audit

**Q10.** Assume for this question that conspiracy doctrines do not apply. Is Victor guilty of the shooting as an accomplice under traditional aiding and abetting rules?

(a) No, because there is no evidence Victor aided the shooting with the specific purpose of facilitating Xavier's use of deadly physical force. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because by acting as a lookout, Victor knowingly provided substantial assistance to the overall criminal enterprise and its activities.
(c) No, because Victor did not provide Xavier with the pistol or otherwise physically contribute to the physical act of shooting Yasmin.
(d) Yes, because Victor had a legal duty to prevent his business partner from using excessive force and failed to act when Xavier shot.
(e) No, because Xavier's unreasonable fear broke the chain of causation between Victor's lookout duties and the ultimate shooting incident.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Traditional accomplice liability requires that the defendant aid or encourage the principal with the specific purpose of facilitating the target offense. While Victor was a lookout for the drug sales, there are no facts indicating he aided or encouraged Xavier with the purpose of shooting the surrendering Yasmin. (b) confuses accomplice liability with conspiracy; knowing assistance to the overall enterprise is insufficient if he lacks the purpose to facilitate the specific shooting. (c) is incorrect because physical contribution is not required; verbal encouragement could suffice, but Victor provided neither for the shooting. (d) is wrong because business partners do not have a recognized legal duty to prevent each other from committing crimes. (e) is legally incoherent; accomplice liability is based on the accessory's own intent and aid, not proximate causation broken by the principal's state of mind.
