**Q7.** Assume that, whether or not he had a duty to retreat, Xavier claims self-defense for shooting Yasmin after she backed away. Does his claim satisfy the imminence requirement?

(a) Yes, because Yasmin still held the revolver in her hand, creating a continuing condition of generalized danger for the entire Free Zone.
(b) Yes, because a defender's genuine belief that a surrender is a trick automatically satisfies the objective imminence requirement for self-defense.
(c) No, because Yasmin had lowered her weapon, raised an empty hand, stepped back, and announced surrender, meaning the threat of harm was no longer immediate. <!-- correct -->
(d) No, because the imminence requirement strictly mandates that the attacker must have already fired a shot before the defender may return fire.
(e) No, because Xavier's hands were shaking, objectively indicating that he acted out of unreasonable panic rather than a calculated assessment of an imminent threat.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** Self-defense requires an imminent (immediate) threat of unlawful force. Once Yasmin lowered her gun, stepped back, and verbally surrendered, the immediate threat of harm ceased. The law requires the threat to be happening right then. Option (a) fails because a generalized continuing danger is insufficient to satisfy strict imminence. Option (b) is wrong because imminence is an objective temporal requirement; a subjective, speculative fear of a future trick does not make a threat imminent. Option (d) incorrectly states the law; a defender need not wait to be shot at before defending themselves. Option (e) conflates Xavier's physical panic with the objective absence of an imminent threat from Yasmin.
