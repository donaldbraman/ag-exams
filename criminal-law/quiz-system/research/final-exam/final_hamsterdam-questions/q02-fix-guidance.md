**Q2.** Assume Victor is charged with possession of the heroin locked in Xavier's safe. Does Victor satisfy the elements of constructive possession?

(a) Yes, because his role as a lookout and director of buyers demonstrates his power and intent to exercise control over the drugs. <!-- correct -->
(b) No, because his physical inability to unlock the safe precludes the necessary immediate dominion and control for constructive possession.
(c) Yes, because the bilateral agreement to split profits automatically establishes joint physical possession of the contraband in the safe.
(d) No, because Xavier's exclusive possession of the key demonstrates that Victor lacked the requisite intent to distribute the heroin.
(e) Yes, but only if the prosecution can prove Victor physically handled the drugs at some point before Xavier locked them away.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Constructive possession requires the power and intent to exercise dominion and control over contraband, either directly or through others. Victor's actions—standing guard over the safe, directing buyers, and warning of rivals (Fact 4)—show he exercised control over the drug distribution process, satisfying the elements of constructive possession even without the key. (b) is wrong because constructive possession specifically covers situations where the defendant lacks immediate physical capability but retains functional control. (c) confuses legal concepts; an agreement creates conspiracy liability, but constructive possession requires power and intent, not just a profit split, and it is distinct from actual physical possession. (d) fails because Xavier holding the key does not negate Victor's shared power and intent to control the enterprise's stash. (e) is incorrect because constructive possession explicitly eliminates the need to prove physical handling.
