**Q8.** Assume Yasmin dies from the gunshot wound and Xavier is charged with murder. In a jurisdiction that follows the California approach to imperfect self-defense, how does Xavier's genuine belief that her surrender was a trick affect his liability?

(a) It fully acquits him of all homicide charges because a genuine subjective fear for one's life satisfies the reasonable-person standard.
(b) It reduces the charge to voluntary manslaughter because an honest but unreasonable belief in the need for deadly force negates malice. <!-- correct -->
(c) It reduces the charge to involuntary manslaughter because Xavier acted with gross negligence in evaluating Yasmin's physical surrender.
(d) It has no legal effect because imperfect self-defense is only available if the defendant was the initial aggressor in the conflict.
(e) It has no legal effect because an unreasonable belief provides no defense to murder under any jurisdiction's modern self-defense framework.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** Under California's imperfect self-defense doctrine, a defendant who honestly but unreasonably believes deadly force is necessary is entitled to a partial defense. This genuine belief negates malice aforethought, which reduces a murder charge to voluntary manslaughter. Option (a) is incorrect because an unreasonable belief cannot result in a full acquittal (which requires objective reasonableness). Option (c) is wrong because the mitigation is to voluntary, not involuntary, manslaughter. Option (d) fails because imperfect self-defense is not restricted to initial aggressors. Option (e) is incorrect because California and a minority of other jurisdictions explicitly recognize this partial defense.
