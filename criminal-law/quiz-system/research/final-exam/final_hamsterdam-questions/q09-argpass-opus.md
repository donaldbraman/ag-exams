**Q9.** Assume Victor is charged with the shooting of Yasmin under a Pinkerton theory of liability. Is Victor guilty of the shooting?

(a) Yes, because the shooting of an armed rival is a reasonably foreseeable consequence of a violent drug distribution conspiracy. <!-- correct -->
(b) Yes, because Victor's act of standing guard over the safe constituted direct participation in the physical actus reus of the shooting.
(c) No, because Victor did not possess the specific intent to kill or injure Yasmin when he agreed to sell the heroin.
(d) No, because Xavier's decision to shoot after Yasmin explicitly surrendered constituted an independent, completely unforeseeable intervening act.
(e) No, because Pinkerton liability only applies to substantive offenses that were explicitly planned during the formation of the initial agreement.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Under the Pinkerton doctrine, a conspirator is liable for the substantive offenses committed by a co-conspirator if those crimes are in furtherance of the conspiracy and are a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the unlawful agreement. Armed violence, especially against robbers, is widely held to be reasonably foreseeable in a drug distribution conspiracy. Option (b) is wrong because Victor did not directly participate in the shooting actus reus. Option (c) fails because Pinkerton dispenses with the need for the defendant to hold the specific intent for the target offense. Option (d) is incorrect; while the shooting was legally unjustified, an overreaction during a drug robbery is highly foreseeable. Option (e) is false; Pinkerton covers foreseeable unplanned crimes.
