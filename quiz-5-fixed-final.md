## Stem 1: The Shelter Standoff

### Facts

You are an assistant public defender. Your supervising attorney hands you a case file.

**"We have a complex case involving three co-defendants. I need you to analyze the defense options for each. Here's what the investigation established."**

1. Philippa Foot is a 34-year-old social worker who operates a small emergency shelter for unhoused individuals in a converted warehouse in Portland, Oregon. The city issued a condemnation notice for the building, giving Foot thirty days to vacate. Foot believed that closing the shelter in winter would expose the twelve residents to life-threatening cold, and no alternative shelter beds were available anywhere in the city. On the day the condemnation order took effect, Foot refused to vacate and continued operating the shelter in violation of the order.

2. Andrey Markov, one of the shelter residents, has a documented history of paranoid schizophrenia. His psychiatric records confirm that he experiences command hallucinations and delusional episodes. On the evening of Day 15 of the standoff, Markov became convinced that a city building inspector, Abraham Wald, was a "government assassin" sent to poison the shelter's water supply. When Wald arrived the next morning to conduct a routine inspection, Markov confronted him in the doorway, pushed him to the ground, and struck him twice, causing a fractured wrist and bruised ribs. Markov later told police he believed he was "stopping an assassination." Markov's treating psychiatrist, Dr. Ayanna Cole, is prepared to testify that at the time of the assault, Markov was in an acute psychotic episode driven by his schizophrenia, that he genuinely believed Wald intended to kill the shelter residents, and that while Markov understood he was striking a person, he could not appreciate that his actions were wrong. Markov has also been diagnosed with antisocial personality disorder.

3. Oliver Wendell Holmes is Foot's partner. He is not a resident of the shelter but visits regularly. Holmes has a prior conviction for assault. On Day 18 of the standoff, a process server named Lisa Chen arrived at the shelter to deliver an eviction notice. Lisa approached the front entrance, where Holmes was standing. Lisa was carrying a clipboard and an envelope. She identified herself and said she was there to deliver legal papers. Holmes, who had heard Foot say earlier that day that "the city is going to send people to drag us out," believed Lisa was the advance team for a forced removal. Holmes shoved Lisa backward off the front steps, causing her to fall and break her arm. Lisa was five feet, two inches tall and weighed approximately 120 pounds. Holmes is six feet, one inch and weighs 210 pounds. Lisa made no threatening gestures or statements.

4. On Day 20 of the standoff, Holmes received a phone call from his former associate, Friedrich Nietzsche. Nietzsche told Holmes: "I know where your mother lives. If you don't bring me $5,000 by Friday, I'm going to hurt her bad." Holmes believed Nietzsche was serious because Nietzsche had previously hospitalized another person who owed him money. The following day, Holmes stole $5,000 in cash from the shelter's donation safe and delivered it to Nietzsche.

5. On Day 22, a city worker was killed during the forced eviction of the shelter. Assume, counterfactually, that Holmes shoved the city worker off the loading dock because Nietzsche had threatened to kill Holmes's mother if Holmes did not "make sure nobody gets into the shelter," and that the fall resulted in the worker's death.

6. **This jurisdiction follows the common law for necessity and duress. It follows the Model Penal Code for the insanity defense. For self-defense, unless otherwise specified, it applies an objective reasonableness standard with a duty to retreat for deadly force, a castle doctrine limited to the defendant's own dwelling, and does not recognize imperfect self-defense.**

### Questions

**Q1.** Your supervisor asks you to evaluate Foot's strongest defense for her continued operation of the shelter in violation of the condemnation order (Facts 1 and 6). Which of the following is the most accurate analysis?

(a) Foot has a strong necessity defense because the harm avoided (twelve people exposed to life-threatening cold) clearly outweighs the harm caused (occupying a condemned building), the threat was imminent, and no alternative shelter beds were available. <!-- correct -->

(b) Foot has a strong duress defense because the threat of freezing to death compelled her to continue operating the shelter, and a person of ordinary firmness would have yielded to the same pressure.

(c) Foot has a strong necessity defense because under the MPC, the harm avoided need only be "greater than" the harm caused, and protecting twelve lives easily satisfies that lower threshold.

(d) Foot does not have a viable necessity defense because she is automatically deemed at fault for creating the emergency solely by operating a shelter in a building that was later condemned, and the common law bars the defense when the defendant is at fault.

(e) Foot does not have a viable necessity defense because the mere existence of general building safety codes and condemnation procedures categorically preempts the necessity defense by foreclosing any judicial balancing of harms.

**Answer:** (a)

**Explanation:** Necessity is the correct defense here because Foot faces a choice of evils rather than coercion by a human actor. Under the common law (Fact 6), a defendant must show: (1) the harm avoided clearly outweighs the harm caused, (2) the threat is imminent, (3) no reasonable legal alternative exists, and (4) the defendant was not at fault in creating the emergency. These elements are satisfied here. Option (b) is incorrect because duress requires a threat of force from another person, not a natural condition like freezing weather. Option (c) is incorrect because Fact 6 specifies the jurisdiction follows the common law for necessity, not the MPC. Option (d) is incorrect because lawfully operating a shelter prior to its condemnation does not automatically constitute culpable fault in creating the weather emergency itself. Option (e) is incorrect because general building codes are not the type of particularized, specific legislative choice required to preempt the necessity defense.

**Q2.** Your supervisor asks whether Foot's necessity defense against the condemnation violation charge has any impact on the potential liability of volunteers who helped Foot keep the shelter open during the standoff (Facts 1 and 6). Under common law doctrine, which of the following best explains the relationship between Foot's defense and the volunteers' liability?

(a) The volunteers cannot share in Foot's defense because necessity is a personal excuse, meaning each volunteer must independently establish their own excuse defense.
(b) If Foot establishes a successful necessity defense, her act is justified rather than excused; therefore, the volunteers who assisted in that act share in the justification and avoid accomplice liability. <!-- correct -->
(c) The volunteers face accomplice liability regardless of Foot's defense because common law accomplice liability attaches upon purposeful assistance in the prohibited conduct, precluding the transfer of any principal's defense.
(d) The volunteers share in Foot's defense only if each independently satisfies the elements of necessity, because derivative justification requires every participant to independently prove they faced a choice of evils.
(e) The volunteers share in the defense because common law treats necessity as a lesser-evils excuse, and all personal excuses automatically transfer to accomplices who act with the same purpose as the principal.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** Necessity is a justification defense (a "choice of evils"), meaning the defendant's conduct is deemed legally permissible under the circumstances. Because justifications attach to the act itself rather than the actor, an accomplice who assists in a justified act commits no crime and derives the benefit of the justification. Option (a) is incorrect because necessity is a justification, not a personal excuse. Option (c) is incorrect because accomplice liability requires a legally wrongful act by the principal; if the act is justified, there is no crime to aid. Option (d) is incorrect because accomplices to a justified act do not need to independently satisfy the primary justification's elements to avoid derivative liability. Option (e) is incorrect because necessity is a justification, not an excuse, and common law excuses do not automatically transfer to accomplices.

**Q3.** Your supervisor turns to Holmes's liability for shoving Lisa Chen (Fact 3). Holmes claims self-defense. Under the common law standard of self-defense (as applied in *People v. Goetz*), which of the following is the most accurate analysis?

(a) Holmes has a valid self-defense claim because he honestly believed Lisa was part of a forced-removal team, and an honest belief in the need for force is sufficient to establish self-defense under the common law.

(b) Holmes does not have a valid self-defense claim because a reasonable person in Holmes's situation—even after hearing Foot's warning about forced removal—would not have believed force was necessary to defend against an imminent use of unlawful force when faced with a small, unarmed woman identifying herself as a process server. <!-- correct -->

(c) Holmes has a valid self-defense claim because Foot's earlier statement that "the city is going to send people to drag us out" constitutes the kind of prior threat information that automatically makes any subsequent belief in danger objectively reasonable.

(d) Holmes does not have a valid self-defense claim solely because he failed to retreat before using force, and the common law imposes a duty to retreat before any use of force.

(e) Holmes does not have a valid self-defense claim because his prior conviction for assault makes him a "person of violent character" who is categorically barred from raising self-defense under the common law.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** Self-defense requires an objectively reasonable belief that force was necessary against an imminent use of unlawful force. The standard asks what a reasonable person in the defendant's situation would have believed (*People v. Goetz*). Holmes's "situation" legally incorporates his knowledge of Foot's prior warning. However, a reasonable person who hears a warning about forced removal, then encounters a small, unarmed woman who verbally identifies herself as a process server, would not conclude that an imminent use of unlawful force is underway. Therefore, his belief was objectively unreasonable. Option (a) is incorrect because the common law requires objective reasonableness, not merely an honest (purely subjective) belief. Option (c) is incorrect because while prior threat information is incorporated into the circumstances, it does not "automatically" make a subsequent belief objectively reasonable. Option (d) is incorrect because the common law duty to retreat applies only before using deadly force, and Holmes used non-deadly force. Option (e) is incorrect because a prior assault conviction does not categorically bar a defendant from raising a self-defense claim.

**Q4.** Returning to this jurisdiction's default rules (Fact 6), your supervisor asks about Holmes's claim that he stole the $5,000 under duress (Fact 4). Under the common law, which of the following is the most accurate analysis?

(a) Holmes has a valid duress defense because Nietzsche threatened serious bodily injury to Holmes's mother (a close relation), the threat was credible based on Nietzsche's history, and the common law's imminence requirement is satisfied whenever the threatening party has a documented pattern of carrying out threats, regardless of any deadline that places the threatened harm days in the future.

(b) Holmes does not have a valid duress defense because duress requires a threat directed at the defendant personally, and the common law does not extend the defense to threats against third parties, including family members.

(c) Holmes does not have a valid duress defense because the threat was not imminent -- Nietzsche gave Holmes until Friday, and the common law requires a present and immediate threat, not one that will materialize days later. <!-- correct -->

(d) Holmes does not have a valid duress defense because duress is categorically unavailable for theft crimes under the common law, which limits the defense to offenses against persons.

(e) Holmes has a valid duress defense because a threat need not be imminent so long as it is credible and specific, and Nietzsche's history of violence makes the threat both credible and specific enough to satisfy the common law standard.

**Answer:** (c)

**Explanation:** Under the common law (Fact 6), duress requires (1) a threat of death or serious bodily injury, (2) the threat is imminent, (3) the threat targets the defendant or a close relation, and (4) no reasonable legal alternative exists. Nietzsche's threat to "hurt her bad" satisfies the threat threshold and the target requirement (Holmes's mother is a close relation). However, the threat lacks imminence under the common law's strict standard. Nietzsche gave Holmes until Friday -- multiple days away. The common law requires a "present and immediate" threat, not one that will materialize days later. Option (a) falsely claims that the common law's imminence requirement is satisfied whenever the threatening party has a documented pattern of carrying out threats. This conflates the strict common-law rule with the *Dingwall* relaxation; under the common law, imminence requires a present and immediate threat, and a pattern of past violence does not substitute for temporal proximity. Nietzsche gave Holmes until Friday, defeating the imminence element. Option (b) states that the common law "does not extend the defense to threats against third parties, including family members" -- the false proposition is this scope limitation. The common law extends duress to threats against the defendant or a close relation, and a mother is a close relation. Option (d) fabricates a crime-type limitation -- the false proposition is that "duress is categorically unavailable for theft crimes." The common law murder bar applies only to homicide; duress is available for property crimes. Option (e) states that "a threat need not be imminent so long as it is credible and specific" -- the false proposition is this restatement of the imminence rule. The common law requires imminence as a separate element; credibility and specificity do not substitute for temporal immediacy.

**Q5.** Assume, counterfactually, that this case arose in a jurisdiction that has adopted the *Dingwall* reasoning on imminence in duress cases. Assume further that Nietzsche had explicitly commanded Holmes to steal the money from the shelter, that Nietzsche had been threatening Holmes for months, and that Holmes reasonably believed local police were unable to protect his mother. Does the *Dingwall* approach allow Holmes to satisfy the imminence requirement for duress? (Fact 4, modified.)

(a) Yes, because *Dingwall* established that any pattern of continuous, sustained, and unpredictable threats creates a reasonable perception of imminent danger, regardless of the relationship between the coercer and the defendant.
(b) No, because the *Dingwall* reasoning relaxing the strict physical imminence requirement is specifically tied to the dynamics of battering and coercive control, which do not apply to a generalized extortion dispute between former associates. <!-- correct -->
(c) Yes, because *Dingwall* eliminates the imminence requirement entirely, allowing any credible threat of future harm to satisfy the defense if the threatening party has a track record of violence.
(d) No, because the *Dingwall* exception applies only when the defendant is physically confined or restrained by the threatening party at the exact moment the crime is committed.
(e) No, because the *Dingwall* exception is strictly limited to cases where the threatened harm is directed at the defendant personally, and cannot be invoked based on threats to a third party.

**Answer:** (b)

**Explanation:** Under *United States v. Dingwall*, the strict physical proximity and imminence requirements of duress are relaxed for defendants experiencing the effects of battering or coercive control. The specific psychological dynamics of battering can create a reasonable perception of imminent danger even when the coercer is physically absent. However, courts do not extend this exception to generalized criminal extortion or associate disputes, as doing so would swallow the traditional strict imminence rule. Thus, (b) is correct. Option (a) falsely broadens *Dingwall* into a blanket exception for any sustained threats. Option (c) falsely asserts *Dingwall* eliminates the imminence requirement entirely. Option (d) falsely claims physical confinement is required; *Dingwall* expressly held the batterer's physical presence or confinement is not necessary. Option (e) falsely states that duress under *Dingwall* cannot be based on threats to third parties; traditional duress principles covering threats to close relatives still apply.

**Q6.** Your supervisor now turns to Markov's case (Fact 2). Markov is charged with second-degree assault for attacking Wald. Based on the investigation, Markov struck Wald while acting under a schizophrenic delusion that Wald was a government assassin trying to poison the residents. Afterward, Markov told police, "I was stopping an assassination." Dr. Cole is prepared to testify that Markov understood he was physically striking a person, but because of an acute psychotic episode, he could not appreciate that his actions were wrong. Under this jurisdiction's Model Penal Code (MPC) insanity framework, which of the following is the most accurate analysis of Markov's defense?

(a) Markov has a strong insanity defense because he did not know the nature and quality of his act -- he thought he was stopping an assassination, so he did not understand that he was striking an innocent person.
(b) Markov does not have a viable insanity defense because his post-assault statement to police -- "I was stopping an assassination" -- demonstrates that he understood the physical nature of his actions and therefore knew what he was doing was wrong.
(c) Markov does not have a viable insanity defense because paranoid schizophrenia is a condition "manifested only by repeated antisocial conduct" and is therefore excluded from the definition of mental disease or defect under MPC Section 4.01(2).
(d) Markov has a strong insanity defense because, as a result of his schizophrenia (a qualifying mental disease), he lacked substantial capacity to appreciate the wrongfulness of his conduct -- he understood he was striking a person but believed the act was morally justified to save lives. <!-- correct -->
(e) Markov has a strong insanity defense because his schizophrenia entirely destroyed his capacity to control his behavior, satisfying the irresistible impulse test -- he could not have stopped himself from attacking Wald even if he had known the attack was wrong.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** Under MPC § 4.01, a defendant is not responsible if, due to a mental disease or defect, he lacks "substantial capacity" to "appreciate" the criminality/wrongfulness of his conduct. Markov suffers from a qualifying mental disease (schizophrenia) and, because his delusion led him to believe he was saving lives, he lacked substantial capacity to appreciate the wrongfulness of his act. (a) is incorrect because the "nature and quality" test refers to understanding the physical act itself, which Markov understood (he knew he was striking a person). (b) is incorrect because understanding the physical nature of an act does not automatically equate to appreciating its wrongfulness; his statement evidences his delusion. (c) is incorrect because schizophrenia is a paradigmatic mental disease; the MPC § 4.01(2) exclusion targets psychopathy and antisocial personality disorder, not psychotic disorders. (e) is incorrect because the MPC uses the "lacks substantial capacity to conform his conduct" standard, not the "entirely destroyed" standard of the irresistible impulse test, and the facts establish a cognitive rather than volitional impairment.

**Q7.** Assume, counterfactually, that Markov's case arose in a jurisdiction that follows the federal IDRA standard rather than the MPC. Your supervisor asks how this would change the analysis (Fact 2). Which of the following is the most accurate comparison?

(a) The IDRA standard would make no difference because both the MPC and IDRA use identical cognitive tests -- both ask whether the defendant "lacked substantial capacity to appreciate the wrongfulness" of the conduct -- and Markov satisfies both.

(b) The IDRA standard would defeat Markov's defense entirely because IDRA eliminates the wrongfulness prong and requires only that the defendant was unable to appreciate the "nature and quality" of the act, which Markov understood.

(c) The IDRA standard would make the defense easier because IDRA places the burden on the prosecution to disprove insanity beyond a reasonable doubt, whereas the MPC places the burden on the defendant.

(d) The IDRA standard would make the defense harder to establish because IDRA requires that the mental disease be "severe," eliminates the volitional prong, replaces the MPC's "substantial capacity" with "unable to appreciate," and requires proof by clear and convincing evidence rather than preponderance -- but Markov's documented paranoid schizophrenia with acute psychotic episodes would likely satisfy even the stricter IDRA threshold. <!-- correct -->

(e) The IDRA standard would make the defense harder solely because IDRA adds the word "severe" to the mental disease requirement, which excludes common psychotic disorders like paranoid schizophrenia unless the episode causes total incapacitation.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** The federal IDRA (18 U.S.C. Section 17) differs from the MPC in four key ways: (1) the mental disease must be "severe," (2) the defendant must have been "unable" to appreciate wrongfulness (not merely lacking "substantial capacity"), (3) no volitional prong exists, and (4) the defendant must prove the defense by clear and convincing evidence. However, Markov's case is strong even under IDRA: paranoid schizophrenia with command hallucinations is a "severe" mental disease, and an acute psychotic episode in which the defendant genuinely believes he is stopping an assassination is likely sufficient to show he was "unable" to appreciate wrongfulness. Option (a) states that both tests use "identical cognitive tests" including "lacked substantial capacity" -- the false proposition is that IDRA uses "substantial capacity." IDRA uses "unable," which is a meaningfully higher bar than the MPC's "lacks substantial capacity." Option (b) states that IDRA "eliminates the wrongfulness prong" -- the false proposition is this elimination. IDRA retains both the nature-and-quality prong and the wrongfulness prong; it is the volitional prong that was eliminated. Option (c) states that IDRA "places the burden on the prosecution to disprove insanity" -- the false proposition is this burden allocation. IDRA shifted the burden to the defendant, requiring proof by clear and convincing evidence. Option (e) states that IDRA's "severe" requirement "excludes common psychotic disorders like paranoid schizophrenia unless the episode causes total incapacitation" -- the false proposition is that paranoid schizophrenia does not meet the severity threshold. Paranoid schizophrenia with acute psychotic episodes is the paradigmatic "severe" mental disease.

**Q8.** Assume, counterfactually, that Markov's case arose in a jurisdiction that has abolished the insanity defense and follows the mens rea model (like Kansas). (e) Markov could use his mental illness both to negate mens rea and to argue he did not know his actions were wrong, because abolition states permit such evidence for all purposes bearing on criminal responsibility.

**Answer:** (c)

**Q9.** Your supervisor asks a question about Markov's co-occurring diagnoses: Assume that the prosecution argues Markov's antisocial personality disorder -- not his schizophrenia -- drove his violent behavior. Under the MPC insanity framework that this jurisdiction follows (Facts 2 and 6), how should the defense respond?

(a) The defense should concede that the co-occurring antisocial personality disorder defeats the insanity defense because the MPC excludes any defendant who has been diagnosed with a personality disorder from raising the defense, regardless of whether they also have a qualifying mental illness.

(b) The defense should argue that both diagnoses should be treated as a single qualifying mental illness because the MPC provides that co-occurring disorders automatically merge into one legal category when they appear together in the same patient.

(c) The defense should argue that the personality disorder exclusion does not apply because Markov's antisocial personality disorder is accompanied by schizophrenia, and any co-occurring psychotic disorder automatically satisfies the causation element for all purposes, making the prosecution's argument about which disorder "drove" the behavior legally irrelevant.

(d) The defense should argue that antisocial personality disorder standing alone cannot qualify as a "mental disease or defect" under MPC Section 4.01(2), but the exclusion applies only to the threshold disease requirement -- if the qualifying disease (schizophrenia) caused the incapacity, the defense survives even if the personality disorder also contributed to Markov's behavior. <!-- correct -->

(e) The defense should concede that the co-occurring personality disorder creates an insurmountable causation problem because the prosecution need only show that the personality disorder was a but-for cause of the conduct to defeat the defense entirely, regardless of the qualifying disease's role.

**Answer:** (d)

**Explanation:** Under MPC Section 4.01(2), a condition constituting "solely a personality disorder" does not qualify as a "mental disease or defect." The word "solely" does the critical work: a personality disorder standing alone cannot qualify, but a personality disorder co-occurring with a qualifying mental illness does not disqualify the defense. The exclusion applies to the threshold requirement (is there a qualifying mental disease?), not to causation. If the qualifying disease (schizophrenia) caused the incapacity -- even if the personality disorder also contributed -- the defense survives. Option (a) states that the MPC "excludes any defendant who has been diagnosed with a personality disorder from raising the defense" -- the false proposition is this absolute bar. The MPC excludes conditions constituting *solely* a personality disorder; a defendant with both schizophrenia and a personality disorder is not excluded. Option (b) states that the MPC provides for "automatic merger" of co-occurring disorders into "one legal category" -- the false proposition is this merger doctrine, which does not exist. The disorders remain legally distinct; the question is which one caused the incapacity. Option (c) states that a co-occurring psychotic disorder "automatically satisfies the causation element for all purposes" -- the false proposition is this automatic satisfaction. The defense must still prove that the qualifying disease (schizophrenia) caused the incapacity; the presence of a qualifying disease does not eliminate the causation inquiry. Option (e) states that the prosecution "need only show that the personality disorder was a but-for cause" to "defeat the defense entirely" -- the false proposition is this but-for causation standard. The statute does not require the qualifying disease to be the sole cause; if the schizophrenia caused the incapacity, the defense survives even if the personality disorder also contributed.

**Q10.** Returning to the full fact pattern, your supervisor asks about Holmes's potential duress defense to a murder charge for the death of the city worker (Fact 5). Under the common law (Fact 6), which of the following is the most accurate analysis?

(a) Holmes has a valid duress defense because the threat was of death to his mother (a close relation), the threat was credible based on Nietzsche's prior conduct, and the common law's "murder bar" is a sentencing-phase consideration that operates only after conviction to limit a defendant's exposure, not a substantive bar at the guilt phase.

(b) Holmes does not have a valid duress defense because under the traditional common law rule, duress is categorically unavailable as a defense to any murder, including felony murder, regardless of how severe or credible the threat. <!-- correct -->

(c) Holmes has a valid duress defense because the MPC makes duress available for all crimes including murder, and the modern trend favors extending the defense to homicide cases in all jurisdictions.

(d) Holmes does not have a valid duress defense because the threat was directed at his mother rather than at Holmes himself, and the common law requires that the threat target the defendant personally before duress can be raised.

## Stem 2: State v. Kant

### Facts

1. Immanuel Kant was diagnosed with paranoid schizophrenia in 2022 by Dr. Peter Singer, Kant's treating psychiatrist, whose records document recurrent auditory hallucinations and persecutory delusions.

2. Kant's risperidone prescription expired on March 8. Dr. Singer's office was closed for the weekend; the after-hours line directed callers only to emergency rooms. Kant's insurance denied his 72-hour emergency refill. Dr. Singer's records show Kant required inpatient hospitalization in 2024 after he attempted to skip his medication for 48 hours; medical opinion was that a psychotic break was likely within 72 hours of discontinuation. Kant called the nearest emergency room and was told the hospital did not stock risperidone and could not prescribe controlled psychotropics without an on-site psychiatric admission, for which no beds were available for at least 48 hours.

3. On March 9 at 3:14 AM, Kant broke the rear window of Green Street Pharmacy and took a 30-day supply of risperidone (retail value $240). Kant left $240 in cash on the counter.

4. On March 10, Jeremy Bentham approached Kant about serving as the driver for an armored-car robbery planned for March 15. When Kant declined, Bentham stated: "Jarvis will put you in the hospital for a week — and if that doesn't convince you, your mother goes next."

5. Judith Jarvis, a former professional fighter present during the exchange, later confirmed Bentham's statement to investigators. Investigators documented two prior incidents in which Bentham had ordered beatings that sent associates to the hospital with broken limbs requiring surgical repair.

6. Kant agreed to drive. On March 15, Kant drove the crew's van to the First Federal armored-car depot.

7. During the robbery, armored-car guard Thomas Bayes drew his service pistol. Bentham, standing on the loading dock, fired a single round, striking Bayes in the chest. Bayes died at the scene. Kant remained at the wheel of the van throughout, did not exit, and did not discharge any weapon. Kant knew before the robbery that Bentham and the crew would carry firearms -- Bentham had displayed his revolver openly when recruiting him -- but Kant was not told the crew intended to use lethal force on any guard.

8. After the robbery, at the crew's warehouse, Bentham leveled his revolver at Kant's chest from six feet away and demanded Kant's full share of the proceeds. The nearest exit was fifteen feet behind Kant with no intervening cover.

9. Kant drew a concealed pistol from his waistband and shot Bentham once in the head. Bentham died instantly.

10. Kant fled to his ground-floor apartment on Elm Street, which he leased alone.

11. Within ninety minutes, Officer Learned Hand and three other officers surrounded the apartment under a valid arrest warrant and announced their identity through a loudspeaker.

12. Dr. Singer later testified that Kant was at that time experiencing an active psychotic episode and believed the responding officers' human bodies had been seized by demonic forces — that the officers remained human beings whose flesh was being used as vessels by hostile supernatural entities, which Kant believed could be driven out only by destroying the human body each demon inhabited.

13. Dr. Singer further testified that Kant's delusion convinced him that killing the possessed human host was morally required to defeat the ongoing demonic attack. Dr. Singer also testified that, at the time of the stabbing, Kant understood the penal code prohibited killing a human being, but believed the moral imperative to expel the demon from the human body it had seized overrode that legal prohibition.

14. Officer Hand executed a lawful forced entry under the warrant. Kant stabbed Officer Hand once in the thigh with a kitchen knife. Officer Hand survived.

15. A court-appointed psychiatrist, Dr. Pierre-Simon Laplace, concluded on post-arrest evaluation that Kant's psychotic symptoms at the time of the Hand encounter rendered him unable to conform his conduct to the requirements of law and substantially impaired his appreciation of the wrongfulness of the stabbing.

**Model Penal Code § 3.02 (Justification: Choice of Evils).** "(1) Conduct that the actor believes to be necessary to avoid a harm or evil to himself or to another is justifiable, provided that: (a) the harm or evil sought to be avoided by such conduct is greater than that sought to be prevented by the law defining the offense charged; . . . (b) Commitment duration following a not-guilty-by-reason-of-insanity verdict is capped at the maximum sentence the defendant could have received upon conviction; upon expiration, the acquittee must be released regardless of clinical status.

(c) In every jurisdiction that recognizes the not-guilty-by-reason-of-insanity verdict, a psychiatric security review board composed of mental-health professionals has exclusive authority to order release, and courts are categorically barred from overriding the board's decisions.

**Explanation:** (d) correctly describes both the empirical reality and the purpose of the Mutina-style instruction. The Mutina-style instruction addresses the well-documented misconception that a not-guilty-by-reason-of-insanity verdict means release, which jurors consistently overestimate (actual not-guilty-by-reason-of-insanity rate is ~0.26%).
